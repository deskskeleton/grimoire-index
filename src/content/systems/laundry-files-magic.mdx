## Mechanics

Computational demonology rests on a single dangerous premise: certain mathematical operations, when executed with sufficient precision, are not merely abstract — they constitute performative acts that alter the structure of reality. Specifically, they thin the membrane between Earth's spacetime and adjacent dimensions inhabited by entities of varying hostility. The foundational discovery, attributed independently to Alan Turing and a handful of wartime cryptographers, is that the relationship between computation and dimensional topology is lawful, reproducible, and terrifyingly scalable.

In practice this means that any sufficiently powerful computer is a potential summoning device. A smartphone running the right algorithm can open a micro-gate; a server farm executing the wrong neural-network training run can attract attention from things that regard human consciousness as a resource to be harvested. The Laundry — formally the Capital Laundry Services, a legacy front organization of the Special Operations Executive — exists to suppress unauthorized thaumic computation, manage controlled contact with extradimensional entities, and prepare for CASE NIGHTMARE GREEN: the statistically inevitable convergence point at which global computational activity exceeds the threshold the Elder Gods need to locate Earth.

Practitioners use a hybrid toolkit. Traditional summoning circles are mathematical constructs, often rendered digitally. Basilisk guns project computational patterns that destroy neurons on contact — weaponized proofs-of-concept for theorems no sane mathematician would publish. Wards are algorithmic barriers maintained by dedicated hardware. Bob Howard, the series' initial protagonist, carries a smartphone loaded with custom occult apps; his wife Mo wields a bone-white violin that is itself a bound extradimensional entity. The system is rigorously information-theoretic: entropy, bandwidth, and signal-to-noise ratios matter as much in sorcery as in telecommunications.

Later books introduce additional vectors as the approaching convergence amplifies ambient thaumic fields. Vampirism turns out to be a computationally transmissible parasitic syndrome. The Mandate, a hostile foreign intelligence service, deploys its own computational demonologists. Corporate entities attempt to monetize occult capabilities. The system's internal logic — more computation means more risk — transforms the entire trajectory of modern technological civilization into an accelerating countdown.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is sanity. Prolonged exposure to extradimensional entities degrades the practitioner's mental architecture in ways that are cumulative and only partially reversible. The Laundry tracks its operatives' K-syndrome scores — a measure of cognitive contamination — and retires those who cross critical thresholds. Mo's violin slowly consumes her emotional capacity each time she plays it. Angleton, the Laundry's most powerful asset, turns out to be something other than human, having traded his humanity for operational capability decades ago.

Physical costs scale with ambition. Minor workings — a ward, a scrying — require only computational resources and some fatigue. Major summonings demand energy inputs that can drain local power grids, and errors are catastrophically unforgiving: a misdrawn summoning geometry doesn't fizzle, it opens an uncontrolled gate. The series treats magical accidents the way a nuclear-safety manual treats criticality events — low probability, total consequence.

There is also an institutional cost. The Laundry's bureaucratic structure exists partly to slow practitioners down — mandatory paperwork, committee approvals, and interdepartmental reviews serve as procedural circuit-breakers against the human tendency to reach for the most powerful tool available. Operatives chafe against the red tape, but the red tape exists because the last person who freelanced a major working without authorization opened a gate that required a military response to close.

## Practitioner access

Access is restricted to vetted government employees and their equivalents in allied agencies. The Laundry recruits from two pools: academics and hackers who stumble onto dangerous theorems (and are given the choice between employment and memory erasure), and career civil servants who transfer in through internal channels. There is no hereditary component and no innate talent required — anyone with sufficient mathematical aptitude and security clearance can learn to do the work. The barrier is institutional, not biological.

Non-Laundry practitioners exist but are treated as security threats. Unauthorized sorcerers are tracked, recruited, or neutralized. Some religious organizations maintain their own occult traditions, operating under tacit agreements with national agencies. The series implies that every major government has its own equivalent of the Laundry, and that international occult-intelligence cooperation is as fraught as its conventional counterpart.

## Cultural role

Within the Laundry's institutional culture, computational demonology is treated as a civil-service specialty — dangerous but fundamentally a job, complete with pay grades, pension plans, and mandatory health-and-safety training. This deliberate banality is both a coping mechanism and a structural defense: framing cosmic horror as an operational problem to be managed by committee keeps practitioners grounded in a way that solo occultists historically have not been.

The broader public is unaware. The masquerade is maintained not by magical concealment but by institutional secrecy — classification, disinformation, and the sheer implausibility of the truth. The series uses this gap between public ignorance and operational reality as a source of both comedy and dread, particularly as CASE NIGHTMARE GREEN approaches and the masquerade becomes progressively harder to maintain.
