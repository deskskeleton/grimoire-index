## Mechanics

Siden is a golden, life-affirming magical force that flows from the roots of the orange tree planted by Cleolind — the legendary warrior who defeated the Nameless One a thousand years before the novel's events. Members of the Priory of the Orange Tree, a secret matriarchal order based in the South, consume the fruit of the orange tree to channel siden. This power manifests as fire magic (used in combat), warding (creating barriers against draconic corruption), healing, and the ability to sense sterren — the opposing force. Siden is explicitly associated with the Mother, a cosmic being of creation and protection, though the Priory's theology and the reality of the Mother's nature do not always align.

Sterren is the power of the Nameless One, the great fire-wyrm sealed beneath the Abyss. It corrupts everything it touches, empowering those who embrace it while eroding their humanity. Wyrms — fire-breathing, destructive dragons aligned with the Nameless One — are conduits of sterren, and humans who fall under its influence become "the risen," agents of draconic will. Where siden preserves and protects, sterren consumes and dominates.

Eastern magic operates on different principles entirely. In Seiiki and the East, practitioners draw power from celestial sources — starlight, the moon, and the movements of heavenly bodies. Tané, a dragonrider from the East, channels magic through her bond with her water dragon, a relationship built on mutual respect rather than domination. Eastern dragons are water-aligned, benevolent, and ancient, standing in stark contrast to the fire-wyrms of the West. The star-based magical tradition of the East produces abilities including water manipulation, weather influence, and communion with the sea.

The interplay between these three magical traditions — Priory siden, Nameless sterren, and Eastern celestial magic — reflects the novel's geographic and cultural divisions. Each tradition has its own theology, its own practitioners, and its own understanding of the cosmic forces at work, and none possesses the complete picture.

## Limitations and costs

Siden is dependent on the orange tree itself. If the tree were destroyed or its fruit exhausted, the Priory's magic would wane. This creates a strategic vulnerability — the order's power is literally rooted in a single location. Individual practitioners who overextend themselves suffer physical exhaustion and, in extreme cases, burnout that can leave them permanently diminished. The fruit must be periodically consumed to maintain access to siden, making it a renewable but location-bound resource.

Sterren's cost is corruption itself. Those who draw on the Nameless One's power are consumed by it — their wills eroded, their bodies transformed, their identities subsumed into service to the draconic will. There is no safe or moderate use of sterren. It is addiction and enslavement dressed as empowerment.

The cultural cost is perhaps the most significant. The division between magical traditions has hardened into religious schism and political isolation. The West venerates Galian the Deceiver as the one who defeated the Nameless One, erasing Cleolind's role and the Priory's existence from history. The East follows its own traditions and regards Western affairs with suspicion. This fragmentation means that when the Nameless One rises again, the very people who could combine their powers to stop it are separated by centuries of mistrust and theological disagreement.

## Practitioner access

Siden is accessible only to members of the Priory, and membership requires both initiation and the consumption of orange-tree fruit. The Priory recruits women with the potential for magic, training them in combat and magical arts from a young age. Men are excluded from the order's inner circle, reflecting a matriarchal tradition rooted in Cleolind's legacy. This gendered restriction is a deliberate choice by the order, not an inherent limitation of siden itself.

Eastern magical traditions are more broadly accessible but require different qualifications — dragonrider bonds are selective (the dragon chooses), and celestial magic requires training in the astronomical and spiritual disciplines of the East. In both traditions, practitioners are specialists who dedicate their lives to their craft rather than casual users of ambient power.

## Cultural role

Magic in this world is inseparable from religion and politics. The Priory's existence is itself a political secret — if the world knew that a matriarchal order of fire-wielding warriors had been secretly protecting humanity for a thousand years, it would upend the patriarchal religious narratives that underpin Western kingdoms. The Berethnet queens of Inys claim their bloodline holds the Nameless One at bay, a theological fiction that the Priory tolerates because it serves the larger goal of keeping the world safe. In the East, dragons are revered as gods and guardians, and the dragonrider tradition is woven into the fabric of governance and spiritual life. Shannon uses these divisions to explore how magical power becomes institutional power, and how the stories cultures tell about magic shape who gets to wield it.
