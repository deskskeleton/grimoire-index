## Mechanics

Divine Miracles in Black & White operate through a system where the player — a literal god — interacts with the world through a disembodied hand that can pick up objects, stroke or slap villagers, and cast miracles by drawing specific gestures over the landscape. The gesture system requires the player to trace recognizable patterns using mouse movements: a spiral might invoke a storm, a star shape might cast a healing miracle, a wavy line might summon a fireball. Each miracle has an associated gesture that must be performed with sufficient accuracy for the game to recognize the intended effect. The system is deliberately tactile, positioning the player's hand as both a divine instrument and a physical tool whose effectiveness depends on the skill of the person wielding it.

The fuel for all miracles is prayer energy, generated by worshippers who gather at the player's temple and direct their devotion toward the divine presence. Prayer energy accumulates as a resource pool that is depleted by miracle casting and replenished by continued worship. The rate of prayer generation depends on the number of worshippers, their level of devotion, and the strength of their belief — factors that are directly influenced by the god's behavior toward them. A god who consistently provides food, heals the sick, and protects from danger cultivates deep genuine faith that produces steady prayer energy. A god who terrorizes populations through fire and destruction generates frantic, fear-driven prayer that spikes intensely but may prove less stable.

Miracles span a comprehensive range of divine capabilities organized into several categories. Nature miracles manipulate weather, forests, and terrain — rain nurtures crops, storms devastate enemy settlements, and forest growth provides resources. Physical miracles involve the direct manipulation of objects and creatures — hurling boulders, creating food, healing injuries. Spiritual miracles affect belief itself — converting enemy worshippers, strengthening devotion, and projecting divine influence into contested territory. Each category contains miracles of varying power levels, with the most potent effects requiring correspondingly larger prayer energy investments.

The Creature system adds a secondary magical dimension. Each god possesses a giant animal avatar — a creature that can be trained to perform miracles independently through a behavioral learning system. The creature observes its god's actions and, through reinforcement, develops its own miraculous capabilities and moral orientation. A creature raised by a benevolent god learns to heal villagers and nurture crops. A creature raised by a malevolent god learns to eat villagers and set fires. The creature's magical development is therefore a mirror of the god's own moral trajectory, creating a recursive system where divine identity reproduces itself through its avatar.

## Limitations and costs

The fundamental limitation of Divine Miracles is their dependence on belief. A god without worshippers is a god without power — prayer energy cannot be generated without a population that acknowledges the divine presence, and without prayer energy, no miracles can be cast. This creates a vulnerability that is both strategic and philosophical: divine power is not inherent but contingent, sustained only by the continued devotion of mortal beings who could, in principle, stop believing. The game mechanizes this dependency by tying the god's area of influence directly to the presence and faith of worshippers. Regions without believers are regions where the divine hand cannot reach.

The moral-spiritual cost manifests through the alignment system that tracks the cumulative effect of the god's choices. Every action shifts the god's alignment toward good or evil, and this alignment transforms the visual presentation of the god's temple, the landscape surrounding it, the appearance of the divine hand, and the available miracle set. A purely good god inhabits a paradise of vibrant colors and gentle music. A purely evil god rules over a blighted hellscape of fire and shadow. This aesthetic transformation is not merely cosmetic — it reflects a genuine mechanical shift in available capabilities, with certain miracles becoming more or less efficient depending on alignment.

Resource expenditure extends beyond prayer energy to encompass the population itself. Sacrificial miracles — particularly those available to evil-aligned gods — allow the player to sacrifice worshippers directly for immediate bursts of power. This creates a self-consuming economy: the very resource that generates prayer energy can be burned for short-term magical gains, but each sacrifice reduces the population available for ongoing worship. A god who sacrifices too freely may find their power base eroded, creating a strategic tension between immediate magical capability and long-term sustainability.

The attention cost of the gesture system imposes a practical limitation on divine multitasking. Drawing miracle gestures requires focused mouse movement that temporarily prevents other actions — the god cannot simultaneously cast a miracle and manage the Creature, rearrange village buildings, or respond to threats in other locations. In a game where multiple settlements may need attention simultaneously and enemy gods may attack from unexpected directions, the time spent drawing gestures is time spent not doing something else. The most effective divine practitioners develop the ability to draw gestures quickly and accurately, minimizing the attention cost of each casting, but the fundamental single-threaded nature of gesture input remains a constraint.

## Practitioner access

Divine Miracles are accessible exclusively to gods — beings whose existence and power are sustained by mortal belief. The player is one such god, and the rival deity Nemesis is another, but no mortal character in Black & White can independently cast miracles. This strict division between divine practitioners and mortal worshippers is the system's defining structural feature. Mortals can pray, generating the raw material of divine power, but they cannot direct that power themselves. They are fuel, not wielders. This creates a power dynamic that the game explicitly invites the player to examine: the god is dependent on mortals for power, but mortals have no direct access to the power they generate.

The Creature occupies a unique intermediate position in this access hierarchy. As a divine avatar rather than an independent being, the Creature channels its god's power rather than possessing its own. Its miraculous capabilities are derived from and shaped by the god who trained it, making it an extension of divine will rather than a separate practitioner. However, the Creature's behavioral autonomy means it can cast miracles when the god's attention is elsewhere, providing a form of delegated divine agency that no mortal can match. The Creature is not exactly a worshipper and not exactly a god — it is a conduit through which divine power flows semi-independently.

The territorial nature of divine influence creates a geographic dimension to practitioner access. A god's power extends only over lands where they have worshippers, and the boundaries of this influence are visible as a shimmering border on the landscape. Beyond this border, the divine hand cannot reach, miracles cannot be cast, and the god's will has no effect. Expanding influence requires gaining worshippers in new territories — either by attracting them through impressive miracles visible from their current location or by conquering their existing settlements and converting the population. This territorial mechanic means that divine access is not a fixed capability but a dynamic contested space that expands and contracts with the fortunes of belief.

Within the game's cosmology, multiple gods exist simultaneously, each competing for the worship of the same mortal populations. The rivalry between gods is fundamentally a competition for believers, and therefore a competition for magical power. A god who loses worshippers to a rival loses prayer energy, miracle capability, and territorial influence in a cascading decline. This competitive structure means that practitioner access is inherently zero-sum at the population level — the same mortal cannot power two gods simultaneously, and every converted worshipper represents a transfer of magical fuel from one divine practitioner to another.

## Cultural role

The miracle system in Black & White functions primarily as a vehicle for moral philosophy explored through game mechanics. The game's central question — whether it is better to rule through love or fear — is not posed as a narrative choice but as a systemic one. Both benevolent and malevolent miracle use generate prayer energy and expand divine influence. Both paths are mechanically viable for completing the game. The difference lies in the quality of the relationship between god and worshippers, the aesthetic transformation of the world, and the player's own experience of their chosen identity. The system argues that the meaningful distinction between good and evil is not effectiveness but character — what kind of god the player becomes through the accumulated weight of their choices.

The gesture-drawing system carries cultural significance as a design statement about the nature of divine power. By requiring the player to physically perform miracles through manual gestures rather than selecting them from a menu, the game creates an embodied experience of divine agency. Drawing a rain gesture over parched crops feels like an act of nurturing. Drawing a fireball gesture over an enemy village feels like an act of destruction. The physical performance adds emotional weight to moral choices that might otherwise feel abstract, making the player's body complicit in their divine decisions. This design philosophy influenced subsequent games that attempted to make magic feel performed rather than selected.

The belief economy creates a social commentary on the relationship between power and dependency. The game makes explicit what many power structures leave implicit: that authority depends on the consent or submission of those over whom it is exercised. A god who alienates all worshippers ceases to be a god. A god who inspires genuine devotion builds a sustainable foundation of power. A god who rules through terror maintains power through constant threat rather than willing contribution, creating an inherently unstable arrangement that requires ongoing investment in intimidation. These dynamics map onto real-world discussions of political legitimacy with surprising directness.

The Creature system extends this social commentary into questions about moral reproduction. The Creature learns by observing its god, developing a moral character that mirrors the divine example. A compassionate god raises a compassionate Creature; a cruel god raises a cruel one. This mechanic argues that power shapes those who are subject to it — the Creature becomes what its god models, just as the worshippers develop cultural attitudes shaped by their divine experience. The game's world transforms to reflect its god's character not through direct manipulation but through the accumulated influence of consistent behavior, creating an environmental record of moral choices that is visible to everyone who inhabits it.
