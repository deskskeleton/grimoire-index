## Mechanics

Allomancy operates on a strict input-output model. A practitioner ("Allomancer") ingests one or more metals — usually as small flakes suspended in a vial — and then mentally activates, or "burns," a metal to draw on the power it grants. Each of the sixteen Allomantic metals confers exactly one ability, and each ability is consistent across every practitioner who has access to it. There is no improvisation at the level of effect: pewter burners get strength and endurance; tin burners get heightened senses; steel burners can push on nearby metals; iron burners pull. Combination effects exist, but they emerge from disciplined use of multiple metals rather than from creative reinterpretation.

The metals are paired conceptually. Each base metal has an "alloy" counterpart that produces a related but inverted effect. Steel pushes; iron pulls. Pewter strengthens the body; tin sharpens the senses. Brass dampens the emotions of others; zinc inflames them. This pairing structure is one of the system's most distinctive features and makes the full set of effects easier for readers to remember and predict.

Most Allomancers are "Mistings" who can burn only a single metal. A vanishingly rare practitioner — a "Mistborn" — can burn all sixteen.

## Limitations and costs

The system has three layered costs. The first is supply: a metal-burner who runs out of metal cannot use the power, and metals must be ingested in a sufficiently pure form (impure alloys produce sickness rather than power). The second is metabolic: burning metals draws on the user's body, and prolonged use produces fatigue, hunger, and eventually serious harm. The third is structural: pushes and pulls on metal obey conservation of momentum, so a lighter Allomancer pushing on a heavier object will be flung backward rather than moving the object. This last constraint produces much of the system's tactical character — Steelpushing is genuinely athletic, not free flight.

Allomancers are also visible to one another. Burning metals creates a perceptible signature that another practitioner with the right ability can detect at range, which means the system has built-in counterintelligence rules.

## Practitioner access

Allomancy is hereditary. The ability descends through certain bloodlines, originally those of nobility in the early Mistborn novels, though the system's genetic distribution becomes more complex as the series progresses. Latent Allomancers must "Snap" — undergo significant trauma — before their abilities manifest, which gates access behind an additional, often involuntary, threshold.

## Cultural role

In the original Mistborn era, Allomancy is concentrated in a noble caste who use it to maintain political and physical dominance over a non-Allomantic population. Mistings are common enough among the nobility to be tactical assets in any major house's retinue; full Mistborn are vanishingly rare and treated as strategic weapons. By the second era, Allomancy has been democratized somewhat through generations of intermarriage and is treated more as an unusual but unremarkable trait.
