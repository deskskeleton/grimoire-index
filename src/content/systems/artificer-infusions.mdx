## Mechanics

**Artificer magic** channels arcane energy through objects rather than through the caster's body or voice. The core feature is **Infusions** — the ability to temporarily imbue mundane items with magical properties. An artificer can make a suit of armor resistant to damage, turn a simple weapon into a magical one, create a bag that produces ammunition, or give boots the ability to enhance the wearer's speed.

Infusions are limited in number (the artificer knows a set number and can maintain a limited number simultaneously) and are temporary — they last until the artificer's next long rest or until they choose to end one. This creates a loadout-management game: the artificer prepares their infusions each day, distributing magical enhancements to themselves and their allies based on anticipated needs.

Beyond infusions, artificers can:

**Cast spells through tools**: Artificers use artisan's tools, thieves' tools, or alchemist's supplies as spellcasting foci. Their spell list emphasizes utility, repair, and enhancement — mending, cure wounds, fabricate, and creation-oriented magic.

**Create homunculi and constructs**: At various levels, artificers build autonomous servants — mechanical-magical constructs that serve as combat companions, scouts, or assistants. The **Steel Defender** (Battle Smith subclass) and the **Homunculus Servant** are signature creations.

**Craft magical items**: Artificers gain advantages in crafting permanent magical items, reflecting their understanding of how enchantment works at a structural level. In Eberron, this is not individual heroics but industrial process — House Cannith's creation forges mass-produce magical goods.

Subclasses specialize the artificer's approach: **Alchemist** (potions and elixirs), **Artillerist** (magical turrets and area-of-effect damage), **Battle Smith** (combat constructs and weapon enhancement), **Armorer** (power-armor-style magical suits).

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **material dependency**. Artificer magic is mediated through objects — without tools and raw materials, the artificer cannot function. Unlike a wizard who can theoretically cast spells naked, an artificer needs their toolkit.

The second limitation is **infusion capacity**. The number of active infusions is strictly limited and scales slowly with level. An artificer must choose carefully how to allocate their infusions, as each one occupies a "slot" that cannot be used for another.

The third limitation is **spell-list narrowness**. Artificers have a smaller spell list than full casters, focused on utility and support rather than raw damage or battlefield control. They compensate through versatility and item-creation, but in purely spell-to-spell comparisons, they are outmatched.

## Practitioner access

Artificers are trained professionals. In Eberron, artificer training occurs through House Cannith's workshops, independent guilds, and magical universities (notably Morgrave University in Sharn). The craft requires both arcane aptitude and practical engineering skill — not everyone with magical talent has the mechanical inclination, and vice versa. In other D&D settings where the class is available, artificers are rarer and often treated as eccentric inventors.

## Cultural role

In Eberron, artificers are the backbone of a magitech civilization. Everyday magical items — everbright lanterns, sending stones, healing potions — are mass-produced by artificer guilds. The lightning rail, airships, warforged (living constructs), and magical communication networks all depend on artificer infrastructure. House Cannith, the dragonmarked house most associated with artificers, is effectively the setting's industrial conglomerate. In other D&D settings, artificers occupy a niche role as magical tinkerers and inventors, less central to civilization but valued for their practical problem-solving abilities.
