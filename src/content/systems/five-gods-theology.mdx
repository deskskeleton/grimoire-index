## Mechanics

The Five Gods' theology produces two distinct forms of supernatural power:

**Sainthood** occurs when a god channels divine power through a willing mortal agent. The five gods — Father of Winter, Mother of Summer, Son of Autumn, Daughter of Spring, and the Bastard (god of outcasts, orphans, and all things out of season) — each act within their spheres of influence but cannot compel human action. A miracle requires **alignment**: the human must genuinely desire the same outcome the god intends. This is not simply praying for divine aid — it requires the saint to have become, through their life choices and spiritual development, a person whose deepest will matches divine purpose at that specific moment.

Cazaril's sainthood in *The Curse of Chalion* demonstrates the mechanic: the death miracle he performs requires him to genuinely desire death for the right reasons — not suicide from despair, but willing self-sacrifice in alignment with the Bastard's intent to break a curse. The miracle works because Cazaril's will and the god's will are, for that moment, identical.

**Sorcery** operates through **demons** — elemental spirits of chaos that, when bound within a human host, grant supernatural abilities. Demons are creatures of the Bastard's domain, and the Temple's institutional framework manages their binding and control. A sorcerer (demon-carrier) hosts a demon within themselves, gaining access to its powers while maintaining control through will, knowledge, and the demon's accumulated experience.

**Penric** and his demon **Desdemona** demonstrate the sorcerer-demon relationship in the novellas. Desdemona is a demon who has accumulated the personalities and knowledge of twelve previous hosts over two centuries. She grants Penric abilities including: healing, fire projection, animal communication, enhanced perception, and material manipulation. The relationship is cooperative — Penric and Desdemona negotiate, argue, and collaborate, making sorcery a partnership rather than domination.

A demon that overpowers its host produces a **geas** — a monstrous being of chaos driven by the demon's appetites without human restraint. Preventing this requires the host's will to remain dominant, which is why the Temple trains and monitors sorcerers.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost of sainthood is **alignment difficulty**. Most people never become saints, not because the gods are unwilling but because genuine alignment between human and divine will is extraordinarily rare. The saint must want exactly what the god wants, for the right reasons, at the right moment. Self-deception, hidden motives, or incomplete commitment prevent alignment.

The primary cost of sorcery is **demon management**. The demon must be controlled through constant negotiation and will. If the host weakens (through illness, injury, or psychological crisis), the demon may gain dominance. If the host dies, the demon is released and must find a new host or be recaptured — an unbound demon poses a danger to the nearest person of sufficient spiritual sensitivity.

The second cost is **theological consequence**. When a sorcerer dies, their demon must be properly handled by the Bastard's order, or it escapes into the world. Mismanaged demons — leaping from host to host, accumulating power — are one of the setting's recurring threats. The Temple's institutional structure exists partly to manage this lifecycle.

## Practitioner access

Sainthood is not something one trains for — it is recognized after the fact, when divine alignment produces a miracle. Saints come from all walks of life and are often surprised by their own sainthood. Sorcery requires hosting a demon, which can happen accidentally (demons leap to the nearest suitable host when their previous host dies) or intentionally (the Temple manages controlled transfers). The Bastard's order trains sorcerers in demon management, theological understanding, and the practical use of demonic abilities.

## Cultural role

The Five Gods' religion structures all aspects of society in Bujold's world. Each god has a temple, a priesthood, and a sphere of influence. Sainthood is revered but also feared — saints are instruments of divine will, and divine will does not always align with human comfort. Sorcerers occupy a more ambiguous position — useful, potentially dangerous, and managed through the Bastard's institutional order. Bujold uses the system to explore how institutions mediate between human needs and divine purpose, and how genuine faith differs from performative piety.
