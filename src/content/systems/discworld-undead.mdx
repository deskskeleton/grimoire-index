## Mechanics

On the Discworld, **vampires** and **werewolves** possess innate supernatural abilities that function as biological-magical conditions rather than learned disciplines.

**Vampires** possess a broad suite of powers: **superhuman strength and speed**, **shapeshifting** (into bats, wolves, or mist), **hypnotic gaze** (the ability to control weak-willed mortals through eye contact), **weather manipulation** (commanding storms, though this is rarely used by modern vampires), **flight** (in bat form), and **near-immortality** (vampires can be killed by their weaknesses but otherwise do not age). They can also be restored from death if blood touches their remains — even a pile of dust can reconstitute into a full vampire with sufficient blood.

Vampire weaknesses are specific and culturally conditioned: they include **religious symbols** (any religion's symbols work, as long as the wielder has genuine faith), **sunlight** (fatal on prolonged exposure), **garlic**, **running water** (crossing it weakens them), and a compulsive need to **count scattered objects** (poppy seeds are the traditional scattering agent). Crucially, these weaknesses can be partially overcome through willpower and acclimatization — Otto Chriek, the Ankh-Morpork Times photographer, has trained himself to work with the flash of his iconograph despite the light-sensitivity, and uses a small vial of blood to self-resurrect when the flash reduces him to dust.

**Werewolves** possess the ability to transform between human and wolf forms. The Überwald werewolves can shift at will (not restricted to the full moon) and maintain their intelligence in both forms. Angua von Überwald — a Watch officer — transforms into a large, intelligent wolf that retains her human personality and judgment. Werewolf senses (particularly smell) are enhanced even in human form. Some werewolves, like Wolfgang, embrace the predatory aspect and hunt humans for sport; others, like Angua, carefully manage their dual nature.

**The Black Ribbon Society** is an organization of reformed vampires who have sworn off human blood — substituting animal blood, cocoa, or photography (in Otto's case, the flash addiction replacing the blood addiction). The Society is modeled explicitly on twelve-step recovery programs, and membership is treated as an ongoing struggle rather than a permanent cure.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost for vampires is **blood addiction**. The need for blood is not merely physical but psychological — a craving that dominates the vampire's consciousness. Vampires who abstain (Black Ribboners) are in constant recovery; the urge never fully goes away. The social parallel to alcoholism is explicit and played for both comedy and genuine pathos.

The primary cost for werewolves is **social dual-identity**. Angua must conceal her nature from most of Ankh-Morpork's population, manage the physical logistics of transformation (clothing does not survive the change), and navigate a society that is theoretically inclusive but practically nervous about having a werewolf on the Watch.

Both groups face **prejudice**. The Überwald vampires and werewolves are old-money aristocrats in their homeland but immigrants and minorities in Ankh-Morpork, where they must prove themselves to a suspicious human majority.

## Practitioner access

Vampirism is transmitted through being bitten by a vampire and dying. Lycanthropy is hereditary — werewolves are born, not made, and the condition runs in families (the von Überwalds are an established werewolf noble house). Neither condition can be acquired through training or choice.

## Cultural role

Under Lord Vetinari's administration, Ankh-Morpork has adopted a policy of aggressive multiculturalism — vampires, werewolves, trolls, dwarfs, and other non-human species are granted citizenship and (theoretically) equal rights. The undead community's integration into the city is one of Pratchett's primary vehicles for discussing prejudice, addiction, cultural assimilation, and the gap between legal equality and social acceptance. The powers themselves are largely irrelevant to this commentary; what matters is how vampires and werewolves navigate a society that fears what they are while benefiting from what they can do.
