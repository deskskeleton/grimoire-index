## Mechanics

The Flat Earth is a world before the modern world — a disc resting on pillars, lit by a sun that passes beneath it at night through the realm of the Underearth. Magic here is not a technique or a discipline but an expression of cosmic nature. The beings who wield it most fluently are the three orders of demons who dwell beneath the earth's surface, each order embodying a different register of supernatural power.

The Vazdru are the aristocracy of demonkind: impossibly beautiful, impossibly cruel, impossibly elegant. Their magic is the magic of desire made manifest. Azhrarn, the Night's Master and Lord of Demons, is their prince and the most powerful among them. When a Vazdru wants something — a mortal transformed, a city reshaped, a kingdom undone — the wanting itself is the mechanism. There are no incantations in any systematic sense, no reagents, no circles of power. A Vazdru demon reshapes reality the way a poet reshapes language: through intent, style, and an aesthetic sensibility that treats the world as raw material for narrative. Azhrarn can raise palaces from nothing, transform a mortal into an immortal beauty, grant wishes with exquisite precision and devastating irony, or unmake a person's identity with a whispered word.

The Eshva are the second order — lesser in raw power but no less dangerous. They are creatures of seduction and emotional manipulation, wraith-like beings whose magic operates primarily through influence, illusion, and the amplification of mortal passions. Where a Vazdru reshapes the world, an Eshva reshapes the heart. They can inspire obsessive love, suicidal grief, or murderous jealousy, and they move through the mortal world as shadows, whispering and caressing and vanishing.

The Drin are the laborers — squat, ugly, enormously skilled craftsmen who forge magical objects of extraordinary power. Their magic is the magic of making: enchanted swords, impossible jewels, devices that trap souls or bend time. If the Vazdru are poets and the Eshva are seducers, the Drin are artificers, and the objects they create often outlast the stories that spawned them.

Mortal sorcery exists but is rare and almost always derivative. A mortal might gain magical power through a bargain with a demon (particularly Azhrarn, who delights in granting mortals exactly what they ask for), through the inheritance of demon blood, or through sheer force of will cultivated over an extraordinary lifetime. Simmu, who defies death itself, does so through a combination of demonic gift and personal determination that is unique in the setting. Zhirek gains terrible necromantic power through suffering and cosmic accident rather than study. There is no school of magic, no tradition of apprenticeship. Each mortal sorcerer is an anomaly.

The gods exist above the Flat Earth as distant, largely indifferent presences. Their power dwarfs that of the demons, but they rarely exercise it directly. When they do intervene — reshaping the world, punishing hubris, enforcing cosmic law — it is with the blunt force of natural catastrophe rather than the surgical precision of demonic magic.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost of Flat Earth sorcery is not physical or even spiritual in any conventional sense — it is narrative. Cosmic irony is the governing principle. Every act of magic, particularly demonic magic, generates consequences that are poetically appropriate rather than mechanically predictable. Azhrarn grants a mortal woman eternal beauty, and that beauty destroys everyone who loves her. A demon-forged sword cuts everything its wielder wishes to cut, including the bonds of loyalty that hold his kingdom together. The magic always works; it is the aftermath that exacts the price.

This ironic structure is not a limitation the practitioners acknowledge or can circumvent. Azhrarn is fully aware that his gifts tend toward catastrophe, but he is constitutionally incapable of restraining his aesthetic impulses. When he finds a mortal situation interesting — a beautiful youth suffering unjustly, a proud queen ripe for humbling, a love story begging for a tragic third act — he intervenes because the story demands it, and his intervention invariably makes the story worse in ways that are beautiful to observe and terrible to experience. The demons are bound not by rules but by their own natures, which is a more absolute prison than any system of laws.

The gods impose a second layer of constraint. They are inattentive but not absent, and when demonic meddling reaches a scale that threatens the cosmic order, they respond with overwhelming force. Azhrarn has been imprisoned, punished, and diminished by divine intervention on multiple occasions. The threat of godly retribution does not prevent demonic action — the demons are too proud and too impulsive for that — but it ensures that the most ambitious magical acts carry existential risk for their perpetrators.

For mortals, the costs are more conventional but no less devastating. Bargains with demons always carry a hidden clause, not because the demons are legalistic but because the asymmetry of power between a demon and a mortal ensures that the mortal cannot fully comprehend what they are agreeing to. Mortal sorcerers who gain power through other means — inheritance, accident, sheer will — find that the power isolates them from human society and human concerns, pushing them toward the same aesthetic detachment that characterizes the demons.

## Practitioner access

Magic in the Flat Earth is not democratized. There is no path of study, no curriculum, no tradition of magical education. The demons possess magic as an inherent attribute of their species — the Vazdru are born (or rather, they simply exist) with the ability to reshape reality, the Eshva with the ability to manipulate emotion, the Drin with the ability to forge wonders. For them, magic is as natural and as involuntary as breathing.

Mortals who gain magical power do so through exceptional and unrepeatable circumstances. Azhrarn might take an interest in a particular mortal and grant them gifts — but Azhrarn's interests are capricious and his gifts are double-edged. A mortal might inherit demon blood through some ancient liaison and find themselves possessed of abilities they cannot fully control. A mortal might stumble into a situation of such cosmic significance that the universe itself grants them power — but this is accident, not method.

The result is that every practitioner in the Flat Earth is effectively unique. There are no schools of magic because there is nothing teachable. Simmu's path to power — involving gender transformation, demonic seduction, a quest to the wells of immortality, and a defiance of Death itself — could not be replicated by anyone else because it was shaped entirely by Simmu's specific circumstances and Simmu's specific relationship with Azhrarn. Zhirek's necromantic abilities arose from a specific sequence of tragedies that could not be engineered. Each sorcerer is a one-off event, a story that happens once and cannot be repeated.

## Cultural role

Magic in the Flat Earth does not exist alongside civilization — it generates civilization and destroys it in alternating cycles. Cities rise because a demon found a mortal settlement amusing and decided to beautify it; kingdoms fall because a demon grew bored with the result. The relationship between magic and culture is not institutional but mythic: magic is the force that makes history happen, the invisible hand that turns a shepherd into a king and a king into a cautionary tale.

The mortal populations of the Flat Earth live in a world saturated with supernatural influence but possess no reliable framework for understanding or managing it. There are no magical institutions, no guilds of wizards, no temples that reliably channel divine power. Mortals tell stories about the demons — Azhrarn in particular is a figure of legend, feared and desired in equal measure — but these stories are unreliable guides to the actual behavior of supernatural beings. The Flat Earth is a world where myth is literally true but practically useless, because the beings who populate the myths are too capricious to be predicted.

Across the span of the series, which covers eons of the Flat Earth's history, magic shapes the rise and fall of entire civilizations. Cities of impossible beauty are built in a night and abandoned in a generation. Heroes are created and discarded. Love stories begin with demonic intervention and end with cosmic tragedy. The cumulative effect is a world that feels ancient and layered, where every landscape carries the residue of a dozen forgotten enchantments and every ruin was once somebody's wish come true.

The series' final movement gestures toward a world in which the Flat Earth itself is ending — the disc will become a globe, the demons will retreat, and the age of myth will give way to the age of reason. Magic, in Lee's telling, is not a permanent feature of reality but a quality of a particular era, beautiful and terrible and ultimately transient.
