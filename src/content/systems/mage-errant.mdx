## Mechanics

Every mage on Anastis is born with one or more affinities — innate connections to specific materials, elements, or abstract concepts that define what magic they can perform. The range of possible affinities is staggeringly broad. While classical elemental affinities for fire, water, stone, and wind are common and well-understood, the system accommodates affinities for bone, paper, crystal, dream, stellar matter, spatial relationships, and dozens of other substances and phenomena. This breadth is the system's defining characteristic and the engine of its narrative creativity.

A mage's raw magical capacity is governed by their mana reservoir, an internal store of magical energy that replenishes over time. Mana functions as both fuel and medium — a fire-affinity mage draws on their reservoir to generate, shape, and direct flame, while a spatial-affinity mage expends the same fundamental resource to fold distances or create pocket dimensions. The reservoir's size varies between individuals and can be expanded through sustained practice, though there appear to be upper limits determined partly by talent and partly by the nature of one's affinities.

The practical application of magic is divided broadly between two approaches. Spellforms are structured, repeatable magical constructs — essentially pre-built patterns that a mage learns, memorizes, and activates. They offer reliability and precision at the cost of flexibility. Raw shaping, by contrast, is direct, intuitive manipulation of one's affinity without the scaffolding of a spellform. It is more versatile but demands greater concentration and skill, and is far more prone to failure or unintended effects. Most trained mages use a combination of both approaches depending on the situation.

Enchanting and artifice represent the system's crafting dimension. Mages can imbue objects with persistent magical effects by inscribing spellforms into physical materials, creating items that function independently of the enchanter's ongoing concentration. Enchanted items are economically and militarily significant — much of Anastis's infrastructure depends on them. The quality of an enchantment depends on the mage's understanding of the underlying spellform theory and their ability to integrate multiple affinity effects into a stable construct.

Hugh of Emblin, the series protagonist, is notable for possessing multiple unusual affinities: crystal, bone, wind, dream, and stellar. Multi-affinity mages are rare, and Hugh's specific combination is unprecedented. Much of the series follows his efforts to find creative synergies between affinities that individually seem weak or narrow. His mentor Alustin, a Librarian Errant, demonstrates the ceiling of what a single unusual affinity can achieve — his paper magic, dismissed by many as trivial, becomes extraordinarily dangerous through decades of creative application and deep theoretical understanding.

At the apex of the system stand the Great Powers — mages of such extraordinary strength that they function as geopolitical forces unto themselves. Kanderon Crux, a sphinx and Great Power, demonstrates the scale differential: her abilities operate on a continental level, and her presence at Skyhold is as much a political deterrent as an academic one. Great Powers are not simply mages with more mana; they represent a qualitative shift in what magic can accomplish, often having developed entirely novel applications of their affinities that lesser mages cannot replicate.

## Limitations and costs

Mana is finite. Every spell, every act of shaping, every maintained enchantment draws from a reservoir that takes time to replenish. A mage who exhausts their reservoir is functionally powerless until it recovers, and pushing beyond safe limits risks physical collapse. In combat situations, mana management is often the deciding factor — a mage who burns through their reservoir in the first engagement is defenseless in the second.

Affinities are both gift and constraint. A stone-affinity mage cannot manipulate fire no matter how skilled they become; a dream mage cannot shape metal. This hard boundary means that every mage has fundamental gaps in their capabilities that no amount of training can overcome. Some affinities are widely regarded as useless or nearly so — paper, bone, and other "minor" affinities carry social stigma and limited obvious combat applications, even though creative practitioners can find powerful uses for them.

Multi-affinity mages face their own distinctive challenge. While possessing multiple affinities offers greater versatility, it also divides a mage's training time and mana capacity across multiple disciplines. Many multi-affinity mages end up mediocre in all their affinities rather than excellent in one. Hugh's struggle to integrate his disparate affinities into a coherent fighting style is a central narrative thread. There is also evidence that certain affinity combinations are inherently unstable, posing risks to the mage's health or sanity.

The Great Powers represent a ceiling that the vast majority of mages will never approach. The gap between an ordinary trained mage and a Great Power is not a matter of degree but of kind — it is the difference between a skilled swordsman and an army. Most mages will spend their entire careers operating at a scale that a Great Power would consider trivial.

## Practitioner access

Affinities are innate and typically manifest during childhood or adolescence. A young person discovers their affinity — often accidentally, through an uncontrolled expression of magic — and is subsequently tested and classified. Some affinities run in family lines, suggesting a hereditary component, though the specific affinity a child manifests does not always match their parents'. The mechanism by which affinities are determined remains incompletely understood within the setting.

Once identified, aspiring mages typically seek formal training. Skyhold, a fortress-academy built into an ancient labyrinth, is the premier institution for magical education on Anastis. Its curriculum covers spellform theory, raw shaping technique, enchanting, and combat applications. The labyrinth beneath Skyhold serves as both training ground and testing facility — students must navigate its dangers as part of their education, and the labyrinth trials function as a practical examination of a mage's capabilities under genuine threat.

Not all mages attend Skyhold. Apprenticeship under an established mage is an alternative path, and some regions have their own training traditions. However, Skyhold's resources, library, and concentration of expertise make it the preferred option for anyone with the means and talent to attend. The academy also serves as a sorting mechanism — mages who survive and excel in its program emerge with both practical skills and professional credentials.

The ambient magical environment of Anastis itself plays a role in magical development. Certain locations have higher concentrations of specific types of mana, and prolonged exposure to these environments can influence a mage's growth. The labyrinth beneath Skyhold is one such location, saturated with magical energy that accelerates learning but also increases the danger of training exercises.

## Cultural role

Magic is the foundation of civilization on Anastis. Mages serve in virtually every significant institutional role — as scholars, military officers, diplomats, engineers, and administrators. The distinction between a magical and a non-magical society does not exist here; magic is woven into infrastructure, commerce, warfare, and governance at every level. Enchanted items provide lighting, heating, communication, and transportation. Magical agriculture sustains populations that conventional farming could not support.

The Great Powers sit at the top of this structure, functioning less as individuals and more as strategic assets comparable to nuclear weapons. Their presence or absence shapes the political landscape of entire continents. Alliances and rivalries between Great Powers drive the macro-level conflicts of the series, and nations actively court or appease them. Kanderon Crux's dual role as Skyhold's headmistress and a Great Power illustrates this dynamic — her academic position is inseparable from her political significance.

Skyhold itself occupies a carefully maintained position of neutrality, accepting students from all nations and refusing to align with any single political faction. This neutrality is both principled and pragmatic — it ensures a steady flow of students and research collaboration, while Kanderon's presence discourages any nation from attempting to seize the institution by force. The academy functions as a rare neutral ground in a world where magical warfare between nations is a persistent threat.

Military applications of magic are pervasive and define the character of armed conflict on Anastis. Battles between mage-soldiers involve complex tactical considerations driven by affinity matchups, mana reserves, and terrain. A fire mage is devastating in open terrain but vulnerable underground; a stone mage dominates in fortified positions but struggles at sea. Military doctrine on Anastis revolves around assembling complementary teams of mages whose affinities cover each other's weaknesses — a principle that mirrors the series' central theme of collaboration overcoming individual limitation.
