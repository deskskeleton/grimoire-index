## Mechanics

The fundamental rule of Earthsea magic is simple and absolute: every thing has a true name in the Old Speech, the language used by Segoy at the world's making, and to know that name is to have power over the thing. To call wind, a wizard must know the true name of the wind in question — not "wind" as a category, but the specific wind in the specific place. To bind, transform, or unmake a thing, the wizard must speak its true name in the Old Speech.

This produces a magic system that is structurally constrained even though its surface effects can be dramatic. Wizards spend years memorizing names — of every plant, animal, and feature of an island — and a working wizard's competence is largely a function of the breadth and accuracy of their personal name-vocabulary. The most respected mages are also the most patient students.

Names cannot be lied in. The Old Speech does not permit untruth: anything spoken in it is, by the act of speaking, real. This makes the language extraordinarily powerful and extraordinarily dangerous. A careless wizard who calls something into being in the Old Speech has done so for keeps.

Lesser magic — the local crafts of village witches, weatherworkers, fishermen, and herbalists — operates on simpler invocations and patterned spells, sometimes drawing on fragments of the Old Speech without full understanding. These crafts are real and useful but limited in scope.

## Limitations and costs

The system's central cost is ethical. Every act of magic disturbs the Equilibrium — the cycle of consequences linking all things on Earthsea. A wizard who calls rain in one place withholds it from another. A wizard who heals one wound moves the harm. The book series presents this as a practical fact: the better the wizard, the more reluctant they are to use their power, because a competent practitioner can see the consequences clearly. This produces a culture of restraint among mages that is both the system's defining feature and a recurring point of dramatic tension.

A second cost is preparation. Earthsea wizards train for years at the school on Roke. Mastery is gated by patience, intellectual discipline, and the willingness to listen — not by raw talent.

A third cost is social. A wizard's true name is itself a vulnerability. Knowing another's true name confers power over them; sharing one's own true name is an act of profound trust. This shapes much of Earthsea's social fabric: people use use-names publicly and reveal true names rarely.

## Practitioner access

Wizardry is, in principle, open to anyone with aptitude who can secure training. In practice, formal magery is dominated by men trained at Roke, and a parallel — older, less institutionally validated — tradition of women's magic operates at the village level. The later books in the cycle (*Tehanu* and *The Other Wind*) explicitly engage with the gendered structure of magical authority on Earthsea and complicate the original picture.

A small minority of wizards are exceptionally gifted from childhood; most who can do magic at all become village witches or local craftsmen rather than full mages.

## Cultural role

In the world of Earthsea, magic is real, recognized, and uncommon. Wizards trained at Roke are visible figures — sometimes itinerant, sometimes settled in a particular village or court — and the school itself is both a power center and a moral authority. Village witchcraft is widespread, generally tolerated, and often the only medical or weather-magical resource available to ordinary people. The system is not hidden, but it is treated with care: the existence of magic is one of the things that gives Earthsea its texture.
