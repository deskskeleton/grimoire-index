## Mechanics

Nen is the controlled use of "aura" — the life-energy that all living humans naturally produce. Untrained people leak aura constantly; trained users learn first to seal it (Ten), then to project and shape it deliberately. Four basic skills — Ten (containment), Zetsu (suppression), Ren (concentration to enhance), and Hatsu (the personal expression of aura) — form the universal foundation.

Each practitioner is born aligned to one of six categories: Enhancement (boosting attributes of self or objects), Emission (projecting aura away from the body), Manipulation (controlling a target through aura), Transmutation (changing the apparent properties of one's aura), Conjuration (creating real objects out of aura), and Specialization (a catch-all for abilities that fit none of the others, including category-bending and unique powers). The categories are arranged on a hexagon: a practitioner is most efficient at their own category, somewhat less efficient at adjacent ones, much less efficient at opposite ones. A natural Conjurer can also do credible Manipulation, but will struggle to be a strong Enhancer.

The system's most distinctive feature is Hatsu — the practitioner's individual ability. Hatsu abilities are not standardized. Each practitioner develops their own, typically a single ability or small constellation of related abilities, and the strength and reliability of Hatsu is dramatically increased by self-imposed conditions and vows. A Hatsu might require the user to recite a verbal formula, to speak the target's true name, to hit a specific body part, to use the ability only at night, or to forfeit the use of it permanently if a condition is violated. Severe restrictions produce disproportionately powerful results — a "promise to never use this ability against family" might unlock an effect that would otherwise be impossible.

## Limitations and costs

The four basic skills are taxing: Ren (concentrated aura output) cannot be sustained indefinitely without exhaustion or death, and prolonged Zetsu (suppression) leaves the user defenseless to other Nen attacks. Practitioners must manage stamina actively in extended combat.

The category system is a hard limit. A Specialist is rare, and a Conjurer attempting Emission will produce a much weaker effect than a natural Emitter performing the same action. This produces a tactical landscape where matchups matter and where Hatsu design typically aligns with the user's natural type for efficiency.

The most distinctive cost is the Hatsu condition itself. The conditions a practitioner accepts to strengthen their ability bind them in fiction — not merely as a rule of thumb but as an enforceable contract with the technique. Violating one's own Hatsu condition can revoke the ability outright, render it nonfunctional, or produce backlash. Some characters have built abilities around vows whose breaking would kill them; honoring those vows is, in effect, the cost of access.

A subtler cost is psychological. Effective Hatsu requires the practitioner to be honest about who they are — which type they belong to, what their motivations actually are. Self-deception produces weaker abilities. The series treats this as a real obstacle for its characters.

## Practitioner access

Nen is, in principle, learnable by any human. In practice, learning it requires a teacher who can perform the "initiation" — a ritual or violent encounter that opens the student's aura nodes prematurely. Self-taught practitioners exist but typically discover Nen by chance, often through trauma. Within the world, only a small minority of people are aware Nen exists at all, and most who possess it are licensed Hunters, members of professional crime organizations, or other specialized communities.

## Cultural role

Nen is, by deliberate cultural compact, hidden from the general population. Hunters in particular treat the existence of Nen as something that should not be publicly disclosed; one of the recurring sources of conflict in the manga is the political fallout when Nen-using groups become visible to the broader world. Within the practitioner community, Nen is treated as a profession-defining skill, a survival necessity, and the universal ground rule of any serious conflict.
