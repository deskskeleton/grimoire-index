## Mechanics

The **Abhorsen's Bells** are a set of seven magical bells, each named and each producing a specific effect when rung in the realm of Death or in the presence of Dead creatures. They are the primary tools of the Abhorsen — the hereditary guardian who enters Death to ensure the Dead stay dead — and their corrupted counterparts are used by necromancers to raise and command the Dead.

The seven bells, in order of increasing power and danger:

**Ranna** (Sleepbringer) — the smallest bell, producing sleep in the listener. Used to calm and subdue Dead spirits. **Mosrael** (Waker) — throws the ringer deeper into Death while bringing the listener into Life. The necromancer's bell, used to raise the Dead. **Kibeth** (Walker) — compels the listener to walk — either deeper into Death (banishing them) or back into Life (raising them as revenants). Difficult to control; may compel the ringer to walk instead. **Dyrim** (Speaker) — gives or takes speech and thought from the Dead. Can restore a Dead spirit's identity or strip it. **Belgaer** (Thinker) — restores or removes independent thought and memory. The most subtle bell, capable of returning a Dead spirit's full personality. **Saraneth** (Binder) — the bell of dominion, binding the listener to the ringer's will. The most commonly used bell in combat against powerful Dead. **Astarael** (Weeper) — the largest and most dangerous bell, which sends all who hear it deep into Death, including the ringer. A weapon of last resort.

Each bell has its own personality and will — they resist improper use and can turn their effects on the wielder. A ringer who loses control of a bell may find themselves walked, bound, or thrown into Death by the very instrument they attempted to use. The bells are played using a specific technique (a figure-eight wrist motion) that must be precise; incorrect ringing produces unpredictable and dangerous results.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **risk to the wielder**. Every bell can affect the ringer as well as the target. Mosrael always pushes the ringer deeper into Death. Astarael kills everyone who hears it, including the ringer. Even the "safer" bells like Saraneth require constant control — a moment's lapse in concentration, and the bell's own will takes over.

The second cost is **specificity and expertise**. Each bell does one thing, and choosing the wrong bell in a crisis can be catastrophic. The Abhorsen must assess each situation — what kind of Dead, how powerful, how many — and select the correct bell instantly. This requires years of training and an intimate understanding of Death's geography.

The third cost is **physical and spiritual toll**. Using the bells in Death drains the wielder's strength and warmth. Extended bell-ringing in the deeper precincts of Death can leave the Abhorsen hypothermic, exhausted, and vulnerable to being swept away by the river of Death.

## Practitioner access

The Abhorsen's Bells are hereditary artifacts passed from one Abhorsen to the next. There is only one true set, though necromancers create inferior copies. The bells respond to the Abhorsen bloodline — they are easier to control for those of the correct lineage, though skill and training remain essential. Necromancers who use bells (whether copies or stolen originals) face greater risk, as the bells' inherent resistance to misuse is stronger against those without the Abhorsen's heritage.

## Cultural role

The bells define the Abhorsen's identity. The Abhorsen is sometimes called the "bell-wielder" — the person who carries the bandolier of seven bells and walks in Death to keep the boundary secure. In the Old Kingdom's cultural imagination, the bells represent the thin line between the living world and the chaos of Death. The Abhorsen is simultaneously feared (as someone who traffics with Death) and revered (as the only defense against the Dead). The bells' dual nature — usable for both banishing the Dead and raising them — mirrors the Abhorsen's ambiguous position as a figure who must intimately understand death magic in order to oppose it.
