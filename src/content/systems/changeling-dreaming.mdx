## Mechanics

**Changelings** are fae souls (kiths) incarnated in mortal bodies to survive in the modern world. Their magic operates through **cantrips** — spells constructed by combining an **Art** (what the magic does) with a **Realm** (what the magic affects).

**Arts** include: **Chicanery** (illusion and misdirection), **Legerdemain** (telekinesis and sleight-of-hand), **Primal** (elemental manipulation — fire, water, earth, air, wood), **Soothsay** (prophecy and fate-reading), **Sovereign** (command and authority), **Wayfare** (movement and teleportation), **Chronos** (time manipulation), **Naming** (true-name magic), **Pyretics** (fire and passion), and **Metamorphosis** (shapeshifting).

**Realms** define valid targets: **Actor** (humans), **Fae** (changelings and fae creatures), **Nature** (animals, plants, natural phenomena), **Prop** (crafted objects), **Scene** (environments and locations). A changeling with Primal 3 and Nature 2 could command a storm; the same changeling with Primal 3 and Prop 2 could set a sword ablaze. The Art-Realm matrix creates a combinatorial magic system where versatility comes from breadth across both axes.

The fuel is **Glamour** — the energy of inspiration, wonder, and creative passion. Changelings harvest Glamour from mortals through several methods: **Reverie** (inspiring genuine creative passion in a mortal), **Ravaging** (forcibly extracting creative energy, damaging the mortal's capacity for wonder), or **Rapture** (an intense, mutual creative experience). Glamour is spent to cast cantrips and to fuel various fae abilities.

The opposing force is **Banality** — the weight of mundane reality, disbelief, and rational disenchantment. Banality is both an environmental condition (the modern world is highly Banal) and a personal trait (measured on a character sheet). Exposure to Banality erodes a changeling's fae nature. At extreme levels, the changeling undergoes the **Undoing** — their fae soul is destroyed, and they become fully mortal, forgetting they were ever anything else.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **Glamour depletion**. Cantrips cost Glamour, and Glamour is not easy to replenish. The most ethical method (Reverie — genuinely inspiring a mortal) takes time and care. The fastest method (Ravaging) harms the source. This creates a resource tension that mirrors the game's thematic concern with art's relationship to exploitation.

The second cost is **Banality accumulation**. Acting mundane, spending time in Banal environments, or encountering Banal individuals increases a changeling's Banality score. High Banality makes magic harder, weakens the fae mien (the changeling's magical appearance), and eventually kills the fae soul. The modern world is an actively hostile environment for changelings — offices, bureaucracies, and cynicism are lethal.

The third cost is **the Dreaming's instability**. The near Dreaming (the faerie realm adjacent to the mortal world) is collapsing. Freeholds (pockets of stable Glamour in the mortal world) are shrinking. The game's setting is explicitly one of decline — the fae are losing their war against Banality, and every use of magic is a small act of resistance against an inevitable fading.

## Practitioner access

Changelings are born, not made — a fae soul (from one of many kiths: sidhe, boggan, eshu, nocker, pooka, redcap, satyr, sluagh, troll, etc.) incarnates in a mortal body, typically Awakening to their fae nature during childhood or adolescence. The Chrysalis (fae Awakening) is spontaneous. Mortal-born changelings (commoners) have always lived this cycle; the sidhe (fae nobility) returned to the mortal world from Arcadia during a brief opening in 1969. Training in Arts and Realms occurs within changeling society, often in motleys (adventuring groups) or noble courts.

## Cultural role

Changeling society is split between the **Seelie Court** (honor, tradition, beauty) and the **Unseelie Court** (passion, change, freedom), with a feudal political structure governed by sidhe nobility. Magic is central to changeling identity — without Glamour, they cease to exist as fae. The game's cultural commentary operates through this metaphor: creative people in a world that undervalues creativity, magical beings in a world that has stopped believing in magic, childlike wonder fighting adult cynicism.
