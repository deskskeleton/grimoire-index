## Mechanics

**Warrens** are parallel dimensions, each attuned to a particular aspect of reality. A sorcerer "opens" a Warren by reaching into it mentally — an act variously described as parting a curtain, stepping sideways, or pulling on a thread — and then channels the Warren's power to produce magical effects in the physical world, or physically enters the Warren as a traversable space.

The principal mortal Warrens include **Thyr** (light), **Telas** (fire), **Denul** (healing), **D'riss** (stone/earth), **Meanas** (shadow/illusion), **Rashan** (darkness), **Ruse** (the sea), **Serc** (air/sky), **Mockra** (the mind), **Tennes** (the hearth/land), **Hood's Path** (death), and **Imperial Warren** (a created Warren useable only by Malazan cadre mages). Most practitioners have a natural affinity for one or two Warrens. A rare few — most notably Quick Ben — can access many, an ability treated as extraordinary and dangerous.

Beneath the mortal Warrens lie the **Elder Warrens**: Kurald Galain (Tiste Andii darkness), Kurald Emurlahn (Tiste Edur shadow), Kurald Thyrllan (Tiste Liosan light), Tellann (T'lan Imass fire), Omtose Phellack (Jaghut ice), and Starvald Demelain (the dragon-blooded source). These are older, more powerful, and more dangerous; accessing an Elder Warren without the corresponding racial or divine affiliation is reckless and often lethal.

Before the Warrens existed, the cosmology ran on **Holds** — a power structure organized not by elemental aspect but by archetypal position: King, Queen, Knight, Consort, Magi, Throne, and so on. Holds still function in parts of the world (especially Lether) and interact with the Warren system in complex, often destabilizing ways.

The **Deck of Dragons** is a divinatory tool whose cards map the current configuration of Warrens, Holds, Ascendants, and gods. Reading the Deck is itself a magical act — one that can attract the attention of the powers depicted — and the Deck's composition shifts as the cosmic landscape changes.

## Limitations and costs

The most immediate cost is **physical and psychological strain**. Opening a Warren draws on the practitioner's vitality; sustained channeling produces exhaustion, pain, and — in extreme cases — the dissolution of the mage's body. High-level sorcery can kill the practitioner outright. The strain scales with the Warren's power tier: channeling an Elder Warren is vastly more taxing than a mortal one.

The second cost is **visibility and vulnerability**. Active magic leaves traces. Other mages can sense when a Warren is opened, estimate its type and strength, and sometimes trace it back to the caster. In a world where magical ambush and counter-magic are standard military tactics, being detected while channeling is often fatal.

The third cost is **corruption and contamination**. Warrens are living cosmological structures, and they can be poisoned. The Crippled God's intrusion into the Warren system produces a pervasive sickness (the "poison" afflicting Burn's sleep) that destabilizes magic globally. Practitioners who draw on a corrupted Warren risk madness, mutation, or worse.

A structural limitation is **exclusivity and opposition**. Some Warrens resist combination; using two opposed Warrens simultaneously is dangerous. The Elder Warrens are particularly jealous of their practitioners — drawing on Kurald Galain without Tiste Andii blood, or entering Omtose Phellack without Jaghut nature, invites hostile environmental response.

## Practitioner access

Most mages are born with an innate sensitivity to one or more Warrens, which training then refines. In the Malazan Empire, military mages are organized into **cadres** (squad-level mages) and **High Mages** (strategic-level). Outside the Empire, magical traditions vary enormously: the Bridgeburners have street-trained battle mages, the Bonehunters include a hedge-witch and a spirit-talker, and the Tiste races access their respective Elder Warrens as a birthright. Ascendants and gods operate at a tier above mortal mages, wielding Warren-scale power through direct cosmic affiliation rather than learned technique.

There is no formal academy structure in most of the Malazan world. Mages learn from mentors, from institutional placement (military cadre assignment), or — dangerously — from self-directed experimentation. The skill ceiling is enormous: the difference between a cadre mage and a High Mage is not incremental but categorical.

## Cultural role

Magic in Malazan is openly known and pervasive. Armies field mage cadres as standard tactical assets; cities employ wards and magical infrastructure; gods and Ascendants intervene visibly in mortal affairs. The cultural attitude toward sorcerers varies by polity: the Malazan Empire treats mages as military officers, the Kingdom of Lether treats them as outlaws (favoring the older Hold-based magic), and the Tiste civilizations treat Warren access as a racial inheritance inseparable from cultural identity. The overall tone is pragmatic — magic is a fact of life, weaponized by every institution that can afford it, and distrusted by those who have been on its receiving end.
