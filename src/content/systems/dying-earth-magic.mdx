## Mechanics

In the Dying Earth, magic operates through the memorization of spells — complex formulaic patterns that a magician impresses upon the mind through intense study. Each spell is a self-contained construct with a specific, predetermined effect. When a magician speaks the activating words, the spell discharges its energy and is simultaneously erased from memory. The spell cannot be cast again until the magician returns to a written source and re-memorizes it.

Spells bear the names of their original creators, most of whom lived in prior aeons and are now forgotten except through their surviving works. Names like "The Excellent Prismatic Spray," "Phandaal's Gyrator," and "The Spell of Forlorn Encystment" serve both as identifiers and as indicators of the spell's character. The naming convention implies a lost golden age of magical research that the present age can only scavenge from.

The effects of spells are fixed and non-negotiable. A spell does what it does — there is no improvisation or creative reinterpretation at the moment of casting. A magician's skill lies not in modifying spells but in choosing which ones to carry into a given situation and in deploying them at the right moment. This makes sorcerous combat a contest of preparation and deduction: each magician tries to guess what the other has memorized and to find the counter.

Spells vary enormously in scale and character. Some are straightforward combat effects (bolts of energy, paralytic fields). Others are bizarre and specific (spells that imprison a target inside a pocket dimension, or that summon a specific creature from a specific plane). The total catalogue of surviving spells is finite and apparently not growing — no one in the Dying Earth's final age appears capable of creating new spells, only of recovering and memorizing old ones.

## Limitations and costs

The central limitation is cognitive capacity. The human mind can hold only a small number of spells simultaneously — in some accounts as few as three or four. More powerful and complex spells occupy more mental "space," reducing the total that can be carried. This makes every spell slot a strategic decision, and running out of memorized spells in a dangerous situation is a recurring crisis in the stories.

The second limitation is access to written sources. Spells exist in spellbooks, and spellbooks are rare, jealously guarded, and frequently stolen. A magician without access to a library is a magician who cannot rearm. This creates a secondary economy of theft, trade, and espionage around magical texts. Much of the plot of the Dying Earth stories revolves around the acquisition, loss, and recovery of spellbooks.

There is no evident metabolic or physical cost to casting a spell — the cost is entirely informational. The spell departs the mind cleanly, leaving no fatigue or injury. The danger lies in being left defenseless after expending one's repertoire, not in the act of casting itself.

## Practitioner access

Magic is a learned discipline available in principle to anyone with sufficient intelligence and access to instructional texts. In practice, the number of practicing magicians is very small, because surviving spellbooks are rare, the study required is intense, and the Dying Earth's declining civilization does not support organized magical education. Most magicians are solitary figures who have spent lifetimes assembling personal libraries. There is no hereditary component — magical ability is entirely a function of study, intellect, and access to source material.

## Cultural role

In the Dying Earth's twilight civilization, magicians occupy an ambiguous social position. They are feared and respected for their power but are also seen as eccentric, dangerous, and untrustworthy — which they generally are. There is no organized magical society, guild, or governing body; each magician operates independently, competing with rivals for spellbooks and territory. The relationship between magicians is almost universally antagonistic, and magical duels are a recurring feature of the stories. Ordinary people regard magic with a mixture of awe and wariness, knowing that a magician's aid always comes with strings attached. The overall effect is of a world where magical knowledge is a dwindling, non-renewable resource — one more thing running out as the sun dies.
