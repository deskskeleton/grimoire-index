## Mechanics

**Pact magic** centers on the **binder** — a practitioner who summons **vestiges** (entities that exist outside reality's normal framework) and forms temporary supernatural pacts with them. Each vestige is a unique being with its own history, personality, powers, and physical manifestation.

The binding process is ritualistic: the binder draws a specific **seal** (a geometric symbol unique to each vestige), speaks the vestige's name, and attempts to negotiate the pact. The negotiation is resolved mechanically — the binder makes a binding check, and the result determines whether they achieve a **good pact** (the binder retains full control) or a **poor pact** (the vestige exerts influence over the binder's behavior, imposing personality traits, compulsions, or behavioral requirements).

Each vestige grants a suite of **granted abilities** — typically 3-5 supernatural powers thematically tied to the vestige's nature. For example:

- **Amon** (a ram-headed fire entity) grants fire breath, a ram's charge, and resistance to fire
- **Naberius** (a three-headed dog entity) grants persuasive speech, skill mastery, and the ability to change the sound of one's voice
- **Paimon** (a dancing figure) grants dance-based combat abilities, telepathy, and uncanny dodge

Each binding also produces a **physical sign** — a visible alteration to the binder's appearance that marks the vestige's presence. Amon's sign: the binder's breath steams visibly and their eyes glow red. Naberius's sign: the binder's voice becomes deep and gravelly.

Bindings last 24 hours. The binder can host one vestige at a time initially, gaining the ability to host multiple simultaneously at higher levels. Each morning, the binder can choose new vestiges, allowing daily customization of their power suite.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **personality influence**. On a poor pact, the vestige's personality bleeds into the binder's behavior. Amon's influence makes the binder proud and aggressive; Naberius's makes them argumentative and verbose. The binder can resist these compulsions with Willpower but doing so repeatedly is mechanically penalized.

The second cost is **physical signs**. Every binding produces visible evidence that cannot be suppressed except by a class feature gained at higher levels. In settings where pact magic is feared or illegal (most D&D settings treat it with suspicion), visible signs create social liability.

The third cost is **ritual requirements**. Binding requires a drawn seal, a reasonably quiet and uninterrupted minute, and knowledge of the vestige's name and sign. You cannot bind a vestige in the middle of combat or while fleeing.

The fourth limitation is **vestige availability by level**. More powerful vestiges require higher binder levels. A low-level binder has access to only a handful of vestiges; the full roster opens gradually.

## Practitioner access

Pact magic is a learned discipline — it requires study of vestige lore, memorization of seals, and practice in the binding ritual. There is no institutional framework for teaching it; binders typically learn from forbidden texts, independent mentors, or experimentation. Most churches and arcane institutions regard pact magic with suspicion or outright hostility — the vestiges' nature (entities rejected by both the divine and the natural order) makes binding them taboo.

## Cultural role

Pact magic exists in D&D's cosmological margins. Vestiges are entities that the gods have deliberately excluded from the afterlife — failed deities, cosmic mistakes, beings who offended the natural order. Binding them is viewed by most religious and arcane authorities as trafficking with entities that were banished for good reason. Binders are outsiders — practitioners of a tradition that has no church, no academy, and no social legitimacy. The system's cultural position mirrors that of real-world grimoire traditions: learned from forbidden books, practiced in secret, regarded with fear by the establishment.
