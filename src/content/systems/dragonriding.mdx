## Mechanics

A **dragonriding bond** is a psychic-empathic connection between a human rider and a dragon. Once established, the bond allows the rider to mount and direct the dragon in flight, issue commands (including the Valyrian command "dracarys" to breathe fire), and sense the dragon's emotional state. The dragon in turn responds to the rider's will, though not as a puppet — dragons have their own temperaments and can refuse commands, resist riders, or act independently.

The bond is **exclusive and lifelong**. A rider bonds with one dragon at a time, and a dragon accepts only one rider. If the rider dies, the dragon may eventually accept a new rider. If the dragon dies, the rider may attempt to bond with another — but the process is not guaranteed and is often dangerous. Claiming an unridden dragon involves approaching it and surviving the encounter; many would-be dragonriders are killed or burned in the attempt.

The mechanism of bonding is poorly understood even within the world. Valyrian dragonlords employed sorcery, horn-blowing rituals, and blood magic to establish and maintain bonds. The Targaryens, as the only surviving dragonlord bloodline after the Doom of Valyria, maintained the tradition through a combination of Valyrian ancestry and physical proximity — placing dragon eggs in the cradles of newborn Targaryens to encourage early bonding.

The bond appears to require **Valyrian blood** — the genetic heritage of Old Valyria's dragonlord families. Characters without Valyrian ancestry do not successfully bond with dragons in the established lore, though the precise degree of Valyrian heritage required is unclear. Dragonseeds — individuals with partial Targaryen or Valyrian ancestry — have successfully claimed dragons, suggesting that even diluted bloodlines can carry the necessary trait.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation is **bloodline dependency**. The ability to bond with a dragon appears to be hereditary and confined to Valyrian-descended bloodlines. This creates a natural aristocratic monopoly — dragonriding power cannot be taught, bought, or earned through merit. It is born into.

The second cost is **physical danger**. Dragons are enormous predators with independent wills. Claiming an unbonded dragon is life-threatening. Riding a dragon in battle exposes the rider to the same hazards faced by the dragon. And dragons can be killed — dragonrider dynasties collapse when their dragons die, as the Targaryens discovered after the Dance of the Dragons civil war killed most of their remaining dragons.

The third cost is **political instability**. The one-rider-one-dragon exclusivity means that multiple dragonriders within the same family create a balance-of-power problem. The Dance of the Dragons — a Targaryen civil war fought with dragons on both sides — demonstrates that dragonriding power does not guarantee political unity.

Dragons are also **uncontrollable at scale**. Daenerys struggles to control Drogon as he grows larger and wilder. Historical Targaryen dragons occasionally killed civilians, burned cities, or refused commands. The bond is a partnership, not dominion — and the senior partner is the one who breathes fire.

## Practitioner access

Dragonriding is restricted to those with Valyrian blood. In the current timeline, this means primarily House Targaryen and its offshoots. The Valyrian Freehold had forty dragonlord families; all but the Targaryens were destroyed in the Doom. Dragonseeds — bastards and descendants of Targaryen or Velaryon lines — represent a secondary pool of potential riders. No training tradition exists beyond proximity-based bonding in infancy and the dangerous practice of approaching and claiming an adult dragon.

## Cultural role

Dragonriding defined Westerosi politics for nearly three centuries. The Targaryen conquest united seven kingdoms under one throne specifically because dragons made conventional military resistance futile. The Iron Throne's authority rested on dragonfire. When the last dragons died (roughly a century and a half before the series begins), the political order slowly destabilized — the Targaryens' hold on power weakened without their ultimate weapon, culminating in Robert's Rebellion. Daenerys's hatching of three dragons in the series' timeline represents a geopolitical earthquake: the return of the weapon that built the world order.
