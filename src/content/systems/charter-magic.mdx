## Mechanics

Charter Magic is built on the Charter — a vast, structured lattice of meaning that pervades the Old Kingdom and is anchored by the bloodlines of its founding families. Practitioners cast by drawing or invoking Charter Marks, individual symbols from a repertoire of thousands, each corresponding to a specific concept, force, or instruction. A skilled Charter Mage can perceive Marks in their mind's eye, manipulate them, and combine them into compound effects.

Marks are typically invoked through a combination of three conduits. The Mark may be drawn (with a finger in the air, with a knife or pen on a surface, or with chalk or paint on a prepared object). It may be spoken (Charter Marks have associated words). It may also be visualized and willed — advanced practitioners often cast silently. Most working magic combines drawing and speaking: a Charter Mage in combat draws Marks rapidly with their off hand while sustaining concentration on the working.

Charter Mages are typically born with a Charter mark on their forehead — a baptismal sign that connects them to the Charter at infancy. The Mark is invisible most of the time but visible to other Charter Mages and is an absolute identifier of access. Charter sendings, Charter stones, the Bricks of the Wall, and Charter Mages themselves all participate in this network.

A specialized branch of Charter practice is necromancy. The Old Kingdom's necromancers (and their counter-order, the Abhorsen line) use seven enchanted bells to manage and direct the dead across the nine precincts of Death. Charter necromancy is a separate enough sub-system that it may warrant its own future entry; it sits within the Charter framework but uses bells rather than direct Mark-casting as its primary instrument.

## Limitations and costs

The cost structure is layered. The first cost is preparation: Charter Marks must be learned, practiced, and remembered. Working Charter Mages study for years, and the books are explicit that improvising new Marks at speed is the sign of an exceptional practitioner. The second cost is physical: large workings drain the practitioner, and unconsciousness is a real and recurring outcome. The third is environmental: Charter Magic weakens the further south one goes, and across the Wall into Ancelstierre, where the Charter's reach fades, it fails almost entirely.

The fourth — and most distinctive — cost is the proximity of Free Magic. Free Magic is the older, chaotic counterpart to the Charter, and the two are mutually hostile. A Charter Mage who comes into contact with Free Magic risks contamination; a Free Magic creature wounded by Charter symbols is undone by them. This dyad structures most of the series' antagonism: the principal threats — Free Magic creatures, necromancers gone wrong, Greater Dead — are not Charter aberrations but invasions from the older system.

## Practitioner access

Charter Magic is gated by inheritance and by training. Most Charter Mages come from one of the founding bloodlines (the Royal line, the Abhorsens, the Clayr, the Wallmakers) or from families with bloodline connection. Outsiders can sometimes be marked into the Charter, but the access is more limited. Within bloodline-eligible populations, training is institutional — at the Clayr's Glacier, in the Royal lineages, or through apprenticeship with a sitting Abhorsen — and rigorous.

## Cultural role

In the Old Kingdom proper, Charter Magic is the assumed magical baseline. It underwrites agriculture, defense, communication (Charter sendings), and the social order. The Wall along the southern border is itself a vast Charter working maintained against the dead and against Free Magic incursions. South of the Wall, in Ancelstierre, the Charter functions only intermittently and is treated as folklore or superstition by most of the population, who live a roughly early-twentieth-century existence relying on technology rather than magic.
