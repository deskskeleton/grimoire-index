## Mechanics

The **Imperial Arms** (帝具, Teigu) are 48 legendary weapons and artifacts forged roughly a thousand years before the events of *Akame ga Kill!*, commissioned by the first emperor of the Empire to ensure its eternal dominance. Each Teigu was created from the materials of the most powerful **danger beasts** (the world's monstrous fauna), combined with rare ores and the expertise of the era's greatest craftsmen. The result is a set of unique, irreplaceable tools of war, each possessing a single extraordinary ability.

Teigu take wildly different forms. Some are conventional weapons with supernatural properties: **Murasame** (One Cut Killer) is a katana coated in a lethal poison that kills with any cut that draws blood, injecting a curse-toxin that stops the heart. **Pumpkin** (Spirit Gun) is a rifle whose destructive power scales with the user's emotional distress — the more desperate the situation, the stronger its shots. Others are biological: **Lionelle** (Beast King) is a belt that transforms the user into a lion-human hybrid with enhanced strength, speed, and regeneration. **Incursio** (Demon Armor) is a full-body armor evolved from a still-living danger beast called Tyrant, which continues to adapt and evolve through combat.

Some Teigu are stranger still. **Spectator** (Omnipotent Five Sights) is an eye-shaped artifact granting five distinct visual abilities, including precognition and long-range sight. **Demon's Extract** (Demon God Manifestation) is a substance — the blood of an ice-type danger beast — that grants absolute dominion over ice when consumed, allowing the user to freeze landscapes, create armies of ice soldiers, and stop time within frozen zones. Esdeath, the series' primary antagonist, wields this Teigu to devastating effect.

The defining mechanic of the system is **compatibility**. Each Teigu selects its wielder. A person who is not compatible with a given Teigu will fail to activate it, and in extreme cases of incompatibility, the attempt kills the user outright. There is no reliable way to predict compatibility before the attempt — it is an innate resonance between the person's nature and the weapon's nature. This means Teigu cannot simply be distributed to the strongest soldiers; they must find their matching wielders.

Every Teigu possesses a **Trump Card** — an ultimate technique that represents the absolute limit of its power. Trump Cards are overwhelmingly powerful but carry severe costs. Incursio's Trump Card allows the wielder to fuse completely with the danger beast inside the armor, gaining enormous power at the risk of being consumed by the beast's will. Murasame's Trump Card allows the user to cut themselves and absorb the poison, entering a temporary state of dramatically enhanced speed and strength at the cost of permanent physical damage. Most Trump Cards can only be used once, and many destroy the Teigu or kill the wielder in the process.

The series establishes a brutal narrative rule: **when two Teigu users fight, one of them will die**. This is not a strict mechanical law — exceptions exist — but it functions as the system's central dramatic principle. Teigu battles are existential. There is no sparring, no rematch culture, no safe testing of relative strength. Every engagement is potentially final.

## Limitations and costs

The first and most absolute limitation is **finite supply**. Only 48 Teigu were ever created. Many have been lost, destroyed, or hidden over the centuries. A destroyed Teigu is gone permanently — the craftsmanship and materials that produced them cannot be replicated. Every Teigu lost in battle diminishes the total power available in the world.

The compatibility requirement functions as a hard access gate. An army might possess multiple Teigu with no one compatible to wield them. Conversely, a compatible user might never encounter their matching weapon. The Empire and the revolutionary army Night Raid both spend significant effort seeking compatible wielders, and failed compatibility attempts represent a real risk — death by rejection is documented.

Trump Cards impose a steep cost ceiling. The ultimate expression of a Teigu's power typically requires something in return: the user's body, the weapon's continued existence, or both. Tatsumi's progressive fusion with Incursio gradually transforms his body, threatening to erase his humanity. Mine's final use of Pumpkin's full power leaves her comatose. The system punishes desperation — the moment you need your weapon's full power most is the moment it is most likely to destroy you.

Individual Teigu have specific vulnerabilities. Murasame's one-cut kill does not work on mechanical or inorganic opponents. Lionelle's regeneration cannot regrow severed limbs. Pumpkin's power scaling with the user's danger means it is weakest when the wielder feels safe. These bespoke limitations prevent any single Teigu from becoming universally dominant.

The **Shingu** — a set of weaker, mass-produced weapons created after the original 48 — represent an attempt to circumvent the Teigu's limitations. They are more numerous and lack compatibility requirements, but their power is categorically inferior, and they carry their own side effects.

## Practitioner access

Access to the Imperial Arms is determined by three factors: possession, compatibility, and survival. The Empire hoards most known Teigu, distributing them to its military commanders and elite enforcers. Night Raid, the revolutionary assassination squad, acquires Teigu through battlefield salvage — killing an Imperial user and claiming their weapon, then testing compatibility among their own members.

There is no training system for Teigu use in the traditional sense. A compatible wielder activates the weapon instinctively, and mastery comes through combat experience rather than formal instruction. Some Teigu, like Incursio, actively evolve with their user, becoming stronger and more specialized through repeated use. Others, like Demon's Extract, deliver their full power immediately but demand the user's sanity and willpower to control.

The user base is by definition small — at most 48 people in the world can be Teigu wielders at any given time, and in practice the number is far fewer, given lost and destroyed arms. This scarcity makes every Teigu user a strategic asset of national importance, whether they serve the Empire or fight against it.

## Cultural role

The Imperial Arms are foundational to the Empire's political structure and its thousand-year reign. The first emperor created them explicitly as instruments of state power — tools to ensure that no force could challenge Imperial authority. For centuries, this worked. The Empire's military dominance rested on its monopoly over the majority of Teigu, and its generals and enforcers wielded them as symbols of Imperial might.

The revolutionary war that drives the series' plot is, in part, a war over Teigu redistribution. Night Raid's strategy is not merely assassination but weapon capture — every Imperial enforcer killed is a Teigu potentially transferred to the revolutionary cause. The balance of Teigu between factions functions as a proxy for the balance of power in the civil war.

Within the narrative, Teigu serve as markers of individual identity and moral alignment. The weapon a character wields reflects who they are: Akame's Murasame is clean and lethal, reflecting her surgical efficiency as an assassin. Esdeath's Demon's Extract reflects her absolute nature — total dominion, no half-measures. Tatsumi's Incursio evolves as he does, its progressive transformation mirroring his journey from naive recruit to battle-hardened warrior willing to sacrifice his humanity for his cause. The system's finality — that Teigu battles end in death, that Trump Cards cost everything, that broken weapons are gone forever — reinforces the series' core thematic argument that power in a corrupt system demands irreversible sacrifice.
