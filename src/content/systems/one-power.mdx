## Mechanics

The One Power is drawn from the True Source, the metaphysical engine that turns the Wheel of Time. Channelers — those born able to draw on it — touch the Source and weave threads from one or both of its two halves: saidin, the male half, and saidar, the female half. The two halves are incompatible: a male channeler cannot perceive or interact with saidar, and vice versa. The two halves also feel and behave differently in fiction. Saidin is described as something to be seized and forced; saidar is described as something to be embraced and directed. Mechanically, this manifests in different teaching traditions and different combat textures.

A channeler weaves the One Power into combinations of five elemental flows — Earth, Fire, Water, Air, and Spirit. Specific weaves correspond to specific named effects: a particular weave produces a Fireball, another produces a Healing, another opens a gateway between two locations. The system is closer to a recipe-book than to free-form magic. Channelers learn the named weaves, practice them precisely, and combine them by combining flows. Most channelers have natural strengths in some flows and weaknesses in others, which dictates their specialization.

Channelers vary enormously in raw potential. Strength in the Power is measured on a scale that is consistent across genders, though saidin and saidar occupy different sides of it. Strength is not the same as skill: a strong but poorly trained channeler is dangerous; a weak but disciplined one can outperform a stronger novice. Linking — joining multiple channelers into a single combined weave — multiplies effective strength and is a major strategic consideration.

## Limitations and costs

The signature cost is the taint. Saidin was corrupted at the end of the Age of Legends, and from that point on, every man who channels gradually goes mad and develops a horrifying physical wasting disease. This was the founding catastrophe of the world's history (the Breaking) and remained an active mechanic through most of the main series until the protagonist's actions in the climactic volume cleansed the male half. Until then, male channelers were hunted by the female-led Aes Sedai and forcibly "gentled" — severed from the Source — to prevent their madness from spreading.

Cleansed or not, channeling has secondary costs: it is physically taxing (overuse can cause exhaustion or burnout), it is socially fraught (channelers are politically dangerous and culturally feared in many regions), and it is gated by training (improperly trained channelers are vulnerable to "burning out" or "stilling" themselves through accident).

A particularly distinctive cost is the threat of being severed — losing access to the Source through external action (gentling, stilling) or internal failure (overreach). For a channeler, severing is functionally equivalent to grief or death; many severed channelers in the series lose the will to live.

## Practitioner access

Ability to channel is innate, distributed across populations, and unpredictable. Some channel from birth instinctively; many discover ability spontaneously in adolescence or adulthood. A subset of would-be channelers can only access the Source if formally taught — these are gated by access to a training institution.

The Aes Sedai, headquartered at the White Tower in Tar Valon, are the dominant trained order of female channelers and have been for over three thousand years. Other traditions exist: the Wise Ones of the Aiel, the Windfinders of the Atha'an Miere, the Kin, the Damane and Sul'dam of Seanchan, and (after the cleansing) the resurrected Asha'man order of male channelers founded by the protagonist.

## Cultural role

The One Power is openly known across Randland, but cultural attitudes toward channelers vary enormously. The Aes Sedai are simultaneously feared, sought after as advisors, suspected of manipulation, and treated as a global political power. Male channelers are, until very late in the series, hunted as existential threats. Some cultures (Seanchan) treat channelers as collared property; others (the Aiel Wise Ones) treat them as honored authorities. The geopolitics of channeling is one of the series' most pervasive themes.
