## Mechanics

**Bondsmagi Binding** is the sorcery practiced by the Bondsmagi of Karthain — an exclusive guild of magic-users who operate as the only organized practitioners of magic in the Therin Tableau. Their magic is rooted in **true names** — the knowledge of a thing's essential identity, which grants power over it. A Bondsmage who knows someone's true name can compel obedience, inflict pain, track them across distances, and potentially kill them.

The system's known capabilities include: telepathic communication and mental intrusion, elemental manipulation (fire, wind, weather), compulsion and mind-control through true-name knowledge, animal control (the Falconer's use of birds as extensions of his will), far-seeing and tracking, and direct physical assault through magical force. The Falconer demonstrates high-level combat application — using a scorpion hawk as a weapon, reading minds, and projecting force — while other Bondsmagi show capabilities ranging from subtle political manipulation to large-scale environmental effects.

The true-name mechanism creates an asymmetric power dynamic: a Bondsmage who knows your name has power over you, but you have no reciprocal leverage unless you know theirs. This is why Bondsmagi guard their true names obsessively and use professional titles (the Falconer, Patience, Coldmarrow) rather than birth names. The entire Bondsmagi social structure is organized around the control and protection of true-name knowledge.

The Bondsmagi are born with magical talent — it is innate and hereditary — but raw talent must be trained through years of study at Karthain. The guild identifies children with the gift and recruits (or conscripts) them, ensuring that no rogue practitioners operate outside their control.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation is **institutional constraint**. The Bondsmagi's power is governed by treaties with the major city-states: they may not use magic to seize political power directly, and they hire out their services under contracted terms. These treaties exist because the Bondsmagi, despite their overwhelming individual power, are too few to govern a continent — and because internal factions within Karthain prevent unified action. The treaties are enforced by mutual deterrence: the city-states could not defeat the Bondsmagi, but unrestricted Bondsmagi aggression would unite the world against them.

The second limitation is **true-name dependency**. The most powerful applications of Bondsmagic require knowledge of the target's true name. Without it, a Bondsmage's power is more limited — still formidable, but less precise and less absolute. This creates a practical intelligence requirement: before a Bondsmage can fully dominate a target, they must research or discover their true name.

The third limitation is **factional politics**. The Bondsmagi are not unified — they are riven by internal factions (broadly, those who want to maintain the treaty system and those who want to abandon it and seize direct power). This internal conflict prevents the Bondsmagi from exercising their full collective power and creates opportunities for non-magical actors to play factions against each other.

## Practitioner access

Bondsmagic requires innate talent — not everyone can learn it. The Bondsmagi of Karthain maintain an absolute monopoly on magical practice: they identify all magically gifted children and bring them to Karthain for training. Practicing magic outside the guild is forbidden and enforced with lethal consequences. This monopoly is the foundation of the Bondsmagi's power — they are the only source of magical services in the world, and they set the terms of engagement.

## Cultural role

The Bondsmagi are feared, resented, and indispensable. City-states hire them for wars, espionage, and infrastructure projects, while simultaneously chafing under their monopoly pricing and arrogance. The Bondsmagi see themselves as a superior species — magical talent sets them apart from ordinary humanity, and their guild culture reinforces this sense of elevation. In the narrative, they represent institutional power at its most concentrated: a small group with overwhelming capability that maintains its position through a combination of monopoly control, internal discipline, and the implicit threat of destruction. Locke Lamora's conflicts with the Bondsmagi pit individual cleverness against systemic power — and the question of whether cunning can defeat magic is one of the series' central tensions.
