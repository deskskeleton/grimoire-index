## Mechanics

Spirit Island's power system operates through three interlocking layers: innate powers unique to each spirit, a growing hand of power cards drawn from shared minor and major power decks, and a presence track that governs the spirit's capacity to channel energy and influence the board. Each turn, a spirit chooses a growth option (placing presence, gaining power cards, reclaiming played cards), pays energy to play power cards, and then resolves those powers in two phases — fast powers before the Invaders act, and slow powers after. This temporal division is mechanically critical: fast powers allow preemptive strikes and defensive preparations, while slow powers deliver heavier effects that arrive too late to prevent the current turn's damage but reshape the board for subsequent rounds.

Elemental thresholds govern the activation of a spirit's innate powers, which are printed on the spirit panel rather than drawn from the shared decks. Each power card a spirit plays contributes elemental symbols — Sun, Moon, Fire, Water, Earth, Air, Plant, Animal — and when the total symbols played in a turn meet the thresholds listed on an innate power, that power activates at increasing levels of intensity. This system creates a deckbuilding puzzle layered over the tactical game: spirits must select power cards that not only provide useful effects on their own but also contribute the right elemental mix to trigger their innate abilities. A spirit that gains a powerful major power card with the wrong elemental profile may find that its innate powers stop activating reliably, creating a genuine tension between individual card strength and systemic coherence.

The presence track is the system's most elegant resource mechanism. Each spirit has two tracks — one governing energy income per turn, one governing the number of card plays per turn — with presence tokens covering the track spaces. As a spirit places presence on the island (through growth options), it uncovers higher values on these tracks, gradually increasing its capacity. But presence on the island also defines the spirit's range: most powers can only target lands where the spirit has presence or lands adjacent to it. This dual function means that presence placement is simultaneously resource development and spatial positioning, and the tension between concentrating presence for defensive coverage and spreading it for economic growth creates a core strategic dilemma.

Fear is the system's shared meta-resource, generated by specific power cards and effects that terrorize the colonial Invaders. As the spirits accumulate Fear, they earn Fear cards that impose penalties on the Invaders and alter win conditions — at the highest terror level, spirits win simply by having no Invaders on the board rather than needing to destroy all their settlements. This mechanic reframes the spirits' supernatural powers as instruments of psychological warfare as much as physical destruction, and it creates emergent cooperation between spirits whose individual powers contribute to a collective Fear economy.

## Limitations and costs

Energy serves as the primary resource constraint on spirit powers, functioning as a per-turn budget that limits how many and which power cards a spirit can play. Energy income is determined by the presence track and varies dramatically between spirits — Lightning's Swift Strike generates abundant energy early but has limited card plays, while A Spread of Rampant Green plays many cheap powers but struggles to afford expensive ones. This asymmetry means that cost limitations are not uniform but spirit-specific, reinforcing each spirit's distinct mechanical identity. A major power card costing five energy might be trivial for one spirit and aspirational for another, creating natural variation in how different spirits experience the game's power curve.

The temporal division between fast and slow powers imposes a cost measured in timing rather than resources. Slow powers are generally more powerful than fast powers of equivalent energy cost, but they resolve after the Invaders have explored, built, and ravaged — meaning they cannot prevent the current turn's damage. This creates agonizing decisions about whether to play fast powers for immediate but modest effect or invest in slow powers that address the larger strategic picture while accepting short-term losses. The cost of slowness is not abstract but concrete: lands are ravaged, Dahan are killed, and blight accumulates during the gap between a slow power's payment and its resolution.

Gaining major powers — the system's most dramatic effects, capable of devastating entire regions or fundamentally altering board state — requires a spirit to forget (permanently discard) one of its existing power cards. This replacement cost means that acquiring greater capability always comes with the loss of flexibility or elemental coverage. A spirit that loads up on major powers may find itself with a thin hand of cards that cannot reliably trigger innate power thresholds, or that lacks the cheap utility effects needed to handle small-scale problems. The system resists power accumulation by making growth require sacrifice, ensuring that spirits become different over the course of a game rather than simply stronger.

Blight functions as a shared loss condition that constrains the spirits' tolerance for failure. When the Invaders ravage a land and deal enough damage, blight is added to the board. If too much blight accumulates, the island is overwhelmed and the spirits lose. This means that every turn a spirit fails to adequately address Invader activity represents not just a local setback but progress toward collective defeat. The blight economy creates urgency that prevents spirits from playing conservatively — a spirit that hoards energy and builds slowly may optimize its long-term power curve but contribute to a blight cascade that ends the game before that power can be deployed.

## Practitioner access

Each spirit in Spirit Island is a singular entity with a unique identity expressed through every mechanical element: its growth options, its innate powers, its starting power cards, its presence track values, and its special rules. There are no generic spirits and no character creation — each spirit is a complete, authored design that plays fundamentally differently from every other. Lightning's Swift Strike operates as an aggressive damage-dealer that strikes fast but cannot sustain prolonged defense. Vital Strength of the Earth is a slow, immovable fortress that absorbs damage and protects the land through sheer endurance. These are not variations on a template but distinct mechanical philosophies that require different strategic thinking.

The spirit complexity rating system acknowledges that different spirits demand different levels of player expertise. Low-complexity spirits like River Surges in Sunlight offer straightforward growth paths and intuitive power interactions, making them accessible to new players. High-complexity spirits like Finder of Paths Unseen or Starlight Seeks Its Form require sophisticated understanding of the game's systems, timing, and spatial relationships. This explicit complexity gradient means that practitioner access in Spirit Island is gated not by fictional barriers within the game world but by player skill and experience in the metagame — a spirit's power is limited by the human mind directing it.

The Dahan — the island's indigenous human inhabitants — represent a secondary layer of practitioner access within the fiction. While the spirits are the primary wielders of supernatural power, the Dahan have their own relationship with the island and its magic. Mechanically, the Dahan function as allied units that counterattack Invaders and can be moved, protected, or empowered by spirit powers. Several spirits — particularly those with the Animal and Earth elements — have powers specifically designed to work through and with the Dahan, suggesting a collaborative relationship between human and spirit agency. The Dahan are not passive beneficiaries of spirit power but active participants in the island's defense.

Expansion content has progressively widened the range of spirit identities and mechanical approaches available to players. The base game's eight spirits established the core design space. Branch and Claw added spirits that interact with the expansion's event and disease systems. Jagged Earth introduced spirits with radically unconventional mechanics — Shifting Memory of Ages tracks elements across turns rather than within them, while Many Minds Move as One treats its presence as a swarm of beasts rather than a fixed spiritual entity. Each expansion has pushed the boundaries of what a spirit can be mechanically, demonstrating that the system's framework is capacious enough to accommodate designs that would have seemed impossible within the base game's parameters.

## Cultural role

Spirit Island's power system is inseparable from the game's postcolonial thematic framework. The spirits are not generic fantasy entities defending generic territory — they are indigenous supernatural beings fighting against a colonial invasion modeled on real historical processes of exploration, settlement, and extraction. The Invaders explore new lands, build towns and cities, and ravage the environment in a mechanical cycle that mirrors the actual patterns of colonial expansion. The spirits' powers — destroying settlements, generating fear, empowering indigenous resistance, corrupting colonial infrastructure — are framed as defensive responses to invasion rather than aggressive acts of conquest. This framing transforms every mechanical decision into a thematic statement about resistance, sovereignty, and the relationship between land and power.

The system's treatment of Fear as a win condition carries particular thematic weight. The spirits do not need to kill every Invader to win; they need to make the island so terrifying that the colonial project becomes unsustainable. At Terror Level 3, the mere absence of cities is sufficient for victory — the Invaders flee not because they have been physically annihilated but because their will to colonize has been broken. This mechanic argues that resistance to colonialism is as much psychological as military, and that supernatural power directed at morale and perception can be as effective as direct destruction. The spirits are not warriors but terrors, and the game's most efficient strategies often involve generating Fear rather than removing Invader pieces.

The identity-through-mechanics approach serves a worldbuilding function that extends beyond individual gameplay sessions. Each spirit's mechanical design communicates something about its nature, its relationship to the island, and its philosophy of resistance without requiring extensive flavor text. Ocean's Hungry Grasp, which can only place presence on coastal lands and whose powers push Invaders into the sea, tells a complete story through its mechanics alone. Bringer of Dreams and Nightmares, which generates Fear efficiently but deals almost no direct damage, embodies a specific theory of supernatural power — that the mind is more vulnerable than the body, and that terror is a more sustainable weapon than violence.

The cooperative structure of the game means that spirit powers must be evaluated not in isolation but in combination, which creates emergent narrative about alliance and complementarity. A game where Lightning's Swift Strike and Vital Strength of the Earth work together tells a story about the relationship between aggressive and defensive approaches to resistance. A game pairing Shadows Flicker Like Flame with River Surges in Sunlight explores how deception and natural force can coordinate. These emergent narratives arise from mechanical interaction rather than scripted plot, demonstrating how a well-designed power system can generate thematic meaning through play rather than through predetermined story.
