## Mechanics

Magic in the *Shades of Magic* series operates on two levels: the ambient elemental system available to ordinary practitioners, and the transcendent blood-magic of the Antari.

**Elemental magic** is the baseline. Practitioners in Red London (Arnes) draw on ambient magical energy to manipulate the classical elements — earth, water, fire, air, metal, bone, and occasionally more esoteric elements like shadow or spirit. Most practitioners have an affinity for one or two elements and work them through a combination of will, gesture, and verbal command (the High Royal language of Red London serves as the primary magical tongue). Magical ability varies widely: some people have a trace of elemental sensitivity, while trained magicians compete in the **Essen Tasch** — a triannual tournament demonstrating mastery across multiple elements. The availability of this magic is directly tied to the ambient magical saturation of the world. In Red London, magic flows freely and most people have at least minor sensitivity. In Grey London (our world), magic has almost entirely bled away, and practitioners are reduced to hedge-tricks and delusions. In White London, magic is so scarce that rulers seize power through brute force and hoarding.

**Antari blood-magic** transcends these limitations. Antari are born, not made — marked by one entirely black eye — and possess the ability to use their own blood as a medium for spells that operate outside normal elemental constraints. By cutting themselves and pressing blood to a surface while speaking commands in the Antari tongue (a language older than any of the four Londons), they can accomplish effects that ordinary magic cannot: **As Travars** opens doors between worlds, **As Hasari** commands healing, **As Steno** compels stillness. Each command costs blood — literally — and more powerful workings demand more blood or greater pain. Antari are exceedingly rare; at the time of the trilogy, only two are known to exist (Kell and Holland), though Lila Bard develops Antari abilities over the course of the series.

**Black London** and its entity **Osaron** represent magic without constraint — a world where magic became predatory, self-aware, and all-consuming. The sealing of Black London and the severing of connections between worlds were desperate measures to prevent this contagion from spreading. When Black London's influence leaks through in the trilogy, it takes the form of possession, corruption of elemental magic, and the offer of seemingly limitless power at the cost of autonomy.

## Limitations and costs

Elemental magic is limited by **ambient availability** and **personal talent**. A strong magician in Grey London can barely light a candle; the same talent in Red London could control a bonfire. Even in Red London, few practitioners master more than one or two elements, and the Essen Tasch champions represent the pinnacle of decades of training.

Antari magic costs **blood** — each working requires a fresh cut, and heavier workings demand proportionally more. Sustained or repeated use weakens the Antari physically. Travelling between worlds is particularly taxing, and carrying objects or people across multiplies the cost. Blood-script commands also carry the risk of **unintended consequences** when the language is imperfectly understood; the Antari tongue is ancient and not fully catalogued.

The deepest limitation is **contamination**. Objects and people that cross between worlds carry traces of their origin world's magical signature, and these traces can act as vectors for corruption — particularly Black London's influence. This is why the doors between worlds were sealed and why smuggling between Londons is forbidden: even a small token from the wrong world can serve as a foothold for Osaron's consciousness.

## Practitioner access

Ordinary elemental magic is accessible to anyone with innate sensitivity, which varies by world. In Red London, most of the population has some degree of elemental awareness, and formal magical training is available through institutions and apprenticeships. In White London, sensitivity exists but the scarcity of ambient magic means only the strongest or most ruthless can practise effectively. In Grey London, sensitivity is so rare as to be functionally mythological.

Antari ability is innate and cannot be trained or transferred. It appears to be unrelated to heredity — Antari are born unpredictably, sometimes centuries apart. The black eye manifests at birth. The Antari tongue and blood-script commands must be learned, but the underlying ability to use them is binary: you are Antari or you are not.

## Cultural role

In Red London, magic is central to daily life — it shapes architecture, governance, entertainment (the Essen Tasch is a major cultural event), and social hierarchy. The royal family maintains power partly through magical strength, and Antari serve as ambassadors between the remaining connected worlds.

In White London, magic is a tool of domination. The scarcity of ambient power means rulers maintain control through violence and coercion, and magical strength is the primary qualification for the throne. In Grey London, magic is a fading memory: the few people who sense traces of it are dismissed as mad. This spectrum — from magical abundance to magical starvation — is the series' central worldbuilding argument about how a resource's availability shapes the societies built around it.
