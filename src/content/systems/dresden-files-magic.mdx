## Mechanics

A wizard's magic is the deliberate expression of will. The practitioner draws on **internal life-force**, focuses it through emotion (anger, fear, desire, faith — almost any strong feeling will do), and shapes it into an effect. Two practical modes dominate.

**Evocation** is the fast magic of crisis: throwing fire, raising a wind, hurling a kinetic blast, conjuring a shield of force. Spells are typically cued with a short, often pseudo-Latin word ("*Fuego!*", "*Forzare!*"), a directing gesture, and (for serious work) a focus item the wizard has prepared ahead of time — a staff, a blasting rod, a ring storing kinetic charges. Evocation produces results in seconds but is metabolically taxing and very imprecise; small finely controlled effects are *harder*, not easier, than blunt large ones.

**Thaumaturgy** is the slow magic of ritual. The wizard works in a circle (a literal chalk-or-salt circle that contains and isolates the energies inside it), uses **symbolic links** between target and effect — a strand of hair, a name, a photograph, a piece of clothing — and may take minutes or hours to complete the working. Thaumaturgy can do almost anything evocation can do and many things it cannot (tracking spells, healing, communications across distance, binding entities), provided the wizard has time, materials, and the stamina to pour into the rite. Larger workings can be fueled by another being's life — a critical and morally fraught option.

A handful of additional, rarer modes appear in the series: **necromancy** (manipulation of dead flesh and ghosts; explicitly forbidden), **soulfire** (a divinely granted augmentation that adds creative force to a working, available only via specific patrons), **hellfire** (a demonically granted augmentation, the dark mirror of soulfire), and ritual **summoning and binding** of beings from the spirit world.

## Limitations and costs

The headline cost is the **Seven Laws of Magic**, enforced by the White Council's Wardens with summary execution. They forbid: killing a mortal with magic, transforming another, invading another's mind, enthralling another's will, raising the dead, reaching back through time, and opening the way to the Outside. Each Law produces a real metaphysical mark — a wizard who breaks one becomes both legally hunted and *spiritually* corrupted, with later transgressions easier than the first.

The second cost is **technology disruption**. A practicing wizard's mere presence damages modern electronics: phones drop calls, computers crash, cars stall. Magic interferes with the precise low-energy operations of modern technology, and the effect strengthens with the wizard's stress and casting. The result is a structural reason wizards cannot integrate easily into mundane life and tend to live with deliberate technological underinvestment.

The third is **physical toll**. Evocation is exhausting and dangerous: a wizard who tries to push too much energy at once risks a "feedback" that can incinerate or kill them. Thaumaturgy demands sustained concentration; a broken circle ruins the working and may release whatever was bound inside it. Magic also obeys conservation: the energy for an effect must come from somewhere — the wizard, an external source, or (in forbidden modes) another life.

The fourth is **knowledge and preparation**. Every formal working requires a specific symbolic vocabulary, ritual layout, and (often) materials gathered from particular places at particular times. Improvisation is possible but dangerous; most wizards rely on practiced workings learned over years of apprenticeship.

A fifth, distinctive cost is **soulgazing**. When a wizard meets another person's eyes for too long, both parties involuntarily perceive each other's souls in a single overwhelming vision. The image is permanent and unforgettable. This makes ordinary social interaction more freighted than it appears, and it is also a recurring information source in the series.

## Practitioner access

Magical talent is hereditary, present in roughly one in a million people, and develops in adolescence. Untrained talents typically come to the attention of the **White Council** — the international government of wizards — who offer apprenticeship and Council membership. Refusal, or breaking a Law during the unmanaged early years, frequently leads to the Wardens.

Below the Council-wizard tier, the world is populated by lesser practitioners: focused practitioners (with a single narrow specialty), psychics and sensitives, witches working folk traditions, and ritualists who can perform rote thaumaturgy without true wizardly power. The Council recognizes a hierarchy here; only full wizards are bound by the Laws and entitled to Council protection.

## Cultural role

Magic is hidden from the modern world by long custom, by the technology-disruption effect, and by the active management of the supernatural community: the **Unseelie Accords** govern relations between wizards, vampire courts, faerie courts, demons, and other powers, and breach of the Accords is itself a major precipitating event. The mundane world's awareness of the supernatural is patchy and deniable; wizards prefer it that way, both because it makes their lives easier and because the modern state's response to a confirmed wizardly populace would not be friendly.

Within the supernatural community, wizards are an organized political bloc — the White Council acts as a quasi-state — and Harry Dresden's particular niche, as a publicly listed Chicago wizard taking on paying clients, is treated in-setting as eccentric and slightly disreputable.
