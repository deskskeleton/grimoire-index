## Mechanics

Magic in *Uprooted* divides into two distinct modes of practice, and the tension between them drives the novel's plot and theme.

**Formulaic spell-craft**, as practised by Sarkan (the Dragon) and the court wizards of Polnya, works through precise cantrips — spells with specific words, gestures, and sequences that must be performed exactly. Each spell has a name (often drawn from its inventor), a verbal formula, and a defined effect. The Dragon's magic is intellectual, controlled, and reproducible: a spell that works once will work again if the formula is followed. This tradition treats magic as something like a science, to be studied, catalogued, and refined through precision. The Dragon can transmute materials, ward against corruption, scry at a distance, and work complex layered enchantments — all through disciplined application of known formulae.

**Intuitive magic**, as Agnieszka discovers in herself, works through emotional connection, folk song, and improvisation. Rather than following a formula, Agnieszka reaches into the essence of what she wants to accomplish and pulls magic through whatever channel feels right — often a half-remembered lullaby or village song, hummed and adapted in the moment. Her workings are messy, unpredictable, and structurally incomprehensible to the Dragon, but they access a layer of magical reality that formulae cannot reach. She can feel the health of the valley's living things, sense the Wood's corruption at a visceral level, and work healings and purifications that defeat the Dragon's most rigorous cantrips.

The novel's climactic magic comes from **synthesis**: Agnieszka and the Dragon working together, her intuition providing direction and connection while his precision provides structure and power. Their combined working — a spell called the **Summoning** — is far more potent than either could achieve alone.

The **Wood** operates as a third magical force: a self-sustaining ecology of corruption rooted in ancient grief. The Wood can corrupt people (turning them into *walkers* — puppets controlled by the forest), taint water and soil, and project its influence through infected matter. Its corruption is not merely physical but psychological — it reaches into memory and desire. The Wood Queen, its originating intelligence, is the remnant of a being destroyed by human violence, and the Wood's magic is fundamentally an expression of unresolved trauma.

## Limitations and costs

Formulaic magic requires **extensive study and exact execution**. A mispronounced cantrip fails or backfires. The Dragon has spent centuries accumulating his repertoire, and even he cannot improvise — when a situation falls outside his catalogue, he is limited. His approach also cannot fully reach the Wood's corruption, because the corruption operates on an emotional and spiritual level that formulae do not address.

Intuitive magic is **exhausting and imprecise**. Agnieszka's workings drain her physically, and she cannot always control their scope or effects. Early in her training, she ruins more spells than she completes. Her approach also lacks reproducibility: she cannot teach her methods to others in any systematic way, and what works in one moment may not work in the next.

Both modes require **significant personal energy**. Extended magical work leaves practitioners staggering, and the Dragon's decades of holding back the Wood have visibly worn him despite his longevity. Working against the Wood's corruption is particularly costly because the corruption actively resists — purifying a tainted person or place requires sustained effort against an intelligent, hostile force.

## Practitioner access

Magical ability appears to be innate and rare. Wizards are born, not made, and they emerge unpredictably from the general population — there is no hereditary magical aristocracy. The kingdom of Polnya recognises wizards as a national resource and trains them at court, but the number of active wizards is small (perhaps a dozen in the entire kingdom). The Dragon's arrangement with the valley — taking a girl every ten years — is his method of identifying local-born wizards, though this is not understood by the villagers.

Agnieszka's case suggests that some wizards have a natural affinity for one mode or the other, and that forcing a wizard into the wrong mode (as the Dragon initially tries to force Agnieszka into formulaic practice) produces poor results. The implication is that magical tradition has calcified around formulaic approaches, and that intuitive practitioners have been lost or stifled by a system that does not recognise their kind of talent.

## Cultural role

Magic is openly known and socially valued in Polnya. Wizards hold positions of influence — the Dragon controls his valley as a feudal lord, and the court wizards serve the king directly. However, magical practitioners are also feared and somewhat alienated from ordinary society: the Dragon is respected but not loved, and the valley's people dread his periodic selection of a young woman. The Wood's existence shapes the entire region's culture — villages live in its shadow, and the ongoing struggle against its expansion is the defining fact of life in the valley borderlands.
