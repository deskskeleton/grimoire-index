## Mechanics

The magic of *The Anubis Gates* is powered by the **residual energy of the Egyptian gods** — deities who were real but whose power has been fading since the rise of monotheism displaced worship away from them. The sorcerers who serve these dying gods are practitioners of a tradition stretching back millennia, but they are working with a diminishing power supply. Every spell costs more than it used to, and the reserves are not being replenished.

The novel's most dramatic magical effect is the **time gates** — holes punched through the fabric of time by a massive sorcerous ritual performed in 1802 by the Master Fikee and his servants. These gates connect specific moments in history, allowing physical passage between eras. The gates are unstable, temporary, and unpredictable — they open and close on schedules determined by astronomical conjunctions, and travelers who miss their window are stranded. The ritual that created them consumed an enormous portion of the remaining divine energy.

**Body-switching** is a distinct magical discipline practiced by several characters. Dog-Face Joe (a werewolf created by an enchanted fur coat bonded to his skin) has the ability to switch bodies with other people, transferring his consciousness — and his lycanthropic curse — into a new host while the displaced victim dies in his old, deteriorating body. The clown Horrabin practices a related but distinct form of body manipulation, maintaining a network of beggars whose bodies he has surgically and magically modified.

The **lycanthropy** in the novel is not a traditional werewolf curse but a magical artifact effect: a fur coat, enchanted with Egyptian animal-transformation magic, that bonded permanently to Joe's skin. The transformation is tied to the fur itself rather than to lunar cycles, and it carries a compulsive, maddening quality — the animal nature fights the human mind for control.

Additional magical disciplines include sympathetic magic (using effigies and symbolic connections to affect targets at a distance), protective wards, and various forms of divination. All draw on the same dwindling reservoir of divine power.

## Limitations and costs

The fundamental limitation is **entropy**. The power source is dying. Every major working depletes a finite reserve that cannot be replenished, because the gods whose worship generated the power are no longer worshipped. The sorcerers of the novel are therefore engaged in a long rearguard action — using the last of a once-vast power supply to accomplish increasingly desperate objectives.

The physical costs are severe. Body-switching destroys the vacated body. The lycanthropic transformation is physically agonizing and psychologically destabilizing. The time gates impose no direct physical cost on travelers, but stranding in the wrong era is effectively a death sentence. Major rituals require material components that are difficult or impossible to obtain in the modern era.

The psychological cost is **madness**. Dog-Face Joe has been driven insane by centuries of body-switching and lycanthropic torment. Doctor Romany, the primary antagonist sorcerer, is a fanatical servant of gods he knows are dying. The Master Fikee has sustained himself far beyond a natural lifespan and exists in a state of decayed, desperate obsession. Prolonged contact with the magic erodes identity and sanity.

The magic is also **irreversible** in most of its major applications. A body switch cannot be undone. A time gate, once closed, cannot be reopened at will. The fur coat cannot be removed. The system's most powerful effects are one-way transformations.

## Practitioner access

The tradition is confined to a small number of Egyptian sorcerers and their apprentices, all of whom trace their training back to the pre-Christian priesthoods of Egypt. There is no way to enter the tradition from outside — the knowledge is transmitted through a closed lineage, and the power source is specific to the Egyptian divine framework. By the time of the novel, the number of practicing sorcerers has dwindled to a handful. No new practitioners are being trained because there is barely enough power remaining to sustain the existing ones.

## Cultural role

The magic is entirely hidden from the broader culture of Regency-era London. The sorcerers operate in the criminal underworld and the margins of society — the beggars' guild, the traveling shows, the opium dens. Their activities are interpreted by the mundane world as crime, madness, or coincidence. The time gates are unknown to anyone outside the sorcerous conspiracy. The entire magical tradition is dying alongside the gods who power it, and its practitioners are engaged in a last, desperate attempt to reverse that decline — an attempt the novel makes clear is doomed.
