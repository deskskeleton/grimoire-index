## Mechanics

The Divine Cities' magic system is built on a single premise: the six **Divinities** were real gods whose will directly altered the physical laws of reality. Each Divinity had a distinct domain, personality, and approach to miracle-working, and the miracles they performed were not temporary enchantments but permanent alterations to the fabric of the world.

**Kolkan**, the god of order and justice, created rigid hierarchical laws that were literally inviolable — not merely written rules but physical constraints that prevented certain actions within his domain. **Voortya**, the goddess of war and death, crafted weapons of impossible lethality and raised armies of the dead. **Olvos**, the goddess of wisdom, could perceive truth across any distance and granted her followers clarity of understanding. **Jukov**, the god of chaos, delighted in paradox and created miracles that contradicted the other Divinities' works. **Ahanas** governed fertility and the natural world. **Taalhavras** governed the seas and storms. Each Divinity's miracles reflected their nature completely — they were not casting spells but expressing their fundamental being through reality itself.

The Continent's civilization was built on this divine infrastructure. Cities contained **miracle-architecture**: staircases that led to rooms occupying no physical space, buildings that were larger inside than outside, bridges that existed only when a faithful person walked upon them. Weapons blessed by Voortya struck true regardless of the wielder's skill. Regions governed by a particular Divinity operated under locally altered physics — gravity, time, and causality could differ from one district to the next.

When the Kaj killed the Divinities, most miracles collapsed instantly. Buildings that defied physics crumbled. Impossible spaces folded shut, sometimes with people still inside. Weapons lost their blessing. The Continent, which had been the dominant civilization in the world, was devastated — not by war but by the sudden absence of the infrastructure that had made its cities function. This event, called the **Blink**, was an architectural and social apocalypse.

However, some miracles survived as **residuals** — fragments of divine reality that continue to operate without their creator. A residual might be a room that still exists in impossible geometry, a weapon that still strikes with unnatural accuracy, or a creature that was divinely created and persists because no one has found a way to destroy it. Residuals are unpredictable, often dangerous, and fiercely regulated by the Saypuri government, which outlaws Continental religious practice and confiscates divine artifacts.

## Limitations and costs

The Divinities' miracles were not costless even at their height. Each Divinity demanded **worship and obedience** as the price of their patronage, and the nature of that worship shaped the Continent's culture in ways that the post-divine world is still reckoning with. Voortya demanded military devotion and human sacrifice. Kolkan demanded absolute submission to his moral code. The Divinities' relationship with the Continent was transactional — miracles in exchange for faith — and when the faith structure collapsed with the gods' deaths, so did the social order built upon it.

**Residuals** carry their own costs. They are inherently unstable, operating according to divine logic that no longer has a governing intelligence behind it. A residual weapon might function perfectly for decades and then behave in ways no one can predict. Residual spaces can shift or collapse without warning. Interacting with residuals is dangerous precisely because they are fragments of a system that no longer has a designer maintaining it — they are unpatched code running on hardware that has lost its operating system.

The deeper cost is **civilizational dependency**. The Continent built everything — architecture, agriculture, military power, social hierarchy — on divine miracles. When those miracles ended, the Continent had no fallback. An entire civilization had to rebuild from the foundation up, without the knowledge or traditions of non-miraculous engineering that other cultures had developed. The trilogy frames this as the central tragedy: not that the gods died, but that an entire people had been made dependent on power they did not control and could not replace.

## Practitioner access

In the divine era, miracles were performed exclusively by the Divinities themselves or granted to faithful followers through prayer, ritual, and sacrifice. No mortal could independently produce miracles — they could only petition a Divinity and hope to be answered. The priestly hierarchies of each Divinity mediated this access, creating powerful clerical establishments that functioned as both religious and governmental institutions.

In the post-divine era, no one can create new miracles. The only access to divine power comes through **residual artifacts and spaces**, which are studied, catalogued, and controlled by the Saypuri Ministry of Foreign Affairs. Shara Komayd, the protagonist of *City of Stairs*, is both a spy and a scholar of the divine, approaching residuals with the tools of investigation and political intelligence rather than faith.

## Cultural role

The miracle system is the foundation of the trilogy's central political and philosophical questions. The Continent was an empire built on divine right taken literally — the gods were real, their power was tangible, and their chosen people used that power to enslave the rest of the world. When the gods died, the moral framework that justified Continental supremacy died with them, but the cultural memory and resentment persist on both sides. The Saypuri victors suppress Continental religion and confiscate divine artifacts, fearing a resurgence of divine power. The Continental population mourns its lost gods and lost status, and some seek to restore what was destroyed. The miracles are simultaneously the greatest achievement of Continental civilization and the instrument of its cruelty — and the trilogy refuses to resolve this contradiction simply.
