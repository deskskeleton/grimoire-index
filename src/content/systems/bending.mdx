## Mechanics

Bending is the manipulation of one of four classical elements — water, earth, fire, or air — through deliberate physical motion. Each element has its own martial tradition, philosophical framing, and distinctive choreography: waterbending is fluid and circular, earthbending is rooted and direct, firebending is sharp and propulsive, airbending is evasive and continuous. The motion is not decorative. Bending requires the practitioner to physically move through forms that mirror the desired effect; constrained, injured, or restrained benders lose access to most of their abilities.

Each element comes with sub-disciplines that emerge under specific conditions or with rare aptitude. Waterbenders can heal, and a small minority can bloodbend (manipulate the water within other people's bodies) under the full moon. Earthbenders with refined sensitivity can metalbend or sandbend. Firebenders attuned to celestial cycles can lightning-bend. Airbenders, the rarest of the four, have access to fewer canonical sub-disciplines but more refined movement-based techniques.

A single individual — the Avatar — can bend all four elements and serves as the bridge between the human and spirit worlds. The Avatar reincarnates through the four bending nations in a fixed cycle.

## Limitations and costs

Bending is physical. It demands stamina, balance, precise motion, and (depending on tradition) emotional state. Firebending in particular is sensitive to the bender's confidence and discipline; insecure or unfocused firebenders perform poorly. Waterbenders are limited by available water (though humid air, sweat, and ice all qualify); earthbenders need contact with earth or stone; firebenders generate from within but are weakened in cold or wet conditions; airbenders require breath and space.

Bending is also gated by training and lineage. Innate aptitude is necessary but insufficient — most benders learn within their cultural tradition through structured instruction, and self-taught benders are rare and usually limited.

A subtler cost is moral and cultural. Each bending tradition carries philosophical commitments: airbending pacifism, firebending discipline, earthbending steadfastness, waterbending adaptability. Practitioners who violate the philosophy of their element often plateau or develop in distorted directions.

## Practitioner access

Bending ability is inherited and broadly correlated with the four nations: most waterbenders come from the Water Tribes, most earthbenders from the Earth Kingdom, and so on. Within any given nation, only a fraction of the population are benders — bending families are common but hardly universal. The trait can also appear in mixed-heritage individuals, and (after the events of The Legend of Korra) emerge in non-bending populations through previously dormant lines.

## Cultural role

The four bending nations are organized in significant part around their elemental traditions. Bending is intertwined with religion (the Avatar cycle, fire sage traditions, the air nomad temples), with politics (royal lines and military hierarchies are dominated by powerful benders), and with everyday infrastructure (waterbending as agricultural irrigation, earthbending as construction, firebending as industry). The Hundred Year War — and much of the franchise's conflict — turns on the asymmetric militarization of firebending and the near-extermination of the airbending tradition.
