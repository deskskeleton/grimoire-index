## Mechanics

In **Mage: The Ascension**, reality is not fixed — it is shaped by belief. Mages are individuals who have **Awakened**: gained the ability to perceive and alter the underlying fabric of reality through their personal understanding of how the world works (their **Paradigm**). A Hermetic mage casts spells through ritual invocation and Enochian script because they believe reality operates through occult correspondences. A Virtual Adept hacks reality through code because they believe the universe is information. An Akashic Brother reshapes the world through martial arts and meditation. The system does not privilege any paradigm — they all work, because belief is what makes magic function.

The mechanical framework organizes reality into **nine Spheres**:

- **Correspondence** — space, distance, connections, teleportation
- **Entropy** — probability, decay, fate, fortune
- **Forces** — energy, fire, electricity, gravity, light
- **Life** — biology, healing, shapeshifting, disease
- **Matter** — inanimate physical substance, transmutation
- **Mind** — thought, perception, emotion, memory
- **Prime** — raw magical energy (Quintessence), enchantment, the Tapestry
- **Spirit** — the Umbra (spirit world), spirits, Gauntlet manipulation
- **Time** — temporal perception, precognition, time travel

Each Sphere is rated 1–5 dots. Higher ratings unlock more potent effects. Combining Spheres produces compound effects (Life + Forces to create a lightning-resistant body, Correspondence + Mind to read thoughts at a distance). The system is intentionally open-ended — players describe what they want to achieve, the Storyteller determines which Spheres and ratings are needed, and the dice resolve it.

The critical constraint is **Paradox**. Reality has a consensus — the collective belief of billions of ordinary humans about how the world works. Magic that could be interpreted as coincidence (a "coincidental" effect) — a gun jamming, a lucky escape, a computer glitch — generates little or no Paradox. Magic that openly defies consensus (a "vulgar" effect) — throwing fireballs, teleporting, raising the dead in front of witnesses — generates Paradox, which accumulates and eventually strikes back. Paradox backlash ranges from minor reality glitches to catastrophic events (Paradox Spirits hunting the mage, Quiet — a form of magical insanity — or Paradox Realms that trap the mage in pocket dimensions).

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **Paradox**. The more overtly magical an effect, the greater the Paradox generated. This creates a structural tension: mages can reshape reality, but they must do so subtly or face escalating backlash. The Technocracy — the dominant faction of Awakened practitioners — has won the Ascension War partly by aligning their magic with consensus reality (framing it as technology and science), making their effects coincidental by default while the Traditions' overt mysticism generates constant Paradox.

The second cost is **paradigm dependency**. A mage's magic must be expressed through their paradigm's tools and methods. A Hermetic mage needs ritual components and incantations; a Verbena needs blood and natural materials; a Son of Ether needs pseudo-scientific apparatus. Without their paradigmatic tools (called **foci**), effects become harder or impossible. Experienced mages can shed individual foci, but paradigm itself cannot be abandoned without existential crisis.

The third cost is **political**. The Ascension War — the millennia-long conflict between magical factions over what reality should be — means that practicing magic is inherently political. Every magical act either reinforces or challenges the current consensus. The Technocracy actively hunts Tradition mages, and interfaction violence is constant.

## Practitioner access

Mages are **Awakened** — individuals who have undergone a transformative experience that opens their perception to the true nature of reality. Awakening cannot be reliably induced; it occurs spontaneously, often during crisis. The Traditions (nine mystical factions) and the Technocracy (the techno-rationalist faction) both seek out newly Awakened individuals and recruit them into their paradigmatic frameworks. The vast majority of humanity is Sleeper — unAwakened — and their collective belief forms the consensus that constrains magic.

## Cultural role

The Traditions and the Technocracy represent competing visions of reality. The Traditions (Hermetic Order, Verbena, Dreamspeakers, Akashic Brotherhood, Cult of Ecstasy, Celestial Chorus, Euthanatos, Virtual Adepts, Sons of Ether) each embody a different magical worldview — Western occultism, shamanism, martial mysticism, ecstatic practice, divine worship, entropy mastery, digital magic, and mad science. The Technocracy frames itself as protecting humanity through rational progress while secretly practicing magic disguised as technology. The game's central thematic question is whether magic should be open, diverse, and dangerous or controlled, standardized, and safe — and the text deliberately refuses to provide a simple answer.
