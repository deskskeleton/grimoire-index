## Mechanics

**The House** is an immense labyrinth of classical halls, vestibules, and staircases — an architectural space of unknown origin and possibly infinite extent. Its lower halls are partially flooded by a tidal ocean; its upper halls are filled with clouds and wind. The walls are lined with thousands of stone statues depicting every conceivable subject. The space has its own ecology (fish, birds, algae) and its own weather (tides, storms, celestial light from unknown sources above).

The House is not merely a location but a **metaphysical entity** with properties that blur the line between place and phenomenon:

**Sustenance**: the House provides for those who live within it — Piranesi survives on fish and seaweed, navigates by tidal patterns, and experiences the House as a complete, self-sufficient world.

**Memory alteration**: prolonged residence in the House erodes the inhabitant's memories of the outside world. Piranesi has forgotten his real identity, his previous life, and the existence of the outside world entirely. Whether this is an effect of the House itself or a consequence of magical manipulation by others is ambiguous.

**Communication through pattern**: Piranesi reads the House's tidal patterns, statue arrangements, and architectural features as meaningful — as messages from the House itself. Whether this is genuine communication or pattern-recognition imposed by a mind desperate for meaning is left open.

**Accessibility**: the House is accessed from the real world through ritual or magical knowledge. Arne-Sayles (an occultist) discovered the way in; Ketterley (the Other) uses it as a laboratory for extracting magical power. Multiple people can be present in the House simultaneously, and it appears to connect to our world through specific points.

**Magical extraction**: Ketterley enters the House to extract its "Great and Secret Knowledge" — a power he believes the House contains. The House does appear to generate or embody some form of magical energy, though the nature of this energy and whether it can truly be harnessed is unclear.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **identity loss**. Prolonged residence in the House dissolves the inhabitant's connection to the outside world and their previous self. Piranesi's amnesia is not a malfunction but a feature (or consequence) of deep immersion in the House's reality.

The second cost is **dependence**. Those who live in the House become dependent on it — physically (for food, shelter), psychologically (the House feels like home in a way the outside world cannot match), and perhaps spiritually. Leaving the House feels like loss even when the inhabitant understands that their residence was not voluntary.

The third cost is **exploitation by others**. The House's magical properties attract those who want to use it rather than inhabit it — Ketterley treats it as a resource to be exploited, and his exploitation of both the House and Piranesi drives the novel's conflict.

## Practitioner access

Access to the House requires specific magical knowledge — it is not randomly discoverable. The novel suggests that the House is connected to the magical tradition described in *Jonathan Strange & Mr Norrell* (both are by Clarke, and the connection is implied though not explicit). Very few people know the House exists, and fewer still can navigate it. Piranesi is unique as someone who has lived in it long enough to understand its patterns from inside.

## Cultural role

The House has no cultural role — it is a secret, known to only a handful of people. Its significance is metaphysical and personal: it is a place where the boundary between self and cosmos dissolves, where the built environment becomes a living entity, and where the loss of identity can feel like transcendence rather than degradation.
