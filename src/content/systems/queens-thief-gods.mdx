## Mechanics

The gods of the Attolian world are real entities who take an active, if oblique, interest in mortal affairs. Eugenides the god of thieves is the most prominent, maintaining a particular relationship with his namesake, the human thief Gen. Divine intervention manifests not as bolts of lightning or dramatic transformations but as improbable luck, prophetic dreams, moments of uncanny intuition, and the quiet removal of obstacles. A locked door is found unlocked. A fall that should have been fatal is survived. A choice that seemed arbitrary turns out to have been the only one that could have worked.

The gods also communicate through their sacred sites, through priests and priestesses who serve as intermediaries, and through mythological artifacts. Hamiathes' Gift, a legendary stone, grants its bearer a form of limited invulnerability — but even this artifact operates within the series' characteristic ambiguity, its effects never so dramatic as to be unmistakable.

The system is notable for what it withholds from the reader. Turner constructs scenes that can be read as either divine intervention or human competence, and the series rarely confirms which interpretation is correct until much later, if at all. The gods have their own agendas, which do not always align with what their favorites want, and their gifts frequently come with costs that are not apparent at the time.

The mythological substrate of the world — the stories characters tell about the gods — functions as a parallel layer of meaning. Myths recounted early in the series often turn out to be structurally predictive of events that follow, blurring the line between storytelling and prophecy.

## Limitations and costs

Divine favor is not a resource that can be accumulated or spent at will. The gods choose whom to favor and when to act, and their choices are not always legible to mortals. Gen's relationship with the god Eugenides grants him extraordinary luck, but it also entangles him in plans he did not consent to and outcomes he would not have chosen. The cost of divine attention is a loss of autonomy — the sense that one's life is being shaped by forces beyond understanding or appeal.

Hamiathes' Gift provides physical protection but does not prevent pain, exhaustion, or psychological damage. It also marks its bearer as significant in ways that attract both political and divine attention, making it as much a liability as an advantage.

More broadly, the system imposes a kind of epistemological cost: characters who receive divine aid can never be fully certain of their own competence. Did they succeed because they were skilled, or because a god arranged the outcome? This uncertainty is felt most acutely by Gen himself, who must navigate the gap between his genuine abilities and the suspicion that his achievements are not entirely his own.

## Practitioner access

There are no practitioners of magic in the conventional sense. The gods act on their own initiative, and mortals cannot compel or reliably invoke divine intervention. Priests and priestesses maintain sacred sites and perform rituals, but these function more as expressions of devotion than as mechanisms for producing magical effects. The closest thing to a "practitioner" is someone the gods have chosen to favor — and being chosen is not something that can be earned, trained for, or refused.

## Cultural role

Religion is woven deeply into the political and social fabric of the Attolian kingdoms. Temples are significant institutions, oaths sworn by the gods carry real weight, and rulers who neglect the gods risk both spiritual and political consequences. However, the culture is not uniformly devout — skepticism about the gods' active involvement is common, particularly among the politically powerful, and the series treats this skepticism as reasonable rather than foolish. The tension between belief and doubt is one of the series' sustaining themes.
