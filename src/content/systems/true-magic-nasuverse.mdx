## Mechanics

**True Magic** (魔法, *mahō*) represents the five supreme miracles of the Nasuverse — each one accomplishing something that is genuinely, permanently impossible through any other means, including scientific advancement. Where Magecraft reproduces natural phenomena (and is therefore theoretically replaceable by technology), True Magic does what can never be replicated.

The five known Magics are:

**First Magic** — details largely unknown; associated with the denial of nothingness or the materialization of "nothing." Its user is deceased. **Second Magic: Kaleidoscope** — the operation of parallel worlds. Wielded by **Zelretch**, it grants access to and manipulation of alternate timelines and parallel dimensions. Zelretch can draw energy from parallel worlds, observe alternate outcomes, and transport matter between realities. **Third Magic: Heaven's Feel** — the materialization of the soul. It allows the soul to exist independently of the body as a perpetual energy source, achieving true immortality and unlimited magical energy. The Holy Grail War's ritual is designed to reproduce this Magic. **Fourth Magic** — unknown, its user is unknown. **Fifth Magic: Blue** — associated with time manipulation and the consumption of the future. Wielded by **Aoko Aozaki**, its full capabilities are obscure but include temporal manipulation at a fundamental level.

True Magic is accessed by reaching **Akasha** (the Root / the Swirl of the Root) — the origin point of all phenomena, where all possibilities exist simultaneously. Each Sorcerer has touched the Root through a different path and returned with mastery over one aspect of the impossible. The Mage's Association's entire institutional purpose is ultimately aimed at reaching the Root, though almost no one succeeds.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation is **absolute exclusivity**. Each Magic has at most one living practitioner. When a Sorcerer dies without passing on their Magic, it is lost unless another Magus independently reaches the Root through the same path. The five Magics are not techniques that can be taught — they are unique connections to the Root.

The second limitation is **mystery**. Three of the five Magics are poorly understood even within the setting. Their full capabilities, costs, and mechanics are deliberately left vague, making True Magic a narrative horizon rather than a well-defined power set.

The third limitation is **the Root's inaccessibility**. Reaching Akasha is the life's work of entire mage lineages spanning centuries, and almost all fail. The paths to the Root are not repeatable: each successful Sorcerer found their own way, and their method does not generalize.

## Practitioner access

True Magic is the most restricted power in the Nasuverse. There are at most five Sorcerers alive at any time (currently fewer, as some Magics have no living user). Access requires touching the Root — an event so rare that the entire history of the Mage's Association has produced only a handful of successes. No amount of talent, training, or inherited circuits guarantees access; the path to the Root involves factors that the Mage's Association cannot fully systematize.

## Cultural role

True Magic defines the Mage's Association's reason for existence. The entire institutional structure of Clock Tower politics, family prestige, and research competition is oriented toward the goal of reaching the Root and attaining True Magic. The Sorcerers themselves exist above and outside this competition — they are figures of legend and awe, whose opinions carry the weight of their unique achievement. The distinction between Magecraft and True Magic also structures the Nasuverse's philosophical framework: in a world where science steadily encroaches on Magecraft's territory, True Magic represents the irreducible core of the supernatural — the proof that there are things that will never be explained.
