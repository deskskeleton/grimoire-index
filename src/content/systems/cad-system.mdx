## Mechanics

A CAD — Combat Assistance Device — is a semi-sentient biotech organism of alien origin that bonds permanently with a human host. Upon bonding, the CAD integrates with the user's nervous system and can manifest as a personalized set of weapons, armor, and ability augmentations. The manifestation is physical and visible: when activated, the CAD materializes around the user as tangible equipment — a sword, a shield, full plate armor, ranged projectors — whose form is determined by the CAD's type and the user's combat style. When dismissed, the CAD retreats into a dormant state, typically appearing as a small device worn on the body.

Every CAD is quantified across four core specifications: Offense, Defense, Speed, and Cognition (sometimes called Growth). These stats are numerical, measurable, and publicly tracked. A CAD with high Offense manifests more powerful weapons and striking capabilities; high Defense produces thicker armor and stronger barriers; high Speed enhances the user's reaction time and movement; high Cognition governs the CAD's own processing ability, its capacity to learn from combat, and the rate at which it evolves. Each spec is graded on a tier scale from F (lowest) through D, C, B, A, and S (highest), with the aggregate determining the CAD's overall rank.

CAD types define the user's fundamental combat role. Brawlers emphasize close-range offense with heavy weapons and aggressive abilities. Phalanx types prioritize defense, manifesting heavy armor and shield constructs. Duelists balance offense and speed for one-on-one combat. Mage types channel ranged energy attacks at the cost of physical resilience. Lancers specialize in mobility and reach. Sabers combine speed with moderate offense for hit-and-run tactics. Each type has a distinct combat profile, and the type a user receives is determined by the CAD itself during bonding — users do not choose their type.

Evolution is the system's progression mechanic. CADs grow through genuine combat stress — not through passive training or academic study, but through the physiological and psychological demands of actual fighting. When a user pushes their limits in combat, the CAD absorbs the experience and incrementally improves its specifications. This growth can manifest as stat increases within existing abilities, the unlocking of entirely new abilities, or shifts in the CAD's manifestation form. Evolution is not linear or predictable; a CAD might plateau for months and then make a dramatic leap after a single intense engagement.

The ISCM — Inter-System Collective Military — provides the institutional framework within which CAD users operate. Humanity is engaged in an ongoing war against an alien species called the Archons, and CAD-equipped soldiers form the backbone of the collective defense. Military academies like the Galens Institute train CAD users in combat tactics, teamwork, and the strategic application of their abilities. The academy setting provides the competitive structure — ranked matches, team exercises, tournament arcs — that drives much of the narrative.

Abilities beyond the base weapon and armor manifestation develop as a CAD evolves. These can include elemental projections (fire blasts, lightning arcs), spatial manipulation (short-range teleportation, barrier generation), enhanced sensory capabilities, and various combat augmentations. The specific abilities a CAD develops are influenced by both its type and the user's fighting style, creating a feedback loop between how a person fights and what their CAD becomes capable of.

## Limitations and costs

CAD growth is slow and demands genuine adversity. Sparring and controlled exercises produce minimal evolution; the CAD's biological systems respond primarily to authentic combat stress — situations where the user faces real danger and must push beyond their current capabilities. This creates an uncomfortable incentive structure where the fastest path to growth is also the most dangerous. Users who train safely plateau; users who seek out fights above their level risk serious injury or death but evolve faster.

Starting specifications are determined by a combination of genetics and the CAD bonding process, and the initial lottery is profoundly unequal. Some users bond with CADs that begin at C or B rank across all specs, granting them immediate combat viability and social prestige. Others receive CADs with uniformly low stats. Rei Ward, the series protagonist, bonds with a CAD that registers F-rank in every specification — the lowest scores ever recorded at the Galens Institute. His starting position is so disadvantageous that most people, including his instructors, initially consider his military career nonviable.

The public leaderboard system transforms raw statistics into social currency and social weapon. Every CAD user's current specs, rank, and combat record are visible to anyone who checks. This transparency creates immense pressure — improvement is celebrated, stagnation is noted, and regression is humiliating. For someone like Rei, whose starting position is historically low, the leaderboard is a source of constant social friction. The system rewards high performers with better resources, preferred assignments, and social status, while low performers are marginalized.

CADs can be damaged in combat, and severe damage affects their ability to manifest and function. In extreme cases, a CAD can be killed — permanently destroyed — severing the bond and leaving the user without their capabilities. The CAD-user bond, once formed, cannot be transferred or replicated; a user whose CAD dies does not receive a replacement. This creates genuine stakes in every combat encounter that extend beyond the user's personal safety.

The bond between user and CAD, while providing extraordinary capabilities, is permanent and involuntary in its specifics. A user cannot choose to unbond, cannot swap their CAD for a different one, and cannot alter their CAD's type. A Brawler who would prefer to be a Mage is stuck with their assignment. The CAD's semi-sentience means it has its own tendencies and preferences that may not always align with the user's conscious strategy, occasionally manifesting abilities or responses that the user did not intentionally activate.

## Practitioner access

CAD implantation is universal in human society — every person receives a CAD during adolescence through a standardized bonding procedure. The process is not optional; given the existential threat posed by the Archons, CAD bonding is treated as a civic obligation comparable to military registration. However, the vast range of possible starting specs means that universal access does not translate to universal capability. A significant portion of the population bonds with CADs whose specs are too low for frontline military service, relegating them to support roles, civilian occupations, or administrative positions within the ISCM framework.

Academy admission serves as the primary filter between general CAD users and elite combatants. The Galens Institute, the most prestigious military academy, requires minimum spec thresholds for admission. These thresholds are not extraordinarily high, but they exclude the lowest-performing CAD users from the institution's training resources and competitive opportunities. Rei's admission to Galens despite his F-rank scores is an exception granted under specific circumstances, and his presence at the academy is initially treated as an anomaly by students and faculty alike.

Within the academy, advancement is driven by ranked combat performance. Students compete in individual and team matches that are scored, recorded, and used to determine class placement, resource allocation, and eventually military assignment. The competitive structure is designed to identify the strongest fighters and fast-track them into elite units, while lower-performing students are channeled toward less prestigious but still necessary roles. Tournament arcs — intra-school and inter-school competitions — serve as the primary benchmarking events where reputations are made or broken.

The rate at which a CAD evolves varies enormously between users, and the mechanisms governing growth rate are not fully understood. Cognition spec plays a role — higher Cognition correlates with faster adaptation — but outlier cases like Rei, whose CAD demonstrates unprecedented growth velocity despite starting at rock-bottom specs, suggest that unknown factors are at work. The mystery of why some CADs evolve faster than others, and what Rei's anomalous growth pattern means, is a central narrative question.

## Cultural role

CAD specifications function as the primary social hierarchy in human civilization. A person's rank is public knowledge, instantly accessible, and socially determinative. High-ranked CAD users are celebrities, their matches broadcast and analyzed, their opinions sought on military and political matters. Low-ranked users occupy lower social strata regardless of their other qualities. The system creates a meritocracy in the narrowest sense — merit defined exclusively as combat capability, with all other forms of human value subordinated to that single metric.

Military service is the expected endpoint of CAD training, and the alien threat gives this expectation existential weight. The Archons are not a hypothetical danger; they are an active, ongoing invasion that has killed millions and could potentially exterminate humanity. This context transforms CAD combat from a competitive sport into preparation for species-level survival. The academy's emphasis on combat excellence is not institutional ego — it is a response to the genuine possibility that humanity's strongest fighters may not be strong enough.

The Galens Institute functions as both training ground and social arena. Within its walls, students form alliances, rivalries, and hierarchies that mirror and sometimes challenge the broader society's ranking obsession. Rei's journey from universally dismissed F-ranker to competitive threat disrupts assumptions about what CAD specs mean and whether initial rankings define a person's potential. His rise forces the institution to confront whether its resource allocation — heavily biased toward already-strong students — is strategically optimal.

The public ranking system creates a celebrity culture around top-performing CAD users. High-ranked fighters are followed, sponsored, and idolized in ways that parallel professional athletes, with their matches streamed and their stats analyzed by an engaged public. This visibility adds a performative dimension to combat — fighters are not only trying to win but trying to win impressively, because public perception influences sponsorship opportunities, institutional support, and post-academy career prospects. The intersection of genuine military necessity and spectacle-driven culture creates a society that is simultaneously fighting for its survival and turning that fight into entertainment.
