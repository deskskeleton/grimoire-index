## Mechanics

Alchemy in this setting is presented as a structured science with three explicit steps: comprehension of the material's atomic composition, decomposition of its existing structure, and reconstruction into a new form. Practitioners ("alchemists") perform these steps simultaneously by activating a transmutation circle — a geometric figure inscribed with element-specific symbols — placed in contact with the target material. The circle channels and shapes the energy that drives the reaction; an alchemist without a circle generally cannot transmute, with rare exceptions noted below.

The system operates under "equivalent exchange." Mass, complexity, and elemental composition must balance across the transformation: a kilogram of iron cannot be transmuted into a kilogram of gold (different elements, different complexities), but it can be reshaped into a kilogram of iron sword, or further redistributed across multiple iron objects of equal total mass. Transmutations that violate equivalent exchange either fail outright or produce dangerous, distorted "rebound" effects.

A small group of exceptionally trained alchemists — those who have witnessed "the Truth" through attempted human transmutation — can perform alchemy without drawing a circle, using their own bodies as the geometric vessel. This shortcut comes at significant personal cost (see below).

The energy that powers the reaction is not generated by the alchemist. It is drawn from the planet itself, in the form of slow tectonic and geological flows — a setting-specific naturalistic substrate that distinguishes this magic from systems that draw on the practitioner's own life force.

## Limitations and costs

The headline cost is reciprocal: any transmutation must respect equivalent exchange, and violations produce real, often catastrophic, rebound. This constraint applies absolutely. Edward Elric and his brother Alphonse each lost parts of their bodies attempting to transmute a deceased human, an act the system flatly prohibits at the cost of physical and metaphysical price.

A second cost is preparation. Alchemy is a learned discipline. It requires literacy in its symbolic system, understanding of physical chemistry, and (for serious practitioners) extensive training under another alchemist. The geometric precision of transmutation circles also produces a tactical constraint: an alchemist whose circle is broken, smudged, or destroyed mid-reaction cannot complete the transmutation.

A third cost is geographic. Alchemy in the Amestrian setting depends on access to the underlying geological energy. In settings or under conditions where that flow is blocked or absent, alchemy fails — a constraint that becomes a major plot driver in the franchise's closing arcs.

## Practitioner access

Alchemy is open in principle to anyone willing to study it. Aptitude varies, and exceptional practitioners are rare, but the discipline is not gated by bloodline, divine selection, or innate talent at the level of "you can or can't." Within Amestris, many alchemists work as licensed civilians (researchers, engineers, doctors); a smaller number serve as State Alchemists, military officers granted broad operational authority and access to restricted research.

## Cultural role

In Amestris, alchemy occupies a position roughly analogous to applied physics in a militarized 20th-century state. It is a recognized profession, a field of academic study, and a tool of state power — the State Alchemist program is explicitly a military-research program. The discipline is also subject to ethical regulation: human transmutation is the system's hardest taboo, and the State Alchemist oath formalizes this and other constraints. Around the edges of the empire and in other countries, alternative alchemical traditions exist with different metaphysical foundations and partially incompatible rules.
