## Mechanics

Sorcery in the Hyborian Age operates through three primary channels: pacts with inhuman entities, the recovery of forbidden knowledge from pre-human civilizations, and the use of occult artifacts that serve as repositories or conduits of supernatural power. These channels frequently overlap — a sorcerer might use knowledge stolen from the ruins of Acheron to summon a demon of Set, binding it through a ritual that requires a specific artifact to complete.

Demonic pacts are the most common source of power. Sorcerers bargain with entities like Set the Serpent God, Yog of the Empty Abodes, and various unnamed Elder Things for magical ability. These beings grant power in exchange for worship, sacrifice, and the advancement of their influence in the mortal world. The relationship is transactional but asymmetric: the demon always has the better end of the bargain, and sorcerers who fail to meet their obligations are destroyed.

Artifacts serve as both amplifiers and independent sources of power. Thoth-Amon's Serpent Ring of Set is the most prominent example — without it, Thoth-Amon is reduced to a fugitive; with it, he is one of the most dangerous beings in the Hyborian world. Xaltotun's Heart of Ahriman can resurrect the dead and topple kingdoms. These artifacts are typically relics of older, more powerful civilizations (Acheron, pre-cataclysm Atlantis, Lemuria) and represent knowledge that the current age cannot replicate.

The effects of Hyborian sorcery are varied and dramatic: raising the dead, hypnotic domination, shapeshifting, summoning demons and monsters, projecting illusions, and blighting or transforming the natural world. There is no systematic taxonomy of effects; each sorcerer's repertoire is idiosyncratic, shaped by their particular patron, training, and artifacts.

## Limitations and costs

The most fundamental cost of Hyborian sorcery is moral corruption. Without exception, practicing sorcerers in Howard's stories are villains — cruel, decadent, and inhumane. The implication is that the practice itself degrades the practitioner, though Howard leaves ambiguous whether sorcery corrupts the virtuous or merely attracts the already corrupt. Either way, the result is the same: no sympathetic character in the Conan stories uses magic, and Conan's own response to sorcery is always physical violence.

Material costs are significant. Rituals require specific ingredients — rare herbs, blood sacrifice (often human), astronomical alignments, and extended preparation. A sorcerer interrupted mid-ritual is typically helpless. Artifacts can be lost, stolen, or destroyed, and a sorcerer without their key artifact may lose most of their power. Thoth-Amon stripped of his ring is a mere slave; Xaltotun without the Heart of Ahriman can be defeated by mortal means.

Sorcery is also unreliable against sufficient physical force. Conan repeatedly overcomes sorcerous opponents through direct assault, breaking spells by killing the caster, shattering artifacts, or simply refusing to be affected by enchantments through sheer willpower. Howard consistently frames this not as anti-magic immunity but as the superiority of vital, uncomplicated action over elaborate occult scheming.

## Practitioner access

Sorcery is the province of a small number of specialists, almost exclusively located in the older, more decadent civilizations. Stygia, heir to the serpent-worship of pre-human Acheron, produces the greatest concentration of sorcerers. The Black Ring, a Stygian sorcerous cabal, is the closest thing to an organized magical tradition in the Hyborian world. Elsewhere, sorcerers appear as isolated figures — court wizards, renegade priests, or hermits who have stumbled upon forbidden texts. The barbarian peoples (Cimmerians, Nordheimr, Picts) do not produce sorcerers, though they may have shamanic traditions that Howard treats as qualitatively different from "civilized" sorcery.

## Cultural role

In Howard's moral framework, sorcery is the signature vice of civilizations in decline. Stygia and Acheron practice it openly; the Hyborian kingdoms regard it with suspicion and fear. Sorcerers are never civic leaders in any healthy sense — they are power behind thrones, puppet masters, or tyrants whose rule is sustained by terror rather than legitimacy. The contrast with Conan, who conquers through personal courage and physical strength, is the central thematic engine of the stories. Magic represents everything Howard distrusted about civilization: its artifice, its cruelty, and its tendency to seek power through means other than honest individual merit.
