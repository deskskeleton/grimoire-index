## Mechanics

Feruchemy operates through metal "metalminds" — wearable items, usually bracers or rings, that act as personal storage for a specific physical or mental attribute. Like Allomancy, the system uses sixteen canonical metals, and each metal stores a different attribute: pewter stores physical strength, steel stores physical speed, iron stores physical weight, tin stores acuity of a particular sense, copper stores memory, bronze stores wakefulness, and so on through the full set.

To store, a Feruchemist deliberately reduces the attribute in themselves and channels the loss into a metalmind they are physically wearing or holding. While storing strength, the Feruchemist is weak; while storing speed, they are slow. The attribute is not destroyed — it is banked. Later, the Feruchemist can tap the same metalmind to draw the stored attribute back, gaining a temporary boost above their baseline. The exchange is exact: one hour of stored strength yields one hour of equivalent strength, or thirty minutes of double strength, or fifteen minutes of quadruple strength. The total integral of attribute-time is conserved.

Because storage and retrieval both require the user to be in physical contact with the relevant metalmind, Feruchemy is logistically distinct from Allomancy. A Feruchemist's effective power is bounded not by metal availability but by their own historical willingness to suffer.

## Limitations and costs

The reciprocal cost is the system's defining feature. Every gain requires an earlier deliberate loss of the same magnitude. This makes Feruchemy nearly useless without forethought: a Feruchemist who has not stored cannot tap, and a Feruchemist who has not banked enough cannot exceed their reserves.

The system is also bounded by physical capacity. A particular metalmind of a particular size can hold a finite total. Larger or more numerous metalminds extend capacity but at the cost of needing to be carried. The metalminds are physical objects and can be lost, stolen, or destroyed — there are scenes in the books where a Feruchemist is rendered powerless by losing a single bracer.

A subtler cost is temporal: storing requires real time spent diminished. A character who wants combat-grade strength on demand has to spend many uneventful hours weak in advance, which has practical and dramatic consequences.

## Practitioner access

Feruchemy is hereditary, descending through specific bloodlines distinct from but historically related to those carrying Allomancy. Originally concentrated in the Terris people in the early Mistborn novels, the trait spreads through interbreeding and is more broadly distributed by the second era. Compounding — the rare combination of Allomancy and Feruchemy in the same person — produces dramatically amplified effects and is treated as a strategic-tier capability.

## Cultural role

In the original Mistborn era, Feruchemy is a closely held secret of the Terris people, who were systematically bred to suppress its expression by the Lord Ruler. By Era 2, Feruchemy is openly known and practiced, with Twinborn (people who possess one Allomantic and one Feruchemical ability) forming an unusual but normal stratum of the Elendel basin's society.
