## Mechanics

Magic on the Continent draws on **Chaos** — the raw, elemental energy that permeates the world, concentrated along intersections of force (analogous to ley lines) and released in greater quantities since the Conjunction of the Spheres. Individuals born with innate sensitivity to Chaos are called **Sources**, and without training, their power manifests uncontrollably — bursts of telekinesis, unintended fires, prophetic episodes. Formal magical education channels this raw talent into disciplined sorcery.

**Sorcery** is practised by mages trained at academies: Aretuza (for women) and Ban Ard (for men). Training takes years and involves learning to draw Chaos from the environment, shape it through will and knowledge, and release it as controlled effects — fireballs, teleportation, illusions, healing, telekinesis, weather manipulation, and far more. Sorcerers and sorceresses are immensely powerful individually and politically organised into bodies like the Brotherhood of Sorcerers and later the Lodge of Sorceresses, which seek to influence kings and shape continental politics. Many sorceresses use magic to alter their own appearance, achieving and maintaining idealized beauty — a choice that reflects both the system's transformative capacity and the political culture of its practitioners.

**Witcher mutations** represent a completely different tradition. Boys recruited (or sold) to witcher schools undergo the **Trial of the Grasses** — a brutal alchemical process involving the forced ingestion of mutagenic compounds that rewrites their biology. The mortality rate is extreme; roughly three in ten survive. Those who do gain cat-like eyes with enhanced night vision, dramatically increased reflexes and speed, resistance to disease and poison, slowed ageing, and emotional dampening. Witchers also learn five simple combat **Signs**: Aard (a telekinetic force push), Igni (a directed burst of fire), Yrden (a magical trap that slows enemies), Quen (a protective shield), and Axii (a charm that briefly confuses or calms a target). Signs are far weaker than true sorcery — rough, practical tools rather than the refined art of trained mages.

**Elder Blood** (Hen Ichaer) is a separate category entirely. An ancient genetic lineage originating with the elven sage Lara Dorren, Elder Blood grants its carriers immense power over space and time — the ability to open portals between worlds, travel between dimensions, and manipulate temporal flow. Ciri, the primary carrier in the series, possesses Elder Blood through her maternal line, making her one of the most powerful beings on the Continent despite having no formal magical training. Her power is largely uncontrolled, activating under stress and producing effects she cannot predict or replicate at will.

## Limitations and costs

Sorcery's costs are primarily **political and social**. Mages are powerful but feared and resented by the common population. Witch hunts are a recurring threat, and the political manoeuvring of the Brotherhood and Lodge makes sorcerers as dangerous to each other as to anyone else. Drawing too heavily on Chaos without adequate skill can be physically destructive — novice Sources risk killing themselves or others. There is also a hidden physical cost: many Sources are born with physical disabilities or deformities, which magical training can correct but at the price of fertility. Most sorceresses are sterile, a sacrifice the academies do not always disclose in advance.

Witcher mutations exact their cost **upfront and permanently**. The Trial of the Grasses kills most subjects and permanently alters those who survive — emotionally, physically, and socially. Witchers are widely regarded as mutants, less than human, tolerated for their usefulness in killing monsters but despised and excluded from normal society. The emotional dampening caused by the mutations is debated within the text — Geralt clearly feels deeply despite the supposed suppression — but the social perception of witchers as emotionless killers shapes how they are treated.

Elder Blood's limitation is **control**. Ciri's powers activate unpredictably, often in moments of extreme emotion or danger, and she cannot reliably reproduce specific effects. Multiple factions — the Wild Hunt, Nilfgaard, various mages — seek to capture or breed her for her bloodline, making her power as much a source of persecution as empowerment. The genetic nature of Elder Blood also means it can be bred for, turning Ciri into a commodity in the eyes of those who understand her lineage.

## Practitioner access

Sorcery requires both innate talent (being a Source) and formal training. Not everyone with the gift is identified, and not all who are identified survive the training process. The academies are selective and politically embedded — admission is controlled by existing power structures, and training shapes students into politically useful agents as much as competent mages.

Witcher mutations are imposed on children, not chosen by them. The witcher schools (Wolf, Cat, Griffin, Bear, Viper, and others) recruit or purchase boys, subject them to the Trials, and train the survivors as professional monster hunters. By Geralt's time, most witcher schools have been destroyed, the knowledge of mutation is fragmenting, and no new witchers are being created. Elder Blood is purely hereditary and cannot be acquired through any training or procedure.

## Cultural role

Magic is openly known on the Continent but unevenly trusted. Sorcerers hold advisory positions in royal courts but are subject to periodic purges when political winds shift. The witch hunts led by the Church of the Eternal Fire represent the populist backlash against magical elites. Witchers occupy a marginal social position — itinerant monster-killers tolerated in times of need and reviled in times of peace. Sapkowski uses this layered system to explore prejudice, the politics of expertise, and the ways societies simultaneously depend on and despise those who are different. The parallel between the treatment of witchers, non-humans (elves, dwarves), and other marginalised groups is deliberate and central to the series' moral architecture.
