## Mechanics

Creation in A Practical Guide to Evil operates according to narrative causality — the fundamental forces governing reality are not physical laws but story patterns. The Gods Above and the Gods Below shaped the world as an argument between them, and that argument plays out through the lives of Named: individuals who embody archetypal Roles so completely that Creation itself grants them a Name and the power that comes with it.

A Name is not a title you choose; it is a recognition by the fabric of reality that you have become something. When a young woman from the Wasteland dedicates herself so completely to destroying a city that she becomes the concept of destruction-as-ambition, she becomes the Diabolist. When a soldier's commitment to martial excellence and strategic ruthlessness reaches a threshold, he becomes the Black Knight. The Name arrives as a moment of transition — a crystallization of identity into archetype — and it brings three Aspects: named supernatural abilities that reflect the specific way the bearer embodies their Role. The Squire's Aspects might be Learn, Struggle, and Lead; the Tyrant's might be Rend, Rule, and Wish. Aspects are deeply personal — two bearers of the same Name across generations will have different Aspects reflecting their different relationships to the Role.

The deeper mechanic, and the one that drives the series' plot, is narrative weight. Named do not simply have powers; they have story-gravity. When a lone hero stands against overwhelming odds, the story-pattern of the heroic last stand bends probability in the hero's favor. When a villain monologues before killing the hero, the pattern demands that the monologue create an opening for escape. These are not metaphors — they are tactical realities that experienced Named learn to exploit. The Rules, as practitioners call them, include principles like the Rule of Three (the third attempt succeeds where the first two failed), the prohibition against killing a surrendered opponent without consequence, and the narrative requirement that a band of five heroes can defeat a superior foe.

Catherine Foundling's central innovation — and the series' thesis — is treating these story-patterns as a resource to be engineered rather than a fate to be suffered. She deliberately constructs narrative symmetry, positions herself as the underdog to gain story-weight, refuses to play the patterns that would trap her, and weaponizes genre awareness as a tactical tool. She wins battles by recognizing which story she is in and forcing a transition to a different one.

Conventional magic also exists alongside the Name system. Praesi practitioners summon and bind devils, drawing on infernal contracts for power. Callowan hedge witchcraft works through folk ritual and local tradition. The Fae courts operate on an even more rigid story-logic than the mortal world, their entire existence structured as an endlessly repeating seasonal narrative. Masego, the Hierophant, studies the underlying magical laws with something approaching scientific methodology, treating sorcery as a phenomenon to be understood rather than simply wielded.

## Limitations and costs

The Name system's primary cost is the loss of narrative freedom. A Named individual is bound to their Role's story-patterns, and those patterns constrain behavior in ways that are difficult to escape. The Tyrant must tyrannize — Kairos Theodosian can be brilliantly creative within that constraint, but he cannot stop being a tyrant without losing his Name. The Black Knight must serve as the primary instrument of the villain's cause, subordinate to a greater Evil. A hero who acts unheroically risks losing their Name entirely, and with it all the power and story-weight that came with it.

Narrative weight, the system's greatest advantage, is also its deepest trap. A villain who accumulates too much narrative momentum — conquering too easily, growing too powerful, becoming too arrogant — is building toward a fall that the story-logic of Creation will enforce with or without human intervention. The bigger the villain's wins, the more certain the eventual comeuppance. Conversely, a hero who suffers too little adversity lacks the story-weight to overcome a genuine threat. The system rewards suffering and punishes ease, on both sides.

Aspects can be lost, stolen, or broken. Catherine loses and regains Aspects multiple times across the series, each loss representing a fundamental shift in her relationship to her Role. Named can be killed permanently, and when they die, the Name passes on — eventually — to someone else who embodies the same archetype, but the new bearer starts fresh. There is no inheritance of specific Aspects or accumulated power.

The Gods themselves represent the ultimate constraint. The Above and Below each sponsor their respective Named, and their sponsorship comes with expectations. Heroes are expected to pursue Good; villains are expected to pursue Evil. Named who attempt to operate outside this binary — as Catherine increasingly does — find themselves fighting the fundamental structure of Creation, which is built to sustain the argument between Above and Below rather than to permit a third position.

## Practitioner access

Names come to those who live into them — there is no training regimen, no academy, no initiation rite that reliably produces Named. A person becomes the Squire by being the kind of person who would become the Squire: driven, combative, willing to learn through violence, positioned as a subordinate to a greater Named figure. The Role must fit like a skin, and the fit is determined by the totality of a person's character, choices, and circumstances.

Villainous Names come from Below, the Gods of Evil, who favor ambition, ruthlessness, and willingness to impose one's will on the world. Heroic Names come from Above, the Gods of Good, who favor self-sacrifice, justice, and the defense of others. The alignment is not always clean — a villain can have genuine loyalty and love, a hero can be rigid and merciless — but the binary is structurally enforced by the system itself.

Some Names are unique to their time — there is only one Black Queen, one Hierophant, one Tyrant of Helike. Others recur across generations, the same archetype reincarnating in new bearers as the old ones die or transition. The Dread Emperor/Empress of Praes is a recurring Name that has been held by dozens of bearers across centuries, each bringing their own Aspects and their own relationship to the Role. The White Knight is similarly recurring, the champion of Above reborn as needed.

Conventional magic is more accessible than Names. Praesi mages train in magical academies, learning diabolism and sorcery through study and practice. Callowan practitioners inherit folk traditions. But conventional magic is fundamentally lesser than Named power — a skilled mage can be formidable, but they lack the narrative weight that allows a Named to bend probability, survive impossible odds, and shape the course of history.

## Cultural role

The Name system does not merely exist within the political landscape of the Guideverse — it is the political landscape. Nations are built around Named rulers: the Dread Empire of Praes has been governed by a succession of Dread Emperors and Empresses for millennia, its entire political culture shaped by the reality that its ruler will always be a Named villain with supernatural power and narrative weight. Callow's identity as a heroic nation is not metaphorical; it literally produces heroes at a rate that other nations do not, and its resistance to Praesi conquest is narratively reinforced.

The Good/Evil divide is institutionalized in ways that shape every aspect of society. The Tenth Crusade is not merely a war but a narrative event, drawing on centuries of accumulated story-weight from previous crusades. The Free Cities cycle through tyrannies and liberations in patterns that are as much narrative as political. The Dead King, an ancient Named of overwhelming power, exists as a narrative counterweight — the threat so great that it justifies temporary alliances between Good and Evil.

The entire series is fundamentally about whether this system can be changed. Catherine's arc, from a young woman who takes a villainous Name to pursue heroic goals, to a figure who attempts to break the Good/Evil binary entirely, is an argument about structural reform at the most literal level possible. She is not merely trying to change political institutions; she is trying to change the narrative laws of reality. The Liesse Accords, her crowning political achievement, are an attempt to impose rules on Named conflict that supersede the story-patterns Creation enforces — to replace narrative determinism with negotiated law.

The system's self-awareness is its most distinctive feature. Characters in A Practical Guide to Evil know they live in a world governed by story-logic, and their sophistication in manipulating that logic varies enormously. Amadeus, the Black Knight, is a rationalist villain who has spent decades studying how story-patterns work and engineering situations that avoid the traditional villain-downfall arc. Kairos, the Tyrant, is a chaotic force who exploits narrative expectations by being so unpredictable that story-patterns cannot lock onto him. The result is a setting where the magic system is also the political system, the theological system, and the genre itself, all examined with relentless, often comedic self-awareness.
