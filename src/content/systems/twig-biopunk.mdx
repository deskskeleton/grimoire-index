## Mechanics

Academy Science rests on the Wollstone ratios — a set of fundamental principles governing the manipulation of living tissue, discovered in this timeline's version of the early nineteenth century. Where our world's Industrial Revolution was driven by thermodynamics and mechanical engineering, the Twigverse's equivalent breakthrough was biological. The Wollstone ratios describe how living cells can be reprogrammed, how tissues can be grafted across species boundaries, how growth can be directed and accelerated, and how the fundamental architecture of organisms can be redesigned. They are not magic; they are science, but a science so advanced in its biological applications that the results are indistinguishable from it.

The most visible product of Academy Science is the stitched — reanimated and reassembled organisms, usually human, that serve as the Crown's primary labor and military force. Stitched range from crude assemblages of corpse parts (functional but limited, used for manual labor and cannon fodder) to sophisticated constructs with enhanced capabilities, implanted skills, and designed physiologies. The creation of stitched is routine Academy work; every student learns the basics. More advanced practitioners create stitched with specialized combat abilities, integrated biological weapons, or enhanced sensory capabilities.

Beyond stitched, Academy scientists engineer parasites, symbiotes, tailored plagues, living architecture, and designer organisms for every conceivable purpose. Buildings can be grown from engineered coral-like organisms. Surveillance networks use modified insects. Communication systems employ biological signal transmission. Weapons range from targeted pathogens that attack specific genetic profiles to massive war-beasts grown for battlefield deployment. The Crown's military superiority rests entirely on its monopoly over these biological technologies.

The Lambs — the series' protagonists — are themselves Academy experiments, each modified in a different way that illustrates the system's range. Sylvester has had his brain chemistry repeatedly adjusted to maximize social intelligence, pattern recognition, and manipulative capability, at the cost of requiring regular chemical treatments that are slowly degrading his brain. Gordon was engineered for physical perfection — strength, speed, reflexes, healing — but his modifications place unsustainable demands on his body. Mary is a combat-optimized stitched, raised from childhood with implanted skills and reflexes, grappling with questions of identity and autonomy. Jamie (later Jessie) has a modified brain that grants perfect recall and information processing, but the modifications required replacing Jamie's original brain structures entirely, raising questions about continuity of identity. Each Lamb embodies a different application of Academy Science and a different set of costs.

Academy training follows a rigorous institutional structure. Students enter young and progress through years of increasingly specialized study. The best become professors and researchers; the competent become doctors and field practitioners; the rest serve in support roles. The Academy system spans the Crown's entire empire, with major institutions in every significant city and a hierarchy of prestige and influence among them.

## Limitations and costs

The defining limitation of Academy Science is degradation. Biological modifications do not last. The Wollstone ratios enable extraordinary transformations, but living tissue resists being held in unnatural configurations. Stitched require ongoing chemical treatments to prevent decomposition. Enhanced organisms need regular maintenance to keep their modifications stable. The Lambs each have an expiration date — a projected point at which their modifications will fail catastrophically, killing them or reducing them to something less than functional.

Sylvester's situation is the most viscerally illustrated. His regular chemical treatments adjust his brain chemistry to maintain his enhanced social cognition, but each treatment cycle does cumulative damage. His memory degrades. His emotional stability frays. He is, by design, a consumable asset — brilliant now, burned out soon. The Academy that created him views this as an acceptable engineering trade-off, not a moral failing.

Biological engineering is also inherently unpredictable at scale. Tailored plagues can mutate and escape their designed parameters. Engineered organisms can breed, adapt, and evolve beyond their creators' intentions. War-beasts can go feral. The Crown maintains control through institutional monopoly and ruthless enforcement, but the underlying technology is volatile — biological systems are complex, and the Wollstone ratios do not grant perfect control over that complexity.

The resource costs are substantial. Academy Science requires extensive infrastructure — laboratories, chemical supplies, biological feedstock, trained personnel, and time. Creating a sophisticated stitched or a targeted pathogen is not a field-expedient operation; it requires institutional support. This concentrates power in the Academy and the Crown, which is by design — the system's limitations reinforce the political structure that controls it.

## Practitioner access

Academy Science is restricted to those trained within the Crown's institutional framework. The Academies are the sole legitimate source of biological engineering knowledge, and the Crown enforces this monopoly with lethal thoroughness. Unlicensed practice — attempting biological engineering without Academy credentials — is a capital offense. The Crown treats independent bio-engineers the way a nuclear-armed state treats proliferation: as an existential threat to be eliminated.

This monopoly is both practical and political. Practically, Academy Science is genuinely dangerous without proper training; an untrained practitioner is as likely to create a runaway plague as a useful organism. Politically, the Crown's power rests on its biological supremacy — if biological engineering became widely available, the Crown's military and economic advantages would evaporate.

The Lambs occupy an unusual position within this system. They are Crown property, not independent agents. They were created by Academy scientists, maintained by Academy doctors, and deployed at Academy direction. Their growing autonomy and eventual rebellion against this status is one of the series' central arcs. They have intimate knowledge of Academy Science — Lillian is training to become a full doctor, and all of them understand biological engineering at a practical level — but they are products of the system, not its masters.

Rebel factions within the setting use stolen or independently developed biological engineering, but they operate at a severe disadvantage. Without institutional infrastructure, their work is cruder, less reliable, and more dangerous. Some rebels are former Academy students or defectors, bringing expertise but lacking resources. The tension between the Crown's monopoly and the rebels' improvisation drives much of the series' conflict.

## Cultural role

Academy Science is not a hidden art or a specialized discipline — it is the dominant technology of the entire civilization. The Crown rules through biological supremacy the way historical empires ruled through military and industrial superiority. Stitched serve as soldiers, laborers, and servants. Engineered organisms provide food, building materials, transportation, and communication. The entire infrastructure of society is biological rather than mechanical.

This has profound social consequences. The stitched occupy a position analogous to slavery — they are manufactured people, denied autonomy, treated as property. The ethical status of stitched (particularly sophisticated ones like Mary) is one of the series' recurring questions. The Academy's willingness to experiment on human subjects — children, prisoners, marginalized populations — is presented not as aberrant cruelty but as institutional normalcy, which is precisely what makes it horrifying.

The Crown's political structure is maintained through biological control. Nobles receive life-extending treatments that create a literal biological aristocracy. Military loyalty is reinforced through biochemical conditioning. Dissent is suppressed through targeted pathogens and biological surveillance. The series uses Academy Science as a lens for examining how institutional power operates — how systems of control become self-reinforcing, how the people within them internalize their logic, and how resistance is possible even within structures designed to prevent it.

The ethical horror of treating people as raw material — as substrates for experimentation, as components for assembly, as problems to be solved through biological engineering — is the series' central theme. Academy Science is brilliant, powerful, and genuinely wondrous in its capabilities. It is also monstrous in its applications, not because the science itself is evil but because the institutions that control it prioritize power over humanity. The system asks what happens when a civilization's greatest technological achievement is inseparable from its greatest moral failing.
