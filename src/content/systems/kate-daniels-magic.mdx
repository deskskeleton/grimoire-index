## Mechanics

The foundational mechanic of the Kate Daniels world is the **magic/technology oscillation**. Since the Shift, magic and technology have alternated in unpredictable waves. During a **magic wave**, technology fails — engines die, electric grids go dark, skyscrapers warp under magical stress (many have already collapsed), and firearms become unreliable. Magical creatures gain strength, ley lines flare with power, and spells operate at full potency. During a **tech wave**, the process reverses: electricity flows, combustion engines run, computers function, and magic weakens to near-uselessness. The waves are irregular — sometimes lasting hours, sometimes days — and the transition between them can happen mid-sentence or mid-sword-swing.

**Power words** represent the system's most elite and dangerous form of magic. A power word is a single spoken command in a language that predates human civilization, and it compels reality to obey. Kate Daniels inherited this ability from her father Roland — the ancient immortal builder of the Tower of Babel, who has survived for millennia by periodically sleeping and waking as magic levels shift across centuries. Power words like *Hesaad* ("obey"), *Amehe* ("release"), and *Osanda* ("kneel") bypass normal magical resistance and impose their effect directly. However, each use costs blood and life force — Kate literally bleeds when she speaks them, and overuse can be fatal.

The world supports multiple coexisting magical traditions beyond power words. **Shapeshifters** carry Lyc-V, a virus that enables transformation into animal forms; they organize into Packs under a Beast Lord and maintain strict social hierarchies to manage the beast within. **Necromancers** (the People) pilot **undead vampires** — not romanticized aristocrats but mindless, predatory corpses animated by the magic waves — through direct psychic connection, using them as remote-controlled weapons and scouts. **Volhvs** (Slavic pagan priests) channel the power of old gods, witches practice traditional herbalism and ritual magic, and various mythological beings from dozens of cultural traditions have re-emerged in physical form.

## Limitations and costs

The magic/technology oscillation is itself the system's most pervasive constraint. No one can rely on magic or technology exclusively — a warrior who depends on a gun will be helpless when the magic hits, and a mage who relies on spells will be vulnerable during tech. The practical result is redundancy: people carry both swords and firearms, maintain horses alongside cars, and build with stone as well as steel. This dual-infrastructure requirement shapes the economy, architecture, and daily life of post-Shift Atlanta.

Power words exact a direct **physical toll**. Speaking a power word causes Kate to bleed from the nose, eyes, or mouth, and the cost scales with the word's power and the resistance of the target. Extended use produces exhaustion, disorientation, and risk of death. There is also a **secrecy cost**: Kate's power-word ability marks her as Roland's daughter, and revealing it would draw the attention of an immortal who has destroyed civilizations and who views his offspring as either tools or threats. For much of the series, Kate's survival depends on concealing the very ability that makes her effective.

Shapeshifter transformation carries the risk of **losing control** — the beast within is a genuine psychological entity that can overwhelm the human mind, particularly during emotional stress or injury. Loup-garous (shapeshifters who have gone permanently feral) are treated as existential threats and executed on sight. Necromantic piloting requires intense concentration; a navigator who loses their psychic grip on an undead vampire releases a mindless predator into the environment. The unpredictability of wave timing means that any magical working can suddenly lose its power source without warning.

## Practitioner access

The post-Shift world is one where magical ability exists on a broad spectrum. At the top are unique figures like Kate and Roland, whose power-word magic is tied to a specific ancient lineage. Below them, shapeshifters carry their abilities through the Lyc-V virus (which can be transmitted through bites, though the survival rate for involuntary infection is low). Necromancers are trained specialists — the People operate as a corporation with formal apprenticeship programs. Witches, volhvs, and other tradition-based practitioners learn their craft through cultural lineage and study.

At the broadest level, the magic waves affect everyone. Ordinary humans experience the tech-magic oscillation as a daily inconvenience and occasional danger, and some develop minor sensitivities or talents as ambient magic levels increase over the series. The Order of Merciful Aid trains knight-protectors who combine combat skill with limited magical aptitude, occupying a middle ground between mundane and supernatural. The world trends toward greater magical saturation as the series progresses, with magic waves becoming longer and stronger — a trajectory that suggests technology may eventually lose the oscillation entirely.

## Cultural role

Magic in post-Shift Atlanta is not hidden — it is an undeniable, infrastructure-destroying fact of daily life. The political landscape is shaped by magical factions competing openly for territory and influence: the Pack controls neighborhoods, the People run a corporate empire built on undead labor, the Order provides law enforcement, and various smaller groups (witch covens, volhv congregations, individual powers) stake their own claims. The mundane government persists but has limited authority over supernatural disputes.

The magic/technology oscillation functions as a metaphor for systemic instability — a world where the rules keep changing and no single strategy guarantees survival. Kate's arc, concealing her heritage while building alliances across faction lines, reflects the series' interest in how power structures form and reform in conditions of permanent uncertainty.
