## Mechanics

The **Sacrifice and Apostlehood** system is the primary supernatural mechanism in *Berserk*. At its center is the **Behelit** — a small, egg-shaped artifact with scrambled facial features (eyes, nose, mouth arranged randomly across its surface) that activates in a moment of absolute despair. When the Behelit's features align into a face and it sheds tears of blood, it tears open the boundary between the physical world and the demonic realm, summoning the **God Hand** — five transcendent demonic entities who offer the Behelit's owner a choice: sacrifice what you love most, and receive power beyond human limits.

If the owner accepts, their loved ones are marked with the **Brand of Sacrifice** and consumed by demons. The owner is transformed into an **Apostle** — a being of superhuman power with a demonic true form, immortal or near-immortal, capable of feats far beyond human ability. Each Apostle's demonic form is unique, reflecting their psychology and desires. The transformation is irreversible.

The **God Hand** themselves are Apostles who underwent a special, once-in-several-centuries version of the sacrifice called the **Eclipse** — a cataclysmic event in which the sacrifice is on a vastly larger scale and the resulting being transcends even Apostle-level power to become one of the five God Hand. Griffith's Eclipse — in which he sacrificed the entire Band of the Hawk to become Femto — is the series' central event.

The **Brand of Sacrifice** marks those offered in the sacrifice. The Brand draws evil spirits and demons to its bearer, making them targets for supernatural attack at all times, especially at night and during periods when the boundary between worlds thins. The Brand also bleeds in the presence of demonic entities, serving as both a curse and a warning system. Guts and Casca both bear the Brand, placing them in perpetual danger.

Beyond the Behelit system, the world contains other supernatural elements: **sorcery** (practiced by characters like Schierke and Flora, drawing on elemental spirits and astral projection), **elf magic** (the Flower Storm Monarch and other fairy-realm powers), and the **Berserker Armor** (a cursed suit of armor that removes the wearer's pain and fear responses, allowing them to fight beyond physical limits at the cost of their body and sanity).

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **absolute moral destruction**. The sacrifice requires the Apostle to kill the person or people they love most in the world. This is not metaphorical — the loved ones die in agony, consumed by demons while the would-be Apostle watches and chooses. Every Apostle carries this knowledge, and the series examines how different characters cope with (or refuse to cope with) what they've done.

The second cost is **the Brand's perpetual torment**. Those who survive a sacrifice (as Guts and Casca did) are marked forever. The Brand draws demons, prevents rest, and ensures that the survivor's life is one of constant combat and suffering. There is no known way to remove the Brand.

The third cost is **servitude to causality**. Apostles serve the God Hand, whether they acknowledge it or not. Their existence is part of a larger pattern of causality shaped by the Idea of Evil — a primordial force that exists because humanity needed a reason for suffering. Apostles believe they have gained freedom through power, but they are instruments of a cosmic design they did not choose.

## Practitioner access

Behelits appear to find their destined owners rather than being sought. The artifacts circulate through the world, eventually reaching the person fated to use them. Not everyone who possesses a Behelit activates it — the activation requires a specific emotional state (absolute despair, the death of hope) and the willingness to sacrifice. The system is designed by the God Hand to produce Apostles at a rate that serves their purposes.

## Cultural role

In the world of *Berserk*, the supernatural is largely hidden from ordinary people. Most humans live mundane lives, unaware of Apostles, the God Hand, or the metaphysical structure of their world. Apostles disguise themselves as human rulers, warriors, or monsters depending on their inclinations. The Holy See (the setting's religious institution) is aware of some supernatural threats but understands them through a theological framework that does not grasp the true cosmology. Berserk uses this hidden structure to explore how institutional power (the church, the nobility) fails to comprehend or address the real forces shaping the world — a thematic parallel to the series' broader critique of how power systems obscure their own foundations.
