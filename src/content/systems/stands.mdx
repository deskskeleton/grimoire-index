## Mechanics

A Stand is a personalized spirit-being, manifested by a single user, that embodies one specific ability and one specific design. Each Stand is unique — no two users have the same Stand, even if their abilities overlap thematically. The Stand is partly a separate entity (it has a posture, a costume, often a face or mask, and sometimes a personality of its own) and partly an extension of the user's will (it cannot reason or act outside the user's intent, and it shares the user's senses).

The system's defining structural rule is that user and Stand share damage. A wound dealt to the Stand reflects on the user's body in the same location with the same severity. If the Stand bleeds, the user bleeds; if the Stand is cut, the user is cut. This makes a Stand both an offensive weapon and a defensive vulnerability, and it tightens the fictional logic that a Stand cannot leave its user unattended without consequence.

A second structural rule is range vs. power. Most Stands fall into a rough trade-off: short-ranged Stands (operating within a meter or two of the user, like Star Platinum or Crazy Diamond) tend to be physically powerful and precise; long-ranged Stands (operating many meters or even at indefinite range, like Killer Queen's bombs or The Hand's deletions) tend to be physically weaker or more specialized. There are exceptions, but the trade-off is the genre's working assumption.

Each Stand has a named, bespoke ability — a single specific power that defines what it does. Star Platinum is a high-speed, high-precision close combatant that can briefly stop time. The World stops time as its primary effect. Gold Experience grants life to inanimate matter. Killer Queen turns objects into bombs at the touch. Crazy Diamond restores damaged things to a previous state. Made in Heaven accelerates time itself. The roster across the manga is extensive, and most arcs are structured around revealing and outwitting a new Stand's specific rules.

Stand abilities are typically rated within the manga on a six-axis chart — Destructive Power, Speed, Range, Persistence, Precision, Developmental Potential — each scored from A (highest) to E (lowest) or "None." The chart is partly ironic and partly informative; some of the most dangerous Stands have low scores in the conventional axes and overwhelming scores in one specific category.

## Limitations and costs

The shared-damage rule is the system's principal cost. A Stand that reaches out to hit something must accept that hitting it back hits the user. This bounds combat geometry significantly.

Range is a real constraint. A user out of range of their Stand is operationally weaker. Long-range Stands trade physical effectiveness for distance.

Most Stands cannot be perceived by ordinary humans. Only Stand users (and a small set of canonical exceptions) can see Stands. This produces both the hidden-world structure of the franchise and a tactical layer where a Stand user fighting in public is mostly invisible to bystanders.

Bespoke abilities are bound to their user. A Stand cannot be transferred. When a user dies, their Stand dies with them (with rare canonical exceptions). When two users have similar abilities, the abilities are nonetheless distinct in their precise rules and cannot be substituted.

A subtler cost is that mastering a Stand is a learned discipline. New Stand users in the manga frequently misuse, misread, or are surprised by their own abilities until they have practiced. The series treats Stand-discovery as a coming-of-age beat for several characters.

## Practitioner access

Stand users emerge in three canonical ways. Some are born to it (children of Stand users have a high chance of developing Stands of their own, related to but distinct from their parents'). Some are awakened by being pierced with a Stand-bestowing artifact (the Bow and Arrow, principally). Some are the result of more obscure metaphysical events tied to the long Joestar-line plot.

Across the series, Stand users are uncommon but not vanishingly rare — each Part follows a small group of them within a much larger non-user world, and antagonist factions tend to be Stand-using groups.

## Cultural role

Stands are hidden from the general public — most ordinary people in the JoJo world do not believe in or perceive Stands. Stand users themselves form a loose recognition community: they can see each other's Stands, and most encounters between Stand users in the manga begin with a tacit acknowledgment that both parties are in the masquerade. Specific institutional groups (the Speedwagon Foundation, the Passione mafia, the Saints' Corpse cult, the Foundation in Stone Ocean) have organized around Stand activity, but society at large remains unaware.
