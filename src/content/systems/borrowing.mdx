## Mechanics

**Borrowing** is an advanced technique within Discworld witchcraft in which the witch projects her consciousness into an animal's mind. Unlike warging or shapeshifting in other traditions, Borrowing is explicitly **not possession** — the witch does not control the animal. Instead, she rides as a passenger, perceiving through the animal's senses, observing its thoughts and instincts, but not directing its behavior. The animal continues doing whatever it would normally do; the witch simply watches from inside.

Granny Weatherwax Borrows routinely — she rides in hawks to survey the landscape, in bees to understand colony behavior, in cats to observe the village at night. The experience is vivid and immersive: the witch does not merely see what the animal sees but experiences its mode of consciousness. A hawk's world is all visual acuity and thermal currents; a bee's world is dance-language and flower-maps; a cat's world is territory and contempt.

While Borrowing, the witch's own body is in a **deep trance** — effectively comatose. It breathes but does not respond to stimuli. A sign reading "I ATEN'T DEAD" is traditionally placed on the body to prevent premature burial, a practical measure that reflects both the danger and the regularity of the practice.

The technique requires **mental discipline** of the highest order. The witch must maintain a thread of self-identity while immersed in a consciousness structured entirely differently from her own. The animal's perspective is seductive precisely because it is simple — no moral complexity, no social obligations, no awareness of mortality. The simplicity is the trap.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **identity loss**. A witch who stays too long in an animal's mind risks losing the thread of her own identity. She begins to think like the animal — to forget that she is human, to forget that she has a body to return to. Granny Weatherwax's iron will makes her exceptionally resistant to this, but even she acknowledges the danger.

The second cost is **physical vulnerability**. The body is defenseless during Borrowing. A witch who Borrows in an unsafe location can be robbed, moved, or harmed without awareness.

The third limitation is **non-intervention**. Because Borrowing is observation and not control, the witch cannot use the animal as a tool. She cannot make the hawk attack a specific target or direct the cat to a specific location. She can only watch. This makes Borrowing powerful for intelligence-gathering but useless for direct action.

## Practitioner access

Borrowing is known to a small number of Lancre witches and is considered an advanced technique even by witchcraft's informal standards. It is not widely taught — most witches either lack the mental discipline or recognize the danger and avoid it. Granny Weatherwax is the most accomplished Borrower known, and Tiffany Aching learns aspects of the technique during her training.

## Cultural role

Borrowing exists within the broader framework of Lancre witchcraft — a tradition that emphasizes **headology** (psychology, suggestion, and practical wisdom) over overt magical displays. Borrowing is consistent with this philosophy: it is a way of knowing, not a way of doing. Granny Weatherwax uses it as a reconnaissance tool, gathering information that she then acts on through entirely mundane (or headological) means. In the witchcraft tradition's informal hierarchy, the ability to Borrow safely is a mark of exceptional mental control — and the willingness to do it regularly is a mark of Granny's particular brand of stubborn self-confidence.
