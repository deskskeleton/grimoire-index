## Mechanics

Hemalurgy operates by transferring an attribute or ability from one person to another by way of a metal spike driven through the donor's body in a precise location. The spike must pierce the heart or another specified organ; the donor must die from the wound; and the spike, now charged with whatever it was positioned to capture, can be implanted in a recipient's body to grant them the captured attribute.

The metal of the spike determines what is captured. The same sixteen metals used by Allomancy and Feruchemy serve here, but each performs a different role under Hemalurgy: a steel spike captures an Allomantic ability, a brass spike captures a Feruchemical attribute, and so on. The exact rules are notoriously esoteric — the system is presented in fiction as a closely guarded craft of which only a few practitioners (the Lord Ruler, the Inquisitors) understood the full mechanics during the original Mistborn era.

Once a spike is charged and implanted, the recipient gains the captured attribute (or some fraction of it — repeated transfers degrade the strength of what is preserved). Multiple spikes can be implanted in the same recipient, granting compound abilities, but each additional spike opens the recipient's mind to greater control by Ruin (the Shard whose magic Hemalurgy primarily channels). Heavily spiked individuals can be remotely influenced or directly controlled.

## Limitations and costs

The headline cost is moral and structural. Every transfer requires a victim who dies in the process, and the system cannot be performed cleanly. Hemalurgy is the Cosmere's clearest example of "costed magic" extended to its grimmest consequence: a Hemalurgist's power scales directly with how many people they have killed and how surgically they have killed them. Most practitioners in the series are explicitly antagonists.

A second cost is degradation. Hemalurgic transfer loses some of the captured attribute's strength — a stolen Allomantic gift is weaker than the original, and the more times a quality is transferred between spikes, the more it degrades. This means Hemalurgy cannot simply concentrate ability indefinitely; there is friction.

A third cost is loss of control. Each implanted spike opens a "hole" in the recipient's spiritual self, through which Ruin (or, by extension, certain other powers) can influence or directly puppet them. The Inquisitors are an extreme case — heavily spiked individuals who are simultaneously dangerously powerful and entirely subordinate to whoever holds the leash.

## Practitioner access

Hemalurgy is in principle available to anyone willing to perform the ritual correctly. There is no innate or hereditary gating; the system works for whoever performs it. In practice, the procedural knowledge has been tightly held — by the Lord Ruler, the Inquisitor corps, and a small number of later practitioners — and is not widely disseminated. The recipients of Hemalurgic transfers do not need any magical aptitude themselves; they only need to receive and survive the spike implantation.

## Cultural role

In the original Mistborn era, Hemalurgy is the Lord Ruler's secret weapon, used to create the Inquisitor enforcement caste and a small number of other servants. The general population has no knowledge of the system. By the second era, Hemalurgy is openly discussed within the small community that knows about the Metallic Arts, and is broadly regarded as morally beyond the pale. It is the Cosmere's most direct illustration of magic-as-atrocity: a system whose mechanics encode the ethical cost into the act itself.
