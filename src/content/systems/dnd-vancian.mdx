## Mechanics

D&D's magic is organized into discrete spells, each defined by a numeric level (typically 1 through 9), a casting time, a range, a duration, a set of components (verbal, somatic, material), and a specific effect. To cast a spell, a character expends a "spell slot" of equal or higher level. Slots are a per-day resource that recover with rest, which gives casting a tight resource-management character.

Different spellcasting classes acquire spells through different fictional and mechanical pipelines. Wizards study, scribe, and prepare a daily list from a written spellbook. Sorcerers know a small fixed list intuitively, drawing on innate magical heritage. Clerics and druids receive spells from a divine or natural patron, prepared each day from a class-wide list. Warlocks make pacts with otherworldly entities for a smaller pool of recharge-on-short-rest slots and powerful per-cast invocations. Bards lean on charismatic performance; rangers and paladins blend martial and partial-caster mechanics. The social and metaphysical framing differs significantly between classes; the underlying slot economy is broadly shared.

Casting a spell requires fulfilling its components. Verbal components fail under silence; somatic components fail when hands are bound; material components fail when ingredients are unavailable, though in modern editions a generic spellcasting focus replaces most of these. Some spells require concentration to maintain, which limits a single caster to one ongoing concentration effect at a time.

The effect catalogue is enormous and spans nearly every category of magical action a fantasy RPG might want: damage in every elemental flavor, healing, divination, illusion, enchantment, conjuration, abjuration, necromancy, transmutation. Spells of the same level are, in principle, balanced against one another — a 3rd-level slot used on Fireball costs the same as one used on Counterspell.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is per-day resource expenditure. A 5th-level wizard has a defined number of spell slots at each level and cannot cast more than that without rest. Higher-level slots can power lower-level spells, sometimes with bonus effects, but the reverse is not true. This bounds caster output and forces tactical sequencing.

A second cost is preparation. Wizards must scribe new spells into a spellbook (an in-fiction document the system treats as a real, losable, stealable artifact) and choose which to prepare each day. Clerics and druids similarly select prepared spells from their class list; they cannot cast a spell they did not prepare, regardless of available slots. Sorcerers and warlocks have shorter known-spell lists and trade flexibility for spontaneity.

A third cost is component-based. Some powerful spells require expensive material components that are consumed on use; others have non-consumed but expensive foci. A handful of spells require components that are difficult to source in fiction (a diamond worth a specific monetary value, a rare herb), creating natural quest hooks.

A fourth cost is interactive: most damaging spells allow the target a saving throw or attack roll, and counterspell mechanics let opposing casters negate effects in flight. Magic is rarely an unilateral guarantee.

## Practitioner access

Access depends on class. Wizards are trained — typically through formal study at a college, under a master, or through self-directed work with recovered grimoires. Sorcerers manifest power innately, often through a heritage trait (draconic, wild, divine soul, or other origins). Clerics and druids are called or claimed by a divine or natural force. Warlocks bargain with a patron. Bards develop their craft through performance. Most settings permit any humanoid race to take any caster class, with mechanical bonuses or flavor differences depending on edition and setting.

## Cultural role

Within most published D&D settings, magic is recognizably uncommon but not vanishing. Major cities support wizardly academies, temple hierarchies, and adventuring guilds; rural settings see hedge-witches, druids, and itinerant clerics. Magical effects have shaped infrastructure (continual flame lighting, teleportation circles between major cities, healing magic in lieu of medicine), economy (spell scrolls and magic items as tradeable goods), and politics (high-level casters as state assets or threats). The exact texture varies enormously by setting — Eberron treats arcane magic as industrial, Dark Sun treats it as ecocidal, the Forgotten Realms treats it as widespread but dangerous — but the underlying slot-based mechanics carry across.
