## Mechanics

The Land's magic operates on two fundamentally different levels. **Earthpower** is the ambient magical force that pervades all living matter and geological features of the Land. It is the reason the Land's grass can close wounds, its *aliantha* berries can sustain a starving traveller, and its stone can resist corruption. Earthpower is ordered, natural, and responsive to knowledge and will. The **Lords of Revelstone** study and channel Earthpower through the **Staff of Law** (a powerful focus-artifact) and through accumulated lore — the body of knowledge called **Kevin's Lore**, named for the last High Lord who possessed its full extent before committing the catastrophic Ritual of Desecration. Earthpower can also be accessed through more primal channels: the **Forestals** sing it into the trees to ward off corruption, the **Ramen** attune to it through their bond with the Ranyhyn (great horses), and the **Stonedownors** and **Woodhelvennin** work it through *orcrest* (stone) and *lomillialor* (wood) respectively.

**Wild Magic** is categorically different. It is not native to the Land but enters through Covenant's white-gold wedding ring — an alloy that does not exist in the Land's natural order. Wild Magic is chaotic, virtually unlimited in potential, and fundamentally uncontrollable through knowledge or training. It is the **keystone of the Arch of Time**: its power can unmake the structure of reality itself, which is precisely why Lord Foul wants Covenant to use it destructively. When Covenant channels Wild Magic, it manifests as white fire and silver lightning, capable of effects ranging from obliterating armies to healing the Land's deepest wounds — but always at the edge of catastrophe, because the wielder cannot fully govern what happens.

The two forces interact in essential ways. The Staff of Law — the primary focus for Earthpower — serves as a counterbalance and container for Wild Magic, giving it direction and limiting its destructive potential. Without Law to shape it, Wild Magic is as likely to destroy as to heal. In the Second Chronicles, the **Sunbane** — Lord Foul's corruption of Earthpower itself — perverts the Land's natural cycles into a destructive phenomenon of alternating desert, rain, pestilence, and fertility, each one a weaponised inversion of Earthpower's generative nature.

## Limitations and costs

Wild Magic's primary limitation is **uncontrollability**. Covenant cannot summon or direct it reliably; it responds to emotional extremity — rage, despair, love — rather than to intention or skill. Each major use risks annihilating something he did not mean to destroy. The ultimate cost is existential: full release of Wild Magic would shatter the Arch of Time, freeing Lord Foul but destroying the Land and possibly all of reality.

Earthpower's costs are more conventional but no less severe. Kevin's Lore was lost when Kevin performed the **Ritual of Desecration**, destroying the Land's health in a futile attempt to destroy Lord Foul — the definitive cautionary tale about power wielded in despair. The Lords of Covenant's era possess only fragments of Kevin's knowledge and cannot match the ancient mastery. The Staff of Law can be broken (and is, in the Second Chronicles), leaving Earthpower without its primary focus.

Physical toll attends both systems. Channeling Earthpower through lore or the Staff exhausts practitioners; channeling Wild Magic leaves Covenant shattered and often unconscious. The moral cost is paramount: both forms of power are corrupted by the wielder's intentions, and Foul's entire strategy depends on provoking Covenant into using Wild Magic in anger or despair.

## Practitioner access

Earthpower is, in principle, accessible to everyone in the Land — it pervades everything, and the Land's ordinary inhabitants benefit from it constantly through healing mud, nourishing plants, and resilient stone. Focused channeling requires training: the Lords study at Revelstone, the Gravelers and Woodhelvennin learn village-level working, and the Forestals operate through an older, deeper tradition of song.

Wild Magic is accessible only to the white-gold wielder — in the original series, solely Thomas Covenant. Linden Avery later wields both the Staff of Law and, briefly, Covenant's ring, but Wild Magic resists anyone who tries to govern it through pure will. The Haruchai, Ramen, and Giants — the Land's other peoples — have their own relationships with Earthpower but cannot access Wild Magic at all.

## Cultural role

Magic in the Land is openly known and culturally central. The Lords govern from Revelstone as scholar-warriors whose authority derives from their command of Kevin's Lore. The **Oath of Peace** — a vow against the destructive use of power — shapes their entire civilisation's self-understanding after Kevin's Desecration. Earthpower is not merely a tool but a theology: the Land's peoples understand their world as alive and sacred, and the corruption of Earthpower (whether through Desecration or the Sunbane) is experienced as a spiritual catastrophe, not merely a practical one. Wild Magic exists outside this cultural framework entirely — it is an alien force wielded by an alien visitor, and the Land's relationship to it is one of desperate hope and justified terror.
