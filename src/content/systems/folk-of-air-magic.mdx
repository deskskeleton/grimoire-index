## Mechanics

Fae magic in Elfhame operates through several interconnected abilities that are innate to the fae rather than learned. Glamour is the most pervasive — the ability to cast illusions that alter how mortals perceive the fae, their surroundings, or reality itself. A glamoured fae can appear human, make a hovel look like a palace, or render themselves invisible to mortal eyes. The strength of glamour varies by individual; powerful fae can maintain elaborate illusions indefinitely, while lesser fae produce only fleeting effects.

Compulsion is glamour's more dangerous cousin. Fae can issue spoken commands that mortals are supernaturally compelled to obey. The mechanism is not mind control in the traditional sense — the victim remains aware of what they are doing — but they cannot resist the instruction. This ability operates on a spectrum; casual suggestions may produce only mild compliance, while a direct command from a powerful fae is effectively irresistible to an unprotected mortal.

Beyond glamour and compulsion, individual fae possess varied magical talents: shapeshifting, elemental control over water or plant life, enhanced physical abilities, and the enchantment of objects. These abilities are hereditary and vary by family line, though they do not follow strict rules of inheritance. The fae are also universally long-lived, difficult to kill, and possessed of heightened senses.

The system's most distinctive constraint is the fae inability to lie. Fae can mislead, omit, imply, speak in riddles, and tell truths designed to produce false conclusions, but they cannot make a statement they know to be factually untrue. This constraint transforms language itself into a magical arena — every conversation with a fae is potentially a trap constructed entirely from true statements.

## Limitations and costs

Glamour and compulsion have limited effect on mortals who have built resistance through prolonged exposure to Faerie. Jude Duarte, raised in Elfhame from childhood, is functionally immune to glamour, though she remains vulnerable to direct compulsion. This partial resistance is rare and not well understood — it appears to be a product of long habituation rather than any deliberate training.

Iron is toxic to fae, causing burns on contact and weakening magical abilities in proximity. This material vulnerability gives mortals a potential equalizer, though openly carrying iron in Elfhame is a provocative act. Rowan berries can also counteract some fae enchantments, and certain charms and protections can shield mortals from glamour.

The binding nature of oaths and geasa represents a cost structure built into the fae social fabric. A geas — a magical prohibition or compulsion placed on an individual — cannot be broken without consequence, and the consequences are often severe or fatal. Oaths sworn by fae carry supernatural binding force, meaning that a carelessly worded promise can become an inescapable trap. This applies equally to fae and to mortals who swear oaths in Faerie — the magic enforces the letter of the agreement, not the spirit.

## Practitioner access

All fae possess some degree of magical ability, but power is unevenly distributed along lines of birth and rank. The royal and noble families of Elfhame tend toward greater magical potency, while common fae may possess only minor glamour and enhanced longevity. There is no training tradition or academy — fae magic is instinctive, and a fae either has a particular ability or does not.

Mortals are categorically excluded from practicing fae magic, though they can use fae-crafted objects, exploit the binding nature of oaths, and leverage their immunity to certain effects. Jude's arc demonstrates that a mortal can operate effectively within a magical system not by gaining power but by understanding and manipulating its rules — particularly the obligation structures of oaths, debts, and geasa.

## Cultural role

Magic in Elfhame is inseparable from politics. The ability to glamour, compel, and bind oaths means that every social interaction among the fae is a potential exercise of power. The Court of Shadows — an intelligence network operating through the manipulation of oaths and secrets — represents the institutionalization of magical social control. Succession to the throne depends as much on the ability to navigate magical obligations as on military strength or popular support.

The fae-mortal power imbalance is the trilogy's central concern. Fae treat mortals as entertainment, servants, or curiosities precisely because glamour and compulsion make mortals trivially easy to control. Jude's resistance — and her eventual rise to a position of political authority despite her mortality — challenges the assumption that magical superiority equates to political legitimacy, reframing the question of power from "who has the strongest magic" to "who understands the rules well enough to exploit them."
