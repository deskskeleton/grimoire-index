## Mechanics

**Imaging** is the ability to visualize something and make it physically real. An imager concentrates on an object, substance, or effect — holding its image clearly in their mind with full structural detail — and it materializes in the physical world. The process is instantaneous: the imager thinks, and the thing exists.

The system's power scales with the imager's knowledge and visualization capacity. Simple imaging (creating a small object with a structure the imager understands well) is relatively easy and low-cost. Complex imaging (creating intricate mechanisms, specific chemical compounds, or large structures) requires both detailed knowledge of the thing's composition and immense concentration. An imager who understands metallurgy can image a steel blade with the correct carbon content and crystal structure; one who doesn't will produce something that looks like a blade but shatters on impact.

Offensive applications include: imaging objects inside a target (a lethal technique — imaging a sliver of iron inside someone's brain kills instantly), imaging fire or acid, imaging barriers and walls, and imaging structural failures in buildings or fortifications. Defensive applications include: imaging shields, reinforcing structures, and creating concealment.

Imaging can also **remove** matter — an imager can image something away, effectively disintegrating it. This is as dangerous as creating matter, as the energy involved must be absorbed by the imager.

The **Collegium Imago** (in later-era books) serves as the institutional center for training and governing imagers, providing education, political protection, and the structural knowledge that makes imaging effective and safe.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **physical exhaustion**. Imaging draws on the imager's physical stamina. Creating small, simple objects is mildly tiring; creating large or complex objects can cause collapse, coma, or death. An imager who overreaches — imaging beyond their capacity — simply dies. This makes every imaging act a risk calculation.

The second cost is **knowledge requirement**. An imager can only create what they understand. Imaging a poison without knowing its chemical structure produces something inert or random. Imaging a machine without understanding its mechanisms produces a non-functional shape. This means the most powerful imagers are necessarily the most educated — they must learn chemistry, metallurgy, anatomy, architecture, and other sciences to expand their capabilities.

The third limitation is **political vulnerability**. Imagers are feared because their abilities are inherently dangerous (imaging a knife into someone's heart is nearly undetectable murder). This fear produces political persecution, institutional control, and social restriction. The Collegium exists partly to protect imagers from society and partly to protect society from imagers.

## Practitioner access

Imaging is an innate talent — some people are born with it, most are not. Talent manifests in childhood or adolescence, often through accidental imaging (unconsciously creating objects or effects). The Collegium identifies and trains imagers, providing the education needed to use the talent safely and effectively. Untrained imagers are dangerous to themselves and others; the Collegium's training emphasizes control and knowledge before power.

## Cultural role

In Solidar (the primary nation), imagers occupy a politically complex position — feared for their power, valued for their utility, and controlled through the Collegium's institutional framework. The Collegium is simultaneously a school, a guild, and a political faction, negotiating between the rex (king), the High Holders (aristocracy), and the factors (merchant class). Modesitt uses the imager's position to explore how societies manage powerful minorities — the tension between protecting imagers from persecution and protecting society from imagers' potential for harm mirrors real-world debates about institutional control of dangerous capabilities.
