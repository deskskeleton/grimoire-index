## Mechanics

The old gods are beings that exist in darkness — in shadows, in the spaces after sunset, in the hours between dusk and dawn. They can be called upon by anyone desperate enough to pray after dark, though doing so is profoundly dangerous. Each old god has a different nature and makes different kinds of deals, but all share a common trait: their bargains are designed to give the petitioner exactly what they asked for in the most devastating way possible.

Addie LaRue, a young woman in 1714 France, prayed to the darkness for freedom — freedom from the marriage her father had arranged, freedom from the village that confined her, freedom from the mortal constraints of a single human life. Luc, the god who answered, granted her wish precisely. She became immortal, unaging, and free. But the price was equally absolute: no one who meets her can remember her after she leaves their presence. She cannot write her name — the ink smudges, the letters rearrange. She cannot be photographed — images blur. She cannot sign a lease, hold a job, maintain a friendship, or sustain a love affair. She is free, but she is also utterly alone.

The curse operates with mechanical consistency. The moment Addie leaves someone's line of sight, their memory of her begins to fade. Within minutes, she is forgotten entirely. Objects she has touched retain no trace of her ownership. Records she attempts to create destroy themselves. The effect is not illusion or mind-control — it is a fundamental alteration of her relationship to the world, as though reality itself refuses to acknowledge her permanence.

Luc himself visits Addie annually, on the anniversary of their bargain, to offer her a way out: she can surrender her soul and end the curse, but ending the curse means ending her life. He is patient, charming, and cruel — a tempter who treats their arrangement as a game played across centuries. His interest in her appears to grow as she refuses to break, suggesting that the old gods are sustained or entertained by the suffering their bargains produce.

## Limitations and costs

The curse's absoluteness is its defining feature. There are no loopholes, no workarounds, and no ways to cheat the system — or so it appears for three hundred years. Addie learns to survive through petty theft, charm, and the exploitation of the brief window before she is forgotten, but she cannot build anything lasting. Every relationship, every home, every identity she constructs dissolves the moment she steps away from it.

The psychological cost is staggering. Three centuries of being forgotten have left Addie resilient but deeply wounded. She has watched lovers forget her mid-sentence, seen friends look at her with blank incomprehension hours after sharing an intimate conversation, and been ejected from homes she has lived in for months because the occupants no longer know who she is. The curse does not merely isolate her — it erases the evidence that she ever existed as a social being.

The exception — Henry Strauss, who can remember her — reveals that the system is not quite as absolute as it appeared. Henry's ability to remember Addie is connected to his own deal with darkness, suggesting that the old gods' bargains can interact with and modify each other in ways that neither petitioner anticipated. This crack in the system's logic introduces the possibility that the curse can be circumvented, but only through mechanisms as dangerous and costly as the original bargain.

## Practitioner access

The old gods' bargains are theoretically available to anyone willing to pray after dark, but the system is not one that anyone would seek out with full knowledge of its costs. Addie's deal was born of desperation, not informed choice, and the story strongly implies that this is the intended dynamic — the old gods prey on the desperate, the cornered, and the young, offering escape at a price the petitioner cannot comprehend until it is too late.

Henry's separate deal with Luc confirms that the old gods make bargains with multiple people, each tailored to the individual's specific vulnerabilities. The system is not a discipline to be mastered but a trap to be endured, and the only practitioners are those who have already been caught.

## Cultural role

The pact exists entirely in secret. The surface world has no awareness of the old gods or their bargains, and Addie's three-century existence has left no mark on history, culture, or collective memory — which is precisely the point. Schwab uses the invisibility curse to explore what it means to matter, to be known, to leave a mark on the world. Addie's response to her curse — influencing art, inspiring creativity, touching lives in ways that cannot be traced back to her — becomes a meditation on the difference between fame and significance, between being remembered and having an impact. The magic system is less a worldbuilding element than a philosophical thought experiment given narrative form.
