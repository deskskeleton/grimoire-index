## Mechanics

**Magecraft** (魔術, *majutsu*) is the primary magical discipline in the Nasuverse. It encompasses all supernatural techniques that reproduce natural phenomena — from elemental projection to bounded fields to familiar creation. Magecraft is practiced by **Magi** — individuals born with **Magical Circuits**, organ-like pathways in the body that convert life force (od) or ambient magical energy (mana) into usable power.

The core mechanic is the **Magical Circuit**. Circuits are partially hereditary — mage families cultivate their bloodlines over generations to produce descendants with more numerous and higher-quality circuits. A Magus activates their circuits to channel mana, shape it through learned formulae, and produce effects. The number and quality of circuits determines a Magus's raw capacity, but technique, knowledge, and specialization determine effectiveness.

Magecraft is organized into numerous disciplines: **Elemental manipulation** (fire, water, wind, earth, ether), **Reinforcement** (strengthening the properties of objects or bodies), **Projection** (creating copies of objects from mana), **Bounded Fields** (territorial barriers and detection zones), **Alchemy** (transmutation and material manipulation), **Rune Magic** (Norse-derived inscription magic), **Necromancy**, **Puppetry**, and many others. Each mage family typically specializes in one discipline, refining it across generations.

The **Mage's Association** (headquartered at the Clock Tower in London) is the institutional body governing Magecraft. It maintains the secrecy of magic, arbitrates disputes between mage families, and conducts research aimed at reaching **Akasha** (the Root) — the origin of all phenomena. The Association is rigidly hierarchical, with families ranked by the age and refinement of their magical lineage.

**Crest inheritance** is a key mechanic: each mage family develops a **Magic Crest** — a concentrated record of accumulated magical knowledge inscribed on the body — which is passed from parent to heir. The Crest contains the family's spells, research, and accumulated wisdom, giving the heir access to techniques that took generations to develop.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **circuit strain**. Overusing magical circuits causes physical pain, internal damage, and potential burnout. Kiritsugu Emiya's Time Alter technique damages his body with each use. Circuits have a finite capacity per activation, and exceeding it risks permanent damage or death.

The second cost is **the Magecraft-Science boundary**. As technology advances, techniques that were once exclusively magical become scientifically reproducible, and those Magecraft disciplines are devalued. This creates an existential pressure on the mage community: their art is slowly being made obsolete by mundane progress. Only by reaching True Magic — the genuinely impossible — can a Magus transcend this decline.

The third cost is **secrecy maintenance**. The Mage's Association enforces strict secrecy about magic's existence. Magi who expose magic to the public face punishment up to execution. This constraint limits how and where Magecraft can be used, preventing Magi from operating openly in modern society.

## Practitioner access

Magecraft requires Magical Circuits, which are primarily hereditary. Established mage families — those with generations of selective breeding and crest accumulation — have enormous advantages over first-generation Magi. The Clock Tower's social hierarchy directly reflects this: old families with refined circuits and extensive crests hold political power, while newer practitioners are marginalized. Technically anyone born with circuits can practice Magecraft, but without family knowledge, a Magic Crest, and institutional support, reaching meaningful proficiency is extremely difficult.

## Cultural role

Magecraft exists in hiding within the modern world. The Mage's Association operates behind the façade of academic institutions, and most humans are unaware that magic exists. Within mage society, Magecraft is simultaneously a scholarly tradition, a family legacy, and a competitive pursuit — mage families are effectively aristocratic houses competing for prestige, research priority, and proximity to the Root. The Holy Grail War (the Fate series' central conflict) is the ultimate expression of this competition: seven Magi summoning heroic spirits to fight for the Grail, which is believed to grant access to True Magic.
