## Mechanics

**Skinchanging** (called **warging** when the bond is specifically with a wolf or direwolf) is the ability to project one's consciousness into an animal and take control of its body. While inside the animal, the skinchanger perceives through its senses — seeing through its eyes, smelling through its nose — and can direct its actions. The skinchanger's own body falls into a trance state during the experience, limp and vulnerable.

The connection is not purely mechanical. The skinchanger and the animal develop a persistent psychic bond even outside active warging sessions. Direwolf-bonded Starks experience "wolf dreams" — involuntary slips into their wolf's perspective during sleep. The bond deepens over time, and a skinchanger who spends too long in an animal begins to absorb its instincts, desires, and way of thinking.

Skinchanging works most readily with animals the practitioner has a bond with, but powerful skinchangers can seize unfamiliar animals. Varamyr Sixskins controls a shadowcat, an eagle, and three wolves simultaneously — an extraordinary feat that still requires effort to maintain. Some animals resist more than others; cats are notoriously difficult to control.

The most extreme and taboo application is **skinchanging into a human**. This is considered an abomination by the wildling skinchangers who maintain what little tradition exists around the practice. It is possible — Bran enters Hodor's mind — but it overrides the target's consciousness and causes visible distress.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **identity erosion**. A skinchanger who spends extended time in an animal's body begins to lose the boundaries of human selfhood. Varamyr's backstory illustrates this: his mentor warns that a skinchanger who dies while inside an animal will persist as a "second life" within the beast, gradually losing human memory and becoming fully animal. This is presented as both a last resort and a trap.

The second cost is **physical vulnerability**. The skinchanger's body is comatose during warging and cannot defend itself. Death of the body while the consciousness is elsewhere has unclear consequences — the suggestion is that the mind could survive in the animal, but increasingly degraded.

The third cost is **moral**. Taking a human mind is an abomination. Taking an animal's will is presented more neutrally by the narrative but still involves overriding another creature's autonomy. The wildling skinchanging tradition names three abominations: eating human flesh while in beast form, mating while in beast form, and entering a human mind.

## Practitioner access

Skinchanging is an innate ability with no known training method. It runs in bloodlines — the Stark children all have some degree of it, linked to their direwolf bonds — but it also appears among the free folk beyond the Wall with no Stark ancestry. The ability is rare everywhere. Among the free folk, skinchangers are recognized and somewhat respected, though the abomination taboos mark clear behavioral boundaries. South of the Wall, the ability is essentially unknown — dismissed as legend or confused with madness.

## Cultural role

Among the free folk, skinchanging is a known phenomenon with rudimentary cultural frameworks — naming conventions, taboos, and loose mentorship. South of the Wall, it has no cultural presence at all. The Starks' warging abilities develop without guidance or understanding, which Martin uses to emphasize the isolation and confusion of discovering supernatural abilities in a world that has largely forgotten magic exists. The Old Gods tradition and the weirwood network are thematically connected to skinchanging but operate through different mechanisms.
