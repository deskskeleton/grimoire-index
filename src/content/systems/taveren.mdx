## Mechanics

**Ta'veren** is not a power that is wielded but a condition that is borne. In the Wheel of Time's cosmology, the **Pattern** — the grand weave of fate spun by the Wheel — occasionally designates certain individuals as ta'veren: focal points around whom the threads of the Pattern gather and bend. When a ta'veren is present, probability distorts. Unlikely coincidences become common. People make decisions they would not ordinarily make. Events that "should" take years to unfold compress into days.

The effect is **area-based and passive**. A ta'veren does not choose to activate their influence; it radiates from them constantly, affecting everyone and everything nearby. The strength of the effect scales with the ta'veren's importance to the Pattern's current needs. Rand al'Thor — the Dragon Reborn and the strongest ta'veren in the current Age — produces effects so intense that marriages, deaths, and building collapses occur spontaneously in his vicinity, probability swinging wildly in both favorable and catastrophic directions.

Ta'veren can be **detected** by certain individuals. Some channelers (notably Siuan Sanche) have the Talent to see the glow of ta'veren status around a person. The Forsaken and other highly knowledgeable channelers recognize ta'veren effects when they encounter distorted probability.

The three ta'veren of the main series — Rand, Mat, and Perrin — are connected to each other; the Pattern uses all three to advance its needs, and their ta'veren nature sometimes causes them to sense each other's conditions or locations.

## Limitations and costs

Ta'veren is **uncontrollable**. The probability warping is not directed by the ta'veren's will — it serves the Pattern's needs, not the individual's desires. Rand cannot choose to make favorable coincidences happen; they happen around him because the Pattern requires it, and the results are as often harmful as helpful. A ta'veren might cause an ally to suddenly declare loyalty — or an enemy to appear at exactly the wrong moment.

The second cost is **social**. Ta'veren status makes other people uncomfortable, suspicious, or hostile. People near a strong ta'veren feel an irrational pull toward them — a compulsion to follow, serve, or oppose — and this manipulation of free will is noticed and resented. Several characters describe the experience of being near Rand as a loss of personal agency.

The third cost is **duration and purpose**. Ta'veren status is temporary, linked to a specific need of the Pattern. When the need is fulfilled, the Pattern releases the ta'veren, and they become ordinary again. This means the ta'veren exists in service to a cosmic design they do not control and may not understand.

## Practitioner access

No one can become ta'veren by choice, training, or inheritance. The Pattern selects individuals as needed. The frequency of ta'veren varies by Age; in the current crisis (the approach of Tarmon Gai'don, the Last Battle), the Pattern has produced three simultaneously — an unprecedented concentration.

## Cultural role

Ta'veren is a recognized concept across Randland's cultures, though understanding varies. Aes Sedai study it as a phenomenon of the Pattern and can sometimes identify ta'veren by sight. The general population is broadly aware that some people are "fated" or "destiny-touched" but treats the concept more as folklore than as an observable force. The political and social disruption caused by the three ta'veren in the main series — armies forming around them, nations reorienting, power structures shifting — is one of the series' central narrative engines.
