## Mechanics

The Endless are not practitioners of magic in the conventional sense — they are the substrate on which certain categories of reality depend. Dream does not cast spells to control the Dreaming; the Dreaming is him, an extension of his being that contains every dream ever dreamed, every story ever imagined, and every nightmare that has ever woken a sleeper in terror. When Morpheus shapes the Dreaming, he is reshaping an aspect of himself. His tools — the helm, the ruby, and the pouch of sand — are not sources of power but symbols and foci that concentrate his authority, and he can function without them, though diminished.

Each Endless operates with absolute authority within their domain. Death meets every living being at the moment of its birth and again at its death. Destiny reads from a book chained to his wrist that contains all events, past and future. Desire can kindle want in any heart. Despair can make any soul feel the weight of hopelessness. Delirium perceives and can induce states of madness, confusion, and shifted perception. Destruction, uniquely, has abandoned his function — he walked away from his realm in the seventeenth century, proving that the Endless can abdicate but not escape what they are.

Beyond the Endless themselves, the cosmology includes multiple tiers of supernatural power. Gods exist and draw strength from worship, but they are younger and lesser than the Endless. Hell is a real dimension, governed first by Lucifer and then by a consortium after Lucifer resigns his post and hands the key to Dream. Faerie, the Silver City, and numerous other realms intersect with the Dreaming. Mortal sorcerers can interact with these forces — a coven of human occultists successfully captures Dream for seventy years — but they are manipulating powers vastly beyond their comprehension.

The rules governing the Endless are strict and inviolable. An Endless who fails to perform their function creates a vacuum that reality will fill, often catastrophically. An Endless who kills a member of their own family sets in motion consequences that cannot be undone. Dream's central tragedy is that he is bound by rules he helped create, and his sense of duty and pride will not allow him to bend them even when rigidity leads to his own destruction.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation on the Endless is obligation. They are not free agents but functionaries of the universe, bound to perform their roles whether they wish to or not. Dream must maintain the Dreaming, adjudicate disputes among its inhabitants, and ensure that the boundary between dreams and waking reality remains intact. When he was imprisoned for seventy years, the Dreaming decayed, sleepers fell into comas or endless nightmares, and the fabric of imaginative reality frayed. His absence had consequences not because he chose to neglect his duties but because the universe cannot function without what he represents.

The Endless are also constrained by each other. They cannot directly destroy one another without existential repercussions, and their interpersonal dynamics — particularly Dream's fraught relationships with Desire and Despair — create vulnerabilities that mortal enemies exploit. Desire's manipulation of Dream's love life directly contributes to the events that lead to Morpheus's end.

For mortal practitioners who interact with these cosmic forces, the costs are simpler and harsher. The occultist Roderick Burgess intended to capture Death and instead trapped Dream, gaining nothing from the imprisonment and losing his son to madness in the process. Those who bargain with the Endless or attempt to use their artifacts find that cosmic power operates on a scale that human ambition cannot safely accommodate.

## Practitioner access

The Endless are seven, and there will only ever be seven. They are not a category that admits new members through effort, talent, or ambition. When an Endless ceases to be — as Morpheus eventually does — a new aspect of the same function emerges (Daniel Hall becomes the new Dream), but this is not promotion or inheritance in any human sense. It is the universe reasserting a necessary function.

Mortals can interact with the Endless through summoning rituals, bargains, or accidental contact (entering the Dreaming through sleep, encountering Death at the moment of dying), but they cannot wield Endless-level power. The sorcerers, witches, and gods who populate the series operate within their own more limited traditions, borrowing or negotiating for fragments of power that the Endless embody absolutely.

## Cultural role

The Endless exist at the boundary between metaphysics and narrative. Within the fiction, they are known to some cultures through myth and legend — Dream has been worshipped as a god in some traditions and feared as a demon in others — but they are not dependent on belief the way gods are. They predate worship and will outlast it. Their cultural role is structural rather than devotional: they are the reason dreams exist, the reason death occurs, the reason desire burns.

The series uses the Endless to explore questions about duty, identity, and change. Dream's arc is fundamentally about whether an eternal being can grow and transform, and the answer — that transformation for Dream requires the end of one version of himself and the beginning of another — treats the cosmic and the personal as inseparable. The magic system is the thematic argument.
