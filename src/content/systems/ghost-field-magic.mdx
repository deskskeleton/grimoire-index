## Mechanics

Ghost-field magic in Blades in the Dark operates through the same position/effect framework that governs all other actions in the game, which is its most consequential design decision. When a Whisper attunes to the ghost field, they make an action roll using the Attune action rating, and the GM sets position (controlled, risky, or desperate) and effect level (limited, standard, or great) based on the fictional circumstances. There is no spell list, no mana pool, no separate subsystem — the supernatural is resolved through the same mechanical language as picking a lock or intimidating a gang boss. This unification means that the boundaries of what magic can accomplish are always negotiated at the table rather than pre-defined in a rulebook.

The ghost field itself is a pervasive supernatural layer overlaid on the physical world of Doskvol, a city lit by electroplasmic street lamps and surrounded by crackling lightning barriers that keep the ghosts of the dead at bay. Electroplasm — the residue of death and spiritual energy — serves as both a narrative element and a practical resource. It powers the city's technology, fuels occult rituals, and manifests as the ectoplasmic substance of ghosts and demons. This dual role as infrastructure and supernatural medium means that the occult is woven into the economic and political fabric of the setting rather than existing as a separate domain.

Ritual magic provides the system's most structured supernatural framework while still operating within Blades' improvisational ethos. A character performing a ritual must identify an appropriate source of arcane knowledge, gather specific components or conditions, and then make a series of action rolls to complete the working. The GM determines the number of rolls required and the consequences of partial success or failure. This structure gives rituals a sense of weight and preparation that distinguishes them from the immediacy of attuning to the ghost field mid-score, but the underlying resolution remains the same flexible conversation between player intent and fictional positioning.

Band of Blades extends this supernatural framework to a military campaign scale, where the Chosen — powerful supernatural commanders — wield miracles that function as narrative-scale interventions rather than individual actions. The Chosen's abilities still resolve through position and effect, but their scope encompasses entire battles and strategic situations. This scalability demonstrates the robustness of the fiction-first approach: the same mechanical principles that govern a Whisper communing with a single ghost can accommodate a demigod reshaping a battlefield, because the system's resolution is calibrated to fictional impact rather than numerical power levels.

## Limitations and costs

The position/effect framework itself functions as the primary cost mechanism for ghost-field magic. Every supernatural action carries risk proportional to its ambition, and the consequences of failure or partial success in occult matters tend toward the catastrophic. A Whisper who rolls poorly while attuning might attract unwanted spiritual attention, suffer possession, take harm from electroplasmic feedback, or open a breach in the ghost field that allows hostile entities to pour through. These consequences are not mechanical penalties applied from a table but fictional complications established by the GM, which means they scale naturally with the narrative stakes.

Stress serves as the game's universal resource for pushing past limitations, and supernatural characters burn through it rapidly. A Whisper can resist consequences by spending stress, push themselves for greater effect, or use special abilities that cost stress directly. Since stress also measures how close a character is to acquiring a trauma — a permanent psychological condition — heavy use of the supernatural gradually degrades the practitioner's mental stability. Characters who rely heavily on ghost-field powers tend to accumulate traumas like paranoia, obsession, and detachment, which the game frames not as punishments but as the natural psychological toll of regularly perceiving a world layered with the dead.

The setting itself imposes structural limitations on supernatural practice. The Spirit Wardens — Doskvol's official organization for handling the dead — monitor and regulate occult activity. Electroplasm is a controlled substance. Ghosts are dangerous and unpredictable entities that resist easy manipulation. Demons require pacts that always carry hidden costs. These fictional constraints mean that even successful supernatural actions create complications: a Whisper who successfully binds a ghost must then deal with the political consequences of unauthorized spirit handling, the logistical challenge of containing the entity, and the ever-present risk that the binding will degrade.

The clock system amplifies these costs over time. Long-term supernatural projects — establishing a spirit network, researching ancient rituals, cultivating demonic contacts — are tracked through progress clocks that advance through play. Hostile forces get their own clocks that tick forward in response to the crew's activities. A Whisper's occult ambitions inevitably draw attention from Spirit Wardens, rival practitioners, and predatory entities, creating an escalating web of supernatural entanglements that the position/effect framework renders increasingly dangerous.

## Practitioner access

The Whisper playbook provides the most direct access to ghost-field magic, with special abilities like Compel (forcing a ghost to appear and obey), Ritual (performing extended supernatural workings), and Tempest (channeling electroplasmic energy as a weapon). However, Blades' flexible action system means that any character can attempt to interact with the supernatural through the Attune action — they simply lack the special abilities and action rating dots that make a Whisper effective at it. This design creates a spectrum of supernatural competence rather than a binary divide between magical and mundane characters.

The playbook structure means that supernatural specialization comes at the cost of other competencies. A character who invests heavily in Attune and Whisper special abilities has fewer action dots and abilities for social manipulation, combat, or technical skills. The game's crew-based structure mitigates this trade-off — a well-composed crew includes specialists who cover different domains — but it means that the Whisper's supernatural power is always balanced against their relative vulnerability in non-occult situations. A Whisper who can commune with ancient spirits may be helpless in a knife fight, which creates natural interdependence within the crew.

Special heritage and background options allow any playbook to have some supernatural connection. A character with a ghost heritage might perceive the ghost field naturally. A character who served as a Spirit Warden has institutional knowledge of the supernatural. These options create characters with partial supernatural access — enough to engage with the ghost field narratively but without the mechanical depth of a dedicated Whisper. This layered approach to practitioner access means that the supernatural pervades the crew's experience without being monopolized by a single character.

The broader setting establishes that supernatural knowledge exists on a continuum from folk practice to institutional expertise. Ordinary citizens of Doskvol know basic facts about ghosts and electroplasm — these are mundane features of their world, not secrets. Trained Spirit Wardens possess systematic knowledge of the dead. Academic occultists study the ghost field theoretically. Whispers occupy a specific niche as practical, field-operative supernatural specialists, distinguished from these other categories by their willingness to engage directly with dangerous entities for criminal purposes.

## Cultural role

Ghost-field magic serves as the primary vehicle for Blades in the Dark's themes of desperation, corruption, and the costs of ambition. In a city literally haunted by its dead, where the sun has been shattered and the seas are filled with ravenous spectral leviathans, the supernatural is not a source of wonder but a pervasive environmental hazard. Magic in Doskvol is survival technology — the lightning barriers keep the ghosts out, the electroplasmic lamps hold back the perpetual darkness, the Spirit Wardens process the dead before they can rise. This framing transforms the supernatural from a power fantasy into a reflection of the game's core question: what are you willing to do, and what are you willing to become, to survive in a world that is actively hostile to human existence?

The ghost field functions as a worldbuilding engine that generates faction conflicts, score opportunities, and long-term campaign arcs. The Reconciled (ghosts who have achieved a kind of undead society), the Spirit Wardens (institutional handlers of the dead), the Path of Echoes (a cult seeking communion with spirits), and the Dimmer Sisters (reclusive occultists hoarding supernatural knowledge) all represent different relationships to the ghost field, and their competing agendas create a web of supernatural politics that the crew can navigate, exploit, or get crushed by. Every supernatural element in the setting is simultaneously a narrative hook and a potential threat.

Within the fiction-first framework of Forged in the Dark games, ghost-field magic demonstrates how supernatural elements can be integrated into a game without requiring mechanical segregation. The lesson that Harper's design teaches — and that dozens of Forged in the Dark hacks have since adopted — is that magic does not need its own subsystem if the core resolution mechanic is flexible enough to accommodate it. Position and effect can evaluate the risk of communing with a demon just as readily as the risk of breaking into a warehouse, because both are ultimately fiction that the table negotiates into mechanical terms.

The extension into Band of Blades reveals how the same supernatural framework adapts to different genre contexts. Where Blades frames the ghost field as a feature of urban criminal life, Band of Blades frames the supernatural as a military strategic resource. The Chosen's miracles are not personal powers but campaign-level assets that must be deployed judiciously across a retreating army's desperate march. This recontextualization demonstrates the modularity of Harper's approach: the ghost field is not just a setting element but a design pattern for integrating the supernatural into fiction-first RPGs at any scale.
