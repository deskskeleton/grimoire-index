## Mechanics

The **Small Science** is the term Grisha use for their innate ability to manipulate matter at the molecular level. Rather than conjuring energy from nothing, Grisha work with existing matter — accelerating molecules to create fire, slowing them to produce ice, manipulating air currents to control weather, or adjusting the chemistry of the human body to heal wounds or stop hearts.

Grisha are organized into three orders, each with specializations:

**Corporalki** (the Order of the Living and the Dead): **Heartrenders** manipulate the human body — accelerating heartbeats, inducing pain, controlling blood flow, and at extreme levels, killing with a gesture. **Healers** repair physical damage by manipulating tissue at the cellular level. **Tailors** alter physical appearance by reshaping features, skin, and hair.

**Etherealki** (the Order of Summoners): **Squallers** manipulate air and wind. **Inferni** manipulate combustion by exciting molecules to produce flame (they do not create fire from nothing — they require a spark or existing heat). **Tidemakers** manipulate water.

**Materialki** (the Order of Fabrikators): **Durasts** manipulate solid materials — metal, glass, stone — shaping and strengthening them. **Alkemi** manipulate chemical compositions, creating poisons, medicines, and explosives.

The **Darkling** (General Kirigan) possesses a unique ability: **Shadow Summoning** — the manipulation of darkness itself, a power so rare it has appeared only twice in Grisha history. Alina Starkov possesses its mirror: **Sun Summoning** — the ability to create and direct pure light. These two abilities exist outside the standard Grisha classification and are central to the series' conflict.

A key mechanic is **jurda parem** — an amplifying drug introduced in the *Six of Crows* duology that dramatically enhances Grisha abilities beyond their normal limits, allowing feats previously thought impossible (a Heartrender controlling a person like a puppet, a Tidemaker walking on water). The drug is catastrophically addictive and ultimately lethal, making it a weapon and a threat rather than a tool.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **physical exertion**. Using the Small Science is physically demanding — prolonged or intense use causes exhaustion, nosebleeds, and eventually collapse. Grisha must eat and rest to recover, and overextension can kill. This grounds their abilities in bodily reality and prevents unlimited power.

The second cost is **political persecution**. In Fjerda, Grisha are considered witches and hunted by the drüskelle (witch-hunters). In Ravka, they are conscripted into the Second Army — protected but also controlled as state assets. In Kerch, they are sometimes exploited as indentured servants. The Small Science makes its practitioners valuable, feared, and vulnerable in different ways across different nations.

The third limitation is **specialization rigidity**. A Grisha's order and specialization are innate and fixed — a Heartrender cannot learn to be a Squaller, and a Durast cannot become a Healer. This creates interdependence within Grisha communities and strategic limitations in conflict.

## Practitioner access

Grisha ability is innate and manifests in childhood. It cannot be learned by those who lack the ability, though training refines control and power. Grisha are identified young — in Ravka, by the Second Army's recruiters; in Fjerda, by the drüskelle who hunt them. The Little Palace in Ravka serves as the primary training institution, where young Grisha are educated in their specific disciplines. Outside Ravka, Grisha training is informal, dangerous, or nonexistent.

## Cultural role

The Small Science's cultural position varies by nation — a feature Bardugo uses to explore how different societies respond to the same form of power. In Ravka, Grisha are military assets: valued, controlled, and ultimately subordinate to political authority. In Fjerda, they are abominations: hunted, burned, and defined by religious persecution. In Kerch, they are commodities: useful, tradeable, and subject to market logic. This tripartite structure allows Bardugo to examine how power, fear, and commerce each produce different forms of oppression, and how the insistence that Grisha abilities are "science, not magic" is an attempt to claim legitimacy in a world that would rather fear them.
