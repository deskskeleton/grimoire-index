## Mechanics

Monsterhearts resolves supernatural abilities through the same Powered by the Apocalypse move structure that governs all other actions in the game. When a character uses a supernatural power, they roll 2d6 plus a relevant stat — typically Dark for manipulation and occult acts, or Hot for seduction and social dominance. On a 10+, the player gets what they want. On a 7-9, they get a compromised version with complications. On a 6 or below, the MC makes a hard move that escalates the situation. This resolution mechanic means that supernatural powers are never reliable tools; they are dramatic provocations that generate unpredictable narrative outcomes. A Vampire's hypnotic gaze is not a guaranteed effect but an opening gambit in a scene that might go anywhere.

Each Skin — the game's term for character playbook — provides unique moves that express its monster archetype through mechanical specificity. The Vampire gets "Hypnotic," which allows them to hold someone's attention and issue commands. The Werewolf gets "Unstoppable," which activates when they become their Darkest Self and makes them immune to physical harm while they rampage. The Fae gets "The Constant Bargain," which compels them to keep promises and grants power when others break theirs. These moves are not generic abilities reskinned with monster flavor — they are mechanically distinct expressions of what each monster type means as a metaphor for adolescent experience. The Vampire's predation is about desire and control. The Werewolf's rage is about the terror of losing composure. The Fae's bargains are about the binding power of social commitments.

The Strings economy provides the system's most distinctive mechanical layer. Strings are tokens representing social leverage, emotional connection, and interpersonal power. Characters gain Strings on others through specific triggers — turning someone on, making them jealous, catching them in a vulnerable moment — and spend them for mechanical benefits like adding to rolls against that person, offering experience points in exchange for compliance, or causing them harm. Supernatural powers interact heavily with the Strings economy: the Vampire gains Strings when feeding, the Witch spends them to cast hexes, the Queen uses them to direct their gang. This integration means that social and supernatural power are mechanically identical — manipulation, seduction, and sorcery all flow through the same economy of interpersonal leverage.

Monsterhearts 2 refined and sharpened these mechanics while maintaining their core logic. The revised edition clarified that the game's supernatural elements exist to serve its exploration of messy teenage identity, not the other way around. Mechanical changes — simplifying certain moves, adjusting how Strings function, and revising several Skins — all served the goal of keeping supernatural powers focused on interpersonal drama rather than drifting toward the tactical or the spectacular. Masks: A New Generation, while not a direct sequel, applies similar PbtA design principles to teen superheroes, where powers serve as expressions of identity crisis rather than combat resources, and the "Labels" system tracks how others' perceptions shape a young hero's self-understanding.

## Limitations and costs

The Darkest Self mechanic functions as the system's primary cost for supernatural identity, and it operates through compulsion rather than resource depletion. Each Skin has a specific trigger condition — the Vampire's Darkest Self activates when they are denied something they desire, the Werewolf's when they feel cornered or threatened — and once triggered, the character must behave according to their monster's worst impulses until a specific escape condition is met. The Vampire becomes a predator who feeds without empathy. The Werewolf becomes a berserker who attacks anyone nearby. The Ghost becomes invisible and forgotten, unable to affect the world. These are not power-ups but catastrophes — moments when the supernatural aspect overwhelms the human identity and the player temporarily loses agency over their character's behavior.

The social costs of supernatural power in Monsterhearts are structural rather than transactional. Using monstrous abilities publicly risks exposure, alienation, and the breakdown of social relationships that the characters depend on for emotional survival. A Vampire who feeds too openly, a Werewolf who transforms in front of witnesses, a Witch whose hexes produce visible effects — all risk being identified as monsters in a social environment where difference is already dangerous. The game does not track these costs through a numerical resource but through fictional positioning: the MC and other players respond to supernatural acts within the fiction, and the consequences ripple through the social landscape of the high school setting.

The Strings economy imposes reciprocal costs on supernatural manipulation. Gaining Strings on someone creates vulnerability as well as power — the target knows, on some level, that they are being manipulated, and they may gain Strings in return. Spending Strings depletes social capital that took effort to accumulate. The Witch's hex magic, which is the Skin closest to traditional spellcasting, requires spending Strings on the target as its primary cost, which means that the Witch must first establish emotional leverage before they can work magic. This creates a narrative rhythm where supernatural power requires social groundwork — you cannot hex a stranger, only someone you have already entangled yourself with.

The move structure itself imposes a cost through its graduated success system. Every supernatural action risks a 7-9 result that gives the MC ammunition for complications, or a 6- result that invites a hard move. Because Monsterhearts' MC moves tend toward social catastrophe rather than physical danger — exposing secrets, creating jealousy, forcing uncomfortable truths — failed supernatural actions generate interpersonal fallout rather than hit point damage. A Fae who fails to enforce a bargain does not lose mana; they lose face, credibility, and social standing. This cost structure keeps the game focused on what matters in its genre: not whether the magic works, but what it costs the character's relationships and self-image when it does or does not.

## Practitioner access

Practitioner access in Monsterhearts is determined by Skin selection at character creation — each player chooses a monster archetype, and that choice grants a specific set of supernatural moves that no other Skin can access. There is no multiclassing, no spell acquisition, and no mechanical path to gaining another Skin's abilities (though the "Season Advance" option allows a character to retire their current Skin and adopt a new one, representing a fundamental identity transformation). This design ensures that each character's supernatural abilities are tightly bound to their thematic identity. You cannot cherry-pick the Vampire's hypnosis and the Werewolf's strength because those abilities are expressions of fundamentally different metaphorical experiences, not interchangeable power modules.

The game assumes that all player characters are supernatural beings, but the nature of that supernaturality varies dramatically between Skins. The Vampire and Werewolf are recognizable classical monsters with relatively overt supernatural abilities. The Queen and the Mortal, however, operate at the boundary between the supernatural and the mundanely social — the Queen's power over their gang and the Mortal's obsessive attachment to a supernatural being function as supernatural moves mechanically but could be interpreted as extreme social dynamics rather than magic. This ambiguity is deliberate: Monsterhearts blurs the line between "this character has supernatural powers" and "this character is experiencing adolescence so intensely that it functions like supernatural power."

The absence of notable practitioners in the canonical text reflects a design philosophy that prioritizes emergent character creation over authored characters. Monsterhearts does not provide example characters, iconic NPCs, or canonical practitioners of its Skins. Each group's Vampire, Werewolf, or Witch is their own creation, shaped by the specific dynamics of their table's fiction. This approach treats supernatural identity as something generated through play rather than received from lore, which aligns with the game's thematic interest in identity as something constructed, negotiated, and performed rather than fixed and essential.

Masks: A New Generation extends the practitioner-access framework into superhero territory, where the question is not "what monster are you?" but "what kind of hero are you becoming?" Each Masks playbook — the Bull, the Nova, the Outsider — grants specific powers and moves, but the game's mechanical emphasis falls on the Labels system, which tracks how the character sees themselves across five identity dimensions. Powers in Masks are secondary to identity: a character's ability to fly or shoot energy blasts matters less than whether they see themselves as Danger or Savior, and how adult authority figures and peers reshape those self-perceptions. This inversion — where identity determines the meaning of power rather than power determining identity — represents the logical extension of Monsterhearts' metaphorical approach to the supernatural.

## Cultural role

Monsterhearts' Skin powers represent one of the most fully realized examples of magic-as-metaphor in tabletop RPG design. The game's central argument — that teenage experience is monstrous, and that monster stories are really about the terror and exhilaration of adolescence — is not merely thematic window dressing but the generative principle behind every mechanical decision. The Vampire's powers are about desire and consent. The Werewolf's powers are about anger and the fear of one's own capacity for violence. The Ghost's powers are about invisibility, marginalization, and the feeling of being unseen. The Witch's powers are about the intoxication and danger of having power over others. These are not metaphors applied to a preexisting power system; they are the reason the power system exists.

The Strings economy makes a specific argument about the nature of power in social environments: that power is relational, not absolute. A character's strength in Monsterhearts is measured not by their individual capabilities but by their web of social connections and the leverage they hold over others. This framing rejects the individualist power fantasy common in RPGs — the lone hero whose abilities make them self-sufficient — in favor of a model where power is always entangled with vulnerability. Gaining Strings on someone means you are also entangled with them; spending Strings means depleting a resource that you can only replenish through further social engagement. Isolation is not freedom in Monsterhearts; it is powerlessness.

The game's treatment of the Darkest Self as a cost rather than a power makes an argument about the relationship between supernatural identity and self-destruction. In most RPGs, a character's special abilities are assets — tools to be deployed strategically toward the player's goals. In Monsterhearts, the most extreme expression of a character's supernatural nature is a loss of control that serves the monster's drives rather than the character's desires. The Vampire's Darkest Self does not make them a more effective Vampire; it makes them a predator who cannot stop feeding. This inversion — where maximum supernatural expression equals minimum personal agency — captures something essential about the adolescent experience of being overwhelmed by feelings that seem to come from outside oneself.

The influence of Monsterhearts' approach can be traced through subsequent PbtA designs and beyond. Games like Urban Shadows (which applies similar metaphorical supernatural mechanics to urban fantasy politics), Thirsty Sword Lesbians (which uses supernatural abilities as expressions of queer identity and desire), and Masks (which translates the framework to superhero coming-of-age) all build on the premise that supernatural powers in RPGs can be designed primarily as metaphorical instruments rather than tactical resources. Alder's contribution to magic system design is the demonstration that the question "what can this power do?" is less important than the question "what does this power mean?" — and that mechanical design can encode meaning just as effectively as narrative prose.
