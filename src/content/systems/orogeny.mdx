## Mechanics

An **orogene** ("rogga," in the slur form used by ordinary Stillness society) senses the earth around them as a constant tactile awareness. They perceive faults, pressure gradients, the heat of magma, and the vibration of nearby movement in something like the way ordinary humans perceive temperature or balance. With training they can act on what they sense.

The basic mechanic is a transfer of energy. An orogene reaching for power forms a roughly toroidal field — the **torus** — around themselves, and within that torus they pull thermal and kinetic energy out of the environment to fuel an effect. The most reliable technique is **stilling**: damping a tremor or fault by absorbing its kinetic energy into the torus and grounding it elsewhere. A trained rogga can quiet a serious quake in moments. The same mechanic works in reverse: the orogene can project energy outward to crack stone, raise a wall of basalt, redirect a lava flow, or — at the upper end of the discipline — initiate a tremor of their own.

The energy drawn into the torus comes from somewhere. Most commonly it comes from **ambient heat**: a careless or hard-pressed orogene flash-freezes the area around them, killing nearby plants, animals, and (often) people. This is the iconic visible signature of orogenic use — a sudden fall in temperature, ice rimming surfaces, sometimes a cone of frozen corpses. Skilled practitioners can pull from deeper or more distant sources to spare the immediate environment, but doing so requires sustained concentration and reach.

A second, advanced layer is the **obelisks** — vast, semi-translucent crystalline artifacts of the lost Syl Anagist civilization, drifting overhead or buried in the earth. An orogene of sufficient power can connect to an obelisk and use it as an amplifier, accessing scales of effect (continental, even planetary) that an unaided rogga cannot reach. Obelisk connection is dangerous: a botched connection kills, and prolonged use begins to convert the user's body to stone. Mastery of multi-obelisk networks is the secret of the trilogy's highest-tier abilities.

The Fulcrum, the trilogy's training institution, formalizes orogene rank as numbered **rings** earned through demonstrations of precision and scale (one-ring through ten-ring, with ten-ring practitioners able to operate continentally). Beyond ten-ring lies the unspoken rank of the obelisk-wielders.

## Limitations and costs

The most immediate cost is **ambient damage**. An untrained or stressed orogene drawing power kills everything in their torus through cold; even a trained one, in an emergency, may have to pay this price to act in time. Children develop the ability spontaneously and frequently kill family members on first manifestation.

The second cost is **physical toll**. Sustained orogenic work exhausts the practitioner; large-scale work (stilling a continental fault, opening a rift) leaves them barely able to stand. Pushing past one's limits ruptures arteries or stops the heart.

The third cost is **obelisk corruption**: prolonged connection causes parts of the body to crystallize into a form of living stone, progressing irreversibly with continued use. The trilogy's most powerful workings are paid for in literal flesh.

The fourth cost is **social**, and is much of the trilogy's substance. Orogeny is hated and feared. An identified rogga in an ordinary community is killed; a Fulcrum-trained rogga lives under a Guardian's surveillance and is sterilized, bred, or killed at the institution's discretion. The system's mechanical effects cannot be separated from this enforcement structure.

A fifth, technical cost is **precision**. A rogga at any rank can break a city; quieting a city requires a higher rank than ruining one. Most apparent "destructive overreaches" by inexperienced orogenes are precision failures, not power excesses.

## Practitioner access

Orogeny is hereditary. The trait surfaces unpredictably, often in children of non-orogene parents, and once it manifests it cannot be suppressed without fatal consequence. Identified children are taken — by Guardians, by a rural lynch mob, or by the Fulcrum — and the survivors are channeled into one of two destinies: Fulcrum service, or quiet death.

Within the Fulcrum, training is rigorous, brutal, and supervised by Guardians whose own (separate) abilities suppress and contain orogenic action. A subset of senior orogenes are also briefly free of the Fulcrum's control — the ten-ringers and rogue practitioners who form much of the trilogy's principal cast.

## Cultural role

Orogeny is openly known to exist and openly persecuted: a "regulated, licensed" framework in the strictest possible sense. Imperial law treats roggas as state-owned weapons and infrastructure, deployed to maintain road and rail safety across the continent of the Stillness, and treats unregistered roggas as immediate threats. The **Stone Lore**, the world's accumulated knowledge of how to survive recurring civilization-ending Seasons, is the principal cultural memory in which orogeny is positioned as both a tool and a hazard. The trilogy's overall arc depends on the gap between what orogenes can do and what their society allows them to be.
