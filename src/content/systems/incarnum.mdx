## Mechanics

**Incarnum** is spiritual energy — the raw substance of souls across all of time (past, present, and unborn). Practitioners shape incarnum into **soulmelds**: semi-physical constructs of solidified soul-energy that bind to the practitioner's body and grant supernatural abilities.

Each soulmeld occupies a specific **body slot** (crown, brow, throat, shoulders, arms, hands, waist, feet) — the same slots used by conventional magic items. A soulmeld in the hands slot might manifest as spectral claws; one in the feet slot might appear as ghostly boots. This creates a direct trade-off: a soulmeld occupying a slot prevents wearing a magic item in that slot (and vice versa).

The power of soulmelds is modulated by **essentia** — a personal pool of soul-energy. The practitioner allocates essentia points across their active soulmelds at the beginning of each turn (reallocating freely). More essentia invested in a soulmeld increases its power: a soulmeld with 1 essentia might grant a +1 bonus, while the same soulmeld with 4 essentia grants a +4 bonus and additional abilities. The total essentia pool is limited, so enhancing one soulmeld weakens others — creating a dynamic resource-balancing game.

Soulmelds can be **bound to a chakra** — a deeper connection that locks the soulmeld into a specific body slot but unlocks more powerful effects. Chakra binds are limited by class and level, making them a progression mechanic.

Three base classes use incarnum: the **Incarnate** (an alignment-focused practitioner who shapes incarnum according to moral/ethical orientation), the **Totemist** (who shapes incarnum into animal-spirit forms, creating natural-weapon attacks and beast-like abilities), and the **Soulborn** (a martial-incarnum hybrid, a holy warrior who channels soul-energy into combat prowess).

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **essentia scarcity**. The essentia pool is small and must be divided across all active soulmelds. Maximizing one soulmeld means minimizing others — there is never enough essentia to fully power everything simultaneously.

The second cost is **slot competition**. Soulmelds compete with magic items for body slots. In a system where magic items are expected at higher levels, choosing a soulmeld over a cloak of resistance or gauntlets of ogre power is a meaningful sacrifice.

The third limitation is **soulmeld capacity**. The number of soulmelds a practitioner can shape simultaneously is limited by class level. At low levels, only a few soulmelds are available; the full complement comes online gradually.

## Practitioner access

Incarnum use requires an innate attunement to soul-energy — a sensitivity that manifests as the ability to perceive and shape the spiritual substrate. The three incarnum classes represent different traditions: Incarnates follow ethical-philosophical frameworks, Totemists connect to primal animal spirits, and Soulborn combine martial training with soul-channeling. Incarnum traditions are rare and culturally specific — most D&D settings do not prominently feature them.

## Cultural role

Incarnum occupies a marginal position in D&D's magical landscape. It is neither arcane (learned from books) nor divine (granted by gods) nor psionic (generated by the mind). It draws from the collective soul-reservoir of all existence, making it cosmologically significant but culturally obscure. In settings where incarnum is recognized, practitioners are often associated with ancestor worship, spiritual traditions, or philosophical schools concerned with the nature of the soul.
