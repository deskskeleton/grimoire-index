## Mechanics

Necromancy in the Nine Houses operates through the manipulation of two complementary energies: **thanergy** (the energy released by death) and **thalergy** (the energy of living things). All necromantic workings are fundamentally energy-conversion problems — a necromancer takes thanergy from dead matter or channels thalergy from living sources and converts it into effects. The system operates under conservation laws: energy spent on one working is unavailable for another, and large workings require proportionally large inputs.

Each of the Nine Houses has developed specialisations in different branches of necromancy, functioning as distinct scientific disciplines:

**Bone magic** (Ninth House specialty) involves the manipulation of skeletal matter — raising bone constructs, hardening bones into armour, animating skeletal servants. Harrowhark Nonagesimus can construct elaborate bone structures from fragments and animate them with precise control, using thanergy as the animating fuel.

**Spirit magic** involves the manipulation of ghosts, revenants, and the lingering energy patterns of the dead. The Eighth House specialises in spirit-calling and the binding or banishing of the restless dead.

**Flesh magic** (Third House) concerns the manipulation of living and dead flesh — healing, reshaping, and preserving biological matter. At its most advanced, it approaches true regeneration.

**Psychometric and analytical necromancy** (Sixth House) treats necromantic phenomena as data: reading death impressions, analysing thalergetic signatures, and constructing theoretical models of necromantic processes. Palamedes Sextus approaches necromancy as a research discipline.

**Soul magic** involves the direct manipulation of the soul itself — the most dangerous and least understood branch, and the foundation of Lyctorhood.

The **cavalier-necromancer pairing** is both social institution and magical mechanism. Every necromancer is partnered with a cavalier — a sword-wielding protector whose role extends beyond combat. The cavalier's living body serves as a thalergetic anchor and reserve for the necromancer, and the bond between them has genuine magical significance. The ultimate expression of this bond is **Lyctorhood**: a process discovered by John Gaius's original saints, in which the necromancer permanently absorbs the cavalier's soul, gaining immortality, vastly amplified power, and the ability to regenerate from almost any injury. The cavalier dies — fully, permanently — and their soul becomes fuel for their necromancer's eternal existence.

John Gaius himself — the God Emperor, the Necrolord Prime — accomplished something beyond even Lyctorhood ten thousand years ago: a planetary-scale act of necromancy that killed an entire world and used its thanergy to resurrect his dead companions, bootstrap the Nine Houses civilisation, and render himself functionally divine.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **life-force**. Necromancy draws on death energy, and when ambient thanergy is insufficient, the necromancer must generate it — by killing living things, by drawing on their own thalergy (which weakens and can kill them), or by drawing on their cavalier's reserves. The conservation law is strict: a necromancer who overdraws collapses, and sustained overuse causes organ failure.

**Lyctorhood's cost** is absolute and irreversible. The cavalier's soul is consumed — not temporarily borrowed, not held in stasis, but used as permanent fuel. The Lyctor gains effectively unlimited power and immortality, but at the price of another person's entire existence. The original Lyctors' guilt over this cost is a driving force of the series' plot, and the discovery that John Gaius's original Lyctoral process was flawed — that the cavalier's soul is not peacefully integrated but perpetually consumed — adds a layer of horror.

**Specialisation constrains generality.** A bone specialist cannot easily perform flesh magic, and vice versa. Cross-disciplinary necromancy requires either broad training (rare) or Lyctoral power (which grants access to all branches).

**Resurrection Beasts** represent the cosmic cost of John's original sin: each planet he killed in his rise to power generated a Resurrection Beast — a thanergetic horror of planetary scale that hunts him across the cosmos. The Nine Houses' entire civilisation exists in flight from these entities.

## Practitioner access

Necromantic ability is hereditary, concentrated in the ruling families of the Nine Houses. Each House's population is small, and necromancers are a minority even within them — many House members are cavaliers, servants, or non-practitioners. The strongest necromancers typically come from the oldest bloodlines, and the Houses practice deliberate breeding to concentrate ability (the Ninth House's two-hundred-child massacre, which Harrow's parents engineered to concentrate all the House's necromantic potential into a single child, is the most extreme example).

Training occurs within each House according to its specialisation. There is no central academy — each House's adepts learn their discipline from their elders. Cross-House training is rare, which reinforces specialisation and limits versatility.

## Cultural role

Necromancy is the Nine Houses' entire civilisation. There is no non-necromantic culture; every institution, social role, and power structure derives from necromantic practice. The God Emperor's authority rests on his status as the universe's most powerful necromancer. The cavalier-necromancer pairing structures everything from military organisation to romantic norms. The Houses' religious observances centre on death, bone, and the Necrolord's conquest of mortality. In this setting, necromancy is not transgressive or hidden — it is the air the civilisation breathes, and questioning its foundations is the truly radical act.
