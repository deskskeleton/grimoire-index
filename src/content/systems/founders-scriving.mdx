## Mechanics

Scriving works by inscribing sigils — characters from an ancient hierophantic language — onto an object in sequences that form logical arguments about the object's physical properties. A scrived cannonball might be inscribed with sigils that convince it that it is always falling toward its target; a scrived wheel might believe that it is perpetually rolling downhill regardless of the actual terrain. The object does not know it is being lied to — reality, at the local level, reshapes itself to match the argument.

The hierophantic language was not invented by humans. It predates human civilization and was originally used by the Makers, divine beings who used it to define the fundamental rules of reality. Humans discovered fragments of this language and reverse-engineered it into a practical technology. Modern scriveners work with an incomplete lexicon, building functional arguments from the symbols they have deciphered while aware that vast portions of the language remain unknown.

Scriving operates on strict logical principles. A poorly constructed argument will fail, produce unintended effects, or (in the worst cases) cause catastrophic physical paradoxes. Successful scriving requires understanding both the language itself and the physical properties being manipulated — a scrivener must know how gravity, momentum, or material stress actually work in order to construct a convincing false argument about them. This makes scriving simultaneously a linguistic art, an engineering discipline, and a branch of natural philosophy.

The most powerful scrived devices use "lexicons" — large stationary reference engines that provide a shared definition framework. Sigils on individual objects reference the lexicon for their base definitions, much as a software application references a shared library. This architecture means that scrived devices within a lexicon's range are more powerful and reliable than standalone devices, but also that destroying a lexicon disables every device that depends on it.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation is knowledge. The hierophantic language is incompletely understood, and expanding the known lexicon requires painstaking research, experimentation, and occasionally dangerous field work to recover ancient inscriptions. A scrivener can only build arguments using symbols they understand, and the most powerful effects require symbols that are rare, poorly documented, or entirely unknown.

Material constraints are the second limitation. Sigils must be physically inscribed on objects, which requires skilled labor and specialized tools. The complexity of an argument is limited by the physical surface available for inscription and by the precision of the scrivener's work — a misaligned sigil can invalidate an entire argument or produce a dangerous malfunction.

The lexicon dependency creates a structural vulnerability. Tevanne's merchant houses build their power on proprietary lexicons, but those lexicons are single points of failure. A targeted attack on a house's lexicon can disable its entire scrived infrastructure in an instant.

At the highest levels, scriving confronts a philosophical cost. The Makers used the complete language to define reality itself, and attempting to use Maker-level commands risks not just local failure but existential consequences — rewriting the rules of physics in ways that cannot be undone.

## Practitioner access

Scriving is a learned skill accessible to anyone with sufficient education and aptitude. The merchant houses of Tevanne train scriveners in proprietary academies, jealously guarding their accumulated knowledge and lexicon innovations. Independent scriveners exist but operate at a disadvantage, lacking access to the deep research libraries and powerful lexicons of the houses.

Sancia Grado represents an unusual case: through an experimental procedure, she gained the ability to intuitively sense scrived objects and their arguments, giving her a form of direct perception that trained scriveners lack. This ability is not natural and came at significant personal cost.

There is no innate magical talent required. Scriving is engineering — anyone who can learn the language and understand the physics can practice it. The barriers to entry are institutional (access to training and lexicons) rather than biological.

## Cultural role

Scriving has produced a full industrial civilization in Tevanne, with scrived devices performing the roles that steam engines, electricity, and computing fill in real-world industrialization. The merchant houses function as something between corporations and feudal powers, their economic and military dominance built entirely on their scriving monopolies. The social consequences mirror real industrialization: extreme wealth concentration, an exploited labor class, and the treatment of transformative knowledge as proprietary intellectual property rather than shared human heritage. Bennett uses the system to examine how technological revolutions create and entrench power imbalances, regardless of whether the technology is mechanical or magical.
