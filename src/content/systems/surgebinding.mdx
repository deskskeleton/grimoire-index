## Mechanics

Surgebinding is structured around three interlocking elements: the bond, the oaths, and the Surges themselves.

A practitioner — a "Surgebinder," typically called a Knight Radiant when fully active — forms a bond with a sapient spren, a sentient spirit corresponding to a specific human ideal or quality. There are ten orders of Radiants, each defined by its associated spren type: Windrunners bond honorspren, Lightweavers bond cryptics, Edgedancers bond cultivationspren, and so on. The bond is not granted; it is earned through the practitioner's behavior matching the spren's ideal closely enough that a bond becomes possible.

Each order grants access to two of ten universal Surges — fundamental forces underlying the world. Examples include Adhesion (the surface-attractive surge), Gravitation (manipulation of weight and falling direction), Transformation (changing one substance to another, used most famously for Soulcasting), Illumination (creation of light, sound, and illusion), Progression (accelerated growth and healing), and Cohesion (manipulation of solid form). Different orders share Surges with their numerical neighbors, producing an interconnected ten-by-ten structure.

The system is fueled by Stormlight, a usable form of Investiture deposited across Roshar by recurring superstorms. Practitioners inhale and hold Stormlight in spheres or gemstones, then expend it to power Surgebinding effects. Stormlight reserves are visible (the practitioner glows), depletable, and renewable only by passing through a highstorm with empty gemstones or by buying charged ones.

## Limitations and costs

The system has three layered costs. The first is resource: Stormlight is a tangible currency, and a Surgebinder out of Stormlight is largely powerless. The second is the oath structure: each order has a sequence of progressively demanding oaths that practitioners must sincerely swear (and live) to access higher tiers of power. Lying — to oneself or to the spren — does not work, and breaking an oath in spirit can sever the bond entirely, killing the spren and stripping the practitioner of their abilities.

The third cost is the spren themselves. Surgebinders carry around a sentient companion who watches their behavior, has opinions, and can withdraw. The practitioner is in a relationship, not just wielding a tool. Several characters in the series struggle with this directly.

## Practitioner access

Access requires both a willing spren and a candidate human whose temperament, history, and ideals align closely enough with that spren's order. The bond is not strictly inherited. It tends to form during moments of personal crisis — a candidate "attracts" a spren by demonstrating a particular kind of resolve in extremis. Once bonded, progression up the oath ladder is gated by character growth, not study.

## Cultural role

In the historical setting of the series, the Knights Radiant abandoned their oaths millennia ago, in an event called the Recreance, and the orders went functionally extinct. The active plot of *The Stormlight Archive* is in significant part the return of Surgebinding to the world. Within the books' contemporary present, a Surgebinder is initially treated as a mythological figure or a fraud, and only gradually accepted as a renewed political and military reality.
