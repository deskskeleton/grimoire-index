## Mechanics

Neuromods represent the convergence of alien biology and human neuroscience, functioning as injectable devices that physically restructure the brain to install new capabilities. Each Neuromod is manufactured from exotic material derived from the Typhon, an alien species whose biology operates on principles fundamentally different from terrestrial life. The installation process involves inserting a needle-like device through the eye socket directly into the brain, rewriting neural pathways to encode abilities that the recipient's body could never have developed naturally. The result is not magic in the traditional sense but something that blurs the boundary between technology and the supernatural, granting powers that defy conventional physics.

The ability tree splits into two fundamentally distinct tracks: Human and Typhon. Human Neuromods enhance existing biological capabilities to extraordinary degrees, improving strength, hacking proficiency, engineering skill, medical expertise, and combat reflexes. These abilities, while exceptional, remain within the conceptual bounds of human potential pushed to its theoretical maximum. Typhon Neuromods cross into genuinely alien territory, granting abilities reverse-engineered from the Typhon organisms themselves. These include Kinetic Blast, which projects concussive force; Electrostatic Burst, which generates electrical discharge; Superthermal, which creates thermal explosions; and the deeply unsettling Mimic Matter, which allows the user to shapeshift into nearby objects.

The Mimic Matter ability deserves special attention as one of the most creatively distinctive powers in gaming. Derived from the Mimic Typhon's shapeshifting biology, it allows Morgan Yu to transform into virtually any small object in the environment: a coffee cup, a banana, a roll of toilet paper. This is not merely cosmetic; the transformed state allows passage through small gaps, evasion of enemies, and creative problem-solving that the game's immersive sim design fully supports. The ability captures the unsettling essence of Typhon biology, which treats form as entirely mutable, and transplants it into the player character's repertoire.

Psychoshock represents the system's most direct anti-psychic weapon, capable of nullifying other Typhon abilities and dealing damage that bypasses conventional defenses. Mind Jack allows direct control of human NPCs and certain Typhon organisms. Phantom Genesis can reanimate corpses as allied Phantom entities. Each Typhon ability carries implications that extend far beyond its mechanical utility, raising questions about identity, humanity, and the ethical boundaries of power acquisition that the game explores through its branching narrative.

## Limitations and costs

The resource-expenditure cost of Neuromods is severe and multi-layered. Each Neuromod is a physical consumable that must be found, fabricated, or salvaged from limited supplies aboard Talos I. The raw materials required for fabrication, particularly the exotic Typhon material, are themselves scarce and dangerous to obtain. This creates a genuine resource economy where every ability purchase represents an opportunity cost: Neuromods spent on one ability are unavailable for others, and the total number accessible in a single playthrough is finite. Players must make consequential choices about their build path with incomplete information about what challenges lie ahead.

The moral-spiritual cost of Typhon Neuromods constitutes the system's most distinctive limitation. Installing alien neural architecture does not simply add capabilities; it fundamentally alters the user's cognitive makeup, gradually shifting their consciousness toward something less human. This transformation is not merely philosophical but mechanically consequential: Talos I's security systems, including turrets and Operator drones, are calibrated to detect Typhon neurology. As Morgan installs more Typhon Neuromods, these systems progressively reclassify them as a hostile alien entity, turning previously safe environments into combat zones. The station's defenses do not distinguish between a Typhon organism and a human who has absorbed too much Typhon material.

Physical toll manifests through the installation process itself and its neurological side effects. The eye-socket injection procedure is viscerally uncomfortable even in its sanitized corporate presentation, and the game hints at extensive testing casualties during Neuromod development. More insidiously, Neuromod removal erases all memories formed after installation, meaning that the technology is inherently tied to questions of identity continuity. Morgan's amnesia at the game's opening is not an accident but a direct consequence of Neuromod manipulation, revealing that the cost of these abilities extends to the integrity of the self.

The unpredictability risk escalates with deeper investment in Typhon abilities. The Typhon are not merely hostile aliens but something closer to a fundamental ontological threat: organisms that consume consciousness and exist partially outside normal physics. Integrating their biology into a human brain carries risks that TranStar's research only partially understood. The game's multiple endings hinge on whether Morgan's adoption of Typhon abilities has compromised their humanity beyond recovery, positioning the entire Neuromod system as a Faustian bargain where the true price remains unclear until the final moments.

## Practitioner access

Access to Neuromod technology is tightly controlled by TranStar, the megacorporation that operates Talos I and holds a monopoly on Typhon research. In the world of Prey, Neuromods are not available to the general public but are distributed through corporate channels to employees, researchers, and select clients. The technology exists behind layers of corporate secrecy, classified research protocols, and deliberately obscured information about its alien source material. Most of the Earth's population knows Neuromods only as a revolutionary self-improvement technology, unaware that its production depends on harvesting material from captive alien organisms.

Within Talos I, access follows a clear institutional hierarchy. Senior researchers and key personnel like Morgan and Alex Yu have extensive Neuromod privileges, allowing them to install multiple ability upgrades across both Human and Typhon tracks. Lower-ranking employees may receive limited Human Neuromods relevant to their job functions. Test subjects in the Psychotronics division occupy the most disturbing position in this hierarchy, serving as experimental platforms for Typhon Neuromod installation under conditions that prioritize data collection over subject welfare. The access structure mirrors corporate power dynamics with lethal precision.

The Typhon organisms themselves represent the original and most complete practitioners of the abilities that Neuromods attempt to replicate. Mimics, Phantoms, Telepaths, Technopaths, and the Nightmare Typhon each embody specific ability sets that human Neuromod users can only partially approximate. A Telepath's mind control, for instance, operates with a subtlety and range that Typhon Neuromods can approach but never fully match. This asymmetry underscores a central tension of the system: humans are borrowing power from a source they do not fully understand, using technology to bridge a gap that evolution never intended to cross.

Morgan Yu occupies a unique position as both a Neuromod researcher and its most extensively modified subject. Their access to both Human and Typhon ability tracks, combined with the narrative context of repeated memory wipes and reinstallations, makes them simultaneously the system's greatest success story and its most troubling case study. The player's choices about which Neuromods to install transform Morgan from a passive recipient of corporate technology into an active agent reshaping their own cognitive architecture, with consequences that ripple through the game's narrative and ultimately determine its resolution.

## Cultural role

The Neuromod system functions as a pointed critique of corporate technology culture and the commodification of human potential. TranStar markets Neuromods as the ultimate self-improvement product, promising customers the ability to learn any skill instantly, from playing piano to performing neurosurgery. This consumer-friendly facade conceals the reality that the product depends on alien biological material harvested under ethically catastrophic conditions. The parallel to real-world technology companies that obscure exploitative supply chains behind polished marketing is unmistakable and deliberate.

The masquerade surrounding Neuromod technology operates on multiple levels. The general public is unaware of the Typhon's existence, believing Neuromods to be a purely human technological achievement. Within TranStar, different clearance levels reveal different layers of truth: some employees know about the Typhon but not the full scope of the research program, while only the innermost circle understands the existential threat the Typhon represent. This layered secrecy structure mirrors the information asymmetries that characterize real corporate and governmental power, where those at the top control what others are allowed to know.

Questions of identity and humanity permeate every aspect of the Neuromod system's cultural presence within the game's narrative. If a person's memories can be erased by removing a Neuromod, are they the same person after reinstallation? If Typhon neural architecture changes how someone thinks and perceives, at what point do they cease to be human? These questions are not abstract philosophical exercises but lived realities for the characters aboard Talos I, particularly Morgan, whose entire sense of self has been repeatedly constructed and demolished by Neuromod technology. The system transforms transhumanist aspirations into a source of existential horror.

The game's final revelation recontextualizes the entire Neuromod system as an experiment in empathy and hybrid consciousness. The choices Morgan made throughout the game, whether to save or sacrifice, whether to embrace Typhon power or reject it, are ultimately evaluated not by their strategic effectiveness but by what they reveal about the possibility of coexistence between human and alien modes of being. The Neuromod system, initially presented as a tool for individual empowerment, becomes a bridge between species, suggesting that the technology's highest purpose may not be granting power but fostering understanding across an unbridgeable cognitive divide.
