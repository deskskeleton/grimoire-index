## Mechanics

Divine magic in Kingfisher's world operates through the relationship between active gods and their mortal agents. Each god grants powers aligned with their domain. The **White Rat** — god of small comforts, fair dealing, and practical mercy — empowers a temple of healers, lawyers, and administrators. White Rat priests can perform minor healing, detect lies in formal testimony, and invoke divine authority in legal proceedings. Their magic is unglamorous but deeply effective: the most powerful temple in the setting runs on bureaucratic competence backed by genuine divine sanction.

The **Dreaming God** grants prophetic visions to chosen followers, though these visions blur the line between insight and madness. Dreamers often struggle to distinguish prophecy from hallucination, and prolonged contact with the Dreaming God's gifts can erode a person's grip on waking reality. The **Forge God** empowers artificers and craftspeople, granting intuitive understanding of materials and mechanisms.

The **Saint of Steel** was a minor war god who granted his paladins a divine **battle-tide**: a berserker state of supernatural speed, strength, and combat awareness. While the god lived, the tide was controlled — it rose when needed and receded when the battle ended, guided by the god's will. When the Saint of Steel died (suddenly, without warning or explanation), his seven surviving paladins were left with the tide but no divine hand on the reins. The residual power surges unpredictably — triggered by threat, strong emotion, or sometimes nothing at all — and when it rises, the paladin becomes a killing machine with diminished capacity to distinguish friend from foe. Managing the tide is the central struggle of the paladin protagonists, who have developed breathing techniques, meditation practices, and mutual support systems to contain what was once a gift and is now a barely controlled affliction.

The **Clocktaur Wars** introduces additional magical elements: demon-possessed clockwork constructs (the clocktaurs themselves, war machines animated by bound demons), and **gnole magic**, an earth-and-fungal tradition practised by gnoles — badger-like creatures with deep connections to soil, mycelia, and underground ecosystems. Gnole magic is slow, patient, and organic, standing in sharp contrast to the dramatic divine powers of the temples.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost of divine magic is **dependency on the god**. When the Saint of Steel died, his paladins did not simply lose their powers — they were left with powers that no longer had a control mechanism. The battle-tide became an affliction rather than a gift, and several paladins were destroyed by it (one killed civilians during an uncontrolled episode; two were mercy-killed by their brothers). The survivors live with the constant knowledge that they are one bad moment away from becoming monsters.

White Rat magic is limited by its **institutional nature** — it works through the temple's structures and is strongest when exercised within the god's domain of fairness, mercy, and practical aid. A White Rat priest cannot call down divine wrath; they can detect a lie, heal a wound, or invoke legal protection. The god's power is real but deliberately modest in scope.

Prophetic gifts from the Dreaming God carry the cost of **psychological erosion**. Prolonged exposure to divine visions can make it difficult to maintain a stable sense of self, and the god's communications are cryptic, fragmented, and sometimes arrive too late to act upon.

Gnole magic requires **patience and organic materials** — it cannot be rushed, operates on ecological timescales, and is useless in situations demanding immediate action. It is powerful in its own domain but fundamentally incompatible with the urgency of combat or crisis.

## Practitioner access

Divine magic is not learned but **granted**. Gods choose their agents — paladins, saints, priests — through mechanisms that are theologically understood but not controllable by mortals. The Saint of Steel chose children with the right temperament for battle and bonded them to his service. The White Rat works through its institutional temple, granting minor abilities to trained priests and stronger gifts to those who demonstrate exceptional alignment with the god's values. The Dreaming God reaches out to individuals seemingly at random, often choosing people who are ill-equipped to handle prophetic gifts.

Gnole magic appears to be a cultural tradition passed through gnole communities, unrelated to the divine system. It is not available to humans and operates on entirely different principles — ecological rather than theological.

## Cultural role

The temples are major civic institutions, providing healthcare, legal services, and social infrastructure. The Temple of the White Rat functions as a combination hospital, law firm, and social services agency, making divine magic a practical part of daily life rather than a source of awe. The paladins of the dead Saint of Steel occupy an uncomfortable position — revered for their past service but feared for their uncontrolled powers, supported by the White Rat temple out of compassion but never fully trusted. Kingfisher uses this dynamic to explore how societies treat veterans, how institutions respond to broken people, and how faith persists (or transforms) when the object of devotion is gone.
