## Mechanics

**Hedge Magic** is the umbrella term for all non-Hermetic magical traditions in the Ars Magica setting. Where Hermetic Magic uses the universal Technique × Form matrix (allowing any combination of verb and noun), hedge traditions use narrower, specialized systems that are often more limited in scope but sometimes possess unique capabilities outside Hermetic reach.

Major traditions include:

**Folk Witches** — practitioners of a tradition combining herbalism, healing, flight (via broomstick or ointment), and shapeshifting. Their magic is less versatile than Hermetic but deeply embedded in rural communities, where they serve as healers, midwives, and advisors. **Vitkir** — Norse rune-casters who inscribe magical effects into runes carved on wood, bone, or stone. Their magic is powerful but slow (requiring physical inscription) and tied to Norse cultural and mythological frameworks. **Gruagachan** — a Gaelic tradition using face-paint and body decoration as the medium for magical effects, specializing in blessings, curses, and transformations. **Learned Magicians** — quasi-academic practitioners who use astrological charts, alchemical preparations, and scholarly methods to produce magical effects, operating closer to Hermetic methods but without the Technique × Form framework.

Each tradition has its own internal logic, advancement system, and supernatural abilities. The game mechanics give each tradition a set of **Supernatural Abilities** (rated skills) and **Methods** (how they apply those abilities) that define what they can and cannot do. A folk witch can fly and shapeshift (abilities outside standard Hermetic magic) but cannot throw fireballs or create magical items in the Hermetic sense.

**Mystery Cults** within the Order of Hermes also fall into this space — sub-traditions like House Merinita (Faerie Magic), House Bjornaer (heartbeast shapeshifting), and House Verditius (item-crafting focus) that extend Hermetic Magic with unique practices drawn from pre-Hermetic traditions or supernatural realms.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation is **narrow scope**. Each hedge tradition can do specific things well but lacks the universal flexibility of Hermetic Magic. A vitkir cannot improvise a spell on the fly (runes must be carved); a folk witch cannot affect things at great distance without special preparations. This specialization is both their weakness and their defining character.

The second cost is **institutional vulnerability**. Hedge practitioners lack the Order of Hermes' political protection. They are individually vulnerable to Hermetic magi who might view them as threats, resources, or curiosities — and the Order's Code of Hermes gives Hermetic magi legal authority over non-Hermetic practitioners in ways that the hedge wizards have no power to contest.

The third cost is **cultural specificity**. Each tradition is embedded in a specific cultural context — Norse, Gaelic, Mediterranean, Islamic — and transplanting a tradition outside its cultural environment weakens it. A vitkir in Italy is far from their sources of mythological power; a gruagach in Scandinavia is cut off from the Gaelic spirit world.

## Practitioner access

Each hedge tradition has its own methods of training and transmission. Folk witchcraft is passed from mother to daughter (or mentor to apprentice) in rural communities. Vitkir train in Norse cultural contexts. Gruagachan learn within Gaelic highland communities. Learned Magicians study in quasi-university settings. Access to any tradition requires finding a practitioner willing to teach and a cultural context that supports the tradition.

## Cultural role

Hedge Magic traditions are deeply embedded in the cultures they serve. Folk witches are the healers and wise women of rural Europe; vitkir are the magical practitioners of Norse society; gruagachan serve Gaelic highland communities. Their relationship to the Order of Hermes is the setting's primary source of political tension: the Order claims sovereignty over all magical practice in Europe, but hedge traditions predate the Order by centuries and resist incorporation. Some traditions have been absorbed (House Bjornaer was originally a non-Hermetic shapeshifting tradition); others resist, hide, or fight. This dynamic models real-world tensions between imperial knowledge systems and indigenous practices.
