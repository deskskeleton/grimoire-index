## Mechanics

Magic in the *Rivers of London* series operates through **formae** — specific mental shapes or patterns that a practitioner holds in their mind to channel magical energy into a defined effect. Each forma produces one result: a *lux* generates a small ball of light (a werelight), an *impello* delivers a kinetic push, a *scindere* cuts, and so on. The practitioner must learn each forma individually through sustained practice, much like learning to play a musical instrument, building up a repertoire of effects over time.

The energy for magic appears to come from the environment and from the practitioner themselves, though the precise source remains under investigation within the series. Peter Grant, the protagonist and narrator, approaches magic with a scientific and experimental mindset, attempting to measure, categorize, and reproduce magical effects under controlled conditions. This empirical attitude is presented as unusual — his mentor Nightingale learned through a more traditional apprenticeship model at a pre-war academy.

**Vestigia** are psychic impressions that accumulate on objects, places, and sometimes people as a result of strong emotions, significant events, or magical workings. Practitioners with sensitivity can read vestigia to gain information about what happened in a location or who handled an object. Peter has a particularly strong vestigium sense, which he often uses in his police work to reconstruct crime scenes.

The magic has measurable side effects: excessive or careless use causes **degradation** of brain tissue, a condition historically called *hyperthaumaturgical degradation* (and more colloquially, going "ethically challenged"). This degradation is cumulative and irreversible, providing a hard structural limit on reckless spellcasting.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost of practicing magic is the risk of brain damage. Hyperthaumaturgical degradation destroys neural tissue through overuse of formae, and the damage is permanent. Historical records at the Folly document practitioners who lost cognitive function, impulse control, and eventually sanity from excessive casting. This risk is managed through careful training and moderation, but it cannot be eliminated — every use of magic carries some degree of neurological wear.

Learning is itself a significant limitation. Each forma must be mastered individually through weeks or months of practice, and attempting a forma before achieving sufficient control risks unpredictable and dangerous results. Peter's training progresses slowly despite his talent, and the series makes clear that Nightingale's formidable abilities represent decades of accumulated skill. There are no shortcuts: raw talent without disciplined practice produces nothing useful.

The institutional infrastructure for training wizards was largely destroyed during the Second World War, when most of Britain's magical practitioners were killed. Nightingale is the sole surviving graduate of the old academy system, which means the accumulated pedagogical knowledge of centuries is filtered through a single person's memory and teaching ability.

## Practitioner access

Magical talent appears to be relatively rare but not strictly hereditary. The old system identified and recruited promising candidates into the academy, but with the academy destroyed, the process of finding and training new practitioners has effectively halted. Peter Grant is Nightingale's first apprentice in over fifty years. The series suggests that there may be untrained practitioners scattered through the population, producing minor and uncontrolled magical effects without understanding what they are doing.

The Folly's institutional position within the Metropolitan Police gives its practitioners a unique legal and professional standing — they are warranted police officers with authority to investigate supernatural crimes, a role that has no equivalent elsewhere in British law enforcement.

## Cultural role

Magic is hidden from the general public through a combination of institutional secrecy, public incredulity, and the relatively small scale of most magical effects. The Metropolitan Police maintains the Folly as a specialist unit, but most officers are unaware of its true purpose. The broader British government has some awareness of the supernatural — enough to maintain the Folly's funding and authority — but active knowledge is confined to a very small circle.

The supernatural community itself is structured around territorial claims: river gods and goddesses control stretches of waterway, genius loci inhabit specific places, and various other beings negotiate boundaries and obligations. The Folly acts as a mediating authority between these powers and the mundane world, a role that is part diplomatic, part policing, and part crisis management.
