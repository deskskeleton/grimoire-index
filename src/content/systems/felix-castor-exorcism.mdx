## Mechanics

Exorcism in the Felix Castor series operates on a deceptively simple principle: every ghost has a unique **tune** — a personal psychic frequency that defines its attachment to the physical world — and an exorcist who can find and play that tune can compel the ghost to depart. The process is intuitive rather than formulaic. Castor perceives ghosts as a kind of psychic presence, and through a combination of sensitivity, concentration, and trial-and-error, he searches for the right sequence of notes. When he finds it, he plays it on his **tin whistle**, and the ghost is drawn away — dissolved, banished, or simply unmoored from whatever anchored it to a particular place or person.

The tune is not a spell or incantation in any traditional sense. It has no fixed relationship to the ghost's identity — a particular melody does not correspond to a particular name, sin, or unfinished business. It is closer to a resonance frequency: the right combination of sounds vibrates at the exact pitch of the ghost's connection to the world and shakes it loose. Different exorcists use different instruments and methods. Some use spoken prayers or chanting. One character uses a church organ. Another uses dance and physical movement. The instrument is a personal conduit, not a universal requirement — what matters is the practitioner's ability to perceive and reproduce the tune.

The world contains three categories of the returned dead. **Ghosts** are disembodied spirits, the most common manifestation, ranging from faint presences barely perceptible to the sensitive to fully visible, interactive apparitions capable of affecting the physical environment (poltergeist activity). **Zombies** are spirits that have returned to and reanimated their own corpses — they retain their personalities and identities but inhabit decaying bodies. **Demons** (or **were-things**, as loup-garous are sometimes called) are something else entirely: entities of unknown origin that possess living hosts, fundamentally altering them. Loup-garous — humans possessed by animal spirits — transform physically, becoming something between human and beast.

The cause of the rising is the series' great unanswered question. Roughly a decade before the first novel, the dead began returning in numbers far exceeding any historical precedent. No one — not the church, not the scientific establishment, not the exorcists themselves — has a definitive explanation. Theories abound (thinning of a barrier between worlds, a spiritual ecosystem change, divine or demonic intervention), but Carey deliberately refuses to resolve the mystery, treating it as an ambient condition rather than a solvable problem.

## Limitations and costs

Finding a ghost's tune requires sustained **concentration** and psychic sensitivity, and it is not guaranteed to work quickly. A simple haunting might take minutes; a powerful or deeply anchored ghost might resist for much longer, and some ghosts are tenacious enough that an exorcist can fail entirely. The process is also **physically taxing** — extended exorcism sessions leave Castor exhausted, and the psychic exposure carries a cumulative toll on mental health and emotional stability.

The most significant cost is **moral**. Exorcism banishes ghosts — it does not send them to a better place or resolve their unfinished business. Castor does not know where the ghosts go when he plays them away, and the series treats this uncertainty as genuinely troubling. Some ghosts are dangerous or malicious, but many are simply lost, confused, or frightened people who happen to be dead. The ethics of making them disappear for a fee — particularly when the client is a property developer who wants a ghost-infested building cleared for renovation — are a persistent theme.

Demons present a qualitatively different challenge. They cannot be exorcised by the same methods as ghosts — they are not dead humans returning but entities of a different nature entirely. Castor's attempts to deal with demons using his standard toolkit are consistently inadequate, and the series treats demonic possession as a category error that exposes the limits of his craft.

## Practitioner access

Exorcism is a **knack** — an innate psychic sensitivity that some people are born with and most are not. It cannot be learned by someone who lacks the basic perceptual ability to sense the dead. However, the knack alone is insufficient; it must be developed through practice, and the specific method of expression (instrument, prayer, movement) is arrived at through individual experimentation. There is no formal training institution, no guild, no licensing body. Exorcists learn by doing, often stumbling into the profession after discovering their sensitivity by accident.

The demographic of exorcists is consequently eclectic and disorganized. Castor is a freelancer who advertises in the Yellow Pages. Other practitioners work through churches, operate private consultancies, or are employed by institutions that need ghosts managed. The lack of professional structure means there is no quality control, no ethical oversight, and no collective bargaining — exorcism occupies the same economic niche as unlicensed contracting, with all the precarity and corner-cutting that implies.

## Cultural role

The series treats the return of the dead as a mundane urban problem rather than a cosmic event. Ghosts are discussed in the same register as vermin infestations or antisocial behaviour — a nuisance to be managed, a property value concern, a public health issue. The church offers theological interpretations but no practical solutions. The government studies the phenomenon without reaching conclusions. Exorcists fill the gap as unregulated service providers, and the social status of the profession reflects its unglamorous reality: it is blue-collar work, paid by the job, performed by people who are often one step from financial precarity.

This deliberate de-mystification serves Carey's interest in class and labor. Castor's London is a city where the supernatural is real but unsurprising, where ghosts are as much a part of the urban landscape as traffic or pollution, and where the people who deal with them are working stiffs rather than chosen heroes. The refusal to explain the rising's cause reinforces this — in a world that cannot understand why the dead are returning, pragmatic management is the only available response, and the ethical questions that management raises go largely unaddressed by a society that prefers not to think about them.
