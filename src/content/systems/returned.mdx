## Mechanics

The **Returned** are individuals on the Cosmere world of Nalthis who died and were sent back to life by the Shard **Endowment**. Each Returned is resurrected with a single, enormously powerful **Divine Breath** — a quantum of Investiture far more potent than the ordinary Breath every Nalthian is born with. This Divine Breath transforms the Returned into an idealized physical form: tall, beautiful, ageless, and visibly radiant with an aura that shifts the colors of nearby objects.

The Returned possess several passive abilities tied to their Divine Breath. They are functionally immortal (they do not age), supernaturally attractive, and resistant to disease and poison. They experience prophetic dreams — fragmentary, symbolic visions that are poorly understood even by the Returned themselves but are treated by Hallandren's priesthood as divine revelation. A Returned can also, once, give up their Divine Breath to heal a single person of any ailment, including mortal wounds — but doing so kills the Returned permanently.

The Returned do not remember their previous lives. They awaken with no personal memories, only vague impressions and their new divine identity (a name and title assigned by the priesthood). This amnesia is a structural feature of the system, not an accident — it prevents the Returned from simply resuming their old lives and forces them into the institutional framework of the Court of Gods.

## Limitations and costs

The defining cost is **Breath consumption**. A Returned must consume one Breath per week or die. This Breath is provided by voluntary sacrifice from Hallandren's citizenry — each week, one person gives up their Breath (and with it, a degree of vitality and color perception) to sustain one god. The moral mathematics of this arrangement is a central theme of *Warbreaker*: each Returned god costs the community fifty-two Breaths per year, permanently diminishing fifty-two people.

The second cost is **amnesia**. The Returned cannot remember why they were sent back. Endowment presumably chose them for a reason, but neither the Returned nor their priests know what it is. This produces a class of gods who are genuinely invested with divine power but have no access to divine purpose — they must infer their mission from fragmentary dreams and priestly interpretation.

The third cost is **one-shot healing**. A Returned can heal one person, once, by sacrificing their Divine Breath. This is treated as the Returned's ultimate purpose — the reason they were sent back — but using it ends them. A Returned who never finds the right person to heal may live indefinitely, consuming Breaths, without ever fulfilling their purpose.

## Practitioner access

No one chooses to become Returned. Endowment selects individuals at or near death and sends them back. The selection criteria are unknown to the characters in the novel; Sanderson has confirmed in external commentary that Endowment selects those who died in a moment of particular bravery or selflessness. The Returned population in Hallandren at any given time numbers a few dozen.

## Cultural role

In Hallandren, the Returned are worshipped as living gods and housed in the **Court of Gods**, an opulent palace complex where they are attended by priests, servants, and petitioners. They serve as the ruling council of the nation, voting on matters of state — though their actual political influence varies (some are puppet figures, others are shrewd operators). The weekly Breath sacrifice that sustains them is a voluntary civic-religious obligation, culturally normalized but economically significant. The rival nation of Idris regards the Returned as abominations — fraudulent gods sustained by an exploitative Breath economy — and this theological disagreement is the political axis of *Warbreaker*.
