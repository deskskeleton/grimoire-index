## Mechanics

The wizarding world's primary magic is wandwork. A witch or wizard channels innate magical capacity through a wand — a wood-and-magical-core implement, paired to its owner with the framing that "the wand chooses the wizard." Spellcasting typically requires three components: a verbal incantation (usually a Latinate or pseudo-Latinate phrase), a wand-motion (described as precise but rarely depicted in detail), and the user's intent. All three matter. A spell with the right words and motion but wrong intent will misfire or fail; a spell with intent and motion but wrong words may produce a different effect.

Spells span an enormous functional range. Defensive (Protego), offensive (Stupefy, Expelliarmus, the three Unforgivable Curses), transformative (Transfiguration), illusory and informational (charms, divination magic), healing (limited, with explicit boundaries — most healing requires either potions or a specialist), spatial (Apparition, Disapparition, Side-Along Apparition), and many minor utility spells (Lumos, Alohomora, Wingardium Leviosa). The Hogwarts curriculum organizes these into named subjects: Transfiguration, Charms, Defense Against the Dark Arts, Potions, Herbology, History of Magic, Astronomy, plus electives (Ancient Runes, Arithmancy, Divination, Care of Magical Creatures, Muggle Studies).

Wandwork is the dominant tradition, but adjacent magical disciplines exist within the same world: potion-brewing (a non-wand discipline with its own school subject), magical-creature handling, broom-flying, animagus transformation (a learned ability to transform into a single specific animal), and patronus-casting (an emotional-channeling defensive discipline against Dementors). These are presented as overlapping rather than fundamentally separate systems.

Some spells are formally restricted. The three Unforgivable Curses — Avada Kedavra (instant death), Crucio (sustained pain), Imperio (mind control) — are illegal under wizarding law, with use of any of the three on another human carrying a life sentence in Azkaban.

Wandless and wordless casting are presented as advanced techniques. Nonverbal casting is taught from sixth year at Hogwarts. Wandless casting is rare, typically the province of exceptionally powerful or specifically gifted witches and wizards (Dumbledore, Voldemort), and sometimes manifests in young children before they're trained.

## Limitations and costs

The first cost is preparation. Spells are learned, practiced, and graded. Hogwarts is a seven-year boarding school whose curriculum is organized around exactly this. Untaught children produce magic only sporadically, often emotionally — accidental magic — and require formal instruction to develop reliable control.

The second cost is concentration. Spells require focus, and a distracted or panicked caster casts poorly. Several plot points in the books turn on a character's ability or inability to keep concentration under combat conditions.

The third cost is institutional and social. The Ministry of Magic regulates magic use — underage magic, magic in front of Muggles, illegal spells, and unregistered magical creatures are all subject to enforcement. The International Statute of Secrecy is the underlying constraint structuring magical society's relationship with the non-magical world.

The fourth cost is wand-dependent. A witch or wizard without their wand is significantly weaker — not entirely powerless (wandless casting exists, accidental magic exists, magical creatures retain their nature) but operationally hampered. Wand-loss, wand-theft, and wand-allegiance shifts are recurring plot drivers.

A fifth cost worth flagging is moral. The Dark Arts — a category that overlaps with but is broader than the three Unforgivable Curses — are presented as carrying a corrupting effect on their users. Heavy reliance on Dark Arts is associated in the books with progressive moral and physical degradation; Voldemort's Horcrux-induced disfigurement is the canonical extreme.

## Practitioner access

Magical capacity is hereditary but inconsistently expressed. Children of two magical parents are usually but not always magical (Squibs are an exception); children of one magical and one non-magical parent (Half-bloods) are typically magical; children of two non-magical parents are sometimes magical (Muggle-borns), at rates the books treat as somewhat mysterious. Hogwarts admissions identify magical children automatically through a magical Quill and Book.

Once admitted, training is institutional. The seven-year curriculum at Hogwarts (or its international counterparts: Beauxbatons, Durmstrang, Ilvermorny, Castelobruxo, Mahoutokoro, Uagadou) is the standard path. Self-taught magic exists but is rare and limited.

## Cultural role

The wizarding world maintains the International Statute of Secrecy — a binding masquerade — concealing magical existence from non-magical populations. Magical society has its own government (the Ministry of Magic in Britain, with international counterparts), its own commercial districts (Diagon Alley), its own banks (Gringotts), its own sport (Quidditch), its own newspapers (The Daily Prophet), and its own enforcement bodies (the Auror Office). The books' political plot turns on factional divisions within wizarding society — pure-blood supremacy, Muggle-born status, the role of magical creatures and house-elves — that the magic system both reflects and helps create.
