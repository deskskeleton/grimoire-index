## Mechanics

The **qhal Gates** are ancient constructions — arched portals of otherworldly stone — built by the qhal, a long-lived and powerful elder race. Each Gate connects to others across both space and time, allowing instantaneous travel between worlds and eras. The Gates operate through principles never fully explained to the reader; they are technology so advanced it reads as magic to the feudal cultures that live in their shadow. A Gate can be activated by anyone with the knowledge, but controlling where and when it opens requires mastery that few possess.

**Changeling**, Morgaine's sword, is a miniature Gate built into a blade. Anything struck by Changeling is consumed — drawn into the Gate-void and annihilated. It does not cut so much as erase, making it one of the most terrifying weapons in fantasy literature. Changeling can also be used to manipulate Gate-force at a distance, creating localized spatial distortions.

The critical problem with the Gates is **temporal corruption**. When a Gate is used to travel backward in time and the traveller alters events, the resulting paradox does not resolve cleanly — it damages the fabric of reality itself. Over millennia of qhal use, this corruption has compounded across multiple worlds, creating zones of warped time, duplicated individuals, and causality loops. Morgaine's mission, inherited from a destroyed company of Gate-closers, is to pass through each world's Gate and seal it behind her, preventing further damage at the cost of never being able to return.

**Shaping**, the wizardry of the Fortress series, operates on a different but thematically parallel principle. A Shaper imposes their will on reality through acts of naming and definition. To Shape something is to declare what it is, and reality conforms to that declaration. Mauryl Shaped Tristen — literally created him as a being — by defining his nature through wizardly will. Tristen does not remember being born because he was not born; he was Shaped into existence at Ynefel, and his gradual discovery of his own nature and memories forms the emotional core of the series.

## Limitations and costs

The Gates' primary limitation is their own danger. Every use of a Gate risks further temporal corruption, and the cumulative damage is already severe enough to threaten the existence of multiple worlds. Morgaine's mission is explicitly one of **destruction** — she is not learning to use the Gates better but rather eliminating them entirely. The ethical cost is enormous: closing a Gate strands populations, destroys trade routes, and sometimes condemns entire civilizations that depend on Gate-travel for survival.

Changeling's cost is more personal. The sword consumes indiscriminately — Morgaine must exercise extreme care in combat to avoid accidentally erasing allies, bystanders, or structural elements whose destruction would cause collateral harm. The weapon cannot be sheathed safely; it is always hungry.

Shaping's costs are subtler but equally severe. A Shaping that defines reality too rigidly becomes brittle — it resists change and eventually shatters. Hasufin Heltain, the Fortress series' antagonist, is a wizard whose Shapings have become increasingly desperate and unstable as he fights to maintain his existence across multiple deaths. Tristen's own nature as a Shaped being means he exists in a state of ontological uncertainty: he is what Mauryl declared him to be, but Mauryl is dead, and Tristen must decide for himself what that declaration means.

The deeper cost in both systems is **the corruption of the wielder**. Gate-lords who used the Gates for power became tyrants; wizards who Shaped too freely became monsters. Both Morgaine and Tristen are defined by their refusal to follow this pattern — and by the constant, grinding effort that refusal requires.

## Practitioner access

Both systems are restricted to a vanishingly small number of practitioners. The qhal Gates can theoretically be operated by anyone with knowledge, but the knowledge itself is nearly extinct — Morgaine may be the last living being who understands Gate mechanics fully. Vanye, her oath-bound companion, can activate Gates under her direction but does not truly understand them.

Shaping is rarer still. In the Fortress series, true wizards (as distinct from mere sorcerers who work lesser magics) are singular figures: Mauryl, Hasufin, Emuin, and Tristen himself. The ability appears to be innate but is so rare that each wizard is effectively unique. Tristen occupies an even stranger category — he is both practitioner and product, a Shaped being who can Shape in turn.

## Cultural role

In the Morgaine Cycle, the Gates are objects of fear and reverence. The cultures living near active Gates treat them as holy sites, cursed ground, or sources of political power depending on their history with Gate-lords. Morgaine herself is regarded as a figure of dread — a pale, foreign woman carrying an annihilating sword who appears from nowhere, closes the Gate, and moves on, leaving disrupted societies in her wake.

In the Fortress series, wizardry is remembered more than practised. The great wizards are historical figures, and the world's current political structures were shaped (in both senses) by their past actions. Tristen's emergence forces the kingdom of Ylesuin to confront the reality that wizardry is not safely in the past — and that the neat categories of "wizard" and "ordinary" may not hold.
