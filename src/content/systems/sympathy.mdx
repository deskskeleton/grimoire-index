## Mechanics

Sympathy is taught at the University as one of several formal disciplines and is presented as a structured, almost engineering-style practice. The core technique is the sympathetic binding: a sympathist establishes a mental link between two objects on the basis of perceived similarity, then channels force, heat, or motion between them. A binding between two pieces of an iron ingot might let a sympathist move one piece by moving the other; a binding between a candle flame and an enemy's body might let the sympathist transfer heat. The bindings are not interchangeable — a stronger likeness produces a more efficient transfer, and the loss factor is real and tracked.

Maintaining a binding requires the Alar — a sustained mental conviction that the two objects are, in the relevant sense, the same thing. The Alar must be held throughout the binding. Distraction breaks the link. Skilled sympathists train the ability to hold multiple Alars simultaneously, treating mental discipline as a measurable, rankable skill.

Energy must come from somewhere. Common sources include a fire, a battery (a "sympathy lamp" stores heat for later use), or — at significant personal risk — the sympathist's own body. Binding without external fuel is possible but draws warmth from the practitioner's flesh, producing a physiological cooling that can become medically dangerous quickly.

## Limitations and costs

The headline cost is thermodynamic. Energy moves between the bound objects at a fixed efficiency determined by the strength of the binding, and any work done must be paid for. Sympathists who try to overdraw their fuel source — especially their own bodies — risk hypothermia, organ damage, and death. The series treats this as a real and recurring danger.

The second cost is preparation. Sympathy is a learned discipline. Practitioners begin as students at the University; the named masters are senior academics, not chosen vessels. The system rewards ingenuity and engineering thought (improvising a binding from available materials) but punishes carelessness.

The third cost is concentration. Binding effects fail if the Alar lapses. A sympathist in real combat has to manage attention as a strategic resource, not just power.

## Practitioner access

Sympathy is open in principle to any student who can hold an Alar and is admitted to the University. It is gated by aptitude (most people cannot reliably hold an Alar at all) and by institutional access. The University is selective and expensive; sympathy outside its walls exists but is amateur and often dangerous.

Within the world, sympathists are recognized professionals — a working sympathist might be employed as a moneylender's enforcer, a ship's safety officer, or a freelance problem-solver. The University itself gates and credentials practitioners through a master-apprentice ranking.

## Cultural role

The Four Corners treats sympathy as a real and somewhat feared discipline. Many people regard sympathists with suspicion ranging from religious anxiety to functional respect, and rural areas may not draw a clear distinction between sympathy and witchcraft. Within educated and urban settings, sympathy is understood as a technical field whose practitioners can be hired, trained, or — in extremis — prosecuted. Two other University disciplines, Sygaldry (rune-craft) and Alchemy, occupy adjacent niches in the same intellectual tradition; Naming is older and more exotic.
