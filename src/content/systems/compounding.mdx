## Mechanics

**Compounding** is not a standalone magic system but a hybrid exploit that emerges when a single practitioner has both **Allomantic** and **Feruchemical** access to the same metal. The technique works as follows:

1. The practitioner stores an attribute in a **metalmind** using Feruchemy (e.g., storing health in a gold metalmind).
2. The practitioner then **swallows and burns** that charged metalmind using Allomancy.
3. Instead of receiving the normal Allomantic power of the metal, the practitioner receives a vastly amplified return of the Feruchemically stored attribute — orders of magnitude more than was originally stored.
4. The excess can be stored back into metalminds for later use, creating a feedback loop of effectively unlimited attribute supply.

The result is that a Compounder has access to a practically infinite supply of whatever attribute their shared metal governs. A gold Compounder (gold stores health in Feruchemy) has functionally unlimited healing. An atium Compounder (atium stores youth in Feruchemy) can reverse aging indefinitely. A steel Compounder has access to near-unlimited speed.

The **Lord Ruler** — the primary antagonist of the original *Mistborn* trilogy — was a full Mistborn (all Allomantic metals) and a full Feruchemist (all Feruchemical metals), meaning he could Compound every metal simultaneously. This is the in-universe explanation for his thousand-year reign, his apparent immortality, and his overwhelming power.

## Limitations and costs

Compounding requires **dual access** — both Allomantic and Feruchemical ability for the same metal. In the original Mistborn era, this combination was vanishingly rare: Allomancy was concentrated in the nobility and Feruchemy in the Terris people, and the Lord Ruler deliberately prevented interbreeding between the two populations to prevent rivals from arising. By Era 2, intermarriage has produced a few Compounders, but they are still exceptional.

The second cost is **metal consumption**. Compounding still requires ingesting and burning metal. The practitioner needs a supply of their Compounding metal and must manage their reserves.

The third cost is **psychological dependency**. Characters who Compound healing or youth describe a growing dependence on the enhanced state. Miles Hundredlives (a gold Compounder in Era 2) is effectively addicted to his regenerative ability, and his willingness to take extreme risks is partly a product of knowing he can heal from almost anything.

The technique also requires knowledge. Compounding is not intuitive — a practitioner with dual access might never discover they can do it without understanding the interaction between the two systems.

## Practitioner access

Compounding requires the genetic lottery of inheriting both Allomantic and Feruchemical powers for the same metal. In Era 1, only the Lord Ruler (who used Hemalurgy to supplement his natural abilities) achieved full Compounding. In Era 2, natural Compounders exist but are rare, typically possessing the combination for only one metal.

## Cultural role

Compounding is not a culturally recognized institution — it is a rare individual exploit. In Era 1, the Lord Ruler was the only known Compounder, and his power was treated as divine rather than understood mechanically. In Era 2, Compounders like Miles are known to law enforcement and treated as extreme threats. The broader cultural awareness of Compounding is limited; it is an esoteric detail of Metallic Arts theory rather than a publicly understood phenomenon.
