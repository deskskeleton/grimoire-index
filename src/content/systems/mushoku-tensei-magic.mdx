## Mechanics

Magic in the Six-Faced World operates on a fundamental mechanic: mana exists as an ambient environmental substance, practitioners draw it into their bodies, and they shape it into specific effects through incantations — verbal formulas that function as mental scaffolding for the desired outcome. The incantation system is structured, taxonomized, and taught in formal academies, producing a legible hierarchy of magical power that the world's institutions recognize and reward.

Seven schools of magic form the standard curriculum: Water, Fire, Earth, Wind (the four attack-oriented elemental schools), Healing, Detoxification, and Divine (the three support-oriented schools). Each school is internally ranked from Beginner through Intermediate, Advanced, Saint, King, Emperor, and God tiers. A Water Saint-ranked spell is categorically more powerful and difficult than a Water Advanced-ranked spell, and a mage's rank in each school is a recognized professional credential. Roxy Migurdia holds Water King rank; Rudeus achieves comparable ranks across multiple schools, which is itself unusual and marks him as exceptional.

The incantation system works because the verbal formula structures the caster's mental image of the desired effect. A fireball incantation does not command fire into existence through mystical language — it provides a framework that helps the caster's mind shape ambient mana into the correct pattern. This distinction matters because it explains the system's most important variant: chantless casting. A practitioner who can hold the complete mental image of a spell without the verbal scaffolding can cast silently and instantaneously, eliminating the casting time that incantations require.

Chantless casting is rare and marks a qualitative divide in combat effectiveness. An incantation-dependent mage requires several seconds to cast a high-tier spell — seconds during which they are vulnerable. A chantless caster produces the same effect with no warning. Rudeus casts chantlessly across multiple schools because he began training as an infant, before fully developing language, and his mind learned to form spell-images directly. Sylphiette also casts chantlessly, having learned the technique from Rudeus in childhood.

Mana capacity is the system's second major variable. Every person possesses a mana pool — a finite internal reservoir that regenerates over time. The size of this pool is partly innate and partly determined by early training: mana capacity grows most rapidly in childhood and becomes fixed in adolescence, meaning that a child who trains intensively will have a permanently larger mana pool than one who starts late. Rudeus's enormous mana capacity is a direct result of his reincarnation advantage — he began practicing magic as a toddler, compounding years of growth that no native-born child could replicate without the same early start.

Original spell creation sits above the ranked curriculum. Mages who understand underlying principles can design new spells by constructing novel mental images. Rudeus creates several original spells by combining isekai knowledge of real-world physics with the Six-Faced World's magical framework — his stone cannon spell uses earth magic to accelerate projectiles at speeds the conventional curriculum does not contemplate.

Touki — battle aura — is the parallel system for physical fighters. Warriors channel mana passively through their bodies, reinforcing muscle, bone, and skin to superhuman levels. The strongest Touki fighters — Sword God style practitioners — can match or exceed mages in direct combat through sheer physical supremacy. In practice most individuals specialize in spellcasting or Touki; Rudeus is unusual for supplementing his magic with physical combat training.

At the top of the hierarchy sit entities whose abilities exceed the ranked system entirely. The Dragon God Orsted wields techniques predating the current framework, and the Teleport Incident — which scattered thousands across the continent — operates at a level no living human mage can replicate. These god-tier effects establish that the ranked system describes only the portion of magical reality that mortal institutions can measure.

## Limitations and costs

Mana depletion is the system's primary hard constraint. Every spell draws from the caster's internal reservoir, and when that reservoir empties, the caster cannot cast until it regenerates. Regeneration is slow — hours or days for a significant recovery — meaning that a mage who exhausts their pool in a single battle is functionally defenseless for the remainder. High-tier spells consume proportionally more mana, and a mage who casts at Emperor or God rank may drain their entire pool in one or two effects.

The capacity ceiling, once set in adolescence, is permanent. An adult who neglected training in childhood will never match the mana pool of one who trained from infancy. This produces structural inequality: nobles and mage families who invest in early childhood training produce practitioners with permanently larger reservoirs, while commoners who begin later are capped at lower ceilings.

Incantation dependency creates tactical vulnerability. A mage who must chant for three seconds before casting a defensive barrier will die to a Sword God practitioner who closes the distance in one. The ranked system's emphasis on verbal formulas means that the majority of trained mages are vulnerable to fast opponents, ambushes, and situations where speaking is impossible. Silence, surprise, and speed all counter conventional magic effectively.

Physical toll accompanies extreme casting. Mana exhaustion manifests as nausea, disorientation, and eventually unconsciousness. Prolonged overcasting damages the body directly, and magic at the highest tiers strains both body and mind.

Knowledge prerequisites scale with spell tier. Higher-ranked spells require more precise mental images, more complex incantations, and deeper understanding of underlying principles. A mage cannot attempt a King-tier spell through willpower alone — they must master the conceptual framework first. The vast majority of mages plateau at Intermediate or Advanced rank because they lack either the instruction or the comprehension to advance further.

## Practitioner access

Magic is accessible to the general population. Most people in the Six-Faced World can learn basic spells — lighting a fire, purifying water, healing minor wounds — and rudimentary magic use is a common part of daily life in many regions. The Ranoa Magic University serves as the setting's premier institution for formal magical education, accepting students from across the continent and providing structured curriculum through the ranked system.

The distribution of talent is uneven. The Migurd demon race produces disproportionately skilled water mages, certain human lineages carry above-average mana capacity, and individual variation means some students never advance past Beginner rank while others reach King rank with dedicated study. Rudeus is an extreme outlier — his combination of isekai knowledge, infant-onset training, and naturally large mana pool places him outside the normal distribution entirely.

Touki access follows a different path. Battle aura training is primarily the domain of the three great sword schools — Sword God (offensive speed), Water God (defensive counter-technique), and North God (adaptability and improvisation). Entry requires finding a teacher and surviving physically demanding training.

The god-tier practitioners — Orsted, Laplace, the Seven Great Powers — represent a category of access that no institutional pathway provides. Their abilities derive from divine heritage, centuries of accumulated power, or knowledge from previous iterations of the world's timeline. The ranked system does not scale to their level.

## Cultural role

Magic is woven into the Six-Faced World's economic and social fabric. Healers are essential infrastructure in every settlement. Water mages purify drinking supplies. Earth mages construct buildings and fortifications. Fire mages provide industrial heat. The practical applications of magic at Beginner through Advanced rank sustain the setting's pre-industrial civilization, and the ranked system provides a legible credentialing framework that employers, governments, and military organizations use to assess personnel.

The Ranoa Magic University functions as both educational institution and political entity — a neutral ground where students from hostile nations study alongside one another. The university's neutrality is a deliberate political arrangement: magic is too valuable for any single nation to monopolize.

The Teleport Incident shatters this stability. When a magical catastrophe scatters the population of the Fittoa region across the continent, it demonstrates that magic can produce disasters beyond institutional control. The aftermath — refugees, political recrimination, collapsed infrastructure — reframes the setting's relationship with magic from comfortable utility to existential risk. Rudeus's personal quest to find his scattered family members is also the story of a world reckoning with the fact that its foundational technology can fail catastrophically.

The Seven Great Powers ranking — a world-acknowledged list of the seven strongest individuals — structures geopolitics around personal combat supremacy. Nations defer to Great Powers not for institutional authority but for individual capacity for destruction. Magic in the Six-Faced World is simultaneously a public utility, an academic discipline, a professional credential, and a weapon of mass destruction — and the tension between these roles is the setting's central political question.
