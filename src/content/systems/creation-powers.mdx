## Mechanics

The power system of *Re:Creators* operates on a single extraordinary premise: fictional characters from anime, manga, light novels, and video games have crossed into the real world, and they retain the supernatural abilities they possess in their original stories. A mecha pilot brings her mecha. A magical knight brings her sword and spells. A villain brings every power her fans have ever given her.

The mechanism of manifestation is never fully explained in physical terms — Meteora, the most analytically minded of the Created characters, theorizes that the barrier between fictional worlds and the real world has weakened, allowing sufficiently resonant characters to cross over. What matters mechanically is not how they arrived but how their powers function once present.

Each Created character's abilities operate exactly as they do in their source fiction. Selesia Upitiria, a mecha-fantasy protagonist, can summon her mecha Vogelchevalier and wield fire magic. Yuuya Mirokuji, from an action manga, fights with his persona-spirit Hangaku. Alicetelia February, a fantasy knight, rides an armored horse and commands devastating lance strikes. The powers are real, physically destructive, and interact with the real world's matter and energy.

The critical constraint is **audience acceptance**. A Created character's abilities are bounded by what the public — the collective audience of their source work — would find plausible for that character. If a Creator (the real-world author of a fictional character) writes a new ability for their creation, it only manifests if the audience would accept it as a natural extension of that character's story. An ability that feels forced, contradictory, or narratively unjustified simply fails to materialize.

This mechanic becomes the central strategic problem of the series. The protagonists need to give their Created allies new, stronger abilities to combat Altair, but each new power must be narratively convincing — it must feel like something that character would plausibly gain within the logic of their story. Creators cannot simply write "my character is now omnipotent" because the audience would reject it as absurd.

**Altair** (the Military Uniform Princess) represents the system's most dangerous edge case. She is a character from a viral fan-created music video series — a character with no single canonical author and no fixed narrative. Because her "story" exists across thousands of fan works, each contributing their own interpretation of her powers, Altair's ability set is defined by the collective imagination of her entire fandom. Her signature ability, **Holopsicon**, allows her to absorb and wield any power that fans have attributed to her. She can negate attacks, rewrite causality, and multiply herself — not because a single author wrote these abilities, but because fans imagined them and the collective audience accepted them.

**Magane Chikujoin** wields a different kind of metafictional power. Her ability, **Infinite Deception of Words** (Hangaku Sekai no Mugen Oitsukushi), allows her to lie about a lie — if someone denies her false statement, the original lie becomes true. This is a purely verbal, logic-exploiting power that requires no physical medium, only conversation. Magane is the system's trickster: her power operates on language and social interaction rather than combat force.

The protagonists' solution to the Altair crisis is the **Elimination Chamber Festival** — a staged, publicly broadcast crossover event disguised as entertainment. By presenting the Created characters fighting Altair in a format the audience interprets as a fictional spectacle, the protagonists generate the massive audience acceptance needed to power their countermeasures. The audience watching believes they are seeing a creative crossover event; their collective acceptance powers real abilities in real combat happening behind the fiction.

## Limitations and costs

The audience acceptance constraint is the system's defining limitation. Raw power is not the ceiling — plausibility is. A Creator can sit down and write any ability they want for their character, but if the writing is unconvincing, if the power feels unearned or contradicts established characterization, it will not manifest. This means the quality of the storytelling is a material combat variable. Bad writing is tactically useless.

The constraint operates on scale. Minor modifications to existing abilities — incremental upgrades, new applications of established powers — require minimal audience buy-in and can be implemented relatively quickly. Radical new abilities, power-level jumps, or fundamental character changes require enormous audience acceptance and careful narrative groundwork. The bigger the change, the more people need to believe it.

Created characters are also bounded by their source material's internal logic. Selesia cannot suddenly use abilities from a different genre or narrative tradition unless her Creator writes a plausible in-story reason. The characters are not infinitely malleable — they are constrained by who they are, and the audience enforces that constraint.

The cost to Creators is psychological and moral. Writing new abilities for a character who is standing in front of you, who is a real person with feelings and agency, transforms the creative act into something uncomfortably close to playing God. Several Creators in the series struggle with the ethical implications of rewriting their creations — characters who now have independent wills and relationships. Sota's guilt over his role in the events that created Altair is the series' emotional core.

Altair's unique position — drawing power from an uncontrolled fandom — makes her nearly impossible to counter through conventional means. Any ability the protagonists develop, her fans might independently imagine a counter for. The only viable strategy is to engage at the level of narrative itself: create a story so compelling that the audience's acceptance flows toward the protagonists' desired outcome.

## Practitioner access

Access to Creation powers is involuntary and non-transferable. Created characters manifest with whatever abilities their stories gave them; they cannot learn new powers through training or effort, only through their Creators writing new material that the audience accepts. They did not choose to cross over and cannot choose to gain new capabilities independently.

Creators — the human authors, artists, and designers — possess indirect power. They can modify their characters by producing new story content, but they cannot manifest powers themselves. Their role is authorial, not combative. The relationship between Creator and Created is the system's central dynamic: one has the power to rewrite, the other has the power to act.

Ordinary humans without creative works have no access to the system whatsoever. The audience's role is collective and unconscious — individual audience members do not knowingly contribute to power manifestation, but their aggregate belief shapes what is possible.

## Cultural role

Within the story, the existence of Created characters is concealed from the general public. The Japanese government collaborates with the protagonists to manage the crisis covertly, and the Elimination Chamber Festival is staged as entertainment precisely to harness audience acceptance without revealing the truth.

The system functions as extended commentary on the relationship between fiction and its consumers. Altair's power scales with fan engagement — she is strongest because she is most loved, most reinterpreted, most fervently imagined. The protagonists can only defeat her by engaging with the same creative ecosystem: producing better stories, earning deeper audience investment, and ultimately offering Altair something that raw power cannot provide. The system argues that stories are not passive entertainment but active forces shaped by the collective emotional investment of everyone who engages with them. The audience does not merely consume fiction — in this world, they literally power it.
