## Mechanics

Magic in the Sword of Truth world operates through two fundamental aspects: **Additive Magic** and **Subtractive Magic**.

**Additive Magic** draws on the world's inherent magical energy to create, enhance, and build. It is the more common form — practiced by wizards, sorceresses, the Sisters of the Light, and various magical creatures. Additive applications include: elemental manipulation (fire, lightning, wind), healing, shielding, enhancement of physical abilities, prophecy, and the creation of magical constructs and artifacts. Most magical practitioners are born with the Gift for Additive Magic and train to develop it through study, will, and practice.

**Subtractive Magic** is the power to destroy, negate, and unmake. It is extremely rare in the current age — associated with the Keeper of the Underworld and considered dark and dangerous. Subtractive Magic can dissolve matter, negate Additive spells, open rifts to the Underworld, and destroy things in ways that Additive Magic cannot reverse. In ancient times, Subtractive practitioners were more common; in the series' present, only a handful of beings possess it.

**War wizards** — of which Richard Rahl is the first in thousands of years — command both Additive and Subtractive Magic simultaneously. This makes them the most powerful magical practitioners in existence, capable of effects that neither type alone can produce. Richard's war wizard abilities are largely intuitive — they respond to need and emotion rather than formal training, which both empowers and frustrates him.

**Confessor power** is a distinct magical tradition wielded exclusively by the Confessors — women who can release their power through touch to destroy a person's will, replacing it with absolute love and devotion to the Confessor. The Confessed person retains their intelligence and skills but loses all independent motivation. Confessor power is classified as Additive (it adds love), but its effect is the complete destruction of the target's identity. Confessors serve as the judicial system of the Midlands — they can Confess criminals to extract truth and ensure compliance.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **physical and emotional toll**. Using the Gift drains the practitioner's strength. The Confessor power has a specific recovery time — after using it, a Confessor needs hours to recover before she can use it again (Kahlan is unique in her ability to recover almost instantly in extreme emotional states).

The second limitation is **the Additive/Subtractive divide**. Most practitioners can only use one type, making them vulnerable to the other. An Additive shield can be dissolved by Subtractive Magic; a Subtractive void can be filled by Additive creation. Only war wizards transcend this limitation.

The third cost is **the Wizard's Rules**. The series' philosophical framework (expressed as numbered "Wizard's Rules") constrains how magic interacts with human nature. The First Rule — "People are stupid; they believe what they want to believe" — establishes that magic operates within a world governed by psychological realities that magical power alone cannot overcome.

## Practitioner access

The Gift is hereditary — it runs in bloodlines, though it can skip generations or manifest unexpectedly. Training is provided by the Sisters of the Light (at the Palace of the Prophets, where time flows differently), by individual wizard mentors, or is self-taught through intuition (as with Richard). Confessor power is strictly hereditary and exclusively female — it passes from mother to daughter. The Confessor line is carefully managed, as a male Confessor (born with the power but unable to control it) would be catastrophically dangerous.

## Cultural role

Magic structures the political geography of the Sword of Truth world. Wizards advise rulers, the Confessors govern the Midlands, and the Sisters of the Light maintain a monastery-like order devoted to training the Gifted. Subtractive Magic is feared and associated with the Keeper of the Underworld. The series uses magic as a vehicle for its philosophical themes: Richard's war wizard abilities are explicitly linked to his philosophical convictions (reason, individual liberty, self-determination), and the magic system's duality mirrors the series' moral framework of creation versus destruction.
