## Mechanics

Burning Wheel's Sorcery subsystem is built on the principle that spellcasting should be a skill like any other — tested, advanced through use, and subject to the same failure-consequence economy that governs swordplay and persuasion. A sorcerer casts spells by rolling their Sorcery skill against an obstacle determined by the spell's parameters, which the player configures at the time of casting. Every spell has a base obstacle, but the caster can manipulate its area of effect, duration, and potency by accepting higher obstacles. This configuration system means that a single spell — say, a fire evocation — can be cast as a small, controlled flame (low obstacle, low risk) or a building-consuming inferno (high obstacle, high risk of failure and catastrophic Tax). The player's choice of configuration is simultaneously a tactical decision and a character statement: how much is the sorcerer willing to risk?

The skill advancement system transforms spellcasting from a static capability into a developing competency with genuine growth trajectory. Burning Wheel advances skills through logged tests at three difficulty tiers — Routine, Difficult, and Challenging — and a character must accumulate a specific distribution of test types to advance. This means that a sorcerer who only casts safe, low-obstacle spells will stagnate; advancement requires the player to seek out situations where they cast at Difficult or Challenging obstacles, accepting real risk of failure and Tax. The system thereby creates an organic pressure toward ambitious casting that emerges from the advancement mechanic rather than from GM fiat or narrative contrivance. A sorcerer who wants to grow must push their boundaries, and pushing boundaries in Burning Wheel's magic system has teeth.

Faith operates on entirely different mechanical principles, reflecting its fundamentally different relationship to power. A faithful character does not memorize spells or learn formulae; instead, they pray for divine intervention, and the outcome is determined by a Faith skill test modified by the character's demonstrated devotion. The key mechanical innovation is that Faith tests are influenced by the character's adherence to their Beliefs — the core character-definition mechanic that drives all of Burning Wheel's drama. A character whose Beliefs align with their religious tradition and who has acted on those Beliefs in play receives bonuses to their Faith tests. A character whose actions have contradicted their professed faith faces penalties. The system thus makes Faith magic a mechanical barometer of the character's spiritual authenticity, testing not whether they have enough mana but whether they truly believe.

Spirit Binding introduces a negotiation subsystem where the caster summons spirits and must convince, coerce, or bargain with them for services. The mechanical resolution uses Burning Wheel's social conflict system (Duel of Wits), meaning that binding a spirit is not a matter of rolling a single check but of engaging in a structured argument where both sides make rhetorical maneuvers and the spirit has genuine mechanical agency. A failed binding does not simply mean "nothing happens" — the spirit may escape, may impose conditions on its service, or may turn hostile. This transforms summoning from a resource-acquisition action into a dramatic scene with uncertain outcomes, and it reinforces the game's broader design philosophy that every mechanically significant action should produce narrative content.

## Limitations and costs

Tax is the Sorcery subsystem's signature cost mechanic and one of the most punishing physical-toll systems in TTRPG design. When a sorcerer casts a spell, they must make a Tax test — a separate roll after the casting roll — and failure results in the accumulation of Tax dice, which are subtracted from the character's physical stats (Forte, the health/toughness stat, bears the primary burden). Accumulated Tax produces increasing penalties to all physical actions, including further spellcasting, creating a degenerative spiral where each spell makes the next more difficult and more dangerous. At high Tax levels, the sorcerer risks physical collapse — falling unconscious or suffering wounds from the sheer strain of channeling magical energy. The design ensures that Sorcery is powerful but physically destructive, modeling a tradition where magic literally burns through the caster's body.

The consequences of failed casting extend beyond Tax into the territory of narrative catastrophe. A failed Sorcery test does not simply mean the spell fizzles — Burning Wheel's Let It Ride principle and the broader failure-consequence framework mean that a failed spell produces a complication determined by the GM in consultation with the game's guidelines. A failed fire evocation might ignite the wrong target, a failed telekinesis might collapse a structure, a failed illusion might reveal something the sorcerer wanted hidden. The system treats magical failure as dramatically productive rather than mechanically inert, ensuring that every casting decision carries stakes beyond the immediate tactical situation. This approach transforms the spellcasting economy from a resource-management puzzle into a risk-management drama.

Faith's limitations are theological rather than physical but equally constraining. A faithful character's prayers are not guaranteed answers; they are genuine tests of devotion that can fail. More importantly, the Faith mechanic requires the character to act in accordance with their religious Beliefs outside of casting situations. A priest who professes devotion but acts selfishly will find their Faith tests increasingly difficult, not because of a mechanical penalty stack but because the GM is instructed to set obstacles based on the character's demonstrated commitment. This creates a cost structure where the price of Faith magic is paid through consistent behavior across the entire game, not just at the moment of casting. The Faith subsystem demands that the player roleplay their character's religious commitment genuinely, because the mechanics will test that commitment.

Dwarven Greed and Elven Grief represent stock-specific magical limitations that are also emotional afflictions. Dwarves in Burning Wheel are driven by Greed — a stat that increases as they acquire wealth and craft magnificent works, and that can eventually consume the character entirely, transforming them from a player character into a dragon-like hoarder. Elven characters suffer Grief, an accumulating emotional weight that reflects their immortal perspective on mortal suffering and loss. Both mechanics function simultaneously as magical fuel (Greed powers Dwarven craft-magic, Grief deepens Elven Song) and as existential threats (maximum Greed or Grief removes the character from play). The design creates character arcs where the source of magical power is also the vector of personal destruction — a tragic structure that emerges mechanically rather than being imposed narratively.

## Practitioner access

Access to Burning Wheel's magic systems is determined at character creation through the Lifepath system, one of the game's most distinctive mechanical features. Rather than selecting a class, players build their characters by choosing a sequence of Lifepaths — periods of life experience that grant skills, traits, and resources. Access to Sorcery requires Lifepaths that include magical training (Neophyte Sorcerer, Arcane Devotee, etc.), and each successive magical Lifepath represents years of study and practice. This means that a character who can cast powerful spells has necessarily spent a significant portion of their life on magical training, at the cost of other skills and experiences. A master sorcerer is mechanically deficient in areas that a character who spent those Lifepaths on martial training or political maneuvering would excel in. The system enforces opportunity cost at the character-creation level.

The Lifepath system also means that magical access carries biographical weight. A character who followed the Lifepath sequence of Born Noble, Page, Arcane Devotee, Sorcerer has a specific life story implied by their mechanical choices: they were born into privilege, served in a noble household, discovered magical talent, and devoted themselves to its study. A different character who reached Sorcery through Born City, Street Urchin, Criminal, Neophyte Sorcerer tells a completely different story through the same mechanical framework. This biographical dimension means that two sorcerers with identical Sorcery skills may have radically different skill profiles, trait sets, and resource levels, reflecting their different paths to magical competence. The system refuses to treat magical access as a simple toggle and instead embeds it in a life story.

Faith access follows a parallel structure but with different requirements. Characters must take religious Lifepaths and, critically, must define Beliefs that reflect genuine religious devotion. The game's expectation is that a faithful character's Beliefs — the three statements that drive Artha (fate point) generation and character development — will include at least one that directly engages with their religious tradition. This is not a mechanical mandate but a design expectation: a character whose Beliefs have nothing to do with their faith will find their Faith skill difficult to advance and their prayers less likely to be answered. Access to Faith magic is thus contingent not just on character creation choices but on ongoing play decisions about what the character values and pursues.

Stock-specific magic — Elven Song, Dwarven craft-magic — is accessible only to characters of the appropriate stock, and this restriction is absolute rather than contingent. No human can learn Elven Song; no elf can develop Dwarven Greed. This hard restriction models the game's assertion that different peoples have fundamentally different relationships to the supernatural, and it creates a world where magical diversity is structural rather than cosmetic. The mechanical consequence is that mixed-stock parties bring not just different skill sets but different magical paradigms to the table, and the interplay between these paradigms — the elf's grief-powered songs and the dwarf's greed-driven craft, the human's taxing sorcery and the priest's devotion-tested faith — produces a magical ecosystem where no single approach is dominant and each carries its own distinctive costs and possibilities.

## Cultural role

Burning Wheel's magic systems serve primarily as character-revelation mechanisms, and this function is inseparable from the game's Beliefs-Instincts-Traits (BIT) engine. Every magical subsystem is designed to interact with the BIT framework in ways that force characters to confront what they value. A sorcerer's decision to push a spell to dangerous levels of Tax is meaningful because it tells the table what the character considers worth suffering for. A priest's prayer succeeds or fails based on whether the character has lived their Beliefs, transforming Faith magic into a public audit of the character's integrity. The game treats magic not as a capability to be optimized but as a lens through which character is examined, and this philosophical commitment produces a play experience where magical moments are always also dramatic moments.

The Tax and Grief and Greed mechanics collectively implement what might be called a tragic theory of magic: the idea that supernatural power is inseparable from personal destruction. This is not a moral judgment — the game does not suggest that magic is evil — but a structural observation that resonates with literary traditions from Faust to the Lord of the Rings. By making the cost of magic autobiographical (it literally changes who the character is, degrading their physical stats or accumulating emotional burdens), Burning Wheel ensures that a character's magical history is visible in their current mechanical state. A heavily Taxed sorcerer, a grief-stricken elf, a greed-consumed dwarf — these are characters whose magical use has left marks, and the system makes those marks legible at the table.

The multiplicity of magical subsystems — each with different mechanics, different costs, and different thematic resonances — also serves a worldbuilding function that operates through mechanical experience rather than exposition. When a player learns that human Sorcery is transactional and physically destructive while Elven Song is emotional and aesthetically driven, they are learning about the different cultures' relationships to power without reading a setting chapter. The mechanical differences between magic systems teach players what it means to be human, elf, or dwarf in Burning Wheel's world more effectively than any amount of descriptive text could. This is a design principle that Crane shares with Stafford's Glorantha — the idea that mechanical diversity in magic systems is a worldbuilding tool — but Burning Wheel's tighter integration with the character engine makes the cultural information more personal and more immediately felt.

Burning Wheel's magic systems also demonstrate a design philosophy about the relationship between mechanical complexity and narrative weight. Each subsystem is genuinely complex — Sorcery alone involves spell configuration, casting tests, Tax tests, and potential failure consequences — but this complexity is not arbitrary. Every mechanical step in the casting process produces a decision point where the player must weigh costs against benefits, and each decision point generates potential narrative content. The system rejects the common TTRPG design assumption that magic should be streamlined for ease of play, instead arguing that the friction of complex mechanics produces engagement that simple systems cannot match. When a Burning Wheel sorcerer completes a difficult casting — navigating configuration, succeeding at the skill test, surviving the Tax roll — the mechanical accomplishment produces a sense of earned triumph that a simple "roll to cast" system cannot replicate.
