## Mechanics

The Related Worlds are a set of **parallel universes** that diverged at critical historical moments. Each world has its own relationship to magic — some are saturated with it, others have almost none, and the rules governing magical practice differ from world to world. In some worlds, witchcraft is hereditary and respected. In others, it is feared and persecuted. In at least one world (*Witch Week*), magic is officially illegal and witches are burned, despite magic being genuinely common.

Within any given world, magic is typically an **innate talent** that manifests in childhood and can be developed through training. The strength and character of a person's magic varies enormously. Most practitioners are minor witches or warlocks capable of small enchantments. A few are powerful enchanters whose magic operates at a fundamentally different scale. The rarest and most powerful category is the **nine-lived enchanter** — a person born with nine lives, each of which carries a portion of their magical power. Losing lives reduces their power; retaining all nine makes them the most formidable magic-user in existence.

The **Chrestomanci** is the current nine-lived enchanter who holds the government office of magical regulator. The office is based in Chrestomanci Castle and carries legal authority to investigate and prevent magical misuse across the Related Worlds. The Chrestomanci can travel between worlds, detect magical disturbances across dimensional boundaries, and intervene when practitioners in one world affect another. The title passes to the next nine-lived enchanter when the current holder dies permanently (losing all nine lives).

Magic in practice varies by world and by book. *Charmed Life* features a world where magic is part of upper-class English society — enchanters hold government positions and magical talent confers social status. *Witch Week* presents a world where magic is criminalized. *The Magicians of Caprona* depicts a world where magical families in an Italian city-state compete through enchanted songs. *The Pinhoe Egg* reveals a hidden tradition of rural hedge-witchcraft operating beneath the Chrestomanci's notice. Jones uses these variations to explore how the same fundamental magical capacity produces radically different social outcomes depending on cultural context.

Specific magical techniques include **enchantment** (permanent magical alteration of objects or people), **spells** (temporary directed effects), **curses**, **divination**, and **world-walking** (crossing between Related Worlds, a rare ability). Magic can be stored in objects, bound into songs, or woven into textiles. The system is flexible enough to accommodate any fairy-tale magical effect but consistent in its underlying logic: magic is a natural capacity, it responds to will and intention, and its misuse has consequences.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation is **regulation**. The Chrestomanci exists because magic without oversight is dangerous. Practitioners who misuse their abilities face investigation and sanction. The system's most important constraint is therefore social and legal rather than physical — magic can do almost anything, but doing it without authorization brings the Chrestomanci's attention.

The **nine-lives mechanism** imposes a unique cost on the most powerful practitioners. Each life lost is permanent, and losing lives reduces magical capacity. Christopher Chant loses several lives during the course of *The Lives of Christopher Chant*, and each loss is genuinely diminishing. The Chrestomanci's power is enormous but finite and non-renewable.

For ordinary practitioners, the costs are more conventional: magic requires **skill and training** to use effectively, untrained magical talent can manifest dangerously (Cat Chant's uncontrolled magic causes serious problems in *Charmed Life*), and ambitious magical workings can fail catastrophically if the practitioner overreaches.

There is also a **moral cost**. Jones consistently presents magical power as a test of character. Gwendolen Chant's magical talent is genuine but her willingness to exploit others makes her a villain. The Pinhoe witches' hoarding of power and secrecy makes them dangerous. Magic amplifies character flaws as readily as it amplifies capability.

## Practitioner access

Magical ability is **innate and hereditary** but not predictable. It can appear in any family, though magical families tend to produce magical children. Training is available through apprenticeship, magical schools, and (in some worlds) family traditions. The Chrestomanci's office identifies and supports talented children, particularly those whose abilities are dangerous without guidance. The nine-lived enchanter is vanishingly rare — there is typically only one alive at any given time across all the Related Worlds.

## Cultural role

Magic's cultural role varies dramatically by world. In some Related Worlds, magic is an ordinary part of the social fabric — enchanters hold government posts, magical services are commercially available, and magical talent is a source of social prestige. In others, magic is feared, persecuted, or hidden. The Chrestomanci serves as the connective tissue between these varied contexts, enforcing a minimum standard of magical governance across worlds that may not welcome the oversight. Jones uses this variation to argue implicitly that magic itself is morally neutral — its social role is determined entirely by the culture that contains it.
