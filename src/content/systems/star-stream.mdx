## Mechanics

The Star Stream is a cosmic broadcasting system that restructures reality into narrative form. When it activates on Earth, the world is divided into apocalyptic scenarios — structured challenges with defined objectives, time limits, and rewards — managed by Dokkaebi, trickster-like entities that serve as scenario administrators. Every surviving human becomes an Incarnation, a participant in these scenarios whose struggles are watched by an audience of Constellations — beings drawn from mythology, history, and fiction who exist in a higher narrative layer.

The fundamental mechanic is the scenario. Each scenario has clear parameters: a main scenario that all Incarnations must complete or die trying, sub-scenarios that offer additional rewards, hidden scenarios that activate under specific unusual conditions, and penalty conditions that punish failure. Early scenarios are brutally direct — survive a monster attack, clear a subway station, defeat a boss enemy — while later ones become increasingly abstract and narratively complex, eventually requiring Incarnations to engage with questions about the nature of stories themselves.

Constellations interact with Incarnations through a coin economy. Coins are the universal currency of the Star Stream, and they flow in both directions. Constellations spend coins to sponsor Incarnations — granting abilities, items, or direct interventions — and Incarnations spend coins to purchase skills, equipment, and services from the Dokkaebi shop. Constellations earn coins by having their sponsored Incarnations perform interesting, dramatic, or impressive feats. This creates an audience-performer dynamic where power is literally fueled by being entertaining and narratively compelling.

Sponsorship takes several forms. A Constellation can offer a contract to an Incarnation, granting them a Stigma — a unique ability derived from the Constellation's own nature and mythology. The Constellation "Prisoner of the Golden Headband" might grant abilities related to the Monkey King's legendary powers; "Abyssal Black Flame Dragon" grants fire-based combat skills. Stigmas are powerful but come with strings attached — the sponsoring Constellation can withdraw support, impose conditions, or attempt to shape the Incarnation's behavior to match their preferred narrative.

Kim Dokja's unique advantage is not a power but knowledge. For over a decade before the scenarios began, he was the sole reader of a web novel called "Three Ways to Survive in a Ruined World" — a story that, it turns out, perfectly predicted the scenario system and everything that would happen. The novel's protagonist, Yoo Joonghyuk, is a real person in Dokja's world, and the novel documented 1,863 regression turns of Joonghyuk living through the scenarios, dying, and restarting. Dokja knows every scenario, every monster, every trick and shortcut, because he read the entire walkthrough. His survival strategy is built on exploiting this foreknowledge while building a party of companions who can handle the situations where reality diverges from the text.

The deeper cosmology reveals that the Star Stream treats narrative itself as a form of power. A "Fable" is a story about an Incarnation that has become sufficiently well-known and emotionally resonant that it solidifies into a tangible power source. Incarnations who accumulate enough Fables can ascend to become Constellations themselves. The most powerful beings in the setting are those whose stories are the most widely known and deeply felt — which is why mythological figures like Sun Wukong and Hades are among the strongest Constellations. Story is not metaphor; it is the literal substrate of power.

## Limitations and costs

The scenario system is brutally zero-sum. Each scenario has a completion condition, and Incarnations who fail to meet it die. There are no second chances within a single timeline, no safe zones where someone can opt out of participation. The early scenarios cull the human population aggressively — millions die in the first days. The Dokkaebi enforce scenario rules with absolute authority, and attempts to circumvent scenario conditions are met with immediate, lethal punishment.

Constellation sponsorship is unreliable by design. Constellations are beings with their own agendas, preferences, and narrative sensibilities. A sponsor might withdraw support because an Incarnation's choices are boring, or because a rival Constellation offered a better deal, or simply because the Constellation lost interest. The coin economy amplifies this instability — Constellations who back losing Incarnations hemorrhage coins, creating incentives to abandon struggling sponsees. The relationship between Constellation and Incarnation is fundamentally transactional, not loyal.

Coins are finite and must be actively earned. The Dokkaebi shop sells powerful items and abilities, but prices escalate rapidly as scenarios progress. Incarnations who hoard coins for safety find themselves outpaced by those who invest aggressively. The economy creates constant pressure to take risks, perform dramatic feats, and attract Constellation attention — playing it safe is the most dangerous strategy.

Kim Dokja's foreknowledge degrades as the story progresses. Every action he takes based on the novel changes reality's trajectory, and as changes accumulate, the novel becomes an increasingly unreliable guide. Scenarios that should play out one way go sideways because Dokja's presence has altered the conditions. Characters who should be enemies become allies, characters who should survive die, and entirely new situations emerge that the novel never covered. The system punishes reliance on any single advantage, including meta-knowledge.

The Star Stream itself is revealed to have an agenda beyond entertainment. The scenario system is not a neutral framework but a selection mechanism, a process designed to identify and cultivate beings capable of participating in conflicts at the cosmic narrative level. The Incarnations are not just contestants — they are raw material being refined into story-beings, and the refinement process is not gentle.

## Practitioner access

The Star Stream is imposed on all of humanity simultaneously. When the scenarios begin, every living person becomes an Incarnation whether they want to or not. There is no Awakening event, no genetic prerequisite, no chosen-one selection. The seven billion people on Earth are all thrown into the same system at the same time.

However, effective power within the system varies enormously. Initial abilities depend on a person's pre-existing attributes — physical fitness, combat training, mental resilience, and relevant knowledge all translate into starting advantages. The scenario system then amplifies these differences through its reward structure. Incarnations who perform well in early scenarios gain coins, skills, and Constellation sponsorship that make them stronger for later scenarios, creating a compounding advantage gap.

Constellation sponsorship is the primary path to significant power, and it depends on a combination of ability, personality, and narrative appeal. Constellations choose Incarnations who interest them — not necessarily the strongest, but the most dramatically compelling. A weak Incarnation with an interesting story might attract a powerful sponsor, while a strong but boring one languishes without support. This makes charisma and narrative instinct practical survival skills.

Kim Dokja's access is entirely knowledge-based. He has no special physical abilities, no innate talent, and no pre-existing Constellation connections. What he has is the complete text of a novel that maps the scenario system in exhaustive detail, and the analytical intelligence to exploit that information. His party members — Yoo Joonghyuk, Jung Heewon, Lee Hyunsung, Han Sooyoung — provide the combat power and practical skills that Dokja himself lacks. He is a strategist, not a warrior, and his survival depends on assembling and maintaining a team.

## Cultural role

Omniscient Reader's Viewpoint uses the Star Stream to interrogate the relationship between stories and the people who consume them. The Constellation-Incarnation dynamic is an explicit metaphor for the reader-character relationship — Constellations watch, judge, sponsor, and abandon Incarnations in exactly the way readers engage with fictional characters. The coin economy literalizes the attention economy of serialized fiction. The system asks, persistently and with increasing directness, what obligation a reader has to the characters whose suffering they enjoy.

The scenario structure transforms society into a series of narrative arcs. Political structures, economic systems, and social bonds are all subordinated to the demands of the current scenario. Some Incarnations form companies — guild-like organizations that pool resources and coordinate strategy — while others operate as independents or antagonists. The strongest Incarnation groups become geopolitical factions, and the story's middle arc involves a war between these factions that mirrors the factional conflicts of the in-story novel.

The meta-fictional dimension is the system's most distinctive cultural feature. Kim Dokja is a reader who has entered the story he read. The novel he read was written by an author who may or may not exist within the story's cosmology. The Constellations are themselves characters from other stories — mythological, historical, fictional — whose existence depends on being remembered and retold. The ending of the series raises the question of whether Kim Dokja himself is real or fictional, and whether the distinction matters if his story has been told and read.

This layered self-awareness makes the Star Stream one of the most deliberately constructed magic systems in the collection. It is not just a mechanism for generating fights but a philosophical framework for thinking about narrative, readership, and the half-life of fictional beings who persist in the minds of those who read about them.
