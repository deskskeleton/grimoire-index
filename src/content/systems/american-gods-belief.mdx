## Mechanics

In Gaiman's cosmology, gods are brought into existence by collective human belief. When immigrants came to America, they carried their worship with them, and that worship made their gods real on American soil. Each god is a localized manifestation — the Odin walking American highways is not the same entity as the Odin worshipped in Scandinavia, but a distinct American version, shaped by the specific character of the belief that sustains him.

A god's power is directly tied to the volume and intensity of worship directed at them. Active sacrifice — literal blood offerings, ritual devotion, sustained prayer — produces the strongest connection. Passive cultural awareness is weaker but still sustaining. Complete forgetfulness is death. The old gods (Odin, Anansi, Czernobog, Easter, and dozens of others) are fading because their worship has dwindled to near-nothing in modern America. The new gods (Media, who feeds on television attention; Technical Boy, who feeds on technological dependency; Mr. World, who feeds on globalized surveillance) are ascendant because Americans pour enormous psychic energy into screens, networks, and consumption.

The gods' abilities correspond loosely to their mythological portfolios but are constrained by their current level of worship. Wednesday retains Odin's cunning and a measure of his storm-magic, but he cannot call down the thunder the way he could when thousands sacrificed to him. The new gods can reshape perception and manipulate information flows because that is the domain in which they are worshipped.

## Limitations and costs

The fundamental limitation is dependency. No god is self-sustaining. A god who loses all believers ceases to exist, and a god whose worship declines loses proportional power. This creates a desperate, scarcity-driven dynamic among the old gods, many of whom resort to confidence schemes, petty crime, or parasitic relationships with mortals to scrape together enough belief-energy to survive.

The cost is also borne by worshippers. Sacrifice — especially blood sacrifice — is the most potent fuel for divine power, and several gods in the novel are shown to have historically demanded terrible prices from their devotees. The transactional nature of the system is explicit: worship is not free devotion but an exchange, and the exchange rate favors the divine.

Gods are also geographically constrained in a loose sense. America is described as hostile to gods — a land that chews up the sacred and replaces it with commerce. Gods who thrived in their homelands find themselves diminished simply by being on American soil, as though the country's cultural character actively erodes the mythic.

## Practitioner access

Strictly speaking, the practitioners of this system are the gods themselves, not mortals. Humans participate by worshipping, sacrificing, and believing, but they do not wield divine power directly. Shadow Moon occupies a liminal position — he is drawn into divine conflicts and eventually revealed to have a closer connection to the divine than an ordinary mortal — but he does not cast spells or invoke powers in the conventional sense.

The system is not available for human use. It is something humans fuel and gods consume.

## Cultural role

The belief system functions as Gaiman's lens for examining American culture. The war between old and new gods is a war over attention, meaning, and devotion in a society that consumes mythology as entertainment and discards it when something shinier appears. The old gods represent tradition, memory, and the immigrant experience; the new gods represent the frictionless, amnesiac present. Neither side is presented as wholly sympathetic, and the novel's resolution suggests that the conflict itself is a con — a manipulation designed to harvest the belief generated by the spectacle of divine war.
