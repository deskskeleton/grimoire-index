## Mechanics

The Abyss is a massive vertical chasm of unknown depth and origin, roughly 1,000 meters in diameter at the surface and extending far deeper than any confirmed exploration has reached. Its defining supernatural property is the Curse — a force that punishes vertical ascent. Descending into the Abyss is physically uneventful in itself, but rising toward the surface triggers physiological and psychological damage that intensifies with depth. The Curse operates through the Forcefield, an invisible energy field that permeates the entire chasm. The Forcefield flows downward, and moving against its current — ascending — is what triggers the Curse's effects. The Forcefield is not uniform; it pools, eddies, and concentrates in specific areas, creating pockets where the Curse strikes with unusual intensity and narrow corridors where it is marginally less severe.

The Curse manifests in distinct tiers corresponding to the Abyss's named layers. In the first layer (0-1,350 meters), ascent produces mild dizziness and nausea. The second layer (1,350-2,600 meters) inflicts heavy nausea, headaches, and numbness. The third layer (2,600-7,000 meters) causes severe vertigo combined with visual and auditory hallucinations. The fourth layer (7,000-12,000 meters) triggers intense full-body pain and bleeding from every orifice. The fifth layer (12,000-13,000 meters) causes complete sensory deprivation followed by loss of higher cognition and self-harming behavior. The sixth layer (13,000-15,500 meters) inflicts loss of humanity itself — or death. Below the sixth layer, the effects remain unknown and are the subject of fearful speculation.

The Abyss is also the source of Relics — artifacts of immense and varied power created by a civilization that predates recorded history. Relics are graded by a classification system ranging from common first-grade artifacts to the priceless and often dangerous special-grade artifacts. Recovery and trade in Relics is the economic engine of the city of Orth, built around the Abyss's rim. White Whistles, the most significant artifacts in the narrative, are Life Reverberating Stones — personal navigation instruments fashioned from the calcified remains of a person who willingly offered their life. Each White Whistle is attuned to a single delver and cannot be used by anyone else.

The Blessing is a phenomenon tied to the Curse's mechanics. When a person bearing deep emotional attachment to another is subjected to the Curse in the other's place — absorbing the full punishment so that the other may ascend — the protected individual occasionally receives a transformation that grants enhanced physical capabilities and a degree of resistance to the Forcefield. Nanachi is the primary example: transformed into a Narehate (a hollow, non-human being) while retaining full sapience and gaining the ability to perceive the Forcefield's currents directly.

## Limitations and costs

The Curse is absolute and cannot be circumvented by willpower, training, or conventional technology. No amount of physical conditioning reduces its effects; it strikes the weak and the strong with equal severity at each tier. The only known mitigations are navigational — threading paths through areas where the Forcefield is less dense, or ascending in very small increments to distribute the damage.

The cost structure is cumulative and permanent. Damage sustained from the Curse does not heal cleanly, and repeated exposure compounds injuries. Delvers who spend years working in the middle layers accumulate chronic conditions — partial blindness, nerve damage, cognitive degradation. There is no restorative medicine that undoes what the Curse inflicts.

Bondrewd's Cartridges represent the system's darkest cost. By surgically reducing a living child into a sealed life-support unit and binding them to himself, Bondrewd redirects the Curse's effects onto the Cartridge occupant, allowing himself to ascend the fifth layer unharmed while the child absorbs the full punishment. This is the system's moral cost made literal: the Curse can be displaced, but only onto another living being, and the displacement kills them.

The Narehate transformation of the sixth layer is presented as both cost and outcome. Delvers who descend to the sixth layer and attempt to return lose their humanity — their bodies warp into forms that may retain intelligence or may not, depending on circumstances the story has not fully explained. The Narehate village of Iruburu demonstrates that this transformation can produce a functioning society, but one built on a transactional economy of personal value that mirrors the Curse's own logic of irreversible exchange.

Reg, as a robotic artifact of the Abyss, is immune to the Curse's biological effects but not to its narrative weight. His Incinerator — a beam weapon of enormous destructive power — costs him consciousness for roughly two hours after each use, leaving the party defenseless in hostile territory.

## Practitioner access

The Curse applies to everyone. There is no immunity conferred by birth, training, or social status. Every living thing that enters the Abyss and attempts to ascend is subject to its effects. This universality is fundamental to the system's narrative function — it is not a power to be mastered but a law to be endured.

Access to the Abyss itself is regulated by the Delvers' Guild of Orth, which assigns ranks by whistle color. Red Whistles are apprentices restricted to the first layer. Blue Whistles may descend to the second. Moon Whistles reach the fourth. Black Whistles are veteran delvers who work the dangerous middle depths. White Whistles — the legendary figures of the setting — are the only sanctioned delvers of the fifth layer and below, each possessing a unique Life Reverberating Stone and the accepted understanding that they will never return to the surface.

The ranking system is social rather than supernatural. A Red Whistle's body would respond to the sixth layer's Curse identically to a White Whistle's; the ranks exist to prevent inexperienced delvers from descending to depths from which they cannot return without catastrophic injury. The phrase "last dive" carries weight at every level of the Guild — every deep descent is potentially one-way.

Reg's robotic nature gives him unique access. He can ascend freely, carry others through Cursed zones with partial shielding, and survive environments that would kill organic delvers. His limitations are mechanical rather than biological — power depletion, memory gaps, and the Incinerator's post-use blackout.

## Cultural role

The Abyss defines the culture of Orth completely. The city exists because of the Abyss, its economy runs on Relic recovery, its social hierarchy is organized around delving ranks, and its mythology is saturated with stories of the depths. Children are raised on tales of White Whistles and dream of descent. The Birthday Death Disease — a local superstition — holds that children born on the same day a delver dies in the Abyss are somehow connected to the dead, a belief that underscores how thoroughly the chasm pervades the city's spiritual life.

The Curse functions as the setting's central metaphor: the cost of knowledge and wonder. Tsukushi frames the Abyss as irresistibly beautiful — bioluminescent ecosystems, impossible fauna, ruins of incomprehensible civilizations — and then makes the act of witnessing that beauty a permanent expenditure of the self. The deeper you go, the more you see, and the less of yourself remains to carry the memory home. White Whistles do not return not because they lack the strength to climb, but because what the Curse would take from them on the way up exceeds what any person can survive.

The system also encodes a critique of exploration as exploitation. Orth's economy depends on sending people into a structure that destroys them, and the Guild's ranking system is less a safety measure than a sorting mechanism for acceptable losses. Bondrewd's Cartridges are an extreme expression of a logic already present in the institution — that the Abyss's treasures justify the consumption of human lives. The Curse makes this transaction visible and visceral in a way that purely social systems of exploitation do not.
