## Mechanics

Each of Yale's eight ancient houses of the Veil practices a specific magical discipline, and each discipline requires rituals fueled by Grays — the ghosts of the dead that cluster in unusual density around New Haven. The societies harvest spiritual energy from Grays through controlled ritual consumption, using the ghosts as a power source the way an engine burns fuel.

The eight disciplines map to the real Yale societies: Skull and Bones practices necromancy and communion with the dead; Book and Snake specializes in prognostication and divination; Scroll and Key creates portals between locations; Wolf's Head works glamours and illusions; Berzelius handles healing and physical manipulation; St. Elmo's manages persuasion and mental influence; Manuscript deals with textual magic and binding; and Aurelian practices alchemical transmutation. Each society guards its techniques jealously, and cross-disciplinary knowledge is rare.

Lethe, the ninth house, was founded to monitor and regulate the other eight. Its delegates — typically two undergraduates and a graduate advisor — attend the rituals of the other societies, ensure compliance with established protocols, and intervene when magic threatens to spill into the mundane world or harm non-practitioners.

Alex Stern's unique ability is her capacity to see Grays without ritual preparation. Most practitioners can only perceive ghosts during specific ritual states; Alex sees them constantly, which makes her invaluable as a monitor but also exposes her to continuous, unwanted contact with the dead.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is ethical. Grays are the remnants of dead people, and using them as fuel involves a form of consumption that the societies prefer not to examine too closely. The rituals effectively destroy or diminish ghosts in the process of extracting their energy, raising questions about whether the dead are being exploited — questions the societies have strong institutional incentives to avoid answering.

Each discipline also has practical limitations tied to its ritual requirements. Prognostication requires specific astrological conditions. Portal-creation needs anchoring locations. Glamours fade under sustained scrutiny. No discipline offers unlimited, on-demand power — each is constrained by timing, preparation, and the availability of Grays to fuel the working.

The physical and psychological toll on practitioners is a secondary cost. Extended exposure to Grays can produce haunting, psychological disturbance, and in extreme cases possession. Alex's constant perception of the dead is presented as a burden as much as a gift, contributing to the trauma and instability that define her character.

## Practitioner access

Access to society magic is gatekept by Yale's existing social structures. Membership in the societies is by invitation, determined by the same networks of wealth, legacy, and institutional connection that characterize real Yale secret societies. The magical abilities themselves are taught through the societies' internal traditions — a student joins, learns the rituals, and practices under supervision. There is no innate magical talent required for most disciplines; the power comes from the rituals and the Grays, not from the practitioner.

Alex Stern is the exception. Her ability to see Grays is innate, not learned, and it exists outside the society framework. This makes her simultaneously essential and threatening to the established order — she can perceive things the societies would rather keep hidden.

## Cultural role

The society magic system functions as a direct allegory for institutional power at elite universities. The societies extract value from invisible, unconsenting sources (the dead) to benefit a privileged few (their members), and the oversight body (Lethe) is chronically underfunded, understaffed, and compromised by its own proximity to the power it is supposed to regulate. Magic here is not a source of wonder but a resource to be hoarded, a privilege to be defended, and a tool whose costs are borne by those with no voice — a framework that Bardugo uses to interrogate class, access, and the moral debts of inherited advantage.
