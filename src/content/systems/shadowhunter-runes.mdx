## Mechanics

Nephilim rune magic derives from the angelic blood that runs in every Shadowhunter's veins. Over a thousand years ago, the Angel Raziel bestowed the Mortal Cup upon the first Shadowhunter, Jonathan Shadowhunter. Humans who drank from the Cup were transformed, their blood mingled with angelic essence, granting them enhanced physical capabilities and — critically — the ability to bear runes without harm. The rune language itself was given by Raziel in the Gray Book (the Codex), a compendium of angelic glyphs that constitutes the core curriculum of Shadowhunter training.

Runes are applied to the skin using a stele — a slender, crystalline instrument made from adamas, the heavenly metal. When a Shadowhunter draws a rune on their body, the mark activates and confers a specific effect. The system is categorical: each rune has a defined purpose and a recognized design. Enkeli grants angelic power. Iratze accelerates healing. The Voyance rune enhances sight. Mendelin runes grant silence, stealth, or speed. Glamour runes render the bearer invisible to mundane eyes. There are runes for courage, for endurance, for tracking a specific person across a city. The Gray Book contains hundreds, and a Shadowhunter's training involves memorizing their forms and appropriate applications.

The parabatai bond represents a specialized runic application. Two Shadowhunters who swear the parabatai oath and accept the bonding runes become linked at a fundamental level — their combat abilities are amplified when they fight together, they can sense each other's location and emotional state, and certain runes drawn by one parabatai on the other are more powerful than they would otherwise be. The bond carries restrictions: parabatai cannot fall in love with each other, and the bond is limited to one per Shadowhunter.

Clary Fray's unusual ability to create entirely new runes — designs that do not appear in the Gray Book — represents a major disruption to the system. Her runes achieve effects previously considered impossible, including the Alliance rune, which allows Downworlders to temporarily bear Shadowhunter marks. This ability is linked to her unique heritage and the extra angel blood her father administered before her birth.

## Limitations and costs

The most fundamental limitation is biological. Mundanes — humans without Nephilim blood — who receive runes go mad, die, or are transformed into Forsaken: mindless, violent shells. This restriction is absolute and serves as both a plot device and a metaphor for the exclusivity of Shadowhunter culture. Even among Shadowhunters, too many active runes at once can cause pain and physical strain, and certain powerful runes carry specific risks.

Runes fade over time. Most marks are not permanent; they activate, serve their purpose, and gradually disappear as the angelic energy dissipates. Some runes — particularly those marking significant life events, like the parabatai bond or the Voyance rune — are permanent, but the majority must be redrawn for each use. This creates a tactical rhythm: Shadowhunters prepare for missions by applying appropriate runes, and running out of applied marks in the field is a genuine danger.

The stele itself is a limiting factor. Without this specific instrument, runes cannot be properly drawn. A Shadowhunter separated from their stele is reduced to whatever marks are currently active on their body and their enhanced physical baseline — still formidable, but significantly diminished. Steles can be lost, broken, or confiscated, and the series uses this vulnerability to create tension.

## Practitioner access

Rune magic is exclusively available to Nephilim — those who carry angel blood either through descent from original Cup-drinkers or through drinking from the Mortal Cup themselves (an exceedingly rare event in the modern era, as the Cup was long believed lost). Shadowhunter society is therefore a closed hereditary caste, with new blood entering only through the rare Ascension process, in which a mundane drinks from the Cup and survives the transformation.

Training begins in childhood. Young Shadowhunters receive their first marks at specific developmental milestones and study the Gray Book's contents at Shadowhunter academies. The Clave — the governing body of Nephilim society — regulates which runes are taught at which age and maintains strict protocols around the more dangerous marks. Downworlders each possess their own distinct magical traditions: warlocks wield innate, individualized magic derived from their demon parentage; vampires and werewolves have supernatural physical abilities; and the fae command glamour and nature magic rooted in their connection to their home realms.

## Cultural role

Runes are inseparable from Shadowhunter identity. A Nephilim's body is a record of their life — the marks they bear tell a story of battles fought, oaths sworn, and roles assumed within their society. The ceremonial application of runes marks every major life event: coming of age, marriage (the wedding rune), parabatai bonding, and mourning (the rune placed on funeral pyres). To be a Shadowhunter is, in a very literal sense, to be marked.

This intertwining of power and identity creates significant social tensions that the series explores extensively. The Clave's insistence on the racial purity of Nephilim blood, their historical mistreatment of Downworlders, and the rigid hierarchy enforced through runic tradition all serve as vehicles for the series' themes about prejudice, tradition, and the cost of inherited power. Clary's ability to create new runes — particularly the Alliance rune that bridges the Nephilim-Downworlder divide — functions as a narrative argument that the system's boundaries are less fixed than its keepers believe.
