## Mechanics

Rukh are luminous butterfly-like particles that constitute the fundamental substance of all life and fate in the Magi world. When a living being dies, their Rukh returns to a vast collective flow sometimes called the Great Flow of Rukh, eventually reincarnating as part of new life. Rukh is simultaneously spiritual matter, the substrate of destiny, and the raw fuel for magic. Under normal conditions Rukh is invisible to most people, but Magi and other highly attuned individuals can perceive and interact with it directly.

Magoi is the personal energy that every living being produces from the Rukh within their body. Magoi functions as the resource currency of the power system — casting magic, activating artifacts, and sustaining transformations all consume Magoi. When a person's Magoi is depleted, they experience exhaustion, and total depletion can be fatal. Magoi regenerates naturally over time, drawing from the ambient Rukh in the environment. The amount of Magoi a person can hold and generate varies by individual, and this variation is a core axis of power differentiation.

Magic in the Magi world is organized into eight elemental types, each associated with specific Rukh affinities: fire (Har-Har Infigar and its variants), water (Sharrar Raqesa), wind (Asfal Riih), lightning (Ramz), light (Flash), sound (Dhoruf Asshara), gravity (Ugo), and life (Bolg). Casting magic involves commanding the Rukh in the environment to produce these effects, channeled through verbal incantations, gestures, and magical tools such as staves and wands. Magicians — people with the innate ability to command Rukh directly — are a minority of the population, and formal magical training is concentrated in institutions like Magnostadt's Academy.

Djinn are immensely powerful beings sealed within Dungeons — enormous, labyrinthine structures that appear spontaneously in the world, raised by the will of the Magi. When a person conquers a Dungeon, its resident Djinn bonds with them, inhabiting a chosen object called a Metal Vessel (typically a weapon or piece of jewelry). The Djinn-user can then invoke their Djinn's power through the Metal Vessel, progressing through three tiers of manifestation. Weapon Equip sheathes the Metal Vessel in the Djinn's elemental power, augmenting its offensive capability. Djinn Equip transforms the user's entire body into a form reflecting the Djinn's nature — granting flight, elemental immunity, and vastly amplified combat power. Extreme Djinn Equip pushes this transformation further, converting the user's body almost entirely into the Djinn's element.

Household Vessels extend this system to subordinates. A Djinn-user's close followers can receive a portion of their Djinn's power, channeled through personal objects that become Household Vessels. These grant lesser but still significant elemental abilities, creating a pyramid of power flowing from the Djinn through the king-candidate down to their retainers.

Magi occupy the apex of the system. A Magi is a magician beloved by the Rukh itself — they can draw on the ambient Rukh of the entire world rather than relying solely on their personal Magoi reserves. This grants them effectively unlimited magical fuel, the ability to raise Dungeons, and the political authority to select king-candidates. Only three Magi exist in the world at any given time (with Aladdin's generation as a partial exception), and their choices about which leaders to support shape the course of civilizations.

## Limitations and costs

Magoi depletion is the system's primary limiter. Every magical action costs Magoi, and even Djinn Equip drains the user's reserves at a continuous rate. Sustained combat in Djinn Equip is a race against exhaustion — a powerful fighter can be rendered helpless if they burn through their Magoi before finishing the engagement. Only Magi can circumvent this constraint, and even they face practical limits on how quickly they can gather and direct ambient Rukh.

Physical toll scales with power output. High-tier Djinn Equip places immense strain on the body, and users who force transformations beyond their capacity risk physical breakdown. Alibaba's early attempts at Djinn Equip are incomplete and unstable precisely because his body and will have not yet adapted to channeling his Djinn Amon's full power.

The moral-spiritual cost is encoded in the Rukh system's cosmology. Rukh responds to the emotional and ethical state of its user. A person who falls into despair, hatred, or the desire for destruction causes their Rukh to invert — turning from golden-white to black. Black Rukh (also called Fallen Rukh or the Rukh of Depravity) grants access to a parallel set of dark powers and opens the user to manipulation by Al-Thamen, the series' antagonist organization that seeks to summon a being from beyond the world. Falling into depravity is not merely a tactical choice; it represents a fundamental corruption of the self at the particle level.

Dungeon conquering is dangerous and non-repeatable in its most significant sense. Dungeons are filled with lethal traps, hostile creatures, and tests that kill the majority of challengers. A conquered Dungeon collapses permanently. The Djinn selects its king-candidate based on criteria the challenger cannot fully control — worthiness is judged by the Djinn, not claimed by the conqueror.

## Practitioner access

The system operates on multiple tiers of access. Ordinary people possess Rukh and Magoi but cannot direct them consciously. They contribute to and draw from the Great Flow passively, and their Magoi can be harvested by external means (a fact exploited by Magnostadt's ruling class).

Magicians — those born with the ability to perceive and command Rukh — represent a small fraction of the population. Magical training is formalized in Magnostadt, a nation-academy governed entirely by magicians, where students progress through rigorous study of Rukh theory and elemental specialization. Access to magical education is gated by aptitude and, in practice, by political and economic factors.

Djinn-users are rarer still. Acquiring a Djinn requires physically entering and surviving a Dungeon, then being chosen by its resident Djinn. Multiple Metal Vessels can be held by a single individual — Sinbad possesses seven, an unprecedented number that reflects both his extraordinary capability and the series' treatment of him as a figure of destabilizing ambition.

Magi are the rarest tier. Their selection is not hereditary or meritocratic but cosmological — the Rukh itself designates them. The three-Magi limit creates a structural scarcity that drives geopolitics: nations compete for the patronage of a Magi because a Magi's support means access to new Dungeons and, by extension, new Djinn-empowered military assets.

The Fanalis, a warrior lineage from the Dark Continent, represent a parallel access track. Their physical capabilities exceed ordinary human limits by an enormous margin, and their Magoi reserves are unusually deep, but they are not magicians and cannot command Rukh directly. Their power is bodily rather than mystical, providing a counterpoint to the magical hierarchy.

## Cultural role

The Rukh system doubles as the series' theological and political framework. The Great Flow of Rukh encodes a philosophy of predestination — all life follows a path set by Solomon, the godlike figure who created the current world order. The central conflict between Solomon's ordained fate and the desire for free will maps directly onto the power system: those who accept the flow wield white Rukh and its associated magic, while those who reject it fall into depravity and wield black Rukh.

This is not a simple morality play. Ohtaka complicates the framework by presenting the Great Flow as a system of control that, however benevolent in intent, denies individual agency. Sinbad's arc culminates in a direct challenge to Solomon's design — not out of depravity but out of a conviction that even a benevolent predestination is still a cage. The Rukh system makes this philosophical argument tangible: you can see fate as glowing particles, and you can choose to reverse their color.

The Magnostadt arc uses the magic system as direct social commentary. Magnostadt is a society where magicians rule and non-magicians are reduced to second-class citizens whose Magoi is literally extracted to fuel the state's magical infrastructure. The Rukh system's stratification between those who can command it and those who merely contain it becomes a metaphor for technocratic governance and the exploitation of the non-specialized majority by a credentialed elite.

Djinn Equip serves a narrative function beyond combat spectacle. Each Djinn Equip visually and thematically transforms the user into a figure from Arabian Nights-inspired mythology, linking the power system to the series' broader project of reimagining and interrogating classical folklore through a shonen lens.
