## Mechanics

Each parahuman is bonded, through brain-attached structures called the Corona Pollentia and Corona Gemma, to a Shard — a fragment of an extradimensional entity. Shards are not gods; they are the dispersed sense-organs of a vast space-faring species ("the Entities") whose civilizational practice is to seed worlds with shards, observe what their bondholders do with the granted powers, and harvest the resulting data when the cycle ends. The setting frames this as a literal experiment running on Earth.

The bond is formed by a "trigger event" — a moment of inescapable suffering, usually traumatic, in which the bondholder's mind crosses a threshold the Shard responds to. The trigger event's details thematically shape the resulting power. A trigger involving claustrophobic helplessness might produce a personal-space-defending barrier power; one involving social humiliation might produce a power that makes the user invisible to scrutiny; one involving losing control of one's body might produce a power that takes control of others'. The book series is built on these resonances.

Once granted, a power is one specific thing. There is no spell-list, no gradual learning into new abilities. A cape with telekinesis has telekinesis; one who can copy abilities can copy abilities; one who can manufacture exotic devices ("Tinker") can manufacture exotic devices, but only of a specific specialization. Within their power's parameters, capes can be enormously creative — the protagonist's bug-control power, initially treated as a low-tier joke, drives much of the series — but they cannot acquire fundamentally new capabilities without retriggering, which is rare, costly, and dangerous.

The Protectorate (the U.S. government's licensed parahuman organization) classifies powers along twelve standard axes: Brute (physical resilience), Mover (mobility), Shaker (area control), Striker (touch-range effect), Blaster (ranged effect), Thinker (information), Master (control of others / minions), Stranger (perception evasion), Tinker (idiosyncratic technology), Trump (powers affecting other powers), Breaker (state-shifting), Changer (form-shifting). Most capes are scored across multiple axes with numerical threat ratings.

A subtler mechanic is "the Manton effect" — a near-universal restriction that prevents a cape's power from directly affecting whichever side of the living/non-living boundary the power does not address. A telekinetic who can move objects cannot use the same power to crush organs; a cape who controls flesh cannot use the same power on inanimate matter. Manton-violations exist (unrestricted capes are extraordinarily dangerous and rare) but are flagged as a serious threat-rating consideration.

## Limitations and costs

The cost structure is unusually layered.

The biological cost is real. Power use is metabolically expensive; sustained or extreme use exhausts the user. Many powers carry idiosyncratic feedback (a regenerator who heals a permanently lost limb feels the original injury indefinitely; a probability-manipulator pays in headaches; a power that copies others has a defined recovery period).

The psychological cost is the system's defining feature. Shards subtly nudge their bondholders toward conflict, because conflict produces data. Capes who use their powers heavily report compulsions toward the conflicts that generated those powers in the first place. Tinkers in particular develop fixations that look like obsession-compulsion. Masters develop creeping dehumanization toward their targets. The "Cauldron formula" subset of capes (those whose powers come from drinking a manufactured serum rather than triggering naturally) often show physical mutations beyond their control. Long-term cape use is, by design, a slow erosion of the user's self.

The social cost is institutional. Capes in the United States either join the Protectorate (licensed, government-funded, public-image-managed) or operate as villains, vigilantes, or rogues. The Protectorate enforces a complex set of unwritten rules — the Endbringer Truce most prominently — and breaches carry real consequences. Outside the U.S. the regulatory regime varies, but parahumans are universally regulated.

A subtler structural cost is that Shards are antagonistic in a specific way. When two capes conflict, their respective Shards in some sense delight in the test. There is a recurring pattern where capes feel, after using their powers in a high-stakes fight, that they "wanted" the fight to escalate.

## Practitioner access

Triggering requires a moment of inescapable suffering that the latent Shard recognizes. Most people never trigger; many who experience comparable suffering do not. The trigger threshold is partly genetic (people with the Corona Pollentia/Gemma — typically inherited from a triggered parent or a related lineage — trigger more easily) and partly contextual. Cauldron, a clandestine organization, also distributes manufactured "vials" of refined Shard material that produce powers without the natural trigger event, at the cost of frequently producing physical mutations.

## Cultural role

In the Wormverse contemporary present, parahumans are common knowledge. The Protectorate, the Wards (the youth division), villain factions, and rogue capes all operate publicly. News media covers cape battles; merchandising exists; civic infrastructure has been adjusted around cape-related risk. The setting has been progressively breaking down under the cumulative weight of cape conflict — entire cities are abandoned, climate has degraded, and "Endbringer" attacks recur every few months. The serialized nature of the work means the social texture is unusually dense; the system is less a backdrop and more the sustained subject.
