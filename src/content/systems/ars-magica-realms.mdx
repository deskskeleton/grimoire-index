## Mechanics

Ars Magica's cosmology is built on **four Realms of Power**: Magic (the source of Hermetic Magic), **Divine** (the power of God, angels, and saints), **Infernal** (the power of Hell, demons, and corruption), and **Faerie** (the power of stories, glamour, and the otherworld). While Hermetic Magic is the primary player-character system, the other three Realms represent supernatural forces that operate by fundamentally different rules.

**The Divine Realm** produces miracles — direct interventions of God's power channeled through saints, holy relics, the sacraments, and divinely inspired individuals. Divine power is absolute within its scope: it cannot be resisted by Hermetic magic, Infernal corruption, or Faerie glamour. A genuinely holy relic's Aegis cannot be bypassed; a true miracle cannot be countermagicked. However, Divine power is not at the practitioner's command — miracles happen when God wills them, not when the saint demands them. This makes Divine power narratively unpredictable: it is the strongest force in the setting but the least controllable.

**The Infernal Realm** produces corruption, temptation, and diabolical power. Infernal magic works through **deception** — it can mimic any other Realm's effects, making it impossible to determine through magical investigation alone whether a given supernatural phenomenon is truly Divine, Magical, or Faerie. A demon can perform false miracles, create false faerie experiences, or produce effects indistinguishable from Hermetic magic. Diabolists gain power through pacts with demons, trading their souls for knowledge, longevity, or supernatural abilities.

**The Faerie Realm** produces glamour — illusions, transformations, and story-logic effects that are real within the context of the story being enacted but may not persist beyond it. Faerie magic operates on **narrative logic**: things happen because the story demands them, not because of physical causation. A faerie sword cuts because it is the right sword for this hero at this moment, not because of its metallurgical properties. Faerie powers are potent but fundamentally unstable — they depend on the maintenance of narrative coherence.

Each Realm produces an **aura** that strengthens its own supernatural effects and weakens others. A church radiates a Divine aura that suppresses Magic and Infernal powers; a faerie grove radiates a Faerie aura; a Hermetic covenant generates a Magic aura. Aura interactions create strategic considerations for all supernatural practitioners.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation is **Realm incompatibility**. Each Realm's power is diminished within another Realm's aura. A Hermetic magus operating in a Divine aura suffers penalties; a faerie creature in a Magic aura is weakened. This creates geographical and situational constraints on supernatural power.

The second cost is **moral and spiritual consequences**. Divine power requires genuine faith and moral conduct — hypocrisy weakens it. Infernal power corrupts the practitioner's soul, leading to damnation. Faerie power binds the practitioner to narrative obligations and faerie bargains that may demand irrational or harmful actions.

The third limitation is **the Infernal deception problem**. Because Infernal power can mimic any other Realm, no practitioner can ever be fully certain that a supernatural phenomenon is what it appears to be. This creates permanent epistemic uncertainty — a core theme of the Ars Magica setting.

## Practitioner access

Divine power is accessible to those of genuine faith — saints, monks, priests, and devout laypeople — but is never under the practitioner's direct control. Infernal power is accessible to anyone willing to make a demonic pact, but the cost is damnation. Faerie power is accessible to those who enter into relationships with the Faerie realm — through bargains, oaths, or by becoming part-faerie themselves. The Merinita house of Hermetic magi specializes in Faerie Magic, integrating faerie methods into the Hermetic framework.

## Cultural role

In Mythic Europe, the four Realms shape the setting's cosmological reality. The Divine Realm's dominance is the official theological position — the Church teaches that God's power supersedes all others. The Infernal Realm is the recognized enemy — demons, witches, and diabolists are actively hunted. The Faerie Realm occupies a liminal position — neither fully endorsed nor fully condemned, faeries are the subject of folklore, fear, and fascination. The Magic Realm (and Hermetic practitioners within it) exists in uneasy tension with all three — tolerated by the Divine (barely), hunted by the Infernal (as targets for corruption), and entangled with the Faerie (as neighbors and sometimes allies).
