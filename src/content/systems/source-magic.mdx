## Mechanics

The Divinity magic system operates on two distinct but interacting layers. The first layer is conventional elemental magic — spells drawn from schools of Pyrokinetic, Hydrosophist, Aerotheurge, Geomancer, Necromancy, Summoning, Polymorph, and others — powered by ability points and cooldown timers in the manner familiar to tactical RPG players. What elevates this layer from competent to exceptional is Larian's commitment to physical simulation. Every spell that contacts the game world leaves a tangible residue: fire spells create burning surfaces, water spells leave puddles, oil spells coat the ground in flammable slick, poison spells generate toxic clouds. These surfaces and fields persist as interactive objects in the game world, subject to further magical or physical manipulation.

The elemental interaction model is where the system's real depth emerges. A rain spell cast on a burning enemy extinguishes the fire but leaves a steam cloud that blocks line of sight. That same rain puddle, if hit with a lightning bolt, electrifies the entire surface and stuns anyone standing in it. Poison clouds can be ignited into explosions, frozen surfaces cause characters to slip and fall, blessed fire creates holy flames that heal allies standing in them. The system produces emergent combinatorial outcomes that the designers could not have exhaustively anticipated — players routinely discover interaction chains that feel like genuine discoveries rather than pre-programmed solutions. This transforms combat encounters from contests of stat optimization into spatial puzzles where terrain manipulation is as important as direct damage.

The second layer, Source magic, operates on a fundamentally different resource economy. Source Points are a finite currency generated by absorbing soul energy — from the spirits of the dead, from living creatures, or from rare environmental reservoirs. Source abilities are qualitatively more powerful than standard spells: a Sourcerer can summon a massive elemental champion, rain meteors across the battlefield, or rewrite the physical laws governing a region. But each use depletes Source Points that cannot be regenerated through rest alone, creating a scarcity dynamic that forces players to make difficult choices about when to deploy their most devastating capabilities.

The mechanical relationship between the two layers creates a satisfying escalation curve within individual encounters. Standard elemental spells establish battlefield conditions — laying surfaces, creating clouds, repositioning enemies — and then Source abilities deliver catastrophic payoffs that exploit those conditions. A player who has spent three turns coating the battlefield in oil, cursing enemies with Decaying Touch, and positioning allies upwind can then unleash a Source-powered Epidemic of Fire that transforms the entire prepared terrain into an inferno. The system rewards patient setup and punishes impulsive resource expenditure, which gives combat encounters a dramatic arc that parallels narrative tension.

## Limitations and costs

The surface interaction system imposes costs through its indiscriminate physics. Friendly fire is not merely possible but nearly unavoidable — a fireball that ignites an oil slick will burn allies standing on it just as readily as enemies. Electrified water stuns everyone in it regardless of allegiance. Poison clouds do not distinguish between the caster's party and their opponents. This environmental indiscriminacy forces players to think spatially about party positioning before committing to elemental combinations, and poorly planned spell sequences can easily cause more harm to the player's team than to the enemy. The system's costs are therefore tactical rather than abstract: the price of a devastating combo is the careful positioning work required to ensure only enemies suffer its effects.

Source magic imposes moral and narrative costs that are unusual for a CRPG resource system. The most reliable method of regenerating Source Points is to consume the souls of spirits or living creatures, an act that is presented as ethically fraught regardless of the target's moral status. Draining a suffering spirit might be framed as merciful release, but draining a hostile enemy's soul feels more predatory, and the game tracks these choices through companion reactions and narrative consequences. The resource economy of Source magic is therefore not merely mechanical but ethical — every powerful Source spell the player casts represents a soul consumed, and the game refuses to let that transaction become invisible through routine.

The Voidwoken represent an existential cost layered on top of the personal ones. In Divinity: Original Sin 2, the use of Source magic attracts Voidwoken — reality-consuming entities from beyond the veil that are drawn to Source energy like sharks to blood. This creates a meta-cost where using Source abilities can trigger additional combat encounters with extremely dangerous enemies, making the decision to spend Source Points a risk calculation as well as a resource management problem. The Magister order's campaign to suppress Sourcery is, within the game's logic, a rational response to this threat: every Sourcerer who goes unchecked is a potential beacon for world-ending invasion.

The cooldown and memory systems impose more conventional mechanical constraints. Characters can only memorize a limited number of spells determined by their Memory attribute, forcing players to curate their loadouts rather than bringing every available tool to every fight. Spell cooldowns prevent the most powerful abilities from being spammed, and the interaction between cooldown timers and surface persistence means that optimal play requires sequencing spells across multiple turns rather than front-loading damage. These constraints work in concert with the environmental system to create encounters where resource management, positioning, timing, and combinatorial awareness all contribute to success.

## Practitioner access

Source magic in the world of Rivellon is a mark of the chosen — literally. Sourcerers are individuals born with an innate connection to Source, the fundamental energy of the divine. This is not a skill that can be learned through study alone; the capacity for Sourcery is either present from birth or it is not. The Godwoken, the player characters of Divinity: Original Sin 2, are specifically identified as individuals whose Source potential marks them as candidates for ascension to divinity itself. This hereditary gating creates a social hierarchy where Sourcerers occupy a precarious position — simultaneously more powerful than ordinary people and more vulnerable to institutional persecution.

The Magister order functions as the primary regulatory body governing magical practice in Rivellon. Operating under the authority of the Divine Order, the Magisters identify, collar, and imprison Sourcerers, ostensibly to prevent them from attracting Voidwoken. Fort Joy, the game's opening setting, is essentially a concentration camp for Sourcerers — a place where those born with magical potential are confined, suppressed, and in many cases subjected to a procedure called "purging" that strips their Source connection entirely. The regulated-licensed status of magic in this world is less a matter of professional certification and more a regime of systematic suppression dressed in the language of public safety.

Standard elemental magic occupies a more ambiguous position. Scholars, merchants, and soldiers throughout Rivellon use basic magical abilities without the stigma attached to Sourcery, suggesting that the capacity for non-Source magic is more widely distributed or at least more socially tolerated. The distinction between "acceptable" elemental magic and "dangerous" Sourcery is one of the game's central tensions — it is never entirely clear whether the Magister order draws this line based on genuine metaphysical difference or political convenience. Characters like Braccus Rex demonstrate that the most dangerous practitioners are those who blur the boundary, using conventional magic as a gateway to Source mastery.

The path to divine ascension — which is the ultimate expression of Source potential — is explicitly framed as a competition. Multiple Godwoken candidates vie for the right to become the next Divine, and their Source abilities develop through a progression that involves absorbing Source from increasingly powerful entities. This competitive framework transforms practitioner access from a binary (Sourcerer or not) into a gradient of power determined by how much Source energy an individual has consumed. The system's deepest implication is uncomfortable: the most powerful Sourcerer is not the most talented or the most disciplined, but the one most willing to devour the souls of others.

## Cultural role

Magic in Rivellon functions as a metaphor for minority persecution and the politics of fear. The Magister order's campaign against Sourcerers maps onto historical patterns of institutional oppression — identifying a population by an innate characteristic, declaring that characteristic dangerous, and then constructing a bureaucratic apparatus of containment and control. Fort Joy is deliberately designed to evoke concentration camps, and the "purging" of Source ability carries overtones of forced assimilation and cultural erasure. Larian does not handle this metaphor with particular subtlety, but the directness serves the game's moral framework: players are meant to recognize the injustice immediately and to feel the weight of operating within a system that treats their fundamental nature as a crime.

The tension between Source magic's genuine dangers and the Magister order's disproportionate response creates the game's most interesting moral territory. The Voidwoken threat is real — uncontrolled Sourcery does attract entities that can devastate entire regions — and several characters in the game make the reasonable argument that some form of regulation is necessary. But the Magisters have gone far beyond proportionate response, using the Voidwoken threat as justification for a totalitarian project that serves the Divine Order's political interests as much as public safety. The player must navigate between the legitimate need for magical governance and the illegitimate exercise of power disguised as protection.

The elemental interaction system carries its own cultural and narrative significance through the way it treats magic as a physical force rather than an abstract resource. In a world where fire spells create real fires and rain spells create real floods, magic cannot be separated from its environmental consequences. A battle fought with elemental magic leaves behind scorched earth, poisoned water, and electrified metal. This physicality means that magical conflict has costs borne by everyone in the vicinity, not just the combatants, which gives the Magister order's anxieties about unregulated magic a visceral dimension — even non-Source elemental magic can be devastating when unleashed without restraint.

The game's theological framework adds a final layer: Source is literally divine energy, and the competition to become the next Divine is a contest to determine who will wield the most fundamental power in the universe. This transforms the cultural politics of magic from a question about minority rights into a question about the nature of divinity itself — whether godhood should be earned through merit, claimed through strength, distributed among many, or abolished entirely. Each of these positions is represented by different characters and factions, and the player's choices about how to use Source magic throughout the game are ultimately choices about what kind of god they want to become, if they want to become one at all.
