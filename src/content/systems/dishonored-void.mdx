## Mechanics

A practitioner gains the Outsider's Mark — a black brand on the back of the left hand — through a personal encounter with the Outsider, a deity-figure who exists in (and personifies) the Void. The Mark grants access to a starting set of powers, which the bearer can expand and upgrade by collecting two crafted resources scattered through the world: **runes** (whalebone-carved tokens that the Outsider responds to) used to unlock and upgrade abilities, and **bone charms** (smaller carved trinkets) that grant minor passive bonuses.

The starter set varies between protagonists but draws from a stable repertoire:

- **Blink / Far Reach** — short-range teleportation (Corvo) or grappling translation (Emily). The signature mobility ability.
- **Dark Vision** — see through walls, reveal hostile sight cones.
- **Possession** — temporarily inhabit a fish, rat, person, or guard, walking the body around.
- **Bend Time** — slow or stop time briefly, with progressively expanded reach as upgrades are purchased.
- **Devouring Swarm** — summon rats to attack and consume targets.
- **Windblast** — kinetic projection that throws targets backward.
- **Doppelgänger** (Emily) — produce a brief duplicate to misdirect or distract.
- **Domino** (Emily) — link multiple targets so anything that happens to one happens to all.
- **Mesmerize** (Emily) — render nearby enemies docile while held.
- **Shadow Walk** (Billie Lurk, *Death of the Outsider*) — transition into a shadow-form.
- **Foresight** (Billie Lurk) — project the senses outward to scout a static frame ahead.
- **Semblance** (Billie Lurk) — temporarily wear another person's face.

Powers cost **Mana**, which regenerates partially after each use and can be replenished fully with an Elixir. Some abilities have cooldowns rather than Mana costs (Bend Time, for instance, is on a timed recovery). The game's physical economy revolves around finding more runes (to unlock more upgrade tiers), more bone charms (for passive synergy), and more Elixirs (to actually deploy abilities in long sequences).

## Limitations and costs

The Mark itself is the gating constraint — power flows only to those the Outsider has chosen, and the criteria are presented as opaque even to him. The Mark is also a permanent identifier: it cannot be hidden from those trained to detect it, and the Overseer Order is institutionally dedicated to identifying and executing the Marked.

Mana is the per-use cost. Heavy ability use depletes the bar quickly, and the regeneration mechanism only refills the bar to a partial threshold without consuming an Elixir. The game is balanced to make Elixirs scarce relative to ambitious play, forcing trade-offs.

The Chaos system is the system's most distinctive cost. The game tracks a Chaos statistic that rises with violence and aggressive ability use; high Chaos shifts the late-game world (more rats, more weepers, darker NPC outcomes, different ending sequences). The Marked are, in effect, paying for their power partly in the moral and political consequences their use imposes on the surrounding setting.

A subtler cost is institutional. The Overseer Order maintains an active inquisition against any sign of the Outsider's influence; certain weapons (the Music Box, the Heart) are tuned specifically to neutralize Marked individuals. Public displays of power are met with lethal force.

A meta-cost worth flagging: in the canonical "low Chaos" path, the Marked is encouraged to use their abilities carefully and sparingly, in keeping with the moral framing that the powers are fundamentally suspect. The fiction and the mechanics align around restraint.

## Practitioner access

Almost entirely closed. The Outsider grants his Mark to perhaps one to four people in any historical generation, by direct encounter and on his own criteria. There is no school, no inheritance, no purchasable initiation. Cult-figures and witches operate at the margins of the system through fragments of Void-touched insight, but the formally Marked are vanishingly rare.

A small number of canonically Marked have appeared across the franchise: Corvo Attano (the *Dishonored* protagonist), Daud (an antagonist later turned playable in *Knife of Dunwall*), Delilah Copperspoon (an antagonist Marked in painting), Emily Kaldwin (Marked at the start of *Dishonored 2*), and Billie Lurk (Marked at the start of *Death of the Outsider*).

## Cultural role

The Empire of the Isles is a culture in which the supernatural is real, common knowledge among the population, and feared. The Overseer Order — the militant religious arm of the state — exists specifically to police any manifestation of the Outsider's influence. Witches, heretics, and any individual showing supernatural ability are subject to summary execution under the Strictures. Marked individuals must therefore operate clandestinely, and the gameplay loop reflects this — the Mark must be hidden, the powers must be used selectively, and detection has consequences.
