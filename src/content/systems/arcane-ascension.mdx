## Mechanics

The attunement system of Kaldwyn is built on a foundational transaction: a person enters one of the Serpent Spires — ancient, tower-like structures of unknown original construction — survives a judgment trial within, and emerges bearing an attunement mark inscribed on their body by one of the Visages, the godlike beings who inhabit each Spire. The mark is both brand and key, granting the bearer access to specific types of mana and a defined set of magical abilities tied to that attunement type.

Each attunement is characterized by the mana types it can access. The setting features a taxonomy of mana varieties — gray (transference), mental, fire, water, air, earth, light, shadow, enhancement, and others — and each attunement grants the ability to draw on a specific combination of two or three mana types. An Elementalist attunement grants fire and air mana; an Enchanter grants transference and mental mana; a Guardian grants life and earth mana. The specific combination determines both the attunement's raw capabilities and the creative space available to the practitioner.

Abilities within each attunement are organized into tiers that unlock as the mage's mana capacity grows. A newly attuned Enchanter can perform basic item identification and minor enchantments; a Sunstone-level Enchanter can create sophisticated magical items with multiple integrated functions; a Citrine-level Enchanter operates at a scale that reshapes how institutions function. The tier system — Quartz, Carnelian, Sunstone, Citrine, and beyond — provides a clear progression ladder that structures both training and social hierarchy.

Enchanting, Corin Cadence's primary attunement specialty, exemplifies the system's depth. As an Enchanter, Corin imbues objects with magical properties by channeling transference and mental mana into structured patterns. The process requires precise understanding of mana flow, material properties, and the interaction between different mana types. Corin's analytical temperament — his tendency to approach every problem as an engineering challenge — makes him unusually effective within this framework, even as his relatively low combat power puts him at a disadvantage in direct confrontation.

Attunement merging represents the system's most dramatic advancement mechanic. Under specific conditions, a mage can fuse two attunements into a single, more powerful hybrid mark. The merged attunement typically gains access to additional mana types and unlocks abilities neither parent attunement possessed independently. Merging is rare, poorly understood, and carries significant risk — failed merges can destroy both attunements or kill the mage outright. Corin's acquisition of the Arbiter attunement alongside his Enchanter mark, and the possibility of merging them, is a recurring thread in the series.

The broader cosmology introduces Dominions — the divine or quasi-divine entities associated with fundamental concepts (the Dominion of Fire, the Dominion of Knowledge, etc.) — and the Visages who serve them. Attunements are, in a real sense, gifts from these beings, and the Visages' criteria for granting specific attunement types during judgment trials remain opaque. This divine dimension adds a layer of mystery to a system that might otherwise feel purely mechanical — the rules are clear, but the rulemakers' intentions are not.

## Limitations and costs

Mana pools are the system's hard constraint. Every attuned mage has a measurable mana capacity that determines how much magic they can perform before exhaustion. This capacity can be expanded through dedicated training — essentially, controlled exercises that stress the mana pool and prompt it to grow — but the process is slow, physically demanding, and subject to diminishing returns. A mage's baseline capacity is partly determined by their attunement type and partly by individual variation, creating a natural hierarchy that training can narrow but rarely eliminate.

Mana scarring is the system's most feared consequence of overuse. A mage who channels mana beyond their safe capacity risks permanent damage to their attunement — scarring that reduces their maximum mana pool, degrades their ability to control specific mana types, or in severe cases destroys the attunement entirely. Scarring cannot be healed by conventional means. The threat of scarring creates a constant tension between ambition and caution: every powerful technique attempted at the edge of one's capacity carries the risk of permanent diminishment.

Attunements impose categorical restrictions. An Enchanter cannot cast combat spells native to the Elementalist attunement regardless of skill level; a Guardian cannot perform the divination functions of a Diviner. These boundaries are absolute, not merely difficult to cross. A mage's attunement defines their role as rigidly as a character class in a tabletop game — a comparison Rowe, with his game design background, clearly intends.

The Spire trials themselves represent a gating mechanism with lethal stakes. Many aspirants die in their judgment trials, and the difficulty is not calibrated to the individual — the Spire presents challenges that it deems appropriate, and those who fail do not receive second chances. This means that the system's practitioners are, by definition, survivors of a genuinely dangerous selection process. Not everyone who enters a Spire emerges with an attunement, and not everyone who enters emerges alive.

Not all attunements are equally useful. Some are oriented toward combat, others toward crafting or support roles. In a society that values military power, combat-oriented attunements carry higher social status, while support attunements — Corin's Enchanter among them — are sometimes dismissed despite their enormous practical utility. This creates a social pressure that the narrative explicitly engages with.

## Practitioner access

The path to attunement begins with the Serpent Spire trials. An aspirant — typically a young person at the threshold of adulthood — enters a Spire and faces a series of challenges that test physical capability, magical aptitude, problem-solving, and moral character. The nature of the challenges varies between Spires and between individual trials; no two aspirants face identical tests. Upon successful completion, the presiding Visage grants an attunement, the specific type determined by criteria that the Visages do not fully disclose.

The attunement type received is not chosen by the aspirant. While an aspirant can express a preference, and while the trial's challenges may be loosely associated with certain attunement types, the Visage's judgment is final. This produces a population of mages whose abilities do not always match their ambitions — a would-be warrior might emerge as a Healer, a scholar might receive a combat attunement. The disconnect between desired and received attunements is a significant source of both personal conflict and narrative tension in the series.

Once attuned, formal training typically follows at one of the continent's academies. Corin attends the Lancaster Academy for the Attuned, where structured curricula develop each student's attunement through mana capacity training, technique instruction, and practical application. Academy training is not strictly necessary — self-taught mages exist — but the institutional framework provides resources, mentorship, and social connections that independent practitioners struggle to replicate.

Some individuals never gain an attunement. The Spire trials are not universally attempted — fear of death is a powerful deterrent — and those who attempt them may fail without dying, emerging unmarked and unable to retry under most circumstances. The unattuned population represents the majority of society, creating a sharp divide between the magically empowered minority and the mundane majority.

## Cultural role

Attunements are the primary determinant of social status and professional opportunity in Kaldwyn's nations. An attuned individual, regardless of their specific mark, occupies a higher social stratum than someone without magic. Within the attuned population, the hierarchy is further stratified by attunement type (combat types outranking support types in prestige, if not always in practical value) and by tier (higher-tier mages command greater respect, authority, and compensation).

The Serpent Spires are simultaneously religious landmarks, military assets, and cultural institutions. They are associated with the Visages who inhabit them, and the Visages are worshipped — or at least deeply respected — as intermediaries of the Dominions. The judgment trials are cultural rites of passage as much as they are practical gating mechanisms. Families with strong martial traditions send their children into the Spires as a matter of honor; families who have lost children in failed trials bear that grief as a social mark.

Enchanters like Corin occupy a critical economic niche. Enchanted items — weapons, armor, tools, infrastructure components — are foundational to the setting's economy and military capacity. A skilled Enchanter can produce items that enhance the capabilities of dozens of other people, making them force multipliers of enormous value despite their lack of personal combat power. The tension between the Enchanter's economic importance and their relatively low social status among attunement types is a running theme in Corin's narrative.

The broader geopolitical landscape is shaped by the distribution of attunements and the strategic control of Spire access. Nations that control Spires control the supply of new mages, giving them a structural advantage in both military and economic terms. The Visages' independence — their refusal to be directed by mortal authorities in granting attunements — is a source of persistent political friction, as governments cannot guarantee that their most promising young people will receive the attunement types the nation needs most.
