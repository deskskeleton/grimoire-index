## Mechanics

The five Signs are simple, single-handed gestural spells, each tied to a defined effect:

- **Aard** — a kinetic burst that knocks back targets, breaks objects, and clears small obstructions.
- **Igni** — a brief gout of fire, useful against vulnerable creatures and for igniting flammables.
- **Quen** — a momentary protective shield that absorbs incoming damage; duration is short but can be re-cast.
- **Yrden** — a placed magical trap or zone that slows, weakens, or pins anything that enters it.
- **Axii** — a short-range mind-influence effect, used in combat to confuse opponents and (in fiction) sometimes as a social tool.

Each Sign is invoked through a specific hand gesture (the in-fiction "weaver's mark") combined with a brief mental focus. Casting takes a beat — fast enough for combat use, slow enough that it cannot be interleaved with continuous swordplay without timing. Witchers train the Signs in their formative years at the Schools (Wolf, Cat, Griffin, Viper, Bear, Manticore in various canonical sources) alongside swordsmanship, alchemy, and monster-lore.

The CD Projekt Red game adaptations add explicit mechanical structure: each Sign has a stamina or "intensity" stat that can be raised by gear and upgrades, two alternative casting modes (a quick flick vs. a longer charged version), and skill trees that expand each Sign's behavior (Igni's "Firestream", Quen's "Active Shield", Yrden's "Magic Trap"). The novels are looser, treating the Signs as a small toolkit used pragmatically.

## Limitations and costs

The Signs are intentionally modest. They are not capable of the regional or strategic effects a trained sorceress can produce; a witcher in a fight against a senior mage is straightforwardly outmatched at range. The trade-off is reliability and combat speed — Signs cost little to cast, do not require formal incantation, and can be deployed in the middle of swordwork.

Sign use is bounded by the witcher's own physiology and training. The mutation process that creates witchers includes a magical component that gates Sign access, but Sign strength is not greatly elevated beyond the human baseline. Overuse leaves a witcher fatigued. The novels are explicit that a witcher who tries to lean on Signs in lieu of swords will lose to a competent opponent.

A second cost is professional. Witchers are a regulated, eyed-with-suspicion caste across most of the Continent. Public Sign use draws attention, and in some cities and at some courts, casting at all is grounds for trouble.

## Practitioner access

Sign access is gated by the witcher mutation process — the survivors of the Trial of the Grasses, an alchemical-magical procedure performed on candidates (usually orphans) at the witcher Schools. The mutation produces sterility, slowed aging, enhanced reflexes, modified pupils, and a magical reservoir small enough to power Signs but not full sorcery. The procedure is dangerous and historically had a high fatality rate. The Schools' formulae are mostly lost in the contemporary fiction, and the witcher caste is in slow decline.

A handful of canonical exceptions exist (notably Ciri in the game adaptation), but as a rule of thumb, only mutated witchers cast Signs.

## Cultural role

Across the Continent, witchers occupy a marginal but recognized professional niche — itinerant monster-hunters whose Signs are part of their distinctive toolkit. The Signs themselves are widely known of, but rarely seen by ordinary citizens, and are generally treated as an unsettling oddity rather than as a respected magical tradition. The Continent's full sorcery, organized around academies like Aretuza and Ban Ard and the Brotherhood of Sorcerers, is the much-larger and culturally dominant magic — the Signs are a footnote within that broader landscape.
