## Mechanics

The world of **Disboard** is governed by the **Ten Pledges** — ten absolute divine laws imposed by **Tet**, the One True God, after he won the ancient war among the gods and claimed dominion over the world. These Pledges are not guidelines or social contracts; they are metaphysically enforced rules woven into the fabric of reality itself. Breaking a Pledge is not merely forbidden — it is impossible. The laws cannot be circumvented through power, cleverness, or divine authority; they are the operating system of the world.

The Ten Pledges establish a framework in which **all conflict is resolved through games**. The key provisions: all murder, war, and robbery are forbidden; all disputes between individuals or nations must be settled by games; the game and the stakes must be agreed upon by both parties before play begins; the wager must be of equivalent value from each side; the terms and rules of the game are enforced by the Pledges once agreed; cheating is permitted as long as neither party can prove the other is cheating; any bet made in the name of the Pledges must be honored; all conflicts conducted under the Pledges are binding and permanent; and Tet's authority as god is absolute.

This framework transforms Disboard from a high-fantasy world into a **game-theory arena**. Nations do not wage wars — they wage games, betting territory, resources, populations, and even their race's collective existence (through **race pieces**, sacred artifacts that represent each of the Sixteen Exceeds). A single game of chess, poker, or any agreed-upon contest can determine the fate of millions.

The **Sixteen Exceeds** are the ranked races of Disboard, ordered by magical power. At the top sit the **Old Deus** (gods) and the **Phantasma** (divine constructs); at the bottom sits **Imanity** (humanity), ranked sixteenth — the only race with no inherent magical ability. Between them are races like the **Flugel** (angelic warriors of devastating power, represented by Jibril), the **Elves** (masters of magical manipulation), the **Warbeasts** (Izuna's race, with superhuman physical abilities and enhanced senses), the **Dhampir** (vampiric seducers), and the **Siren** (oceanic illusionists), among others.

Each Exceed's innate magical abilities remain fully functional under the Pledges — they simply cannot be used for direct violence or theft outside of sanctioned games. Within games, however, magical abilities become tools for gaining advantage. Elves use magic to read opponents' thoughts, manipulate probability, or alter the game environment. Flugel bring near-omniscient knowledge and spatial manipulation. Warbeasts exploit superhuman reflexes and senses. The Pledges do not forbid using these abilities in games; they only forbid being caught cheating. This creates an intricate metagame: every magical race uses its powers to bend, exploit, or borderline-violate the rules, while simultaneously trying to catch opponents doing the same.

**Imanity's position** is unique and desperate. With no magical ability whatsoever, humans cannot detect magical cheating, cannot enhance their game performance supernaturally, and cannot compete on equal footing with any other race. Before Sora and Shiro's arrival, Imanity had been losing territory steadily for millennia, reduced to a single city. Sora and Shiro's genius lies in exploiting what no other race considers: pure intellect, psychological manipulation, game theory, information asymmetry, and the willingness to bet everything on a single calculated gamble.

## Limitations and costs

**Knowledge and preparation** are the primary costs. In a world where games determine everything, intelligence and information are the most valuable resources. Sora and Shiro spend enormous effort researching opponents, analyzing game structures, identifying exploitable loopholes, and constructing multi-layered strategies that account for magical interference they cannot directly perceive. The cost of a game is not mana or physical strain — it is the intellectual labor of preparing to win against opponents who can warp reality.

**Reciprocal balance** is structurally enforced. The Pledges require equivalent wagers from both sides, meaning that any game carries symmetrical risk. Sora and Shiro cannot challenge a nation for its territory without putting something of equivalent value at stake — which, for Imanity's last city, means risking total extinction. Every victory carries the weight of what was risked to achieve it.

**Social costs** pervade the system. Games are public, stakes are known, and losses are permanent. A king who gambles away territory faces the political consequences; a race that loses its race piece faces extinction. The social pressure of making decisions with civilizational stakes shapes behavior — most Exceeds play conservatively, avoiding high-stakes games unless they are confident of victory. Sora and Shiro's willingness to accept catastrophic risk is what makes them terrifying opponents.

**Permanence** is the system's sharpest edge. The Pledges enforce outcomes absolutely. A lost bet cannot be renegotiated, appealed, or reversed through force. When a nation loses territory in a game, the land changes hands instantly and irrevocably. When a person bets their freedom or their memories, the loss is real and permanent. This irreversibility gives every game genuine dramatic weight — there are no do-overs in Disboard.

## Practitioner access

The system is **universally accessible** in theory — anyone can propose or accept a game, and the Pledges apply equally to all beings on Disboard. In practice, however, access to meaningful power within the system is radically unequal.

The Sixteen Exceeds are ranked by innate magical capability, and this ranking directly correlates with game-playing advantage. Higher-ranked races can use more powerful magic to enhance their play, detect opponents' strategies, manipulate probability, and create favorable conditions. Lower-ranked races have fewer tools and must rely more on native intelligence and skill.

Imanity's position at rank sixteen means humans have zero supernatural resources. Their only advantages are cognitive: pattern recognition, psychological insight, deductive reasoning, and the capacity for deception. Sora and Shiro represent the absolute peak of human capability — game prodigies whose combined analytical and intuitive skills allow them to compete with beings that have been playing magical games for millennia.

Between the Exceeds, power dynamics are complex. The Elves' magical sophistication makes them dominant in information-based games. The Warbeasts' physical enhancement gives them edges in reaction-based and athletic contests. The Flugel's near-omniscience makes them formidable in knowledge-based challenges. Each race gravitates toward game types that favor its natural strengths, creating an ecosystem of competitive specialization.

## Cultural role

The Ten Pledges have **restructured Disboard's entire civilization** around games. There is no military, no police force, no judicial system in the conventional sense — all functions that violence would serve in other worlds are replaced by games. Territorial disputes are card games. Criminal justice is trial by contest. Economic negotiations are betting frameworks. The Pledges have not merely prohibited violence; they have replaced it with a complete alternative social infrastructure.

This creates a world that is simultaneously utopian and ruthlessly competitive. There is no war, no murder, no theft — but there is constant, high-stakes competition where the losers can lose everything. The system rewards intelligence, preparation, and the willingness to take calculated risks. It punishes ignorance, carelessness, and emotional decision-making.

The narrative uses this framework for pointed **social commentary**. Disboard is a meritocracy taken to its logical extreme — a world where the strongest mind wins, regardless of birth, wealth, or physical power. But it is also a world where structural inequality (the Exceed ranking system) means that some races start with enormous advantages that no amount of cleverness can easily overcome. Sora and Shiro's campaign to unite the Exceeds under Imanity's banner is simultaneously a power fantasy (the weakest race conquering the strongest) and a critique of systems that rank beings by innate rather than demonstrated capability.

The games themselves serve as the story's primary **problem-solving engine**. Each major arc is structured around a game — its rules, its hidden mechanics, the opponent's strategy, and Sora and Shiro's plan to exploit the gap between what the rules say and what the rules actually permit. The Ten Pledges are not just a magic system; they are the narrative's structural skeleton, providing the constraints that make every victory meaningful.
