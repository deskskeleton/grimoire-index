## Mechanics

The magic of *Last Call* operates through the correspondence between Tarot symbolism and real archetypal forces. A standard poker deck is a degraded Tarot deck — the suits map to cups, swords, wands, and coins, and the face cards retain echoes of the Major Arcana. When a poker game is played with a Tarot-derived deck (or with sufficient symbolic intention), the game becomes a ritual in which players stake not merely money but identity and fate.

The central mechanism is **Assumption** — the act of taking over another person's body and identity. Georges Leon, the novel's antagonist, has maintained himself across decades by playing rigged poker games in which the losing players unknowingly forfeit their bodies. Leon plays the role of the King — specifically, the Fisher King of the Waste Land — and the game enacts a mythic succession in which the old king consumes the young challenger rather than yielding the throne. The cards dealt correspond to archetypal roles (the Fool, the Moon, the Tower), and the outcome of the game determines which archetype each player is forced to inhabit.

The **Fisher King** office is a real metaphysical position. The holder's spiritual condition affects the land itself — when Leon holds the throne through illegitimate means (consumption rather than genuine succession), the desert around Las Vegas becomes a wasteland in more than the geological sense. Restoring the land requires a legitimate king to win the throne through a properly enacted game.

The magic extends beyond card games into a broader pattern of symbolic resonance. Reflections, doubles, and mirrors carry magical significance. Bodies of water serve as conduits. The dead can be contacted through beer (the grain-and-water symbolism connecting to Osiris and the vegetation gods). Nothing in Powers' system is arbitrary — every magical rule connects to an underlying mythic logic — but the logic is associative and poetic rather than systematic.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **identity itself**. Players in the Assumption games risk losing their bodies, their memories, and their sense of self. Scott Crane, the protagonist, was partially consumed in a game played when he was a child and has spent his adult life as an incomplete person — missing some essential core of selfhood that was taken from him at the card table.

The system operates through **compulsion rather than choice**. Once caught in the symbolic pattern, participants find themselves drawn to act out their archetypal roles whether they want to or not. The Fool must wander. The King must defend. The pattern constrains free will, and resisting it requires enormous psychological effort.

There is a deep cost of **sanity**. Practitioners who engage with the system too deeply begin to lose the boundary between symbol and reality. Georges Leon is functionally insane — he has consumed so many identities that his own personality is a palimpsest of stolen selves. Other characters experience hallucinations, compulsions, and a dissolving sense of where their own identity ends and the archetypal role begins.

The magic is also **unpredictable**. Because it operates through symbolic resonance rather than direct causation, effects manifest as coincidence, accident, and pattern rather than as controllable phenomena. A practitioner can set up the conditions for a magical outcome but cannot guarantee the specific form it will take.

## Practitioner access

There is no school or tradition for this magic. Georges Leon learned his craft from fragmentary occult sources and decades of experimentation. The knowledge is obscure, scattered across Tarot lore, Arthurian legend, and vegetation-myth scholarship. Most people who encounter the system do so as victims rather than as practitioners — they are drawn into a rigged game without understanding what is being wagered. The few who practice it deliberately are self-taught obsessives who have pieced together the rules from mythic and occult fragments. Valyrian-style hereditary access does not apply; what matters is knowledge, willingness, and proximity to the archetypal patterns.

## Cultural role

The magic is invisible to the broader culture. Las Vegas is built on games of chance, and the supernatural poker games hide in plain sight within the city's gambling culture. The Fisher King's influence on the land manifests as drought, urban decay, and a pervasive sense of spiritual emptiness that residents attribute to natural causes. The few people who understand what is happening operate in isolation — there is no magical community, no institutional knowledge, no tradition to draw on. The magic is a secret history layered beneath the documented world, visible only to those who have been touched by it directly.
