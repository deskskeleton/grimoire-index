## Mechanics

I Eat Tomatoes constructed one of the earliest and most influential Chinese web novel power systems across his major works, with Coiling Dragon and Stellar Transformations sharing an interconnected cosmology. The central advancement mechanism is comprehension of the universe's fundamental Laws — abstract principles governing reality that practitioners can perceive, study, and eventually wield. This emphasis on understanding over accumulation distinguishes the system from resource-driven cultivation frameworks.

In Coiling Dragon, the mortal tier follows two parallel tracks: the warrior path (physical cultivation through training and body refinement) and the magus path (magical cultivation through spiritual development and elemental study). Warriors progress from ranks 1 through 9 and then into the Saint realm; magi follow a parallel ladder from rank 1 magus through the ninth rank and into Saint-level. The Saint realm represents the peak of mortal power, where practitioners can fly, destroy cities, and live for millennia. Linley Baruch, the protagonist, pursues both paths simultaneously — an unusual choice enabled by his Dragonblood Warrior lineage, which gives him a transformable body that bridges physical and magical cultivation.

Beyond the Saint realm, advancement requires comprehending the profound mysteries within one of the universe's seven fundamental Laws (Earth, Fire, Water, Wind, Lightning, Light, Darkness) or four fundamental Edicts (Life, Death, Fate, Destruction). Each Law or Edict contains multiple "profound mysteries" — sub-aspects that represent specific expressions of the broader principle. Earth, for example, contains mysteries related to gravitational force, the pulse of the earth, the essence of stone, and others. A cultivator who fully comprehends a single mystery becomes a Demigod; comprehending all mysteries within a Law and fusing them together produces a Highgod.

The Higher Planes form the setting for post-mortal cultivation. When a Deity dies in the material plane or willingly ascends, they arrive in one of the seven Divine Planes (corresponding to the Laws) or the four Higher Planes (corresponding to the Edicts). These are entire planes of existence populated by Deities, where mortal-level beings are insignificant. The social structure of the Higher Planes revolves around the Sovereign — beings of immense power who rule through divine authority granted by Sovereign sparks, unique artifacts left over from the primordial creation of the cosmos.

Sovereign sparks are the central bottleneck of the upper system. Only a fixed number exist — seven for each element (one each at the Lesser, Intermediate, and Chief tiers, plus four additional Lesser sparks). Absorbing a Sovereign spark instantly elevates a Highgod to Sovereign status, granting power that dwarfs anything achievable through Law comprehension alone. But sparks are nearly impossible to obtain; most are already held by existing Sovereigns, and acquiring one typically means killing its current holder. Beyond Sovereign lies the Grandmist realm, occupied by a single entity — Hongmeng — who represents the universe's primordial unified state before Laws differentiated.

Linley's unique cultivation advantage is his Dragonblood Warrior heritage, which gives him four elemental souls (earth, wind, fire, water) instead of the normal one. Each soul can independently comprehend a different Law, allowing him to cultivate four paths simultaneously. His eventual achievement of fusing four distinct Law comprehensions — a feat thought impossible — represents the novel's climactic power breakthrough.

## Limitations and costs

Law comprehension requires time on a scale that makes mortal lifespans irrelevant. A Demigod might spend ten thousand years comprehending a single additional mystery. Highgods who have fused multiple mysteries may require millions of years to complete their Law mastery. The system is fundamentally patience-gated — no amount of combat talent or resource expenditure can substitute for the slow, meditative process of perceiving abstract truths about reality. This creates a setting where the most powerful beings are also the oldest, and youth is synonymous with weakness at the upper tiers.

Sovereign sparks are an artificially limited resource. Their fixed number means that advancement to Sovereign status is, in the strictest sense, zero-sum. For every being who becomes a Sovereign, another must have lost their spark (typically through death). The Planar Wars — periodic conflicts orchestrated by the Sovereigns themselves — serve as a culling mechanism that keeps the multiverse's power dynamics in flux. Participation in the Planar Wars is often compelled rather than voluntary, making them a systemic tax on the Deity population.

The Higher Planes are not safe havens. Highgods routinely kill Demigods for their spark-fragments or divine artifacts. Bandits, tyrants, and petty warlords operate throughout the planes with little restraint, because at the Deity level there is no centralized law enforcement — only the power hierarchies established by Sovereigns and their servants. Survival in the Higher Planes requires either overwhelming power or the protection of a faction strong enough to deter attacks.

Transcending to Grandmist is effectively impossible. The novel treats it as a feat that has occurred exactly once in the universe's history, making it more a cosmological fact than a practical aspiration. Even complete mastery of all Laws and Edicts does not approach Grandmist-level power; the gap between Sovereign and Grandmist is presented as categorically different from the gap between any other adjacent tiers.

Soul mutations — the rare occurrence of a being possessing souls attuned to multiple elements — offer an alternate path to extraordinary power but are vanishingly rare and cannot be artificially induced. Linley's four-element soul is presented as a unique or nearly unique configuration, reinforcing that some advantages in this system truly are gifts of birth.

## Practitioner access

The warrior and magus paths are, in principle, open to anyone. Physical training requires no innate magical talent, and even magus cultivation can begin with minimal natural affinity, though progression will be slower. The material plane (where the story begins) has academies, dojos, and apprenticeship systems that provide training to anyone who can access them. Wealth and social station help — noble families have better training resources — but the barriers to entry are economic, not metaphysical.

However, genetic lineage matters enormously at the upper tiers. The Dragonblood Warrior lineage gives Linley physical transformation abilities, enhanced elemental affinity, and the critical four-element soul that enables his unique cultivation path. The Four Divine Beast clans — Azure Dragon, White Tiger, Vermilion Bird, Black Tortoise — each possess inherited abilities that provide significant advantages in Law comprehension. The novel does not frame this as unfair; it simply treats bloodline as one variable among many, albeit a powerful one.

Divine sparks can be absorbed to shortcut the early Deity stages — a killed Deity's spark fragment can be consumed by a mortal-realm cultivator to gain instant Demigod-level power in that element. However, spark-absorbed advancement is considered inferior to natural comprehension, because the absorber gains power without understanding, creating a ceiling on their further progress. The system thus rewards earned comprehension over inherited or stolen power, even though it acknowledges that starting advantages are real.

Access to the Higher Planes is automatic upon becoming a Deity or dying as a Saint-level practitioner. There is no gatekeeper; the planes are simply where Deities exist. This creates a situation where newly arrived Deities — fresh from the mortal plane with no connections, resources, or understanding of Higher Plane politics — are extremely vulnerable.

## Cultural role

The Higher Planes function as complete societies organized around power. Sovereign-level beings maintain organizations of loyal Highgods, control territory, and engage in political maneuvering that spans millions of years. The Four Divine Beast clans, to which Linley belongs through his Dragonblood lineage, are one such faction — once dominant, now diminished, their survival dependent on producing a member capable of reaching the highest tiers.

The relationship between mortals and Deities is one of absolute asymmetry. Mortals in the material plane may worship Deities or Sovereigns as gods, but to the Deities themselves, mortals are background noise. The novel does not sentimentalize this divide — Linley's attachments to his mortal family and home are treated as unusual and somewhat puzzling to his Deity-level peers. The system naturalizes hierarchy as a consequence of power differential.

Sovereign-level politics drive the macro-narrative. Sovereigns maintain alliances and enmities that have persisted for hundreds of millions of years. Their conflicts play out through proxy wars, Planar Wars, and the strategic manipulation of their subordinate organizations. The Sovereigns of Light and Darkness, for example, maintain a rivalry that shapes the entire structure of two Higher Planes, with consequences cascading down through every level of the power hierarchy.

The multiverse cosmology provides the series with its characteristic sense of expanding scope. What begins as a story about a noble boy training to become a warrior in a medieval kingdom gradually expands to encompass an entire material plane, then multiple Higher Planes, then the Sovereign tier of cosmic politics, and finally the Grandmist level of primordial reality. Each expansion recontextualizes the stakes, transforming existential threats into minor inconveniences as the protagonist ascends into a larger arena. This telescoping structure became a template for an entire generation of Chinese web novels.
