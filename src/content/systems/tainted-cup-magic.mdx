## Mechanics

Grafting is the process by which citizens of the Empire of Khanum undergo biological augmentation using substances harvested from the colossal sea-leviathans that periodically threaten the empire's coastal walls. Leviathan tissue, fluids, and biological compounds are processed and introduced into the human body through medical procedures overseen by imperial specialists. The grafting process permanently alters the recipient's physiology, integrating leviathan-derived biology into human tissue to produce enhanced capabilities.

The resulting augmentations vary widely depending on the type of graft and the biological material used. Dinios Kol has been grafted with an enhanced memory that allows him to recall everything he observes with perfect fidelity — an ability that makes him an ideal investigator's assistant. Ana Dolabra has received cognitive grafts that amplify her analytical abilities to superhuman levels, allowing her to solve complex cases through pure deduction. Other grafts enhance physical strength, sensory perception, endurance, or specialised biological functions.

The leviathans themselves are quasi-magical ecological forces of enormous scale. Their periodic landfall events threaten to destroy entire regions, and the empire's defensive infrastructure — massive seawalls, military deployments, and biological countermeasures — exists primarily to survive these events. The leviathans transform the environment around them, and the substances derived from their bodies carry echoes of that transformative power.

Grafting is not a mystical art but an institutional technology — controlled by the empire, administered by trained professionals, and distributed according to imperial priorities. Soldiers receive combat grafts, investigators receive cognitive or sensory grafts, and labourers may receive physical enhancements. The system is deeply embedded in the empire's social hierarchy and power structures.

## Limitations and costs

The most visible cost of grafting is physical transformation. Graft recipients do not simply gain abilities — their bodies change to accommodate the leviathan biology integrated into them. Skin may become bark-like or chitinous, eyes may become compound or multifaceted, limbs may reshape, and the overall appearance shifts away from the baseline human. The more extensive the grafting, the more dramatic the transformation. Recipients become visibly, permanently altered.

The process carries significant risk. Not all grafts take successfully, and the interaction between leviathan biology and human physiology is imperfectly understood. Some grafts produce unintended side effects — sensory overload, cognitive distortion, physical pain, or biological instability. Ana Dolabra's cognitive enhancements, for example, come with constraints that limit her ability to function in ordinary social situations, effectively confining her to her estate.

There is also an ecological dimension to the cost. The empire's entire civilisation depends on harvesting creatures that are also existential threats. The relationship between humanity and the leviathans is exploitative and precarious — the empire needs them for the raw materials of grafting, but their landfall events kill thousands and threaten to overwhelm the coastal defences.

## Practitioner access

Grafting is administered through imperial institutions rather than learned individually. Recipients do not train to use magic — they undergo medical procedures and then adapt to their new capabilities. Access is determined by imperial need, social position, and the availability of leviathan-derived materials. The system produces a population stratified by augmentation: heavily grafted elites and specialists, lightly grafted workers, and ungrafted ordinary citizens.

Training after grafting focuses on learning to use enhanced capabilities effectively rather than on developing magical skill. Kol must learn to manage his perfect memory; Dolabra has structured her entire life around the demands of her augmented cognition. The skill lies in application, not in the augmentation itself.

## Cultural role

Grafting is so deeply embedded in Khanum's civilisation that it shapes every aspect of imperial life — military structure, professional specialisation, social hierarchy, and the empire's relationship with the natural world. The leviathans are simultaneously the empire's greatest threat and its most vital resource, creating a civilisation built on a paradox of dependence and existential danger.

The system raises questions about bodily autonomy, the costs of enhancement, and the ethics of building a society on the exploitation of living creatures. Bennett uses grafting to examine how imperial power structures distribute both the benefits and the costs of transformation — who gets the best grafts, who bears the risks, and who profits from the harvesting of the leviathans that make it all possible.
