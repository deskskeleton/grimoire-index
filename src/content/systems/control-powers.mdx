## Mechanics

Paranatural abilities in the world of Control originate from exposure to and resonance with Altered World Events (AWEs) — points where the boundary between ordinary reality and extradimensional forces thins, allowing paranatural energy to bleed through. These events produce two categories of anomalous objects: Altered Items, which possess a single paranatural property and require containment, and Objects of Power, which are connected to the Astral Plane and can bond with psychically receptive individuals, granting them specific telekinetic or reality-warping abilities. The Federal Bureau of Control exists to identify, contain, and study these phenomena, operating from the Oldest House — a Place of Power in Manhattan whose internal geometry defies Euclidean constraints.

The Service Weapon represents the system's most distinctive magical artifact — a shape-shifting firearm bound to the Director of the FBC through a paranatural selection process mediated by the Board, entities from the Astral Plane who communicate through the Hotline in grammatically fractured, poetically oblique language. The weapon transforms between configurations (Grip, Shatter, Spin, Pierce, Charge) that correspond to different combat functions, drawing ammunition from an ambient energy source that regenerates automatically. This self-replenishing design means the Service Weapon operates outside normal logistical constraints, its paranatural nature expressed through the impossibility of its mechanical function.

Jesse Faden's telekinetic abilities — Launch, Shield, Seize, Evade, and Levitate — are acquired through binding rituals with specific Objects of Power scattered throughout the Oldest House. Each binding requires Jesse to enter the Astral Plane and complete a psychic trial, proving her resonance with the object and her capacity to channel its power. Launch allows her to telekinetically hurl environmental objects with devastating force. Shield materializes a barrier from debris. Seize turns weakened enemies into temporary allies. Levitate grants sustained flight. Each ability is discrete and acquired sequentially, building a toolkit that grows in tactical versatility as Jesse penetrates deeper into the Bureau's compromised facility.

The Hiss — the game's primary antagonistic force — represents a corrupted paranatural resonance that functions as a kind of weaponized memetic frequency. Originating from a slide projector that opened a gateway to another dimension, the Hiss is a resonance-based entity that converts human consciousness into a collective hostile force. Infected individuals chant a distorted incantation and display levitation and telekinetic powers derived from the Hiss frequency, demonstrating that paranatural ability is not inherently aligned with any moral valence — the same forces that empower the Director can be weaponized as an instrument of psychic annihilation. The Hiss Incantation itself functions as both a memetic attack vector and a piece of found poetry.

## Limitations and costs

The sanity and identity costs of paranatural engagement form the system's most pervasive limitation. Prolonged exposure to Altered Items and paranatural phenomena erodes psychological stability, a risk the Bureau manages through containment protocols and Hedron Resonance Amplifiers that provide psychic shielding. Dylan Faden's trajectory illustrates the extreme end of this spectrum — his childhood exposure to the Ordinary AWE and subsequent Bureau containment left him psychologically fractured and eventually susceptible to Hiss corruption. The Bureau's institutional history is littered with personnel who lost their minds to paranatural exposure, their fates documented in the collectible case files that function as both worldbuilding and cautionary narrative.

The attention-concentration demands of telekinetic abilities create tactical limitations during combat encounters. Jesse cannot simultaneously deploy all her abilities — launching objects requires dropping her shield, levitation consumes the same energy pool as other abilities, and the Service Weapon must regenerate between engagements. This shared energy economy means that paranatural power is a finite resource in moment-to-moment combat, requiring the practitioner to make constant tactical decisions about which abilities to deploy and when to conserve. The regeneration mechanic prevents total resource exhaustion but imposes a rhythm of engagement and recovery that prevents sustained maximum-power output.

The moral-spiritual costs of the system manifest through the institutional framework of the Bureau itself. The FBC's mission to contain and control paranatural phenomena requires treating anomalous objects and events as threats to be managed rather than mysteries to be understood. This institutional posture carries ethical weight — the Bureau has imprisoned individuals with paranatural abilities, suppressed public knowledge of AWEs, and maintained a culture of secrecy that prioritizes institutional control over transparency. Jesse's tenure as Director forces her to navigate these moral compromises, wielding paranatural power within a bureaucratic structure that was designed to suppress the very forces she channels.

The environmental dependency of paranatural abilities ties practitioners to specific Places of Power and resonance conditions. The Oldest House's shifting architecture determines which areas are accessible and which abilities function in specific contexts. Thresholds — paranatural barriers that separate sectors of the building — require specific clearance to traverse. The Astral Plane, while accessible to those with sufficient resonance, is a hostile environment governed by the Board's inscrutable agenda. These spatial constraints mean that paranatural power is not freely portable — it exists in relationship with specific locations, objects, and dimensional conditions, making the practitioner's environment as important as their personal capability.

## Practitioner access

Access to paranatural abilities in the world of Control is governed by psychic receptivity — an innate quality that determines an individual's capacity to resonate with Altered Items, Objects of Power, and extradimensional entities. This receptivity is not trained or developed but discovered, typically through exposure to an Altered World Event that catalyzes latent paranatural potential. Jesse and Dylan Faden's childhood encounter with the Ordinary AWE — an event triggered by a slide projector that opened doorways to other dimensions — awakened psychic abilities that the Bureau subsequently identified, studied, and in Dylan's case, attempted to weaponize through its Prime Candidate program.

The Director selection process represents the system's most exclusive form of practitioner access. The Service Weapon, resting in the Director's office, chooses its wielder through a paranatural bonding process that cannot be forced or faked. When Jesse picks up the weapon and it bonds to her, she is simultaneously confirmed as Director and granted access to the Bureau's institutional authority and paranatural resources. This selection is mediated by the Board — Astral Plane entities whose criteria for choosing a Director are never fully articulated, making the process feel less like a job application and more like a cosmic appointment. The previous Director, Trench, was similarly chosen, suggesting a lineage of paranatural selection that predates and supersedes the Bureau's institutional hierarchy.

The Bureau's Ranger and Agent personnel occupy a middle ground between full paranatural practitioners and ordinary humans. These individuals are trained to operate in paranatural environments and handle Altered Items according to strict containment protocols, but most lack the psychic receptivity necessary to bond with Objects of Power or channel telekinetic abilities. Their access to the paranatural is mediated entirely through institutional training and protective equipment — Hedron Resonance Amplifiers shield them from Hiss corruption, and standardized protocols govern their interaction with anomalous phenomena. This creates a two-tier practitioner structure: the psychically gifted who wield paranatural power directly, and the institutionally trained who manage it through procedure.

Ahti occupies a position entirely outside the Bureau's access framework. The Oldest House's Finnish janitor demonstrates paranatural knowledge and abilities that exceed anything the Bureau's institutional categories can accommodate — he traverses areas of the building that should be inaccessible, appears and disappears without explanation, and provides cryptic guidance that proves unerringly accurate. Whether Ahti is a paranatural entity, an exceptionally powerful psychic, or something the Bureau's taxonomy has no category for is never resolved. His presence suggests that the system of paranatural access is far broader and stranger than the institutional framework of the FBC can map, and that the Bureau's confident categorization of paranatural phenomena conceals a fundamental ignorance about the nature and extent of what they are dealing with.

## Cultural role

Control's paranatural system functions as an extended metaphor for the relationship between institutional authority and phenomena that resist institutional comprehension. The Federal Bureau of Control applies the vocabulary and procedures of government bureaucracy — containment protocols, clearance levels, interdepartmental memos, case files — to forces that are fundamentally incompatible with bureaucratic rationality. The Oldest House's impossible architecture, which shifts and reconfigures according to rules the Bureau can document but never fully predict, embodies this tension. The building is simultaneously a government office and a living anomaly, and the Bureau's attempts to impose administrative order on its shifting corridors produce a distinctly contemporary form of cosmic horror: the horror of institutional inadequacy.

The masquerade-hidden nature of the paranatural establishes the game's relationship with conspiracy fiction and government secrecy tropes. The general public has no knowledge of AWEs, Objects of Power, or the Oldest House's true nature, maintained through the Bureau's active suppression of information and the Oldest House's own paranatural property of being imperceptible to those not specifically seeking it. This secrecy framework positions paranatural ability as something the state monopolizes and manages — not because the supernatural is inherently dangerous, but because public awareness would undermine the institutional control structures that define the Bureau's purpose. The game thus asks whether containment serves the public or merely preserves the institution.

The aesthetic fusion of New Weird fiction, brutalist architecture, and government procedure creates a cultural context for paranatural power that is distinctly modern and distinctly American. The SCP Foundation influence is evident in the system's approach to anomalous classification, but Control translates that collaborative internet fiction into a singular authored vision where the weirdness is filtered through the specific texture of mid-century American federal architecture and Cold War institutional culture. The result is a magic system that feels less like fantasy and more like a paranormal extension of existing government infrastructure — as if the Department of Defense had a sister agency for managing impossible physics.

The relationship between Jesse and the Board encapsulates the system's thematic core: the tension between individual agency and institutional authority in the face of forces beyond human comprehension. The Board communicates through the Hotline in language that is simultaneously authoritative and incomprehensible, issuing directives that Jesse must interpret and execute without fully understanding their purpose. This dynamic mirrors the experience of operating within any large bureaucracy — following procedures whose rationale is opaque, trusting institutional authority whose foundations are invisible, and wielding power whose ultimate source remains mysterious. In Control, magic is not an escape from institutional life but its most extreme expression: power granted by entities you cannot understand, exercised within structures you cannot fully navigate, toward ends you cannot entirely perceive.
