## Mechanics

**Potions** is one of the core magical disciplines taught at Hogwarts (and other wizarding schools), distinguished from wand-based magic by its reliance on **ingredients, recipes, and technique** rather than incantations and wand movements. A potion is brewed by combining magical and mundane ingredients in a specific order, at specific temperatures, with specific timing and stirring patterns. The result is a magical substance that produces its effect when consumed, applied, or (in some cases) when fumes are inhaled.

Key potions in the series include:

**Polyjuice Potion** — transforms the drinker into a physical duplicate of another person for one hour. Requires a hair or other biological sample from the target. Takes a month to brew. **Veritaserum** — a truth-compelling serum that makes the drinker unable to lie. **Felix Felicis** (Liquid Luck) — makes the drinker supernaturally lucky for a period of time. Extremely difficult to brew and dangerous if overused. **Wolfsbane Potion** — allows a werewolf to retain their human mind during transformation, though not to prevent the transformation itself. **Amortentia** — the most powerful love potion, producing obsessive infatuation. **Draught of Living Death** — induces a sleep so deep it mimics death.

The discipline's defining characteristic is **reproducibility**. Unlike wand magic (where power, intent, and innate talent determine results), potions follow recipes. A competent brewer following the correct instructions will produce a functioning potion regardless of their raw magical power. This makes potions the most democratic magical discipline — talent helps (Snape's improvements to standard recipes demonstrate expert-level intuition), but the baseline is achievable through study and precision.

Snape's annotated copy of *Advanced Potion-Making* (the "Half-Blood Prince's" textbook) reveals that potions recipes are not final — they can be optimized, modified, and improved by sufficiently knowledgeable practitioners. This positions potions-making as an iterative, experimental discipline more akin to chemistry than to the intuitive art of wandwork.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **time**. Potions take time to brew — from hours for simple preparations to weeks or months for complex ones (Polyjuice Potion requires a full month). This makes potions unsuitable for in-combat use; they must be prepared in advance.

The second cost is **ingredients**. Many potions require rare, expensive, or dangerous ingredients. Lacewing flies, boomslang skin, powdered bicorn horn, and other exotic components must be sourced, and some are controlled by magical authorities.

The third cost is **precision requirements**. Potions are unforgiving of error. Wrong temperature, wrong timing, wrong stirring direction, or wrong quantities produce failed potions (which may be inert or actively dangerous). Neville Longbottom's repeated potions failures demonstrate that the discipline punishes imprecision.

The fourth limitation is **single-person scale**. Potions typically affect only the person who consumes them and last for limited durations. They cannot produce the large-scale, environmental effects that wand magic achieves.

## Practitioner access

Potions is taught as a core subject at Hogwarts from first year through at least fifth year (OWL level), with NEWT-level study available for qualified students. Any witch or wizard can learn to brew potions — it does not require special innate talent, only study and practice. Even Squibs (non-magical people born to magical families) can theoretically follow potion recipes, though this is not explored in the text. The discipline has its own professional track: Potions Masters, apothecaries, and healers who specialize in medicinal preparations.

## Cultural role

In the Wizarding World, potions are ubiquitous — Madam Pomfrey's hospital wing relies on them, Slug & Jiggers Apothecary sells ingredients commercially, and potions are integrated into both medical care and daily life. The discipline has a slight inferiority complex relative to wand magic — Potions is considered the "boring" subject by many students, and its practitioners lack the dramatic flair of duelists or Aurors. Snape's bitterness about this perceived hierarchy is a character detail: he is a genius in a field that gets less respect than flashier magic.
