## Mechanics

Pattern Magic is built on a metaphysical premise that unifies all supernatural phenomena in Earthdawn under a single ontological framework: everything that exists — every person, animal, object, place, and concept — possesses a true Pattern, an astral blueprint that defines its essential nature. Magic in Earthdawn is the manipulation of these Patterns, whether through direct modification, the weaving of astral threads between Patterns, or the channeling of ambient magical energy through a Pattern's structure. This is not a metaphor; it is the literal cosmology of the setting. When a Wizard casts a spell, they are reaching into astral space, perceiving the Patterns of the elements they wish to manipulate, and restructuring those Patterns according to learned formulae. When a Warrior performs a superhuman combat feat, they are channeling magical energy through their own Pattern's martial configuration.

The Discipline system organizes Adept abilities into archetypal paths that determine which Talents a character can access. A Discipline is not merely a class; it is a metaphysical identity. When a character becomes an Adept of the Warrior Discipline, their Pattern literally changes to incorporate the Warrior's astral signature, and this change is what grants access to Warrior Talents like Air Dance (supernatural agility), Wound Balance (the ability to fight through injury), or Earth Skin (temporary physical hardening). Each Discipline has a Circle structure (analogous to levels) that unlocks new Talents and increases the Adept's ability to channel magical energy. Advancing in Circle requires not just experience points but also training, usually from a higher-Circle Adept of the same Discipline, reinforcing the system's emphasis on knowledge transmission and mentorship.

Thread Magic is the system's most distinctive mechanical innovation. Magical items in Earthdawn are not simply found and equipped; they must be attuned through a process of researching the item's history and weaving astral threads to its Pattern. Each thread woven unlocks a new tier of the item's power, and each tier is associated with a specific historical event or story connected to the item. A legendary sword might require the Adept to learn the name of its creator (Thread Rank 1), visit the battlefield where it was first used (Thread Rank 2), defeat a descendant of the enemy it was forged to fight (Thread Rank 3), and so on. This system transforms magical item acquisition from a loot-and-equip transaction into a multi-session narrative arc, and it means that every significant magic item in the game carries built-in story content.

The Step system provides the mechanical resolution framework for all magical (and non-magical) actions. Each Talent, spell, or skill has a Step Number determined by the character's rank in that Talent plus relevant attribute values. The Step Number maps to a specific combination of dice (e.g., Step 12 = 2d10) that the player rolls against a target number. This unified resolution system means that magical abilities and mundane skills operate on the same mechanical axis — a Wizard's Spellcasting Talent and a Thief's Lock Picking Talent are resolved identically, differing only in their fictional context and the Step Numbers involved. Spellcasting adds an additional layer: spells have their own Pattern-based mechanics, including Weaving tests to shape the spell's astral pattern before casting, and the possibility of raw casting (skipping the Weaving test for faster but riskier spellcasting).

## Limitations and costs

The knowledge-preparation cost of Thread Magic is the system's most prominent limitation, and it is deliberately designed to be time-consuming and narratively demanding. Before an Adept can weave a thread to a magical item, they must research the item's Key Knowledges — specific historical facts about the item's origin, previous owners, and significant events in its history. This research can involve consulting sages, exploring ancient libraries, visiting historical sites, performing divination rituals, or interrogating spirits. Each thread rank requires its own Key Knowledge, meaning that fully attuning a powerful item might require multiple sessions of play dedicated entirely to research and quest completion. This cost is not incidental; it is the system's central design feature, turning every magical item into a narrative engine that generates adventures.

The physical and resource costs of spellcasting introduce a concrete mechanical limitation that prevents Adepts from casting without consequence. Spellcasting in Earthdawn requires Strain — a form of self-inflicted damage that represents the physical toll of channeling magical energy through one's Pattern. More powerful spells require more Strain, and Strain accumulates with each casting, meaning that a Spellcaster who goes nova in a single encounter will be physically debilitated for subsequent challenges. Blood Magic — a more powerful form of Pattern manipulation — requires permanent physical damage (Blood Magic Damage) that can only be healed through specific rituals, creating a cost that escalates with use. The most powerful Blood Magic effects require sacrificing attribute points, permanently diminishing the Adept's capabilities in exchange for temporary supernatural effects.

The Horror threat imposes an existential limitation on all Pattern Magic. Horrors — extradimensional entities that invaded the world during the Scourge — are attracted to magical activity and can perceive and corrupt Patterns. An Adept who uses magic recklessly or who interacts with corrupted Patterns risks attracting Horror attention, which can result in Horror Marks (astral parasites that gradually corrupt the Adept's Pattern), possession, or worse. This is not a hypothetical risk; Horror-corrupted items, places, and beings are common in the post-Scourge world, and Thread Magic that connects an Adept to a corrupted Pattern can transmit that corruption directly into the Adept's own Pattern. The result is a magic system where power and danger are ontologically linked — every Thread woven is a potential vector for Horror corruption, and every Pattern touched might be compromised.

The temporal cost of Earthdawn's magic system is significant and often underappreciated. Weaving a spell requires a separate action before casting, meaning most spells take two rounds to deploy (one round to Weave, one to Cast). Raw casting — skipping the Weaving step — eliminates this delay but removes the caster's ability to shape the spell, potentially causing wild and dangerous effects. Thread Magic operates on an even longer timescale: researching Key Knowledges, traveling to significant locations, and performing the thread-weaving ritual all consume in-game days, weeks, or months. This temporal cost means that Earthdawn's magic system rewards planning and patience, penalizing the kind of spontaneous, reactive spellcasting that characterizes faster systems. An Adept who arrives at a confrontation with threads already woven and spells already prepared is dramatically more capable than one who must start from scratch.

## Practitioner access

Adept status in Earthdawn is theoretically available to anyone with sufficient magical potential, but in practice it requires finding a trainer, learning a Discipline, and surviving the dangerous process of awakening one's Pattern to magical sensitivity. The Namegiving races — humans, elves, dwarves, orks, trolls, t'skrang, windlings, and obsidimen — all produce Adepts, though cultural attitudes toward specific Disciplines vary by race. Elven culture prizes the Troubadour and Elementalist Disciplines for their connection to art and nature. Dwarven culture values the Weaponsmith and Wizard for their contributions to craftsmanship and systematic knowledge. Troll culture honors the Sky Raider and Cavalryman for their martial and aerial traditions. These cultural associations influence which Disciplines are readily taught in which communities, creating soft barriers to access that are social rather than metaphysical.

The Discipline system creates a form of access stratification where the type of magic available to a character is determined by their chosen path. A Wizard's spellcasting is learned, systematic, and broadly applicable — they maintain spellbooks, research new formulae, and approach magic as an intellectual discipline. A Nethermancer accesses similar power through communion with spirits and the denizens of the astral plane, trading scholarly rigor for intuitive connection with otherworldly entities. An Elementalist channels the raw forces of nature through ritualized attunement to elemental patterns. A Warrior or Swordmaster performs feats that are unambiguously supernatural — leaping impossible distances, shrugging off lethal wounds, striking with inhuman precision — but these abilities are framed as martial excellence elevated to a magical level rather than as "spellcasting." This framing means that Earthdawn does not draw a sharp line between martial and magical characters; every Adept is a magic user, and the question is what form their magic takes.

Non-Adept access to magic in Earthdawn is extremely limited. Characters who have not awakened to a Discipline can use magical items with pre-woven threads (such as common magic items that require no thread-weaving), benefit from potions and salves created by Adept alchemists, and participate in group rituals led by Adept practitioners. However, they cannot weave threads, activate Talents, or cast spells. This creates a stark division between the magically awakened and the mundane population, with Adepts occupying a social position analogous to skilled professionals — respected, sometimes feared, and always in demand. The post-Scourge world's reliance on Adepts for defense against Horrors, exploration of sealed kaers (underground shelters where populations survived the Scourge), and the general reconstruction of civilization means that Adepts are structurally necessary, not merely individually powerful.

Advancement within the Discipline system follows a mentorship model that reinforces social bonds and knowledge hierarchies. An Adept cannot advance to a new Circle without training from someone of higher Circle in their Discipline, and this requirement creates networks of obligation and loyalty. A Warrior who trained under a legendary Ninth Circle master carries both the knowledge and the social connection that training implies. Discipline-specific organizations — the Great Library of Throal for Wizards, the various troll moots for Sky Raiders, the elven Court of Wyrms for a range of Disciplines — provide institutional support for training and advancement while also serving as social structures that organize Adept life. This means that magical access in Earthdawn is not just a question of personal capability but of social integration into networks of knowledge and tradition.

## Cultural role

Pattern Magic is the foundational infrastructure of Earthdawn's post-apocalyptic reconstruction. The Scourge — a centuries-long period during which Horrors invaded the material world, forcing the Namegiving races to seal themselves in underground kaers — was both caused by and resolved through Pattern Magic. The magical theory that predicted the Scourge came from the Theran Empire's Pattern scholars, the wards that protected the kaers were Pattern-based enchantments, and the process of reclaiming the surface world requires Adepts who can detect and cleanse Horror-corrupted Patterns. This means that magic in Earthdawn is not an adventuring tool that happens to exist alongside a mundane civilization; it is the essential technology of survival, and the game's central narrative — emerging from shelter into a devastated world and rebuilding — is structurally dependent on magical capability.

The Thread Magic system's requirement that magical items be researched and historically contextualized creates a game culture obsessed with history and provenance. Earthdawn characters are, by mechanical necessity, amateur historians. They maintain journals (mechanically important for recording Key Knowledges), consult libraries, interview elders, and perform divinations to learn the stories attached to magical items. This emphasis on historical knowledge transforms the game's adventure structure: dungeon crawls become archaeological expeditions, monster hunts become investigations into corrupted Patterns, and treasure acquisition becomes a research project. The result is a TTRPG where knowledge and narrative engagement are not optional enrichments but mechanical necessities, producing play experiences that are inherently story-driven even when players are primarily motivated by character optimization.

The relationship between Pattern Magic and Horror corruption introduces a moral and existential dimension to magical practice that most fantasy RPGs lack. Every use of magic in Earthdawn carries the implicit risk of attracting Horror attention, and every magical connection (every thread woven, every Talent activated) is a potential vulnerability. This creates a culture of magical caution that coexists uneasily with the practical necessity of magical use. Adepts must use magic to survive and to rebuild, but using magic exposes them and their communities to the very threat that necessitated the Scourge in the first place. This tension is the game's central thematic engine: power is necessary, power is dangerous, and the line between salvation and catastrophe runs through every magical act.

Earthdawn's design influence is most visible in how subsequent games have handled magical item narratives. The idea that a magic item should have a history, that unlocking its power should require engaging with that history, and that the process of attunement should generate playable adventure content has been adopted and adapted by numerous games. The Thread Magic system demonstrated that the relationship between a character and their equipment could be a source of ongoing narrative rather than a solved optimization problem. More broadly, Earthdawn's Pattern ontology — the idea that a single metaphysical framework can unify all magical phenomena, from combat Talents to spellcasting to item enchantment — anticipated the unified magic systems that would become popular in later TTRPG design, showing that mechanical coherence and narrative richness can emerge from the same foundational principle.
