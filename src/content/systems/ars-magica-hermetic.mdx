## Mechanics

Hermetic Magic is built on a fifteen-cell matrix of Techniques and Forms. The five **Techniques** are verbs: Creo (create), Intéllego (perceive / know), Muto (transform), Perdo (destroy / weaken), Rego (control / direct). The ten **Forms** are nouns: Animal, Aquam (water), Auram (air), Corpus (the human body), Herbam (plants), Ignem (fire), Imáginem (image / sense), Mentem (mind), Terram (earth), Vim (raw magic). Every spell, every Hermetic working, is built by selecting one Technique and one Form: Creo Ignem creates fire, Perdo Vim destroys magical effects, Rego Mentem controls minds.

A magus has a numerical score in each Technique and each Form, and the sum of (Technique + Form) sets the magnitude of effects she can cast. Spells are formally rated by a calculated Level (built from base effect plus modifiers for range, duration, target, and special effects), and a magus can cast a Formulaic spell of a given Level if her relevant Technique + Form score plus Stamina plus a die roll meets or exceeds the spell's Level.

Two casting modes are available:

- **Formulaic spells** are pre-researched, pre-named workings the magus has spent in-fiction time learning or inventing in her laboratory. Casting a Formulaic spell is precise, predictable, and can be done quickly. A starting magus has a small repertoire of these.

- **Spontaneous spells** are improvised on the fly. A magus can attempt any Technique × Form combination spontaneously, but the resulting Level is sharply limited (the formula in the rulebook divides by 5 or 2 depending on whether the magus speaks and gestures), and Spontaneous casting fatigues the practitioner. Spontaneous casting is the system's improvisational layer — useful for unexpected situations, but no substitute for proper research.

Beyond casting, the system layers in:

- **Vis** — physical embodiments of raw magical power, harvested from the world (auras, faerie places, magical creatures, regio thresholds), used to fuel Ritual spells (which require vis to cast, even with formulaic preparation), to enchant items, and to bargain in Hermetic politics. Vis is the scarce currency of magical economy.

- **Lab work** — magi spend significant in-fiction time in their personal laboratories, inventing new Formulaic spells, enchanting items, brewing longevity potions, training familiars, and producing apprentices. Each lab activity has a calculated rate-of-progress measured in seasons of game time. The "Wizard's Sanctum" is a real game element, and seasonal time-tracking is part of standard *Ars Magica* play.

- **Parma Magica** — the Hermetic invented protective ward that grants magi resistance to magical effects, including those cast by other magi. The Parma is the institutional foundation of the Order: it makes inter-magus warfare difficult enough to keep the Order intact.

- **Twilight** — the high-end risk of Hermetic practice. A magus who botches a high-level casting, or accumulates too much Warping (a measure of magical strain on the soul), enters Twilight: a state of altered consciousness that can grant insights, scar the magus permanently, or remove her from play.

## Limitations and costs

The first cost is preparation. Spells must be researched and learned. A magus's Formulaic repertoire is built over decades of in-fiction time. Spontaneous casting exists but is limited.

The second cost is institutional. The Order of Hermes is governed by the **Code of Hermes**, a formal covenant binding all member magi. The most important Code provisions are: do not slay another magus, do not deal with demons, do not interfere with mundane authorities (this last is the Quaesitorial concern), do not give magic to mundanes, do not endanger the Order. Tribunals (regional Hermetic governance bodies) try Code violations, and sentences range from fine to Marching (formal license to kill the violator). Hermetic political life is one of the system's central play loops.

The third cost is the masquerade. Mythic Europe is a thirteenth-century setting with a fully realized Catholic Church, a Divine realm that is genuinely threatening to magical practitioners, and an Inquisition that hunts heresy. Open practice of magic before mundanes is a Code violation and pragmatically dangerous.

The fourth cost is Warping. Sustained exposure to magical effects warps the magus's soul — accumulating Warping Points eventually leads to permanent magical Flaws, susceptibility to Twilight, and, ultimately, the magus's transformation into something other than human. Long-lived magi pay this cost in lost humanity.

The fifth cost is temporal. Magi do not gain power overnight. The system explicitly tracks seasons and years, and the difference between an apprentice and a senior magus is measured in decades of fictional time.

## Practitioner access

Hermetic magi are recruited as apprentices in childhood, after a fifteen-year apprenticeship under a sworn parens. Apprentices are usually identified by their Gift — a measurable supernatural quality, partly innate, that magic-resistant individuals can sense as eerie. Most magi share the Gift in some form; specific Houses select for particular variants.

Outside the Order, Mythic Europe also hosts hedge magic traditions (Folk Witches, Solomonic magicians, the Learned Magicians of the Sundered Eagle, etc.) and the Mystery Cults within Hermetic Houses (Verditius's smith-mysteries, Criamon's enigmas, Bjornaer's heartbeast). These adjacent traditions overlap with Hermetic Magic and may merit their own catalog entries.

## Cultural role

Mythic Europe's mundane population mostly does not know the Order exists, and the Order works to keep it that way. Magi live in covenants — fortified communal residences that mix laboratory complex, library, and feudal estate — and most public political weight is held by the Church and the temporal nobility. Within the Order, magic is the working profession: an organized, multi-generational tradition with a continental governance structure, a shared Code, sacred founders, and a deep textual tradition. The setting's depth has made *Ars Magica* the canonical example of "magic as career" in tabletop design.
