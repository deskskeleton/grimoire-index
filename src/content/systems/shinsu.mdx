## Mechanics

Shinsu is the fundamental substance that fills the Tower — a single, omnipresent medium that functions as atmosphere, liquid, energy source, and weapon depending on how it is manipulated. Every floor of the Tower is saturated with Shinsu, and its density increases as one climbs higher, meaning the environment itself becomes more hostile and more power-rich with altitude. At low concentrations Shinsu behaves like air; at high concentrations it becomes viscous, resistant, and eventually solid enough to crush unprepared bodies.

Shinsu manipulation divides into several core disciplines. **Flow control** is the ability to direct Shinsu's movement — pushing, pulling, shaping, and channeling the substance through the environment. A skilled flow controller can compress Shinsu into barriers, project it as concussive blasts, or create currents that accelerate or impede movement. **Density manipulation** involves altering the concentration of Shinsu in a localized area, making it thicker (to slow or crush an opponent) or thinner (to create zones of easier movement). **Shinsu reinforcement** is the practice of infusing one's own body with Shinsu to enhance physical capabilities — strength, speed, durability, and sensory acuity.

Bangs are the standard unit of Shinsu combat. A Bang is a discrete sphere or mass of compressed Shinsu that a practitioner can control independently. The number of Bangs a person can maintain simultaneously is a direct measure of their capability — novices manage one or two, while elite Rankers can control dozens or hundreds. Bangs can be launched as projectiles, shaped into barriers, used as sensory probes, or combined into larger constructs.

Shinsu quality refers to the elemental or conceptual affinity a practitioner develops through training and innate disposition. A user might develop an affinity for flame-type Shinsu, ice-type, lightning-type, or more exotic qualities like light-devouring or space-warping Shinsu. Quality determines not just the aesthetic of a practitioner's attacks but their functional properties — a flame-quality user's Bangs burn, an ice-quality user's Bangs freeze. Some qualities are extraordinarily rare and grant capabilities that transcend normal combat parameters.

The position system structures how Shinsu is used in group combat. The Tower's climbing structure emphasizes team-based trials, and teams are organized into five formalized roles: **Fisherman** (front-line combatant), **Light Bearer** (information and tactical support), **Scout** (reconnaissance and disruption), **Spear Bearer** (long-range offense), and **Wave Controller** (the dedicated Shinsu specialist who manipulates flow and density at range). While any climber uses Shinsu, Wave Controllers develop the deepest mastery of direct manipulation, functioning as the team's primary area-control and utility specialists.

Guardian contracts are a critical structural element. Each floor of the Tower is governed by a Guardian — an ancient, immensely powerful entity native to the Tower. To use Shinsu on a given floor, a climber must receive permission from that floor's Guardian through a contract. The terms of these contracts vary but typically involve passing a test set by the Guardian. This makes Shinsu access not merely a matter of personal ability but a political and contractual negotiation repeated at every stage of the climb.

Irregulars are the exception. An Irregular is someone who entered the Tower from outside — they were not chosen or invited by the Tower's system of Headon's selection. Irregulars are not bound by Guardian contracts and can use Shinsu freely on any floor without permission. This makes them fundamentally disruptive to the Tower's order. Every Irregular in the Tower's history has been a figure of enormous significance — Jahad, the 10 Great Family Heads, Urek Mazino, Enryu, Phantaminum, and Bam himself.

## Limitations and costs

Shinsu resistance is the system's primary gating mechanism. Every person has an innate tolerance for Shinsu density — the maximum concentration they can function in before the substance crushes, immobilizes, or suffocates them. As floors increase in Shinsu density, individuals with low resistance literally cannot survive the environment, regardless of their technical skill. Resistance can be trained and improved, but innate ceilings vary enormously and fundamentally determine how high a person can climb.

Physical and mental toll scales with exertion. Maintaining many Bangs simultaneously requires intense concentration, and sustained high-output combat depletes a practitioner's stamina and mental focus. Overextension leads to loss of control, scattered Bangs, and vulnerability. The attention cost is particularly significant in team battles, where a Wave Controller must track multiple allies' positions, enemy movements, and their own Shinsu constructs simultaneously.

Guardian contracts impose a political cost. A Guardian can refuse to grant a contract, set impossible conditions, or impose restrictions on how Shinsu may be used on their floor. This means that a climber's power is partially contingent on the cooperation of entities with their own agendas. The contracts also mean that power gained on one floor does not automatically transfer to the next — each new floor requires renegotiation.

The Ranker-Regular gap is the system's most severe power disparity. Regulars are climbers still ascending the Tower. Rankers are individuals who have reached the top of the accessible floors and received the Tower's recognition. The gap between even a talented Regular and an average Ranker is described as immeasurable — Rankers have accumulated decades or centuries of Shinsu mastery, survived environments that killed the vast majority of their peers, and had their bodies fundamentally enhanced by prolonged exposure to high-density Shinsu. High Rankers and Family Heads operate at a scale that makes ordinary Rankers seem insignificant in turn.

The resource-expenditure dimension is environmental rather than internal. Unlike systems where practitioners draw from a personal energy pool, Shinsu users manipulate an external substance. But Shinsu is not infinite in a local area — multiple practitioners competing for control in the same space can deplete or destabilize the local Shinsu concentration, creating dead zones or unpredictable turbulence.

## Practitioner access

Access to Shinsu is technically universal — every living being in the Tower exists within Shinsu and interacts with it at a basic metabolic level. Conscious manipulation, however, requires training, innate talent, and contractual permission.

The selection process begins with Headon, the Guardian of the first floor, who chooses which individuals are invited into the Tower to begin climbing. Selection is opaque and its criteria are unknown, but it creates an initial filter — most inhabitants of the Tower's floors were born inside and never climb, living ordinary lives within the settled areas of each floor.

Formal training in Shinsu manipulation occurs through the Evankhell-style academies, floor-specific instructors, and mentorship within climbing teams. The position system channels training along specialized tracks — a Light Bearer's Shinsu training emphasizes information gathering and communication, while a Fisherman's training focuses on body reinforcement and close-range burst output.

The 10 Great Families represent a hereditary access advantage. Descendants of the Tower's original conquerors inherit enhanced Shinsu affinity, unique bloodline techniques, and institutional resources that ordinary climbers cannot match. This creates a de facto aristocracy of power — Great Family members do not necessarily work harder or train longer, but they begin with inherent advantages that compound over a climbing career.

Irregulars bypass the access structure entirely. They need no Guardian contracts, face no selection by Headon (they enter the Tower on their own), and often demonstrate Shinsu manipulation capabilities that defy the established rules. Bam in particular absorbs techniques and abilities at a rate that alarms every established power structure in the Tower, and his exemption from Guardian contracts means no floor can formally restrict his power.

## Cultural role

Shinsu is not merely a power system within the Tower — it is the Tower's physics, ecology, and political economy simultaneously. Because Shinsu is the medium of all life, combat, industry, and governance within the Tower, mastery of Shinsu manipulation is mastery of the environment itself. The Tower's social hierarchy maps directly onto Shinsu capability: Rankers rule, Regulars climb, and the vast majority of the population lives within the settled areas of each floor under a system of governance maintained by those who climbed highest.

The position system embeds Shinsu into the Tower's social architecture. Climbing teams are the fundamental social unit of the ascending population, and the five-position structure creates a formalized interdependence that mirrors military unit composition. A climber's position is as much a social identity as a combat role — Light Bearers are associated with intelligence and strategy, Fishermen with aggression and directness, and Wave Controllers with the mystique of deep Shinsu mastery.

The Guardian contract system introduces a layer of theocratic governance. Guardians are the Tower's true authorities — ancient, enigmatic, and enormously powerful. Their willingness to grant or withhold Shinsu contracts gives them effective veto power over who can climb through their domains. This makes the Tower's power structure not merely hierarchical but contractual in a way that resonates with feudal obligation: you may use power on this floor because the floor's lord permits it.

Irregulars function as a narrative disruption of this order. Every Irregular who enters the Tower upends existing power dynamics precisely because they are exempt from the system's structural constraints. Jahad conquered the Tower and established the current order. Enryu killed a Guardian — something thought impossible. Phantaminum breached the Jahad palace and left alive. Bam's climb threatens to unravel the entire contractual architecture because his mere existence demonstrates that the contracts can be bypassed. The Shinsu system's role in the narrative is therefore as much about the power of institutions and the fragility of established hierarchies as it is about individual combat capability.
