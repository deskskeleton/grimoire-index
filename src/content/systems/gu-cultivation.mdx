## Mechanics

The Gu world's power system centers on Gu worms — living creatures that range from insect-sized to massive, each one a physical embodiment of a fragment of the Great Dao. A Gu worm that embodies a sliver of the fire Dao can produce flames; one that embodies a sliver of the strength Dao can enhance physical power; one that embodies a sliver of the time Dao can accelerate, slow, or reverse temporal flow within limited parameters. There are hundreds of thousands of distinct Gu worms, each representing a narrow aspect of natural law, and a Gu Master's power is determined entirely by which Gu worms they possess, how well they can use them, and how cleverly they combine them.

Gu Masters cultivate by refining their aperture — an internal organ located in the dantian region that stores and processes primeval essence, the fuel that powers Gu worm activation. The cultivation ranks span from Rank 1 (initial awakening) through Rank 5 (the mortal ceiling), with the leap to Rank 6 marking the transition to Gu Immortal status. Gu Immortals possess a blessed land — a pocket dimension that serves as both power source and personal domain. Beyond Rank 6 lie Ranks 7 and 8 (great grandmaster territory), Rank 9 (the peak that only Venerables have achieved), and the theoretical Rank 10 that no being has ever attained.

The refinement and combination system is where the magic achieves its depth. Individual Gu worms are useful but limited. A Gu Master's real art lies in combining multiple Gu into killer moves — composite techniques that chain several Gu worms' effects into a single devastating action. Fang Yuan's signature approach involves assembling killer moves from Gu worms across multiple Dao paths, creating effects that are difficult to counter because they do not follow a single elemental logic. The system rewards creative combination over raw power accumulation.

Gu worms are organized by the Dao path they belong to. The major paths include strength, wisdom, time, space, blood, soul, formation, refinement, food, rule, and many others — each with its own internal hierarchy of Gu worms. A Gu Master typically specializes in one or two paths but may use Gu from any path if they can acquire them. The five regions of the Gu world — Northern Plains, Southern Border, Central Continent, Eastern Sea, and Western Desert — each have distinct ecological conditions that favor different Gu types, making geography a strategic factor in cultivation.

At the Gu Immortal level, the system expands dramatically. Immortal Gu are sentient, willful entities that cannot be forcibly controlled — they must be convinced or tricked into cooperation. An Immortal's blessed land generates resources autonomously but requires management; neglecting it causes degradation. Tribulations — periodic catastrophic events — assault the blessed land at intervals, and surviving them is a constant pressure that prevents Immortals from resting on their achievements.

## Limitations and costs

Gu worms are consumed in use. Activating a Gu worm expends primeval essence, and the Gu worm itself may be destroyed in the process of executing certain killer moves. Mortal-rank Gu can be replenished through refinement, but Immortal Gu are unique and irreplaceable if lost. This consumable nature means that every battle is a resource calculation — using your best Gu recklessly can leave you weaker than before you won.

Feeding is a constant drain. Each Gu worm requires specific sustenance to remain alive and functional. A fire-path Gu might consume fire essence stones; a blood-path Gu might require beast blood. Maintaining a large Gu collection is enormously expensive, and the feeding costs scale with the Gu's rank. Many Gu Masters are forced to sell or release Gu they cannot afford to maintain, creating a triage dynamic where practitioners must constantly evaluate which capabilities are worth their upkeep cost.

Gu worms can conflict with one another. Housing two Gu with opposed Dao natures in the same aperture can cause mutual interference or outright destruction. Building a harmonious Gu set requires deep knowledge of Dao path interactions, and mistakes can be catastrophic. This constraint prevents the simple strategy of accumulating every powerful Gu available — compatibility and synergy matter as much as individual power.

Resources are finite and bitterly contested. The Gu world operates on scarcity at every level. Mortal sects fight over resource-rich territories; Immortals wage wars for blessed land fragments; Venerables reshape entire regions to fuel their cultivation. There are no renewable resources at the highest levels — advancement past certain thresholds requires unique materials that exist in limited quantities, creating zero-sum competition among peak-level practitioners.

The system has no built-in moral constraints. Unlike many cultivation systems where "evil" techniques carry inherent penalties, the Gu world's demonic path (which involves stealing from others, refining humans into Gu materials, and similar atrocities) is mechanically identical to the righteous path. The only practical disadvantage of demonic cultivation is social — the righteous sects will hunt you. This absence of cosmic justice is a deliberate authorial choice that reinforces the novel's thesis about power and morality.

## Practitioner access

Anyone born in the Gu world can potentially become a Gu Master. The initial awakening at Rank 1 requires an aperture, which most humans possess, and access to a starting Gu worm. Sects typically provide both, making early cultivation accessible to anyone who can join a sect. There is no chosen-one mechanic, no bloodline requirement, and no innate talent ceiling that cannot be overcome through superior resources and strategy.

However, resources determine everything. A Gu Master born into a wealthy clan with extensive Gu reserves will advance faster and further than an equally talented orphan with no backing. The system is a pure meritocracy of ruthlessness — talent helps, but resources are the decisive factor, and resources are acquired through competition. Fang Yuan, who possesses the memories of five hundred years of cultivation from his previous life, demonstrates that knowledge is the most valuable resource of all, because it allows efficient acquisition of everything else.

Aptitude varies — some cultivators have larger apertures, purer primeval essence, or natural affinity for particular Dao paths — but these advantages can be compensated for. Gu that enhance aperture capacity exist; primeval essence can be purified through specific techniques; Dao path affinity can be developed through sustained practice. The system systematically undermines the concept of irreplaceable natural talent in favor of accumulated advantage.

The transition from mortal Gu Master to Gu Immortal is the hardest bottleneck. It requires surviving an ascension tribulation and forming a blessed land, both of which have high mortality rates. Many Rank 5 peak Masters never make the leap. Those who do enter a different order of power and politics, where the stakes and resource requirements escalate enormously.

## Cultural role

Sects are the fundamental social unit of the Gu world, functioning less as spiritual organizations and more as resource-control enterprises. A sect claims territory, manages the Gu worm ecology within that territory, trains new cultivators, and defends against rivals. The relationship between sect and member is transactional — the sect provides resources and protection; the member provides labor and loyalty. Sects rise and fall based on their resource base and the strength of their peak cultivators.

The division between the righteous path and the demonic path is framed throughout Reverend Insanity as a political distinction rather than a moral one. Righteous sects maintain their power through institutional control of resources and the social legitimacy that comes with tradition. Demonic cultivators operate outside these institutions, acquiring resources through theft, murder, and exploitation. But the novel consistently demonstrates that righteous sects commit comparable atrocities — they simply have better public relations. Star Constellation Immortal Venerable, a righteous Venerable, was as ruthless as any demon; she simply embedded her ruthlessness within institutional frameworks.

Fang Yuan articulates the novel's philosophical core: all morality is self-interest wearing a mask. The Gu cultivation system embodies this thesis by making power acquisition a naked competition where the rules are the same for everyone and the only meaningful variable is how far you are willing to go. The system does not reward virtue, does not punish vice, and does not distinguish between the methods of a saint and a monster. What it rewards is efficiency, knowledge, and the willingness to pay whatever price advancement demands. This framework makes Reverend Insanity one of the most philosophically uncompromising works in the cultivation genre, using its magic system not as a backdrop for heroism but as an argument about the nature of power itself.
