## Mechanics

Epic powers originate from **Calamity**, a glowing red star that appeared in the sky and began granting random humans superpowers. The selection process appears random — there is no common genetic marker, no ritual, and no way to predict who will become an Epic. Once empowered, each Epic manifests a unique **primary power** that defines their identity: Steelheart can transmute matter around him into steel and is physically invulnerable; Firefight can create hyper-realistic illusions from alternate realities; Prof can generate force fields and disintegrate matter.

Many Epics also develop **secondary and tertiary powers** that complement their primary ability. Steelheart, for example, can also project energy blasts and fly, in addition to his invulnerability and steel transmutation. These supporting powers tend to cluster thematically around the primary ability, creating a coherent power portfolio rather than a random assortment of unrelated abilities.

The most distinctive feature of the system is the **corruption effect**. Every Epic who uses their powers becomes progressively more selfish, cruel, and megalomaniacal. The corruption is not subtle — Epics who were decent people before their transformation become tyrants within weeks or months of gaining powers. This corruption is tied to fear: each Epic has a specific, deeply personal fear, and proximity to or experience of that fear temporarily suppresses both their powers and the corruption. When an Epic confronts and overcomes their fear rather than fleeing from it, they can potentially break the corruption cycle entirely while retaining their abilities.

Each Epic also possesses a unique **weakness** — a specific condition, substance, or situation that negates their powers. Steelheart's weakness is that he can be harmed only by someone who does not fear him. Other Epics have weaknesses tied to specific materials, environmental conditions, or emotional states. The Reckoners' entire strategy revolves around researching and identifying these weaknesses.

## Limitations and costs

The **corruption** is the system's defining cost. Epic powers do not drain physical energy or require material fuel — they can be used freely and repeatedly. But every use deepens the psychological corruption, making the Epic more paranoid, more cruel, and more detached from their former humanity. This creates a tragic irony: the more an Epic uses their gifts, the less recognizable they become as the person they once were. Prof, who tries to use his powers for good as a Reckoner leader, must carefully limit his power use to prevent the corruption from overtaking him — and when he finally loses that battle, he becomes one of the most dangerous antagonists in the series.

The **weakness system** functions as a structural limitation that prevents any single Epic from being truly invincible. No matter how powerful an Epic becomes, there exists some condition that strips their powers entirely. However, weaknesses are often obscure and difficult to discover — the Reckoners spend extensive time and resources researching each target, and getting the weakness wrong in the field is typically fatal.

There is no **power growth or progression**. An Epic's abilities are fixed at manifestation. They cannot train to become stronger, learn new techniques, or expand their power set. What they receive from Calamity is what they have permanently. This makes the system fundamentally different from most fantasy magic — there is no mastery curve, only the binary question of whether one has powers or does not.

## Practitioner access

Calamity selects Epics seemingly at random from the global human population. There is no way to volunteer, no ritual to undergo, and no genetic test that predicts selection. An Epic can emerge from any demographic, any age group, any nation. This randomness is part of what makes Epics so destabilizing — society cannot predict, prepare for, or control who will next receive godlike power.

The vast majority of humanity remains powerless, and the Reckoners represent the ordinary human response: meticulous research, careful planning, and the exploitation of weaknesses to bring down opponents who outclass them in every direct confrontation. David Charleston's journey complicates this division when he discovers that Epics can potentially overcome their corruption, suggesting the boundary between Epic and human is not as absolute as it appears.

## Cultural role

Epic powers have reshaped global civilization into a patchwork of petty tyrannies. Most major cities are ruled by a dominant Epic (or coalition of Epics) who enforces their will through raw power. Governments have largely collapsed or become puppets. Ordinary humans live under Epic rule in conditions ranging from oppressive stability (Newcago under Steelheart, where the city functions but dissent is lethal) to outright chaos (cities ruled by less organized or more destructive Epics). The Reckoners operate as guerrilla cells, and public perception of Epics is defined by fear and resignation. The trilogy's thematic arc argues that the real danger of power is not what it does to the powerless but what it does to those who wield it — and that the only escape from corruption is the willingness to be vulnerable.
