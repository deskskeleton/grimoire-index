## Mechanics

Headology is the working method of Discworld's village witches. Its central insight is that direct magical force is rarely the most efficient solution to a human problem; what people actually respond to is expectation, ritual, fear of expectation, and the small social cues that tell them whether to take you seriously.

A working witch's principal tools are therefore not spells but reputation, observation, and the ability to deliver a known intervention with the right framing. A village witch at a sickbed will assess the patient (Granny Weatherwax's "Borrowing" — sending her mind into another creature's mind to look from the inside is a real and specific magical technique she uses for diagnosis), prescribe a treatment (often a herbal remedy with a real medical effect, but framed as a magical one), and, where necessary, "make it work" through the patient's own conviction. The books are explicit that this is not a deception — the witches understand exactly what they are doing — but it is an intentional layering of psychological technique on top of a thinner sub-stratum of actual magic.

That thinner sub-stratum is real. Witches really can do things that lie outside ordinary skill: the Borrowing already mentioned, weather-working at small scales, communicating with bees and other intermediaries, reading the substance of a place, and — for the senior witches — the sustained presence of will that holds a thing in shape (Granny Weatherwax's most signature ability is essentially refusing, by sustained personal force, to let things go where they would otherwise go). Genuine magical force is held in reserve and used sparingly.

A defining principle of the tradition is "not doing." A first-class witch is someone who could turn a man into a frog and chooses, deliberately, not to. The Lancre tradition treats restraint as a more advanced skill than action, and treats witches who use heavy magic as practitioners who have failed at headology and resorted to brute force.

## Limitations and costs

The system's costs are largely moral and social rather than physical.

The social cost is the structuring fact of the tradition. A witch's working currency is her reputation in the small community she serves; she trades on the village knowing what she is and what she does. This requires staying in place for a long time, knowing the families across multiple generations, attending the births and deaths, and accepting the lived presence of being known. A witch is not a wandering adventurer.

The moral cost is the tradition's central anxiety. The books repeatedly engage with the temptation to use a witch's leverage badly — to manipulate, to coerce, to enjoy being feared. The watch-tower term for a witch who has gone bad is "cackler," a failure mode the working witches monitor in themselves and in each other. The series gives the avoidance of cacklerhood roughly the same weight that other magic systems give the moral cost of dark magic.

The third cost is preparation. Headology is learned by apprenticeship, on long walking circuits between Lancre's farms, by years of observing and assisting senior witches. There is no school. Tiffany Aching's coming-of-age sequence is in significant part the chronicle of that apprenticeship.

A subtler cost is the absence of an external power supply. Headology does not draw on a metaphysical battery the way Discworld's wizardly magic draws on the Disc's ambient field. The witches' working capacity is bounded by their own attention, their own clarity, and (in the senior cases) the personal force of their will. Granny Weatherwax's exhaustion at the end of several novels is a real depletion of the practitioner.

## Practitioner access

Witches are usually self-selecting from young women in rural Lancre, the Chalk, and similar regions, and are recognized and gradually trained by senior witches in the area. The tradition is presented as feminine but not exclusionary on principle — the unique exception of Eskarina Smith in *Equal Rites*, who is born to be a wizard but raised as a witch, is the series' formal counterexample. Most working witches have an inherited family connection to the tradition, but innate aptitude — a particular kind of attentiveness, stubbornness, and refusal to be lied to — is the gating quality.

## Cultural role

In the rural cultures where witches operate, they are simultaneously feared and indispensable. Villagers consult them for medical care, mid-wifery, weather warnings, marital advice, dispute resolution, and protection from the genuinely supernatural threats that periodically wander out of the high country. The witches reciprocate by handling the unpleasant work nobody else will (laying out the dead, cutting away gangrenous limbs, talking the violent down) and by maintaining the moral perimeter of the community. The tradition is not hidden; it is embedded so deeply in the rural economy that most villagers do not separate "witchcraft" from "the witch's other work."
