## Mechanics

The foundation of all magic in the Pact and Pale setting is the Seal of Solomon — a cosmic compact established in antiquity that binds both human practitioners and supernatural Others (faerie, goblins, ghosts, demons, bogeymen, elementals, and stranger things) to a shared set of rules. The most important rule is honesty: once a practitioner is Awakened, they cannot speak a direct lie. This is not a social convention but a metaphysical constraint enforced by ambient spirits that permeate reality. These spirits observe, judge, and enforce. A practitioner who lies loses power, accumulates karmic debt, and may find the universe itself turning against them through cascading misfortune.

The Practice is the broad term for all magical traditions operating under the Seal. It encompasses dozens of distinct specializations: glamour (faerie-derived illusion and emotional manipulation), enchantment (imbuing objects and spaces with power), diabolism (the summoning and binding of demons — enormously powerful and equally reviled), chronomancy (time manipulation), war magic (combat applications), technomancy (merging modern technology with Practice), necromancy (dealing with ghosts and the dead), and many others. Each tradition has its own body of lore, preferred tools, and characteristic risks.

A practitioner's power is built on three pillars. The Familiar is a bonded Other — a supernatural creature that enters a reciprocal pact with the practitioner, granting strengths that complement the practitioner's weaknesses. The Implement is a symbolic tool (a weapon, a book, a piece of jewelry) that channels and shapes a practitioner's magic, reflecting their approach to the Practice. The Demesne is a claimed territory — a space the practitioner has ritually made their own, where their power is strongest and which they must defend. These three together form the core infrastructure of a practitioner's magical identity.

The spirits that enforce the Seal also track a running ledger of karmic debt. Good karma — earned through honest dealing, fulfilled promises, and service — makes magic easier, smooths social interactions with Others, and grants a general tailwind. Bad karma — accumulated through broken oaths, dishonest implications, cruelty, and association with dark powers — makes everything harder. Spirits interpret situations less favorably, coincidences break against the practitioner, and even mundane tasks become more difficult. Karma is inherited through family lines, meaning some practitioner families begin deeply in debt for the sins of ancestors.

The Sight is a perceptual ability that Awakened practitioners possess, allowing them to perceive the supernatural layer of reality — connections between people and Others, the flow of power, glamours and illusions, spiritual residue, and the invisible architecture of the Practice. The Sight is not always a gift; seeing the full truth of what lurks in shadows, what demons have consumed, or what old wounds a place carries can be psychologically devastating.

In Pale, the three teenage protagonists each develop distinct specializations that illustrate the system's breadth. Lucy Ellingson gravitates toward war magic and binding — direct, confrontational, focused on justice and accountability. Verona Hayward explores glamour, fae magic, and creative manipulation — indirect, artistic, willing to bend rules to their breaking point. Avery Kelly develops path magic — navigating the Paths, extradimensional routes between places that are themselves alive and dangerous. Their different approaches to the same underlying system demonstrate that the Practice rewards personal authenticity; magic shaped to fit who you are works better than magic adopted purely for utility.

## Limitations and costs

Every bargain costs something. The Practice is fundamentally transactional, and Others do not give power for free. A goblin might grant strength in exchange for a disgusting favor. A faerie might offer glamour in return for years of servitude or a piece of the practitioner's identity. A ghost might share knowledge in exchange for resolution of its unfinished business. Demons offer power at costs that are often far worse than they initially appear — diabolism is considered the most dangerous branch of the Practice because demons are ontologically destructive. They do not merely harm; they permanently erase things from existence, and the void left behind can never be filled.

Broken oaths carry severe consequences. A practitioner who swears to do something and fails suffers karmic debt, loss of magical potency, and potentially worse — the spirits may withdraw their cooperation entirely, leaving the practitioner Awakened (and therefore unable to lie) but stripped of any power, which is a uniquely vulnerable position. Forswearing — the ultimate failure of an oath — can destroy a practitioner entirely, reducing them to something less than human.

The honesty constraint is both a limitation and a tool. Practitioners cannot lie directly, but they can mislead through careful phrasing, selective omission, and creative interpretation. Much of the tactical depth of the system comes from this — conversations between practitioners are elaborate verbal fencing matches where every word carries potential binding force. Promises made carelessly become chains. Questions asked imprecisely can be answered in technically true but deeply misleading ways.

Others are inherently dangerous. They operate on alien logic — faerie think in terms of stories and narrative roles, goblins thrive on degradation and filth, bogeymen are sustained by fear, and echoes replay trauma on loop. Treating an Other as a human with a different culture is a potentially fatal mistake. Even benevolent Others have priorities and instincts that can make them hazardous to work with.

## Practitioner access

Entering the Practice requires Awakening — a ritual in which a prospective practitioner opens their Sight, declares themselves to the spirits, and accepts the constraints of the Seal of Solomon. This is irreversible. Once Awakened, a person is bound by the honesty requirement for life and becomes visible to Others who might otherwise have ignored them entirely.

Most practitioners come from practitioner families — lineages that have practiced for generations and pass down accumulated knowledge, connections, karmic debts, and magical infrastructure. Family libraries, established relationships with Others, inherited implements, and ancestral demesnes give family practitioners enormous advantages. They also inherit their family's enemies, debts, and obligations, which can be crippling — the Thorburn family in Pact is saddled with generations of diabolist karma that makes Blake's situation nearly impossible from the start.

The Kennet trio in Pale represents the alternative path: three mundane teenagers recruited directly by Others to investigate a murder in their community. They are taught by various Others and visiting practitioners, learning in an ad hoc and sometimes dangerous fashion. Their lack of family connections means they have no inherited karma (good or bad) but also no safety net, no library, and no established allies in the practitioner world.

Self-teaching is possible but extremely dangerous. Without guidance, a new practitioner is likely to make binding promises they do not understand, offend Others whose customs they are ignorant of, or stumble into situations where their ignorance is weaponized against them. The vast majority of humanity never learns magic exists — the Seal's enforcement mechanisms and the practitioner community's traditions keep the Practice hidden from mundane awareness.

## Cultural role

The Practice operates under a thorough masquerade. Mundane humans are Unawakened — they cannot perceive Others, do not notice magical effects unless those effects are blatant enough to force awareness, and generally rationalize away anything supernatural they encounter. Practitioners maintain this separation deliberately; mass Awakening would be catastrophic, as the spirits' enforcement mechanisms are calibrated for a world where most people are not bound by the Seal.

Practitioner families function as secret societies with their own internal politics, rivalries, alliances, and traditions. The Lord system provides supernatural governance: each significant territory has a Lord (often a powerful Other or practitioner) who arbitrates disputes, enforces local rules, and manages relations between the supernatural inhabitants of their domain. Lordship is contested, inherited, or seized — the politics of who controls supernatural territory drives much of the conflict in both Pact and Pale.

Others have their own complex societies and territorial arrangements. Faerie courts operate on narrative logic, casting participants in roles (hero, villain, mentor, trickster) and enforcing those roles with magical weight. Goblin warrens are hierarchies of cruelty and cunning. Ghost populations accumulate in places of historical trauma. These supernatural communities interact with practitioner families through a web of old agreements, grudges, and mutual dependencies.

The Seal of Solomon, for all its constraints, maintains a fragile peace. Without it, the relationship between humanity and Others would likely be open war — Others are numerous, powerful, and in many cases predatory. The Seal gives both sides rules to operate within, mechanisms for dispute resolution, and consequences for violations. Whether this peace is just or merely stable is one of the central questions both series explore, particularly as Pale examines whether the existing order serves everyone or primarily benefits those already in power.
