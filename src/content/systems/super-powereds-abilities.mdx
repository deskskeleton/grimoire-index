## Mechanics

Super abilities in the Super Powereds universe manifest at puberty, typically during adolescence, and each individual receives exactly one power. That power is fixed — there is no learning new abilities, no multi-classing, no power evolution beyond learning to use what you already have more effectively. A person who manifests energy absorption will always have energy absorption; one who manifests telepathy will always have telepathy. The system is generous in the variety of powers that exist (gravity manipulation, luck manipulation, advanced mind reading, shapeshifting, density control, and hundreds of others) but strict in the one-power-per-person constraint.

The foundational distinction of the system is between Supers and Powereds. Supers have conscious, reliable control over their abilities. A Super with super-strength can choose when to exert it and how much force to apply. A Powered with super-strength cannot — they might crush a doorknob trying to open a door or accidentally throw someone across a room. This distinction is not merely practical but legal: Supers have rights and options that Powereds do not. The series' five protagonists begin as Powereds who undergo an experimental procedure to gain control of their abilities, placing them in the legally and socially ambiguous position of being Supers who were not born that way.

The Hero Certification Program is a four-year university-level training program that produces licensed Heroes — the only individuals legally permitted to use their powers in combat situations. The HCP operates within five major universities nationwide, hidden within their regular campus infrastructure. Students are sorted into specializations based on their abilities and aptitudes: Combat (direct confrontation), Subtlety (covert operations, infiltration, intelligence), Focus (technical and support roles), Control (area management and team coordination), and Ranged Combat (distance engagement). Each year, the class is culled — students who cannot meet escalating standards are removed from the program. Of an entering class of roughly fifty, fewer than ten typically graduate.

The Department of Variant Affairs oversees all Super-related regulation, from Hero licensing to Powered registration to incident response. Heroes operate under strict rules of engagement: they must identify themselves, they cannot use lethal force except in extreme circumstances, they are legally liable for collateral damage, and they must coordinate with law enforcement. The DVA functions as a combination of the FBI and a professional licensing board, with the bureaucratic complexity that implies.

Power classification in this system is informal but functionally important. Supers are roughly grouped by power type and assessed by relative strength. The concept of "Strongman" (physically enhanced), "Blaster" (ranged energy projection), "Shifter" (form alteration), "Subtlety" (stealth and intelligence-oriented powers), and "Control" (area or environmental manipulation) appears throughout, though these are training categories rather than rigid taxonomies.

Vince Reynolds illustrates the system's mechanics well. His energy absorption allows him to absorb any form of energy directed at him — kinetic, thermal, electrical, sonic — and redirect it. The power is simple in concept but enormously complex in application: he must learn which energies he can safely absorb, how much he can store, and how precisely he can redirect specific energy types. His growth across four years from an unfocused Powered to a capable Hero tracks the system's emphasis on training and skill development within fixed parameters.

Nick Campbell's luck manipulation is the system's most unusual power and its most revealing. Nick can subtly adjust probability in his favor or against others — not dramatically enough to guarantee outcomes, but enough to tilt odds consistently. The power is nearly undetectable, which makes it ideal for Subtlety specialization but also raises ethical questions about consent and manipulation that the series explores at length.

## Limitations and costs

The one-power constraint is absolute and shapes every tactical consideration. A Super with incredible offensive capability may have no defensive options. A Super with perfect defense may lack any offensive application. This forces Heroes to work in teams, with complementary abilities covering each other's weaknesses — the HCP's training structure is built around this reality.

Powereds face the system's harshest limitation: an inability to control their own abilities. A Powered telepath hears every thought around them constantly, with no off switch. A Powered with enhanced strength lives in fear of breaking everything they touch. The social stigma attached to Powered status compounds the practical difficulties — Powereds are widely viewed as dangerous, pitiable, or both, and face legal restrictions on where they can live and work. The protagonists' Powered-to-Super conversion is experimental, potentially reversible, and socially explosive if revealed.

Physical costs are real but generally proportional. Using powers is exhausting; sustained or extreme use depletes the Super physically. Mary's telepathy gives her headaches proportional to the number of minds she monitors. Vince's energy absorption has storage limits — exceeding them risks catastrophic release. Roy's enhanced form is a separate physical body from his default state as Hershel, meaning time spent as Roy is time Hershel loses.

The social and institutional costs are the system's most prominent limitation. The HCP eliminates most candidates. Licensed Heroes operate under constant scrutiny — their combat decisions are reviewed, their personal lives are constrained by their public roles, and their legal liability for collateral damage is significant. Using powers without a Hero license is a federal crime, punishable by imprisonment. A Super who intervenes in a crisis without authorization faces prosecution even if they save lives.

## Practitioner access

Powers are genetic — they manifest or they do not, and no amount of training, desire, or preparation can produce them in someone without the biological predisposition. The roughly one-in-fifty-thousand manifestation rate means Supers are rare enough to be remarkable but common enough to be a recognized demographic. Powers tend to run in families, though the specific ability a child manifests often differs from their parents'.

The Powered-to-Super conversion undergone by the five protagonists is an experimental medical procedure developed over decades. It is not publicly available, its long-term stability is uncertain, and its existence is a closely guarded secret. The procedure addresses the neurological cause of Powered status — an inability to form the control pathways that Supers develop naturally — but the fix is artificial and potentially fragile. The series treats this as a genuine medical advance with genuine medical uncertainties, not a magical cure.

HCP admission is intensely competitive. Candidates must demonstrate both sufficient power and sufficient character to merit training. The four-year program is designed to produce not just capable fighters but ethical ones — students who understand the weight of the authority they seek and can exercise it responsibly. Wash-out rates are deliberately high. The system produces a small number of highly trained, rigorously vetted Heroes rather than a large number of adequately trained ones.

There is no way to train into having powers. Non-Supers can become involved in the Super world through the DVA, through Hero support roles, or through personal connections, but the fundamental divide between powered and unpowered individuals is biological and unbridgeable.

## Cultural role

Supers are publicly known and culturally integrated. Heroes are celebrities — they have merchandise, fan followings, media coverage, and public personas. The Hero system functions as a combination of law enforcement, emergency services, and professional sports, with the celebrity culture that implies. Major Hero teams operate out of established bases in major cities, respond to emergencies alongside conventional first responders, and maintain public-relations operations.

Powereds occupy a much less comfortable cultural position. They are simultaneously pitied and feared, subject to legal restrictions that Supers do not face, and often unable to live normal lives due to their uncontrolled abilities. The series uses the Super/Powered distinction as a sustained metaphor for disability discrimination and the politics of accommodation — how a society treats those who have capabilities it values versus those whose capabilities it finds inconvenient or frightening.

The DVA represents institutional authority over the Super population — a bureaucratic structure that determines who can use their powers, how, and under what circumstances. The series explores both the necessity of this regulation (uncontrolled Super combat would be catastrophic) and its costs (talented individuals denied the right to help, Powereds stigmatized by institutional classification, meritocracy distorted by politics and legacy).

The HCP's competitive structure serves as the narrative engine for the series, functioning simultaneously as a coming-of-age academic story, a power-scaling progression framework, and an extended examination of what "earning" the right to power actually means. The system asks whether institutional gatekeeping produces better Heroes or merely more compliant ones, and whether the distinction between Super and Powered is a legitimate safety measure or a prejudice encoded in law.
