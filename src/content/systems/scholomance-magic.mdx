## Mechanics

Magic in the *Scholomance* trilogy runs on **mana** — personal magical energy that every wizard possesses in limited supply and must actively generate. Mana is not passively regenerated; it must be built up through sustained effort. The most common methods are physical exercise, repetitive manual labour, musical practice, or any disciplined activity that requires focus and exertion. A wizard who sits idle depletes their mana reserves and becomes vulnerable. This resource economy is the system's central constraint: every spell has a mana cost, and wizards must constantly balance expenditure against generation.

**Spells** are structured through language and shaped by a wizard's personal **affinity** — an innate predisposition toward certain kinds of magic. A wizard with an affinity for metals works metal-related spells more efficiently; one with an affinity for languages learns spells in foreign tongues more easily. Spells can be learned from books, taught by other wizards, or (rarely) spontaneously generated by a wizard working within their affinity. The Scholomance's library provides spell texts in hundreds of languages, and the school's coursework is designed to push students toward discovering and developing their affinities.

**Malia** is the dark alternative. Instead of generating mana through personal effort, a wizard can rip life-force directly from living things — insects, plants, animals, or people. Malia is faster and more powerful than honestly generated mana, but it is addictive, corrupting, and socially abhorrent. Sustained malia use warps the practitioner physically and psychologically, and malia-users (called **maleficers**) are hunted by other wizards. The temptation of malia is the system's central moral axis: it is always available, always easier, and always destructive.

The **Scholomance** itself is a magical construct — a pocket dimension created by enclave wizards and suspended in the Void between reality and the spaces where **maleficaria** breed. Maleficaria are magical predators drawn to mana; they feed on wizards (especially young ones generating mana for the first time) and range from small parasites to building-sized horrors. The school was designed to concentrate all the world's magical children in one defensible location, but its protections have degraded. Maleficaria infiltrate through vents, drains, and structural flaws, and the school's graduation ceremony requires students to fight through a hall full of them.

**Enclaves** are the other critical structural element: permanent magical communities in the mundane world, maintained by vast collective mana expenditure and protective wards. Enclave membership is the difference between relative safety and a life of constant threat. The trilogy's political dimension centres on the inequity between enclave and enclaveless wizards, and El's ultimate project is to restructure the entire system.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation is **mana scarcity**. Wizards cannot cast beyond their reserves, and generating mana is slow, tedious work. A wizard who exhausts their mana mid-combat is simply dead. The Scholomance's lack of adults or teachers means students cannot rely on anyone with deeper reserves to protect them.

Malia's cost is **corruption and addiction**. A wizard who takes malia once finds it easier the second time, and progressively harder to generate honest mana. Heavy malia use produces physical deformity and madness. Malia-saturated areas attract maleficaria, creating a vicious cycle.

**Affinity misalignment** is a subtler cost. A wizard who learns spells outside their affinity pays higher mana costs and produces weaker effects. El's affinity for mass destruction means her most efficient spells are exactly the ones she cannot safely use inside the Scholomance — and the ones the world outside most fears.

The **Scholomance itself** imposes costs through its degraded state: students lose roughly a quarter of their cohort before graduation, and the graduation gauntlet kills many more. The school's course-generation algorithm sometimes provides useless or dangerous material, and its maintenance systems occasionally malfunction catastrophically.

## Practitioner access

Magical ability is hereditary but appears with some randomness — wizard children can be born to mundane parents, and wizard parents can have mundane children, though the latter is rarer. All wizard children manifest abilities in early adolescence and are drawn into the Scholomance at fourteen (or equivalent), spending four years inside before the graduation gauntlet.

Within the wizard community, access is sharply stratified by enclave membership. Enclave children enter the Scholomance with better supplies, pre-arranged alliances, and spells suited to their affinities. Enclaveless children — often from isolated families or first-generation wizards — must scrounge for resources and form ad hoc alliances. This class divide is the trilogy's primary social concern.

## Cultural role

In the mundane world, wizards maintain a **masquerade** — ordinary humans are unaware of magic's existence. Within the wizarding world, magic is the foundation of all social organisation. Enclaves are the primary political unit, and a wizard's standing depends on their power, affinity, and enclave affiliation. The Scholomance is universally known and universally dreaded: every wizard parent sends their child knowing the odds, because the alternative — facing maleficaria alone during adolescence — is worse. The trilogy's arc traces El's effort to replace both the Scholomance and the enclave system with something less brutally Darwinian.
