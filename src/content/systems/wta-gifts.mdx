## Mechanics

The Garou (werewolves) draw on three internal resources and two categories of supernatural abilities.

**Rage** is primal fury — the werewolf's connection to their violent, predatory nature. Spending Rage allows extra actions in combat, activates certain Gifts, and fuels shapeshifting between the five forms (Homid/human, Glabro/near-human, Crinos/war-form, Hispo/near-wolf, Lupus/wolf). The Crinos war form is a nine-foot killing machine that induces supernatural terror (the Delirium) in ordinary humans. Rage is replenished by anger-provoking situations and the cycle of the moon.

**Gnosis** is spiritual awareness — the werewolf's connection to the spirit world. Spending Gnosis activates spiritually-oriented Gifts, powers fetishes (spirit-bound objects), and enables passage into the **Umbra** — the spirit world that exists as a reflection of the physical world, separated by the Gauntlet (a metaphysical barrier). Gnosis is replenished through meditation, sacred sites, and communion with spirits.

**Willpower** represents self-control and determination. It can be spent for automatic successes, to resist frenzy, and to power certain abilities.

**Gifts** are specific supernatural abilities taught to Garou by spirits. Each Gift is learned through a bargain or service to the spirit that knows it — the spirit teaches the Gift in exchange for a task, chiminage, or demonstration of worthiness. Gifts are organized by **tribe** (political-cultural groupings), **auspice** (the moon phase under which the Garou was born, determining their spiritual role), and **breed** (whether born to human or wolf parents). Examples include: commanding fire, communicating with animals, sensing the supernatural, blinding speed, and calling upon the force of the Wyld (primal creative chaos).

**Rites** are formal ceremonies — longer, ritual-based magical workings that achieve effects Gifts cannot. Rites include: summoning and binding spirits, opening passages to the Umbra, consecrating sacred sites, funeral rites, punishment rites, and seasonal celebrations. Rites require time, participants, and often specific materials or locations.

In **Werewolf: The Forsaken** (Chronicles of Darkness), the system is redesigned around **Renown** (five types: Purity, Glory, Honor, Wisdom, Cunning) that gate Gift access, and the spirit-bargaining emphasis is heightened. The Umbra is renamed the **Shadow**, and the relationship between werewolves and spirits is reframed as boundary enforcement rather than ecological warfare.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **frenzy**. High Rage makes a Garou more powerful in combat but more likely to lose control — entering a berserker state in which they attack indiscriminately, potentially harming allies, innocents, or violating the Litany (the Garou's code of conduct). Managing Rage is the central mechanical tension: you need it to survive, but it makes you dangerous.

The second cost is **spiritual obligation**. Gifts are not free — spirits demand services, and the spirit world has its own politics. A Garou who offends spirits may find Gift-learning blocked. Rites require community participation and sacred conditions that are increasingly scarce in a world the Garou are losing (the Apocalypse in the game's title refers to an ongoing spiritual-ecological collapse).

The third cost is **social isolation**. The Delirium prevents werewolves from coexisting openly with humans. The Garou live double lives, and their supernatural nature strains or destroys mortal relationships. The five forms create versatility but also identity confusion — a Garou is neither fully human nor fully wolf.

## Practitioner access

The Garou condition is hereditary — werewolves are born, not made. A Garou is the offspring of a werewolf and either a human or a wolf (or, rarely and problematically, two werewolves). The First Change typically occurs in adolescence, triggered by stress. After the Change, the young Garou is found by existing Garou and inducted into their tribe and sept (local community). Gift learning requires finding and negotiating with spirits, which requires access to the Umbra and the guidance of experienced elders.

## Cultural role

Garou society is organized around the imperative of fighting the **Wyrm** — a cosmic force of corruption and entropy that is winning. Werewolves see themselves as Gaia's (Earth's) warriors, and their powers are tools of ecological and spiritual warfare. Gifts and Rites are not personal advancement but communal resources in a losing war. The tribal and auspice system creates a military-spiritual hierarchy where every Garou has a defined role: Ahroun (full-moon warriors), Theurge (crescent-moon spirit-speakers), Philodox (half-moon judges), Galliard (gibbous-moon storytellers), and Ragabash (new-moon tricksters).
