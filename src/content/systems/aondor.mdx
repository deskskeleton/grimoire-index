## Mechanics

AonDor is the magic system of the Arelenes on the Cosmere world of Sel. It operates by channeling the **Dor** — the concentrated Investiture pooled in Sel's Cognitive Realm — through drawn symbols called **Aons**. An Elantrian practitioner traces an Aon in the air (the line glows as it is drawn), and upon completion the glyph activates, producing its associated effect. Aon Ashe produces light. Aon Ien heals. Aon Daa projects a force blast. Aon Tia enables teleportation.

Each Aon is a stylized representation of the geography around Elantris — the city at the center of AonDor's power. The base glyph, **Aon Rao**, maps the shape of the Arelon coastline, mountains, and the city itself. All other Aons are built on or derived from this base shape. This geographic dependency is the system's most distinctive structural feature: AonDor draws power from the local landscape, and changes to that landscape directly affect the magic's function.

Aons can be modified by adding **modifiers** — subsidiary lines and shapes that adjust the size, intensity, direction, duration, or targeting of the base effect. Skilled practitioners combine Aon modifiers to produce precisely calibrated effects, making AonDor a system that rewards study and precision. Complex Aon constructions can be inscribed on surfaces to produce persistent enchantments.

## Limitations and costs

The system's most dramatic limitation is **geographic fragility**. When an earthquake altered the coastline near Elantris ten years before the novel's events (an event called the **Reod**), every Aon became slightly wrong — its geographic mapping no longer matched reality. AonDor ceased to function entirely. Elantrians, mid-transformation into their glowing, silver-skinned state, were left trapped in decayed, pain-wracked bodies. The central mystery of *Elantris* is understanding and fixing this failure.

The second cost is **practitioner access**: only those who have undergone the **Shaod** — a transformative event that selects individuals seemingly at random from the Arelene population — become Elantrians capable of drawing Aons. Before the Reod, the Shaod granted radiant, effectively immortal bodies; after the Reod, it produces the same transformation but broken, leaving the new Elantrian in constant pain.

The third cost is **proximity degradation**. Because AonDor is powered by the geographically filtered Dor, it weakens with distance from Arelon. An Elantrian far from home would find their Aons increasingly feeble. This is a general property of Selish magic systems.

Drawing complex Aons takes time and concentration. In combat, this is a meaningful constraint — an interrupted Aon dissipates without effect.

## Practitioner access

The Shaod selects practitioners from the Arelene population. Before the Reod, it chose people of particular merit or spiritual alignment (the exact criteria are debated in-world), transformed them into luminous, ageless Elantrians, and granted them the ability to draw Aons. After the Reod, the Shaod continues to select people, but the transformation fails partway — producing cursed, pain-ridden half-Elantrians who are cast into the ruined city.

No one can choose to become an Elantrian. The Shaod's selection mechanism is one of the novel's central mysteries.

## Cultural role

Before the Reod, Elantrians were the benevolent ruling class of Arelon — god-like figures who used AonDor to heal, build, and govern. Their city, Elantris, was the most magnificent in the region. The Reod's catastrophic failure collapsed this social order overnight: Elantrians became objects of horror and pity, Elantris became a prison-slum, and the neighboring theocratic empire of Fjordell (which practices Dakhor, a rival Selish magic) moved to fill the power vacuum. The novel's arc is the restoration of AonDor and, with it, the political and cultural order that depended on it.
