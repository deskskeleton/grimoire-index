## Mechanics

The **Weave** is the fundamental infrastructure of arcane magic in the Forgotten Realms. It is not a spell or a power source but a **medium** — a lattice of magical energy that permeates the world, maintained by the goddess **Mystra** (or her predecessors, Mystryl and the second Mystra). When a wizard casts a spell, they are not creating magical energy from nothing; they are reaching into the Weave, shaping a portion of it according to learned formulae, and producing an effect.

The Weave functions as an interface layer between mortal minds and the **raw magic** of the cosmos (sometimes called the **Raw Chaos** or the **Primal Flow**). Without the Weave, raw magic would be inaccessible — too powerful, too chaotic, and too dangerous for mortal spellcasters to control. The Weave translates raw magic into the structured, predictable forms that D&D spell mechanics represent: specific verbal and somatic components produce specific effects at specific power levels.

The Weave has several properties:

**Universality**: the Weave covers the entire world (Toril). Magic works everywhere the Weave reaches. Areas where the Weave is damaged or absent — **dead magic zones** — are regions where no arcane magic functions at all.

**Regulation by Mystra**: the goddess can and does intervene in how the Weave operates. After Karsus's Folly (a mage who briefly usurped divine power and nearly destroyed the Weave), Mystra imposed a limit on spell power — no mortal spell above a certain level can be cast. This is the in-universe justification for D&D's spell level cap.

**The Shadow Weave**: a parallel, darker lattice maintained by **Shar**, the goddess of darkness and loss. The Shadow Weave allows casting that bypasses Mystra's restrictions but with different strengths and weaknesses — shadow magic is better at enchantment and illusion but weaker at evocation and transmutation.

**Wild magic zones**: areas where the Weave is frayed or unstable, causing spells to produce unpredictable effects — random surges, altered targets, amplified or nullified results.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation is **divine dependency**. The Weave is maintained by Mystra. When Mystra dies — as she has multiple times in Forgotten Realms history — the Weave collapses, producing catastrophic magical events. The **Spellplague** (triggered by Mystra's murder during the Time of Troubles) transformed the landscape, killed thousands of spellcasters, and rewrote the rules of magic for a century. The Weave's divine dependency means that all arcane magic is, at bottom, subject to a single point of failure.

The second limitation is **Mystra's restrictions**. The goddess imposes hard limits on what the Weave can do. Spells above 10th level are forbidden (Karsus's Folly demonstrated why). Certain types of magic are restricted or regulated. These constraints exist to prevent catastrophic misuse but also mean that arcane magic has a ceiling that cannot be exceeded within the system.

The third limitation is **dead magic and wild magic zones**. Regions where the Weave is damaged, absent, or unstable render spellcasting unreliable or impossible. These zones persist for centuries and represent permanent scarring of the magical infrastructure.

## Practitioner access

Anyone with arcane aptitude can learn to access the Weave through study (wizards), innate talent (sorcerers), or patron-granted connection (warlocks). Divine casters (clerics, druids) access magic through different pathways but their magic also interacts with the Weave in most Forgotten Realms lore. The Weave is not a barrier to entry but a medium — like air for sound. Access depends on the caster's tradition, not on a relationship with the Weave itself.

## Cultural role

In the Forgotten Realms, magic is ubiquitous and institutional. The city of Silverymoon has magical streetlights; the nation of Halruaa is a magocracy; Waterdeep has magical law enforcement. The Weave's reliability and Mystra's stewardship enable this — magic works predictably enough to build infrastructure on. The major magical institutions (Mystra's church, the Harpers, the Red Wizards of Thay, the Arcane Brotherhood) all operate within the Weave's framework, and theological debates about the nature of the Weave mirror real-world debates about infrastructure governance — who controls it, who can restrict it, and what happens when it fails.
