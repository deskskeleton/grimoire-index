## Mechanics

In the **Riddle-Master** trilogy, the art of riddlery is the primary intellectual and magical discipline of the realm. A riddle in this tradition is not a casual puzzle but a formally structured proposition: a question with a strict answer, encoding a fundamental truth about the world. The College of Caithnard trains riddle-masters in the accumulation and interpretation of riddles, and a master who wins the riddle-game (a formal contest of questions and answers) demonstrates not merely cleverness but deep comprehension of the world's hidden structure.

The magical dimension of riddlery becomes apparent as Morgon of Hed discovers that understanding a thing's essential nature — which is what a riddle's answer provides — grants power over it. Shape-changing, the ability to take animal form, is learned by comprehending the essential nature of the animal so thoroughly that one can become it. Mind-reading operates on the same principle: to understand a mind fully is to hear it. The trilogy's most powerful practitioners can reshape matter, command the elements, and move between places by understanding the deep structure of the world itself.

The **land-rule** is a related but distinct power. Each kingdom's ruler shares a mystical bond with their land — they sense its condition, feel its distress, and in some cases can command its elements. Morgon, as Prince of Hed, feels his land's fields and harvests as an extension of his own body. This bond is not learned but inherited with the title, and a ruler who abandons or betrays their land suffers as the land suffers.

In *The Forgotten Beasts of Eld*, the art of **Calling** operates on a purer form of the same principle. Sybel, a wizard-woman living on Eld Mountain, summons and commands legendary beasts — the Black Swan, the Boar Cyrin, the Lyon Gules, the Dragon Gyld — by knowing their true names. A true name is not merely a label but a complete expression of the creature's nature; to speak it with full understanding is to compel the creature's presence and obedience. Sybel's father and grandfather built the menagerie through generations of patient study, each learning the names that their predecessors could not.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost of riddle-magic is **knowledge itself**. Power requires understanding, and understanding requires years of study, travel, and risk. An unanswered riddle — one whose answer is unknown — is considered the most dangerous thing in the world, because it represents a gap in understanding that can be exploited. Morgon's three stars mark him for a destiny encoded in riddles no one can answer, and much of the trilogy's tension comes from the danger of incomplete knowledge.

The moral cost is equally central. Calling, in *Forgotten Beasts*, grants absolute power over the called creature — and McKillip treats that absoluteness as inherently corrupting. When Sybel is manipulated into using her Calling against human beings, the violation is presented as a fundamental betrayal of the art's nature. Power over another's essential self is the deepest form of domination, and McKillip's protagonists must learn to wield it with restraint or renounce it entirely.

Shape-changing carries its own risk: a shape-changer who loses themselves in an animal form — who forgets their own essential nature while inhabiting another's — may be unable to return. The boundary between understanding a thing and becoming it is perilously thin.

## Practitioner access

Riddlery as an intellectual discipline is open to anyone willing to study at Caithnard, but the magical applications of riddle-knowledge appear to require an innate capacity that manifests unpredictably. Morgon's abilities far exceed those of other trained riddle-masters, and the trilogy implies that his three stars mark him as something more than an ordinary practitioner. The land-rulers inherit their bond with the title, but the bond's strength varies.

Calling in *Forgotten Beasts* runs in Sybel's family line — each generation of Eld Mountain wizards has expanded the menagerie — but the art is presented as learnable rather than purely hereditary. What it demands is patience, solitude, and a willingness to devote a lifetime to the study of names.

## Cultural role

In the Riddle-Master world, riddlery is a respected scholarly tradition — riddle-masters hold social standing comparable to bards or priests, and the College of Caithnard is a major cultural institution. The land-rule is woven into the political fabric of every kingdom, making each ruler's bond with their land a matter of public concern. The High One, the enigmatic overlord of the realm, is the ultimate riddle-master, and the world's deepest mysteries are encoded in the riddle-tradition.

In *Forgotten Beasts*, Calling is a solitary art practised in isolation. Sybel's power is known but feared, and her involvement in human politics is treated as a disruption of the natural order — a wizard descending from her mountain to meddle in affairs she should have left alone. Both works position magic as something that flourishes in contemplation and is endangered by worldly entanglement.
