## Mechanics

**Psionics** in D&D is a power system that uses mental discipline rather than arcane formulae or divine prayer. Instead of preparing and casting spells from a list, psionic characters **manifest powers** by spending **power points** from an internal pool. This point-based resource system allows flexible allocation — a psion can manifest many weak powers or a few augmented powerful ones.

Powers are organized into six **disciplines**:

**Clairsentience** — perceiving information at a distance or across time (precognition, remote viewing, object reading). **Metacreativity** — creating matter and energy from ectoplasm (constructs, walls, weapons). **Psychokinesis** — manipulating matter and energy with the mind (telekinesis, energy blasts, force fields). **Psychometabolism** — altering one's own body (shapeshifting, healing, enhanced attributes). **Psychoportation** — movement powers (teleportation, dimensional travel, time manipulation). **Telepathy** — mind-to-mind contact (reading thoughts, mental domination, psychic communication).

The key mechanical distinction from Vancian magic is **augmentation**: many psionic powers can be enhanced by spending additional power points, increasing their range, duration, damage, or number of targets. A 1st-level power manifested with 9 power points invested can rival the effect of a 5th-level spell. This flexibility is capped by the manifester's level (maximum power points per manifestation equals manifester level).

In the **Dark Sun** setting (Athas), psionics is the dominant supernatural tradition — nearly every living thing has some psionic potential, and the setting's dying-world ecology makes psionics more reliable than the scarce arcane magic (which requires consuming life force) or divine magic (which is granted by elementals rather than gods).

In 5th edition D&D, psionics was integrated more closely with the standard class framework — the Psi Warrior fighter and Soulknife rogue use psionic dice rather than a separate power-point system, reducing mechanical distinctness.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **power point expenditure**. The psionic power pool is finite and recovers only after rest. Unlike spell slots (which are discrete), power points create a spending-management challenge: burn too many points early, and you have nothing left.

The second limitation is **augmentation caps**. The manifester level limits how many points can be spent on a single manifestation, preventing low-level characters from producing high-level effects even if they have the points.

The third limitation is **display**. Psionic manifestation produces visible, audible, or olfactory side effects — a flash of light, a humming sound, a smell of ozone — that reveal psionic activity to observers. This is less conspicuous than a fireball but still detectable.

## Practitioner access

Psionic ability is innate — it requires a spark of mental potential that not all creatures possess. Training develops this potential into reliable power. In most D&D settings, psionics is rare and poorly understood, often conflated with magic by the uninformed. In Dark Sun, it is ubiquitous — psionic academies exist in every city-state, and even animals and insects possess rudimentary psionic abilities.

## Cultural role

Psionics occupies different cultural niches depending on the setting. In Dark Sun, it is mainstream and institutional — psionic schools are social pillars. In the Forgotten Realms, it is rare and exotic, associated with aberrant creatures (mind flayers, aboleths) and specific organizations. In Eberron, psionics is recognized alongside arcane and divine magic as a legitimate form of power. The cultural role consistently contrasts with arcane magic: where wizardry is external and learned from books, psionics is internal and developed through mental training.
