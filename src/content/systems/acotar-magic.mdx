## Mechanics

Fae Court Magic in Prythian is organized around the seven courts, each ruled by a High Lord whose power is thematically linked to the court's domain. The Night Court's High Lord wields darkness, starlight, and mental manipulation; the Spring Court's controls nature, growth, and shapeshifting; the Autumn Court's commands fire; and so on. These powers are not learned but inherited — when a High Lord dies, power passes to a successor, often a blood relative, though the mechanism of selection is not fully understood. The transfer is immediate and total, marking the new High Lord with the full weight of the court's magical authority.

Beyond High Lord powers, fae possess a range of innate abilities that vary by individual and bloodline. Winnowing allows instantaneous teleportation across distances. Glamour permits the alteration of appearance. Daemati are rare fae who can enter and manipulate the minds of others, reading thoughts, implanting suggestions, or shattering mental defenses. Mating bonds — instinctive magical connections between fated partners — create links that manifest physically and emotionally, and can be accepted or rejected but not fabricated.

The Cauldron is the system's most powerful artifact, a primordial object said to have created the fae themselves. It can unmake and remake beings, strip away humanity, grant new forms, and resurrect the dead — though its use carries enormous risk and its will is not fully controllable. Feyre's resurrection by the seven High Lords, each contributing a spark of their power, demonstrates both the system's cooperative potential and its capacity to produce unprecedented hybrid practitioners.

The magic system also includes bargains — magical contracts sealed with tattoos that carry binding supernatural force. Breaking a bargain invites consequences that range from pain to death, making oaths and agreements a form of magic in themselves rather than mere social convention.

## Limitations and costs

High Lord power is vast but not unlimited. Practitioners tire, and extreme exertion can leave even the most powerful fae depleted for extended periods. The use of daemati abilities against a well-shielded mind requires enormous effort and can be repelled. Winnowing has practical limits of distance and the ability to visualize the destination. Most fae possess only modest magic — enhanced strength, longevity, and minor glamour — with truly formidable abilities concentrated in the High Lords, their inner circles, and certain rare bloodlines.

The Cauldron's power comes with the cost of unpredictability. It has its own will, and those who use it risk being used in return. Beings remade by the Cauldron may emerge fundamentally altered in ways neither they nor the wielder intended. The system's bargain-magic imposes a different kind of cost: the price of magical commitment, where breaking one's word carries consequences that cannot be negotiated away.

The Wall — a magical barrier separating fae and mortal lands — represents a systemic limitation built into the world itself. Maintaining or breaking such large-scale enchantments requires power on a scale that only the most extraordinary practitioners or artifacts can provide.

## Practitioner access

Access to meaningful magical power in Prythian is sharply stratified. All fae possess some degree of magic — enhanced physical capabilities, longevity, and basic glamour at minimum — but the gap between a common fae and a High Lord is enormous. High Lord power is effectively singular within each court, passing from one ruler to the next. Feyre is an exception that proves the rule: her possession of power from all seven courts is treated as unprecedented and deeply unsettling to the existing order.

Mortals are, by default, entirely without magic. The rare exceptions — such as mortals Made by the Cauldron — are transformed rather than trained, their access to power coming through involuntary metamorphosis rather than study or inheritance.

## Cultural role

Magic in Prythian is indistinguishable from political authority. The High Lords rule because they are the most powerful, and their courts' identities are shaped by the nature of their magic. The Night Court's culture of secrecy and mental discipline reflects Rhysand's daemati abilities; the Spring Court's pastoral character mirrors Tamlin's nature magic. Challenges to a High Lord are challenges to the magical order itself.

The fae-mortal divide is the world's central political fault line, and magic is both the source of fae superiority and the justification for mortal fear. The Wall's existence codifies this division into magical infrastructure, and its eventual fall forces both societies to reckon with what coexistence means when one side holds overwhelming supernatural power.
