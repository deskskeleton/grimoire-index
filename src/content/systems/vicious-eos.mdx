## Mechanics

An ExtraOrdinary is created when a person dies — clinically, fully — and is brought back. The power that manifests is not random. It is shaped by whatever the dying person was fixated on in their final moments: their last thought, their deepest fear, their most consuming desire. Victor Vale, who was thinking about pain and control during his death, gained the ability to manipulate the pain receptors of anyone near him. Eli Ever, who was praying for God to find him worthy, came back with a regenerative healing factor that makes him effectively immortal. Sydney Clarke, who drowned in icy water and was fixated on loss and absence, gained the ability to raise the recently dead.

The mechanism behind EO creation is never fully explained in scientific or metaphysical terms. The novels treat it as an observable phenomenon — something that can be studied and deliberately induced — without offering a unified theory of how or why it works. What matters is the correlation between the psychological state at death and the resulting ability.

Each EO's power is singular and specific. There is no progression system, no leveling up, and no way to acquire additional abilities. An EO gets one power, and that power is permanent.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is the death itself. The process is not reliably survivable, and even successful resuscitation carries no guarantee that a power will manifest — or that the power will be useful. Many EOs gain abilities that are narrow, situational, or actively self-destructive.

Beyond the initial cost, EO powers often carry ongoing psychological burdens. The fixation that shapes the power tends to persist as an obsessive trait in the resurrected person, creating a feedback loop between ability and personality disorder. Victor's power over pain leaves him in a state of chronic discomfort that he must actively suppress. Eli's conviction that he was chosen by God hardens into messianic delusion.

There is also a social cost. EOs have no legal status, no institutional support, and no community. Discovery typically leads to either exploitation or extermination. The government agency that becomes aware of EOs in *Vengeful* treats them as threats to be contained, not citizens to be protected.

## Practitioner access

Anyone can theoretically become an EO, but the barrier to entry is death. Deliberate attempts to create EOs — as Victor and Eli attempt in college — require a willingness to die and a partner capable of resuscitation, with no certainty of success. Most EOs appear to be created by accident: survivors of drowning, cardiac arrest, violent trauma, or medical emergencies who return with something extra.

There is no hereditary component, no training regimen, and no way to predict who will gain a power or what it will be. The only common factor is the near-death experience itself.

## Cultural role

EOs exist in secret. There is no public awareness of their existence for most of the series, and EOs themselves have no shared culture, organization, or identity. They are isolated individuals navigating a world that has no framework for understanding them. The series uses this isolation to explore questions about power without accountability — what people do when they have extraordinary abilities and no rules, traditions, or communities to constrain them. The answer, consistently, is that they serve themselves.
