## Mechanics

Magic in Nehwon operates through a loose combination of ritual, incantation, summoning, and the intervention of supernatural beings. There is no formal taxonomy of spells or systematic theory of magical energy — effects are produced through procedures that are part scholarship, part negotiation with otherworldly forces, and part sheer unpredictable inspiration. A wizard might summon a demon through an elaborate ritual one day and achieve a similar result through a muttered word and a gesture the next, depending on the story's needs and the wizard's temperament.

The most prominent magical figures in the stories are Ningauble of the Seven Eyes and Sheelba of the Eyeless Face, the patron wizards of Fafhrd and the Gray Mouser respectively. These two are powerful, enigmatic, and fundamentally unreliable. They send the heroes on quests for their own obscure purposes, provide cryptic prophecies and minimal assistance, and occasionally intervene with dramatic magical effects when it suits them. Their magic is presented as genuinely formidable but deliberately opaque — the reader (and the heroes) never fully understand its rules or limits.

Lesser practitioners range widely in competence. The Gray Mouser himself was briefly apprenticed to the hedge-wizard Glavas Rho and retains some minor magical skill, used sparingly and often ineffectively. Other sorcerers encountered in the stories include genuinely dangerous figures (Hristomilo, with his summoned rats and darkness) and pathetic charlatans. The city of Lankhmar supports an ecosystem of magical practitioners embedded in its thieves' guilds, temples, and merchant houses, none of whom are entirely trustworthy.

Sorcerous effects in Nehwon include summoning creatures and entities, projecting illusions, divining the future and distant events, manipulating elements, creating enchantments on objects, and various forms of combat magic. The effects are vivid and imaginative but deliberately resist systematization — Leiber presents magic as something that should feel strange and unpredictable rather than reliable and rule-governed.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation of Nehwon sorcery is its unreliability. Spells backfire, summonings go wrong, enchantments fade at inopportune moments, and magical plans unravel in ways their architects did not foresee. This unpredictability is not a mechanical rule but a narrative pattern — Leiber uses magical failure as a source of comedy, horror, and plot complication in roughly equal measure.

A second cost is obligation. Wizards who work with supernatural patrons or entities owe debts that are called in at awkward times. Fafhrd and the Mouser's relationship with Ningauble and Sheelba is built on an endless cycle of quests undertaken to repay magical favors, and the patron wizards are shameless about exploiting this leverage. Lesser sorcerers who bargain with demons face similar but more dangerous entanglements.

Physical and material costs appear inconsistently. Some rituals require elaborate preparation, rare ingredients, and specific conditions. Others seem to require nothing more than knowledge and will. Leiber does not enforce a consistent economy of magical expenditure; the costs are whatever the story requires them to be.

## Practitioner access

Magic in Nehwon is accessible to anyone willing to study it, but the quality of available instruction varies enormously. There are no great academies or systematic curricula — apprenticeship to an individual wizard is the primary path, and the quality of the master determines the quality of the student. Many practitioners are self-taught from recovered texts of varying reliability. The Gray Mouser's fragmentary magical education is representative: he knows enough to attempt minor sorcery, but not enough to do it safely or consistently. Truly powerful wizards like Ningauble and Sheelba are singular figures whose origins and training are deliberately left mysterious.

## Cultural role

In Lankhmar and across Nehwon, magic is a recognized part of the social fabric but not a respected one. Wizards are regarded as dangerous eccentrics at best and malicious parasites at worst. The city of Lankhmar tolerates their presence because they are occasionally useful and always dangerous to antagonize, but no sorcerer holds formal civic authority. Temples to Nehwon's numerous gods practice their own forms of divine magic, adding another layer of supernatural activity to an already crowded landscape. The overall cultural attitude toward magic is pragmatic wariness: it exists, it works (sometimes), and it is best avoided unless you have no alternative.
