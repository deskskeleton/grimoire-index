## Mechanics

**Impression** occurs at the moment a dragon hatches from its egg. The hatchling emerges, surveys the candidates assembled on the Hatching Ground, and chooses its rider by making immediate telepathic contact. The bond is instantaneous and permanent. From the moment of Impression, dragon and rider share a telepathic link — they can communicate mentally across any distance, sense each other's emotions, and (in the case of strong bonds) share sensory perceptions. The dragon addresses its rider by a shortened honorific name (F'lar for Fallarnon, F'nor for Famanoran), and the rider becomes the dragon's sole human companion for life.

The dragons of Pern are **genetically engineered descendants** of the native fire-lizards — small, telepathic flying creatures that the original colonists discovered and that the geneticist Kitti Ping Yung bioengineered into full-sized dragons capable of fighting Thread. Fire-lizards themselves bond with humans in a similar but less intense fashion — Menolly's nine fire-lizards demonstrate the wild-type version of the Impression mechanism. The engineering amplified the fire-lizards' natural telepathy, size, and fire-breathing capability while preserving the bonding instinct.

Dragons possess several abilities that make them effective against Thread. They can **chew firestone** (a phosphine-bearing mineral) and belch flame to char Thread in the air. They can fly at high altitudes for sustained periods. And they can teleport — vanishing from one location and appearing at another instantaneously by traveling through a cold, dark void called **between**. Teleportation via *between* requires the dragon to hold a clear mental image of the destination, supplied by the rider. An unclear image risks emerging in solid matter, which is fatal.

The most extraordinary ability is **temporal displacement**. Dragons can travel *between* times as well as places — Lessa's desperate time-jump of four hundred years in *Dragonflight* is the most dramatic demonstration. Temporal travel is extremely dangerous, requires precise knowledge of the destination time, and can create paradoxes and psychological strain.

Dragon colors correspond to **sex and role**. Gold (queen) dragons are the largest, are always female, and their riders are always women. Bronze dragons are the largest males and their riders traditionally lead Weyrs. Brown, blue, and green dragons fill out the fighting wings in descending order of size. Green dragons are female but rendered infertile by firestone; their riders are predominantly (though not exclusively) male. Dragon mating flights directly influence rider behavior — when a queen rises to mate, her rider is psychically drawn into the experience, and the rider of the bronze who catches the queen typically becomes Weyrleader.

## Limitations and costs

The most devastating cost is **severance**. If a dragon dies, the psychic trauma to the rider is so severe that most riders commit suicide — either deliberately or by going *between* and never returning. The bond is not merely emotional attachment; it is a structural component of the rider's mind. Losing it is described as losing part of oneself. The reverse — a rider's death — sends the dragon *between* permanently, dying of grief. This mutual dependency gives every Threadfall the stakes of potential psychological annihilation.

The **social cost** is total institutional commitment. Dragonriders live in Weyrs — communal military installations separated from the Holds and Crafthalls of ordinary Pernese society. Riders eat, sleep, train, and fight together. Their social obligations, sexual relationships, and political allegiances are all shaped by the Weyr structure. A rider does not have an independent life outside the Weyr in any meaningful sense.

The **mating flight** dynamic imposes reproductive and sexual constraints. Queen dragons' mating flights determine Weyr leadership and influence riders' intimate relationships in ways that override personal preference. This is presented in the earlier books as natural and acceptable but has generated significant critical discussion.

Firestone renders **green dragons infertile**, which is treated as a necessary trade-off — greens are needed to fight Thread, and their smaller size makes them the most agile fighters, but the species' reproduction depends entirely on the small number of gold queens. A plague or disaster affecting queens threatens the entire dragon population.

Travel *between* carries a **risk of death** with every jump. A rider who cannot visualize the destination clearly, or whose dragon is too injured or exhausted to concentrate, may emerge inside solid rock or simply never emerge at all. The cold void of *between* is also psychologically disturbing — three heartbeats of absolute sensory deprivation on every transit.

## Practitioner access

Impression is **selective and involuntary** — the dragon chooses the rider, not the reverse. Candidates are Searched by existing dragonriders whose dragons can sense the mental qualities that make a person compatible with Impression. Searched candidates are brought to the Weyr and presented at Hatchings, where the newborn dragons choose among them. Not every candidate Impresses — some stand at multiple Hatchings without being chosen, and there is no way to force or guarantee a bond.

The qualities that make a person suitable for Impression are poorly defined but appear to involve empathy, mental openness, and emotional resilience. There is no hereditary requirement in the strict sense, though dragonrider bloodlines do tend to produce more successful candidates. Social class is not a barrier — candidates are Searched from Holds, Crafthalls, and all social strata.

Fire-lizard bonds are more accessible — anyone who is present when fire-lizard eggs hatch and who offers food to the starving hatchlings can Impress one or more. Fire-lizard bonds are less intense and less exclusive than dragon bonds but follow the same basic mechanism.

## Cultural role

Dragonriders occupy a **privileged and contested** position in Pernese society. During Passes (the periods when Thread falls), riders are essential defenders and receive tithes of food and goods from the Holds they protect. During Intervals (the centuries-long gaps between Passes), riders are seen as parasites — consuming resources while performing no visible service. This cyclical tension between necessity and resentment drives much of the series' political conflict. The Weyrs' independence from Hold and Craft authority, the riders' sexual customs (more permissive than Hold society), and the dragons' sheer physical intimidation create a distinct dragonrider subculture that is simultaneously admired, feared, and resented by the rest of Pern.
