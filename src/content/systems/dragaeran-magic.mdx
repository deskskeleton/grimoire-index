## Mechanics

Dragaeran magic operates through three distinct traditions that coexist in the same world:

**Imperial Sorcery** is the dominant magical tradition. It draws power from the **Imperial Orb** — an artifact that hovers above the Emperor's head and provides ambient magical energy to every citizen of the Empire through a network-like connection called the **Orb link**. Any Dragaeran can access basic sorcery through the Orb — teleportation (the most common application), communication over distance, creating light and heat, and psionic communication. Advanced sorcery requires study and talent, but the Orb provides the energy, making basic magical functions effectively free for all citizens. Sorcery is structured, clean, and institutionally supported.

**Eastern Witchcraft** is the magical tradition of Easterners (humans). It uses the practitioner's own psychic energy — focused through verbal incantations, herbal preparations, and ritual — to produce effects. Witchcraft excels at areas where sorcery is weak: psychic contact with animals (Vlad's familiar Loiosh communicates through witchcraft-enhanced telepathy), emotional influence, divination, and subtle effects that operate on a personal, intimate scale. Witchcraft is harder to detect than sorcery, requires no external power source, and can produce effects that sorcery cannot replicate — but it is physically draining, socially stigmatized, and less powerful in raw terms.

**Pre-Empire Sorcery** (elder sorcery) predates the creation of the Imperial Orb. It draws on raw magical energy without the Orb's mediation, producing effects that are more powerful but less controlled than modern sorcery. Few living practitioners understand it — **Sethra Lavode** (a 250,000-year-old undead Dragonlord) is the primary repository of elder knowledge, and **Morrolan** has studied some pre-Empire techniques. Elder sorcery can accomplish things that Orb-mediated sorcery cannot, but its use is rare and dangerous.

**Necromancy** exists as a further discipline associated with Sethra Lavode and the Paths of the Dead — the metaphysical geography of the afterlife. Sethra's own undead state is a product of these techniques, and the series explores the boundary between life, death, and the various afterlife states available in Dragaeran cosmology.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation of Imperial Sorcery is **Orb dependency**. If the Orb is destroyed or the link severed (as during the Interregnum), sorcery ceases to function entirely. The entire Empire's magical infrastructure — including teleportation, communication, and basic utilities — collapses without the Orb. This creates a single point of failure for civilization-level magical infrastructure.

The primary cost of Eastern Witchcraft is **personal energy expenditure**. Witchcraft drains the practitioner physically and psychically. Extended use produces exhaustion, headaches, and vulnerability. Unlike sorcery, there is no external power source to draw on — every effect comes from the witch's own reserves.

The social cost is **prejudice**. Easterners are second-class citizens in the Dragaeran Empire, and their witchcraft is viewed as primitive, superstitious, and inferior to sorcery. Vlad's use of witchcraft marks him as culturally Other even among Dragaerans who otherwise accept him.

## Practitioner access

Imperial Sorcery is accessible to all Dragaeran citizens through the Orb link — basic functions (teleportation, communication) are available to everyone. Advanced sorcery requires study and talent but is widely taught. Eastern Witchcraft is taught within Eastern communities, passed from practitioner to apprentice. Vlad learned from his grandfather. The two traditions are culturally siloed — Dragaerans do not practice witchcraft, and most Easterners do not have access to sorcery (Vlad is an exception due to his circumstances). Sethra Lavode is the only known practitioner of all three traditions.

## Cultural role

The three-layer system mirrors Dragaera's social hierarchy. Imperial Sorcery is civilization's infrastructure — as taken for granted as electricity, and as dependent on centralized control. Eastern Witchcraft is the folk tradition of a marginalized minority — genuinely powerful but socially devalued. Pre-Empire Sorcery is the forgotten knowledge of a lost age — powerful but poorly understood. Brust uses these layers to explore how power structures determine which forms of knowledge are valued: sorcery is prestigious because the Empire controls it, not because it is inherently superior.
