## Mechanics

**Glass candles** are tall, twisted candles made of obsidian (called dragonglass in Westeros) that were crafted in the Valyrian Freehold before the Doom. When lit — which requires magical conditions that were absent for over a century — they enable several forms of long-distance magical communication and perception.

A lit glass candle can **transmit visions and messages** across vast distances to another lit glass candle or to a receptive mind. Quaithe uses one (or a similar device) to send cryptic messages to Daenerys across continents. The mechanism appears similar to a magical communication network — the candles function as nodes.

Glass candles can also **enter and influence dreams**, sending visions to sleeping individuals. And they permit a form of **remote viewing**, allowing the user to observe distant events — the Citadel's archmaester Marwyn describes them as enabling sight across mountains, seas, and deserts.

The critical constraint is that glass candles require **ambient magical energy** to function. For over a century — since the last dragons died — the candles at the Citadel have been cold, dark, and unlightable. Acolytes in the maesters' order are made to spend a night with a glass candle as part of their initiation, specifically to demonstrate that magic is gone and they should not waste time pursuing it. When dragons return to the world, the candles begin burning again — a fact that disturbs the Citadel's anti-magic institutional culture.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation is **dependency on ambient magic**. Glass candles are inert without a sufficient level of magical energy in the world. They cannot be activated by individual willpower or knowledge alone — they require the broader magical environment to support them.

The second limitation is **rarity and irreproducibility**. The candles are Valyrian artifacts, and the knowledge to craft them died with the Freehold. The number of surviving glass candles is small and their locations are mostly unknown. The Citadel has several; other examples are scattered across Essos.

The third limitation is **interpretive difficulty**. Visions received through glass candles are not necessarily clear or trustworthy. The user must have the knowledge to operate the candle and the judgment to interpret what they see — qualifications that few possess.

## Practitioner access

Using a glass candle requires both **possession of the artifact** and **knowledge of its operation**. The Citadel's maesters have the candles but have lost the knowledge (and inclination) to use them. Marwyn the Mage is presented as one of the very few maesters who takes magical study seriously enough to understand the candles. In Essos, practitioners like Quaithe appear to retain working knowledge. There is no formal training tradition — the knowledge is fragmentary, scattered, and often actively suppressed by institutions that regard magic as extinct or dangerous.

## Cultural role

Glass candles occupy a unique position in Westerosi intellectual culture. The Citadel — the institution responsible for education, scholarship, and advisory service to the noble houses — treats them as proof that magic has ended. The vigil with a dark glass candle is a ritual of disenchantment: accept that magic is gone and focus on natural philosophy. This institutional stance makes the relighting of the candles a crisis of legitimacy — the Citadel's worldview is built on magic's absence, and the candles' renewed function challenges that foundation. Marwyn's departure from the Citadel to seek Daenerys is partly driven by his recognition that the institutional response to magic's return will be denial and suppression.
