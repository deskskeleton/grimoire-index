## Mechanics

**Sorcery** in the Black Company operates at two distinct tiers, both kept deliberately opaque. The upper tier belongs to the **Taken** — ancient sorcerers bound to serve the Lady, each possessing immense destructive and manipulative power. The Lady and her husband the Dominator sit above even the Taken, wielding magic sufficient to reshape political geography, dominate minds across continents, and maintain effective immortality. The lower tier belongs to company hedge-wizards like **One-Eye** and **Goblin**, whose magic is comparatively minor — conjuring illusions, scrying, small curses, and petty magical duels — but still valuable in military operations.

The narrative never provides a systematic explanation of how magic works. Croaker, the company annalist, describes what he sees: the Taken summon storms, animate corpses, fly on carpet-like devices, project terror, and reshape their bodies. He does not know the mechanisms, the energy sources, or the theoretical framework. This is not an omission — it is Cook's fundamental design choice. Magic is experienced from the outside, as power exercised by beings the narrator cannot fully comprehend.

Known capabilities include: elemental destruction (fire, storms, earthquakes), mental domination (the Lady's primary weapon — she can compel obedience and loyalty across vast distances), shapeshifting (Shapeshifter's defining ability), necromantic animation, scrying and remote observation, flight, physical enhancement, and the creation of magical traps and wards. The **Shadowmasters** (Kina's servants in the southern arc) demonstrate a variant tradition involving shadow manipulation — using shadows as weapons, spies, and servants.

The power hierarchy is strict: the Dominator > the Lady > individual Taken > minor sorcerers. When a Taken is stripped of their name (their true name is a vulnerability), their power is dramatically reduced. The Lady's power is eventually stripped entirely through a specific magical ritual, demonstrating that even the most powerful sorcery has points of vulnerability.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **political exposure**. Every powerful sorcerer in the series is also a political target. The Taken serve the Lady under magical compulsion and constant mutual surveillance; the Lady is hunted by the White Rose rebellion; the Dominator is imprisoned and sought to be freed or destroyed. Power makes the sorcerer visible and vulnerable to collective action.

The second cost is **true-name vulnerability**. Knowing a sorcerer's true name provides leverage against them — it is the mechanism by which the Taken were originally bound to service. This creates an information-warfare dynamic where names are weapons and identity is a strategic asset.

The third cost is **escalation and attrition**. Magical combat between powerful sorcerers produces devastation on a scale that ruins armies and cities. The Dominator's wars are described as apocalyptic events that reshaped continents. This destructive potential means that sorcerous conflict is feared even by other sorcerers — the cost of all-out magical war is too high for anyone to win.

## Practitioner access

Major sorcery is restricted to a very small number of individuals — the Taken, the Lady, the Dominator, and the Shadowmasters. How they acquired their power is largely unexplained (consistent with Cook's refusal to systematize). Minor sorcery (One-Eye and Goblin's level) appears to be an innate talent refined through practice, accessible to a handful of individuals per generation. There is no magical academy, no formal tradition of training, and no institutional support structure for sorcerers outside the Lady's empire.

## Cultural role

Sorcery in the Black Company world is a weapon of domination. The Lady rules through magical power enforced by the Taken; the Dominator sought to rule through even greater power. The Black Company's perspective — professional soldiers serving whoever pays — frames magic as just another factor in the military calculus: terrifying, unpredictable, and best avoided when possible. Cook's treatment inverts the typical fantasy relationship between magic and narrative: magic is not the solution to problems but the source of them, and the most competent characters are those who solve problems without it.
