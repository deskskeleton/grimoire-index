## Mechanics

In Lyra's world, every human being's soul takes physical form as a **dæmon** — a sentient animal companion that is born with the person and dies with them. The dæmon is not a pet or a familiar but the person's actual soul, externalized. It speaks, has its own personality (often complementary to the human's), and provides constant companionship and counsel.

**Shapeshifting in childhood**: a child's dæmon can change form freely, shifting between any animal shape — moth, cat, ermine, bird — in response to the child's emotional state, curiosity, or whim. This fluidity represents the unsettled nature of a child's personality.

**Settling at puberty**: at some point during adolescence, the dæmon settles into a permanent, single form. This form reflects the person's fundamental nature: servants' dæmons tend to be dogs, sailors' dæmons tend to be seabirds, scholars' dæmons tend to be something learned. The settling is involuntary and deeply personal — it reveals who you truly are. Once settled, the form cannot change.

**The bond**: the dæmon and its human are connected by an invisible bond that causes agonizing pain when stretched beyond a few meters. Most humans cannot separate from their dæmons at all — the pain is psychologically and physically overwhelming. Only witches (who undergo a specific separation ritual) and certain extraordinary individuals can maintain the bond across large distances.

**Taboo against touching**: touching another person's dæmon is the most profound violation in Lyra's world — equivalent to soul-rape. Even in combat, fighters avoid touching opponents' dæmons. When Mrs. Coulter's agents seize children's dæmons during the intercision experiments, the horror is absolute.

**Intercision**: the Magisterium's experimental procedure that severs the bond between human and dæmon. The separated human becomes docile, emotionless, and diminished — alive but functionally soulless. Intercision is the series' central atrocity, and its connection to the Magisterium's theological agenda (separating humans from Dust/sin) drives the plot.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation is **distance constraint**. The bond physically prevents most humans from moving more than a few meters from their dæmon. This limits exploration, architecture (rooms must accommodate both human and dæmon), and occupation (a person whose dæmon is a large bear has different spatial needs than one whose dæmon is a moth).

The second cost is **vulnerability through the dæmon**. Injuring a person's dæmon injures the person. Threatening the dæmon is the most effective form of coercion. In combat, the dæmon is a second target that must be protected.

The third cost is **revelation of character**. The dæmon's settled form reveals the person's nature to anyone who can read it — a servant's dog dæmon marks them as submissive; a predator dæmon suggests a predatory personality. This makes deception harder and self-knowledge inescapable.

## Practitioner access

The dæmon bond is **universal** in Lyra's world — every human has a dæmon. It is not a power that is acquired but a condition of existence. In other worlds of the multiverse (including our own, as depicted in the series), souls are internal rather than external, and dæmons do not manifest.

## Cultural role

The dæmon bond shapes every aspect of culture in Lyra's world. Architecture, clothing, social etiquette, law, warfare, and religion all account for dæmons. The Magisterium's theology interprets dæmons through the lens of Original Sin — the settling of a dæmon at puberty is associated with the onset of sin, motivating their intercision experiments. The witches' ability to separate from their dæmons marks them as fundamentally different from ordinary humans. The entire social and moral order of Lyra's world is built around the externalized soul.
