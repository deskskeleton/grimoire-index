## Mechanics

In Hill's connected works, supernatural ability manifests through **inscapes** — personal, psychically constructed spaces or pathways that exist because a particular mind is strong (or broken) enough to will them into being. Each inscape is unique to its creator, shaped by their personality, obsessions, and psychological needs. There is no shared magical tradition, no schools or training; each practitioner discovers or stumbles into their ability independently, and each ability operates by its own internal logic.

**Charlie Manx** is a psychic parasite who uses his 1938 Rolls-Royce Wraith as a conduit to **Christmasland** — a pocket dimension that resembles a grotesque, eternal Christmas theme park. Christmasland is not a place in any physical sense; it is a psychic construct sustained by Manx's will and fueled by the life force of kidnapped children. As Manx drains a child during the drive to Christmasland, the child transforms — becoming something joyful and monstrous, with hooks for teeth and an appetite for cruelty disguised as play. Manx himself ages or rejuvenates depending on whether he has recently fed, making him a vampire of creative energy rather than blood.

**Vic McQueen** possesses the **Shorter Way Bridge** — a covered bridge that exists only when she rides her bicycle (later her motorcycle) across it. The bridge connects any two points in space, allowing Vic to find anything or anyone she is looking for. The bridge is not a physical structure that appears and disappears; it is an inscape, a psychic pathway that Vic's mind constructs and maintains. Using it exacts a severe toll: migraines, hallucinations, nosebleeds, and progressive psychological deterioration. The more Vic uses the bridge, the more it damages her, creating a classic addiction dynamic — the ability that defines her is also destroying her.

**Dragonscale** (*The Fireman*) operates on different principles but shares the connection between psychological state and supernatural effect. Dragonscale is a fungal spore (Draco incendia trychophyton) that infects humans and causes spontaneous combustion. Most infected people eventually burn alive. However, those who achieve a state of emotional harmony — joy, connection, inner peace — can control the spore, directing its fire rather than being consumed by it. The Fireman, John Rookwood, has mastered this control to the point of using Dragonscale as a tool: generating controlled flames, healing burns, and even flying. The mechanism is emotional rather than intellectual — understanding the spore scientifically does not help; only genuine emotional regulation allows survival.

**Maggie Leigh** uses Scrabble tiles as a divinatory instrument, drawing letters that spell out answers to questions. Like Vic's bridge, Maggie's tiles are an inscape — a psychic tool shaped by her particular mind — and using them costs her physically (a worsening stutter, cognitive degradation).

## Limitations and costs

The universal cost across Hill's system is **psychological and physical deterioration**. Every inscape user pays with their mind and body. Vic McQueen's bridge gives her crippling migraines and eventually lands her in psychiatric care. Maggie Leigh's tiles progressively impair her speech and cognition. Charlie Manx ages into decrepitude when he cannot feed. The pattern is consistent: the ability is powered by the practitioner's own psychic energy, and using it burns them from the inside.

There is also a **moral dimension** to the cost. Manx's Christmasland requires the destruction of children. Vic's bridge functions best when she is desperate, angry, or afraid — emotional states that make her difficult to live with and damage her relationships. Dragonscale responds to emotional harmony, but the communities that form around controlled Dragonscale use tend toward cult dynamics, replacing individual agency with collective emotional manipulation. Hill suggests that the power to reshape reality is inseparable from the psychological conditions that make a person dangerous to themselves and others.

The **specificity** of each inscape is itself a limitation. Vic can find things but cannot fight, heal, or protect. Manx can travel to Christmasland and drain children but has no other supernatural abilities. Each practitioner is a specialist in the narrowest possible sense, defined and constrained by the particular shape of their obsession.

## Practitioner access

There is no path to acquiring an inscape through training, study, or desire. Inscapes emerge from the intersection of innate psychic potential and a mind shaped by specific experiences — often traumatic ones. Vic's ability is connected to her childhood need to escape a dysfunctional home. Manx's Christmasland reflects a pathological nostalgia for an idealised childhood that never existed. The ability chooses the person as much as the person discovers the ability, and no two inscapes are alike.

Dragonscale is acquired through infection rather than innate gift, but the ability to control it (rather than being killed by it) depends on psychological capacity — specifically, the ability to achieve genuine emotional peace. This makes Dragonscale control accessible in theory to anyone infected but in practice limited to those capable of a specific and difficult inner transformation.

## Cultural role

Inscapes operate in hiding. The broader world in Hill's fiction does not know about Christmasland, the Shorter Way Bridge, or the nature of Manx's crimes. Dragonscale is publicly known (it is a pandemic) but its controllability is not widely understood — most people, including authorities, treat it as a fatal disease and respond with quarantine and extermination rather than attempting to work with the infected. Hill's supernatural operates in the margins, known only to those directly affected and dismissed or misunderstood by everyone else. This isolation reinforces the psychological dimension of the system: inscape users are alone with their abilities, unable to share the burden or find others who understand what they experience.
