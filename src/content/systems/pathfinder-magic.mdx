## Mechanics

Pathfinder's magic system begins from D&D 3.5's Vancian base and extends it in several directions.

**PF1 (2009)** preserves the prepare-and-cast slot system for wizards and clerics while introducing classes with distinct mechanical identities: the **Oracle** (divine spontaneous caster cursed by their own power), the **Witch** (patron-bonded caster who learns spells through a familiar), the **Magus** (sword-and-spell hybrid who channels spells through weapon strikes), the **Summoner** (who bonds with and customizes a powerful extraplanar companion called an eidolon), the **Kineticist** (who channels elemental energy through their body, taking physical damage as the cost — an explicitly non-Vancian design), and the **Psychic** (who casts through mental components rather than verbal and somatic, creating a psionic-flavored tradition).

**PF2 (2019)** reorganizes all spellcasting into **four traditions**:

- **Arcane** — magic understood through study and formula; accesses the broadest spell list
- **Divine** — magic from gods and faith; strongest in healing and against undead
- **Occult** — magic from the collective unconscious, emotion, and hidden knowledge; emphasizes mental and spiritual effects
- **Primal** — magic from nature and the elemental forces; strongest in elemental and animal magic

Each tradition has its own spell list. Classes are assigned a tradition: wizards are arcane, clerics are divine, bards are occult, druids are primal. Sorcerers choose their tradition based on their bloodline.

PF2's **three-action economy** gives spellcasting a distinct tactical feel: most spells cost two actions, but some cost one or three, and a caster can mix spellcasting with movement, raising shields, or other activities within a single turn. This creates more granular tactical decisions than D&D 5e's action/bonus-action system.

The **Kineticist** (remastered in PF2) is Pathfinder's most mechanically distinctive caster: a character who channels raw elemental energy through their body. Instead of spell slots, kineticists use **impulses** (at-will or resource-limited elemental abilities) and manage **overflow** (some powerful impulses require a recovery action before they can be used again). The kineticist explicitly breaks from Vancian traditions — no spell preparation, no spell slots, no traditional casting components.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **spell slot limitation**. Like D&D, Pathfinder casters have a limited number of spell slots per day. PF2 is notably more conservative with caster power than PF1 or D&D 5e — spell DCs, durations, and effect magnitudes are carefully calibrated to prevent casters from dominating the game.

The second cost is **the Kineticist's physical toll**. In PF1, kineticists take nonlethal damage (burn) to fuel their most powerful blasts and infusions. In PF2, the overflow mechanic creates a rhythm of powerful action followed by recovery, preventing continuous maximum output.

The third cost is **tradition restrictions**. Each tradition has blind spots — arcane casters lack strong healing, divine casters lack offensive elemental magic, occult casters lack nature magic, and primal casters lack mental manipulation. No single caster covers all bases.

## Practitioner access

Pathfinder's setting (Golarion) treats magic as a profession — magical education exists in formal institutions (the Arcanamirium in Absalom, the Magaambya in the Mwangi Expanse), and spellcasters are recognized members of society. Access varies by tradition: arcane magic requires study or innate talent, divine magic requires faith and a patron deity, occult magic requires attunement to hidden truths, and primal magic requires connection to the natural world.

## Cultural role

In Golarion, magic is ubiquitous and institutionally supported. Major cities have magical infrastructure, magical academies train practitioners openly, and divine magic is inseparable from religious life. The four-tradition framework reflects a world that categorizes magic the way the real world categorizes academic disciplines — different approaches to the same underlying reality, each with its own intellectual tradition and professional community. The Kineticist represents a fifth approach that stands outside this framework — elemental power that is neither learned, granted, nor studied but channeled through the practitioner's body at physical cost.
