## Mechanics

The Beyonder system organizes supernatural power into twenty-two Pathways, each associated with a tarot archetype and presided over (at the highest level) by an ancient god or Outer Deity. A Pathway is a vertical chain of ten Sequences, numbered from 9 (the weakest, entry-level) to 0 (the strongest, godhood). Advancement along a Pathway involves consuming Sequence potions — alchemical preparations brewed from specific ingredients, many of which come from the bodies of other Beyonders or from exotic supernatural materials. The potion system gives the magic a tangible, almost industrial quality: power is manufactured, not meditated into existence.

Each Sequence within a Pathway grants a distinct set of abilities thematically linked to the Pathway's archetype. The Seer Pathway (The Fool), for example, begins at Sequence 9 with basic divination and premonition abilities, progresses through Clown (misdirection, disguise, acrobatics), Magician (more powerful illusion and sleight-of-hand), Faceless (identity theft and shapeshifting), and eventually reaches Sequence 0: The Fool, which grants authority over fate, trickery, and error. Each step redefines what the Beyonder can do, not merely amplifying existing abilities but adding qualitatively new ones.

The critical innovation is the acting method. Consuming a Sequence potion does not automatically grant its powers; the potion must be "digested," and digestion occurs by embodying the Sequence's name in one's daily life. A Clown must perform — literally putting on shows, using misdirection, making people laugh or gasp. A Shepherd must guide and protect. A Prisoner must endure confinement and restraint. This mechanism ties power progression to lived experience and behavioral commitment, creating a system where advancement demands psychological transformation, not just resource accumulation.

Beyonder characteristics — the supernatural traits associated with each Sequence — can be extracted from a Beyonder's body after death. These characteristics are the primary ingredient in brewing higher-Sequence potions. A Sequence 7 potion typically requires the characteristics of a Sequence 8 Beyonder of the same Pathway, plus supplementary ingredients. This creates a grim economy: advancing almost always requires that someone at your target Sequence dies and their characteristics become available. At the higher Sequences, where fewer practitioners exist, this constraint becomes a brutal bottleneck.

Klein Moretti's Tarot Club serves as a narrative mechanism for exploring multiple Pathways simultaneously. As the mysterious "Mr. Fool," Klein assembles a group of Beyonders from different Pathways, each advancing along their own Sequence chain. This structure allows the novel to showcase the diversity of the system — the martial power of the Warrior Pathway, the knowledge-manipulation of the Spectator Pathway, the solar-themed abilities of the Sunshine Pathway — while maintaining a unified plot.

Above Sequence 1 (Angel level), advancement to Sequence 0 requires hosting the consciousness of the corresponding ancient god or Outer Deity. This creates a profound dilemma: the ultimate power within a Pathway is inseparable from the risk of being consumed by an entity vastly older and more powerful than the practitioner. Several Sequence 0 entities in the setting are not truly the people who consumed the potion but the ancient consciousnesses that overwrote them.

## Limitations and costs

Madness is the defining cost. Every potion consumed increases the Beyonder's connection to the supernatural source of their Pathway's power, and that connection carries psychological contamination. Low-Sequence Beyonders experience manageable distortions — paranoia, obsessive thoughts, heightened emotional reactions keyed to their Pathway's themes. Mid-Sequence Beyonders face serious risks of losing control, entering states of rage, delusion, or dissociation that can endanger themselves and those around them. High-Sequence Beyonders are in a constant battle to maintain their identity against the pull of forces that would subsume them.

The acting method is simultaneously the cure and the constraint. Digesting a potion through proper acting stabilizes the Beyonder and prevents the worst psychological degradation. But the acting method demands genuine behavioral change, not mere performance — a Beyonder who pretends to embody their Sequence name without truly internalizing it will not digest the potion, and the undigested potion will gradually erode their sanity. The system thus penalizes inauthenticity and rewards genuine self-transformation.

Wrong-Pathway potions are lethal. Consuming a potion from a Pathway incompatible with one's current Sequence results in death or, worse, transformation into a mindless aberration. Even adjacent Pathways — those that share some thematic overlap — are dangerous to mix at higher Sequences. This channelizes advancement, preventing practitioners from freely sampling powers and locking them into their chosen Pathway.

Ingredient scarcity gates progression. Higher-Sequence potions require ingredients of extraordinary rarity — materials found only in specific supernatural locations, harvested from dangerous entities, or available only through the accumulated collections of ancient organizations. The churches and secret societies that control access to these ingredients hold enormous power, and independent Beyonders face severe disadvantage in acquiring what they need to advance.

The Outer Deities represent a systemic-level threat. These entities exist beyond the known Pathways and exert a constant corrupting influence on reality. High-Sequence Beyonders who touch the boundary between mortal reality and the Outer Deities' domain risk attracting their attention, and that attention is catastrophically dangerous. The entire Beyonder power structure is, in a sense, built atop a containment system designed to keep these entities from fully manifesting.

## Practitioner access

Sequence 9 potions are relatively accessible, distributed through the major churches (the Church of the Evernight Goddess, the Church of the God of Steam and Machinery, the Church of the Lord of Storms, among others) and various secret societies (the Psychology Alchemists, the Hermits of Fate, the Moses Ascetic Order). Joining one of these organizations provides access to initial potions, guidance on the acting method, and a social network of fellow Beyonders. Independent acquisition is possible but difficult and dangerous.

Higher Sequences are increasingly restricted. The churches treat Beyonder advancement as a controlled resource, promoting members based on loyalty, accomplishment, and institutional need rather than pure talent. Secret societies are more meritocratic but less stable. Above Sequence 5, the number of practitioners in any given Pathway drops to the dozens or fewer across the entire world; above Sequence 3, each individual is a known and politically significant figure. Sequence 1 (Angel) practitioners can be counted on one's fingers, and Sequence 0 entities are singular — there can be only one Sequence 0 per Pathway at any time.

The requirement to kill existing Beyonders for their characteristics creates a Darwinian selection pressure. Advancement beyond the mid-Sequences almost always involves conflict with other Beyonders, whether through sanctioned organizational competition, outright murder, or scavenging from battlefields. This makes the Beyonder world deeply adversarial despite the collegial surface of organizations like the Tarot Club.

Klein's own advancement is enabled by unusual circumstances — reincarnation from another world, possession of the Sefirah Castle (a reality-anchoring artifact), and the Tarot Club's network of allies. The novel makes clear that without these advantages, even a brilliant and determined individual would struggle to advance beyond the mid-Sequences.

## Cultural role

The Beyonder world operates as a masquerade within a Victorian-era industrial setting. Ordinary people in the cities of Backlund, Trier, and Feynapotter live lives shaped by steam-age technology, social class, and colonial politics, largely unaware that supernatural beings walk among them. The churches maintain the masquerade as a matter of policy, presenting themselves as conventional religious institutions while secretly managing Beyonder affairs, hunting aberrations, and containing threats from the Outer Deities.

Secret societies function as the underworld counterpart to the churches — organizations that pursue power, knowledge, or ideological goals outside the churches' control. The tension between churches and secret societies generates much of the setting's political conflict, with independent Beyonders caught between institutions that each demand loyalty and obedience.

The ancient gods' history forms the deepest layer of the setting's lore. The twenty-two Pathways were not always organized as they are now; ancient wars between gods reshuffled Pathway control, destroyed civilizations, and created the current arrangement of power. Understanding this history is not merely academic — it reveals the true nature of the Pathways, the identities of the Outer Deities, and the mechanics of Sequence 0 advancement. Knowledge of the past is itself a form of power, and the novel's mystery-driven structure treats historical investigation as a form of combat.

The Beyonder system's cultural impact within the setting mirrors real-world industrialization — power that was once the province of isolated mystics has been systematized, institutionalized, and brought under organizational control. The churches operate Beyonder advancement programs with bureaucratic efficiency; the secret societies function as competing firms in a supernatural marketplace. This industrialization of magic gives the setting a distinctive flavor, blending Lovecraftian horror with the social dynamics of late-nineteenth-century institutional power.
