## Mechanics

Narcomancy is the art of entering and manipulating dreams to harvest four magical substances — the dream-humors — from the sleeping mind. All narcomancy derives from the goddess Hananja, who granted humanity the gift of dreaming, and is practised exclusively by the priests of the Hetawa temple in Gujaareh.

The four dream-humors are **dreamblood**, harvested from the experience of peace, pleasure, and contentment; **dreambile**, drawn from fear, anxiety, and anger; **dreamichor**, the essence of life force and vitality; and **dreamseed**, the substance of creativity and imagination. Each humor has distinct magical properties and applications, and narcomantic practitioners specialise in different uses.

**Gatherers** are the most elite and feared of the Hetawa's priests. They enter the dreams of those judged corrupt, terminally ill, or suffering beyond aid, and harvest their dreamblood by granting a final dream of perfect bliss — a peaceful, painless death. The Gathered soul passes into the dream-goddess's embrace, and the harvested dreamblood is stored in special vessels for use by other priests. Gathering is considered a sacred duty and a mercy, but it is also killing, and the Hetawa regulates it carefully.

**Sharers** are dream-healers who use the harvested humors to treat illness, injury, and psychological trauma. By entering a patient's dreams and administering the appropriate humor, a Sharer can accelerate healing, calm madness, ease pain, or restore damaged minds. Sharers are the most common narcomantic practitioners and serve as Gujaareh's primary medical professionals.

**Sentinels** use dream-humors for protective and combative purposes — warding against hostile narcomancy, guarding the temple, and enforcing the Hetawa's authority. Their applications are less well-known to the general public but essential to the institution's security.

## Limitations and costs

The most profound cost of narcomancy is psychological and moral. Gatherers kill people. However compassionate the method, however willing the subject, the act of Gathering is the deliberate ending of a life, and Gatherers must maintain absolute emotional discipline to prevent that act from corrupting them. A Gatherer who loses control — who begins to enjoy the killing or who Gathers from the unwilling — becomes a **Reaper**, a narcomantic predator who kills indiscriminately, draining dreamblood from anyone they can reach.

The Reaper state represents narcomancy's most catastrophic failure mode. A Reaper is addicted to dreamblood in its most concentrated form — harvested directly from the dying — and their power grows with each kill. They can enter dreams without physical proximity, drain multiple victims simultaneously, and are extraordinarily difficult to stop. The existence of Reapers is the dark mirror of the Gatherers' sacred duty.

Dream-humor supplies are finite and must be continually replenished. The Hetawa manages its stores carefully, and shortages of particular humors can limit what Sharers and Sentinels can accomplish. Dreamblood in particular requires Gathering to produce, creating an institutional dependency on sanctioned killing. This creates a moral tension at the heart of Gujaareh's culture: the city's peace and health depend on a priesthood that kills.

## Practitioner access

Narcomancy is exclusively institutional — practised only by priests trained within the Hetawa temple from childhood. Potential narcomancers are identified young, often through signs of dream-sensitivity, and undergo years of rigorous training in meditation, dream-navigation, and the handling of dream-humors. The Hetawa's hierarchy determines which role a priest assumes: the most talented and disciplined may become Gatherers, while others train as Sharers or Sentinels.

Women were historically excluded from the highest ranks of narcomantic practice, particularly the role of Gatherer. The Shadowed Sun challenges this restriction through Hanani, the first female Sharer to be considered for advancement, whose abilities ultimately prove essential.

## Cultural role

Narcomancy is not merely a magical practice in Gujaareh but the foundation of the city's entire social order. The Hetawa temple functions simultaneously as religious institution, medical system, judicial authority, and moral arbiter. Gathering serves as both euthanasia and capital punishment — the corrupt are Gathered as surely as the suffering, and the Hetawa's judgment of who deserves Gathering carries enormous political weight.

Gujaareh's reputation as a city of peace rests on narcomancy's ability to ease suffering, heal the sick, and remove the violent and corrupt from society. But the duology reveals the fragility of that peace: when the political leadership co-opts the Hetawa's power for conquest, and when a Reaper emerges from within the priesthood itself, the system designed to maintain harmony becomes an instrument of destruction. Jemisin uses narcomancy to examine how institutions built on compassionate principles can be corrupted when those principles serve power rather than people.
