## Mechanics

**Malazan sorcery** is the practical application of Warren access — the discipline of drawing energy from one or more Warrens and shaping it into controlled effects. Where the Warren system is cosmological infrastructure, sorcery is what practitioners do with it.

A mage accesses a Warren by **opening** it — creating a connection between themselves and the dimensional space. Through this connection, they draw energy and shape it according to their training, talent, and intent. The effects correspond to the Warren's nature: **Telas** (fire) produces flames, **Thyr** (light) produces illumination and purification, **Meanas** (shadow/illusion) produces illusions and concealment, **Denul** (healing) restores flesh and cures disease, **Hood's Path** (death) produces death magic and interacts with the undead, **Serc** (sky) produces wind and weather magic, **Ruse** (sea) commands oceanic forces, and so on.

The scale of effects varies enormously. A **hedge witch** might produce small healings and minor wards. A **cadre mage** serves in military units, providing tactical-scale combat magic — fireballs, protective shields, concealment. A **High Mage** operates at strategic scale — leveling fortifications, destroying armies, opening Warren portals for mass transportation, or engaging in magical duels that scar the landscape for centuries.

**Quick Ben** is notable for his ability to access multiple Warrens — at least seven, possibly more — a feat that should be impossible and whose explanation involves his having absorbed the souls of other mages. Multi-Warren access allows compound effects: layering fire, shadow, and force into attacks that are nearly impossible to counter because defending against one Warren does not protect against another.

The **Deck of Dragons** serves as both a divinatory tool and a map of the current magical-political landscape. Its cards correspond to Houses (each associated with an Ascendant and a Warren), and a reading reveals the current state of play among the gods, Ascendants, and mortal powers.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **physical and mental toll**. Channeling Warren energy is exhausting. Extended or intensive use produces fatigue, pain, and potentially permanent damage. Mages who overextend risk backlash from their Warren — a violent feedback that can destroy them.

The second cost is **vulnerability during use**. Opening a Warren creates a detectable magical signature. Other mages, Ascendants, and Warren-native entities can sense the opening and either attack through it or trace the mage's location. Stealth and sorcery are often incompatible.

The third cost is **Warren instability**. Warrens can be damaged, poisoned (the Crippled God's alien magic corrupts Warrens it touches), or contested by entities that claim dominion over them. A mage who relies on a single Warren is vulnerable if that Warren becomes compromised.

The fourth cost is **Ascendant attention**. Each Warren has associated Ascendants (near-divine beings) who may take interest in — or offense at — mortal use of their domain. Drawing heavily on Hood's Path risks attracting the attention of Hood, the God of Death. This divine attention is unpredictable and frequently unwelcome.

## Practitioner access

Sorcerous talent is partly innate (some individuals are born with Warren affinity) and partly trained (military mages are trained in cadres, academic sorcerers study at institutions). The quality and tradition of training varies enormously: the Malazan military produces competent combat mages; the Bridgeburners' cadre mages are battle-hardened specialists; independent sorcerers like Bauchelain operate outside institutional frameworks entirely. Some practitioners are self-taught, having discovered their abilities through accident or crisis.

## Cultural role

In the Malazan Empire, sorcerers are integrated into the military structure — cadre mages serve in squads, High Mages advise commanders and serve as strategic weapons. The relationship between military command and magical power is a persistent source of tension. Outside the Empire, sorcerous traditions vary: the Letherii have their own frameworks, the Tiste races have Elder Warren traditions, and independent powers (Ascendants, gods, ancient entities) operate above mortal magical hierarchies entirely. Magic in Malazan is not a profession so much as a fundamental axis of political and military power — every major conflict in the series involves sorcerous actors at strategic scale.
