## Mechanics

The foundational mechanic of D'Angeline power is **divine ancestry**. Every D'Angeline carries the blood of Blessed Elua and at least one of his eight Companions, and this bloodline manifests as gifts that are subtle but unmistakable. All D'Angelines possess enhanced physical beauty and a charisma that other nations find unsettling or intoxicating. Beyond this baseline, specific gifts follow the Companion lines.

**Naamah's** descendants inherit her gift of desire — an enhanced capacity for both giving and receiving pleasure that the D'Angeline culture has formalized into the institution of the Night Court, thirteen Houses of trained courtesans each embodying a different facet of Naamah's service. **Cassiel's** line (technically a paradox, as Cassiel swore to remain celibate) manifests as supreme martial discipline and self-denial, embodied by the Cassiline Brotherhood of warrior-priests. **Kushiel's** gift is the capacity to find redemption through punishment — in its rarest form producing an **anguissette**, a person who experiences pain as pleasure. Phedre no Delaunay is the first anguissette born in living memory, and her unique nature makes her a superlative spy (she cannot be broken by torture) and a figure of theological significance.

**Shemhazai's** gift is an insatiable thirst for knowledge. **Eisheth's** is the gift of healing. **Anael's** is mastery of husbandry and the growing arts. **Azza's** is pride and ambition. **Camael's** is martial prowess. These gifts are rarely dramatic — they manifest as heightened aptitude, not supernatural powers — but they are real, measurable, and theologically grounded.

Beyond the D'Angeline gifts, the world contains other magical traditions that operate on different principles. The **Master of the Straits** commands the waters between Terre d'Ange and Alba through an ancient binding tied to the lost pages of the Book of Raziel. Skaldic **rune-magic** draws on the old northern gods. Carthaginian **horologia** is forbidden demon-summoning that extracts power through blood sacrifice and binding. The Tsingani practise the **dromonde**, a divinatory gift that reads the threads of fate. The Yeshuite tradition carries its own messianic prophecy. In each case, the magic is real because the gods and spirits behind it are real.

## Limitations and costs

The D'Angeline gifts are inherently **limited in scope**. They enhance rather than transform — a Naamah's servant is supernaturally alluring but cannot cast spells; a Cassiline is a superb fighter but not invulnerable. The gifts operate at the personal scale and cannot be projected or amplified except through divine intervention, which is rare and unpredictable.

The primary cost is **theological obligation**. Elua's one precept — "Love as thou wilt" — sounds permissive but functions as a binding moral law. D'Angelines who betray this precept (through coercion, cruelty, or denial of love) suffer consequences that are social, psychological, and occasionally divine. Phedre's nature as an anguissette is simultaneously a gift and a burden: her inability to refuse pain makes her vulnerable to manipulation, and her role as Kushiel's chosen instrument places demands on her that no ordinary person could bear.

The non-D'Angeline magical traditions carry heavier and more explicit costs. Horologia requires blood sacrifice and risks the summoner's soul. The Master of the Straits' power is maintained through centuries of bound servitude. The dromonde shows possible futures but the knowledge frequently brings suffering rather than advantage. Even Skaldic rune-magic demands physical endurance and ritual precision.

Divine intervention — when it occurs — is never free. The gods act through their chosen instruments, and being chosen is rarely comfortable. Hyacinthe spends years bound as the Master of the Straits. Phedre undertakes a quest to the literal underworld. Imriel bears the consequences of his mother's sins. The gods' gifts are real, but so are their prices.

## Practitioner access

All D'Angelines carry some measure of divine heritage, making the baseline gifts (beauty, charisma) effectively universal within the nation. The specific Companion gifts vary by bloodline and manifest with varying intensity — most D'Angelines show aptitude in their Companion line's domain without being extraordinary, while a rare few manifest gifts of exceptional power.

The anguissette is the rarest manifestation: Phedre is described as the first born in several generations, marked by a mote of red in her eye (Kushiel's dart). The Cassiline Brotherhood accepts only those of appropriate lineage and subjects them to years of ascetic martial training. The Night Court selects and trains its adepts from childhood.

Outside Terre d'Ange, magical traditions are culturally specific. The dromonde runs in Tsingani bloodlines. Horologia is a learned art (though a forbidden one). The Master of the Straits' power passes through a specific binding mechanism rather than bloodline. Access to magic in this world is fragmented along national and cultural lines, with no single tradition being universal.

## Cultural role

In Terre d'Ange, the divine gifts are not merely acknowledged but form the entire basis of national identity and social organization. The thirteen Houses of the Night Court, the Cassiline Brotherhood, the provincial structure mirroring the Companions' domains — all of D'Angeline civilization is built on the theological foundation of Elua and his Companions. Religion and politics are inseparable, and theological arguments have immediate practical consequences.

The D'Angeline attitude toward their gifts is one of cultivated gratitude. They view themselves as blessed, not superhuman, and Elua's precept functions as a cultural touchstone that shapes law, custom, and personal ethics. The tension between this self-image and the reality of D'Angeline political intrigue — which is ruthless, sophisticated, and occasionally murderous — provides much of the series' dramatic energy. The surrounding nations view D'Angeline gifts with reactions ranging from desire to suspicion to outright hostility, and the collision between D'Angeline theology and other nations' traditions drives the larger conflicts of the series.
