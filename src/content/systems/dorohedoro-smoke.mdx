## Mechanics

Magic in Dorohedoro is a **biological function**. Sorcerers produce smoke inside their bodies through a specialized internal organ, and this smoke is the medium through which all magical effects manifest. When expelled — typically through the hands, though some sorcerers channel it through other body parts — the smoke carries a single, specific magical effect unique to that individual. One sorcerer's smoke transforms living things into mushrooms. Another's heals any wound. A third's manipulates time. The effect is fixed at birth and cannot be changed, learned, or expanded. You get one power, and that power is you.

The smoke itself is **visible and tangible** — a dark, often colored vapor that envelops targets and produces its effect on contact. Different sorcerers produce different volumes and qualities of smoke. Volume determines how many targets can be affected simultaneously, while quality (often described in terms of purity and density) determines the potency and reliability of the effect. A sorcerer with high-quality smoke can produce devastating results with small amounts; one with poor-quality smoke may need to drench a target to achieve a partial effect.

**Masks** are integral to sorcerous practice. Every sorcerer wears a mask, and a properly fitted mask — one crafted by a Devil to match the wearer's face — amplifies smoke production and quality. The mask is not the source of power but a resonance tool; wearing someone else's mask provides no benefit and may actively hinder smoke production. Mask crafting is a sacred art performed by Devils, and receiving a custom mask marks a sorcerer's passage into full professional status.

The most powerful manifestation of smoke magic is the **Devil transformation**. Devils are not a separate species but sorcerers who, through some combination of overwhelming power, specific disposition, and circumstances of death, transform into horned, immensely powerful beings after dying. Devils retain their original smoke ability but gain dramatically enhanced power, physical transformation, and authority within sorcerer society. The process is poorly understood and cannot be deliberately pursued — it simply happens to certain sorcerers, making it the ultimate expression of the system's biological lottery.

**Black Powder** is a crystallized form of ambient smoke that rains down during the annual **Blue Night** festival. When inhaled or absorbed, it temporarily boosts every sorcerer's smoke production and quality. The Blue Night is both a cultural celebration and a practical power-enhancement event, and the existence of Black Powder confirms that smoke is not purely individual — there is an environmental component, a shared magical atmosphere that the Sorcerers' World generates collectively.

## Limitations and costs

The most fundamental limitation is **singularity of effect**. Each sorcerer gets exactly one magical function. A sorcerer whose smoke produces insects cannot also heal, transform, or teleport. This constraint creates absolute dependency between sorcerers — no individual is self-sufficient, and complex operations require teams with complementary abilities. It also means that some sorcerers are born with powerful, versatile effects (Noi's healing, En's mushroom transformation) while others receive marginal or highly situational abilities, creating inequality that no amount of training can overcome.

**Smoke quantity is finite** within any given period. Extended use depletes a sorcerer's reserves, and recovery requires rest. In prolonged combat or large-scale operations, running out of smoke is a genuine threat, leaving the sorcerer reliant on physical capability alone. This depletion is biological — akin to exhaustion or dehydration — and cannot be shortcut through willpower or technique.

The system's most pervasive cost is **social**. Smoke quality and effect type determine a sorcerer's place in society. Those with powerful or useful effects ascend to positions of wealth and authority. Those with weak or bizarre effects are marginalized, forming the underclass of sorcerer society. The **Cross-Eyes** — a gang composed primarily of sorcerers with inferior magic — represent the structural violence of this hierarchy. Their members often resort to using processed smoke in liquid form (harvested and concentrated from other sorcerers) to compensate for their own inadequate abilities, creating a black-market economy around stolen magical potential.

Physical toll manifests primarily through **overuse**. Sorcerers who push beyond their natural smoke capacity experience fatigue, nausea, and physical breakdown. The biological nature of smoke production means it is subject to the same limitations as any bodily function — illness, injury, or malnutrition can reduce output, and no sorcerer can produce smoke indefinitely.

## Practitioner access

Magic in Dorohedoro is **species-level hereditary**. Sorcerers are a distinct population from humans, born with the capacity to produce smoke. Humans cannot learn, acquire, or develop magic under any circumstances. This biological divide is the foundational social fact of the series' world — sorcerers and humans are separate populations with radically different capabilities, and the relationship between them is defined by exploitation.

Sorcerers are born in the **Sorcerers' World**, a parallel dimension connected to the human world (called the Hole) through magical doors. Within the Sorcerers' World, all citizens are magic users, and society is organized around smoke ability. Young sorcerers attend training institutions like **Black House**, where they learn to control and refine their smoke output, but the fundamental ability and its specific effect are innate. Training can improve volume, control, and tactical application, but it cannot change what the smoke does.

The **power hierarchy** is steep. At the bottom are sorcerers with weak or impractical effects — those whose smoke produces trivial or narrowly useful results. In the middle are competent practitioners with solid combat or utility effects. At the top are figures like En, whose mushroom-transformation smoke is so powerful and versatile that he controls a crime empire, and Noi, whose healing smoke makes her one of the most valuable individuals alive. Above all sorcerers stand the Devils — transformed posthumous beings of immense power who serve as religious figures, mask-crafters, and arbiters of sorcerer culture.

Humans have **no access** to the smoke system. The residents of the Hole — the grimy, impoverished human city that sorcerers use as a testing ground for their abilities — are victims of magic rather than participants in it. Sorcerers routinely travel to the Hole to practice their smoke on human subjects, treating the population as disposable experimental material. This dynamic is the series' central injustice and the engine of its plot.

## Cultural role

Smoke magic is not a subsystem of Dorohedoro's world — it **is** the world. The entire social structure of the Sorcerers' World is organized around smoke production. Status, employment, relationships, and identity all flow from what your smoke does and how well it does it. En's crime family functions because he can offer protection and resources to sorcerers who pledge their abilities to his organization. The Cross-Eyes exist because the system produces a permanent underclass of weak magic users with no legitimate path to power.

The **mask tradition** reinforces magic's role as identity. A sorcerer's mask is their public face, their professional credential, and their connection to the Devils who govern sorcerer spirituality. Going unmasked is a mark of low status or outsider identity. The elaborate mask designs reflect individual personality and magical affinity, turning the biological fact of smoke production into a cultural performance.

The relationship between the Sorcerers' World and the Hole is the series' most pointed piece of **social commentary**. Sorcerers treat humans as test subjects, practice targets, and raw material — not out of active malice (usually) but out of structural indifference. Humans are simply not part of the sorcerer social contract. This dynamic mirrors real-world patterns of exploitation between powerful and powerless populations, and the series refuses to romanticize either side. The Hole is miserable and violent. The Sorcerers' World is glamorous and violent. Magic is the axis on which this inequality turns.

The **Devil system** adds a theological dimension. Devils are the closest thing sorcerer society has to gods — they craft masks, preside over ceremonies, and possess power that dwarfs living sorcerers. But Devils are not transcendent beings; they are dead sorcerers who won a posthumous lottery. The religious structure of sorcerer culture is therefore built on the same biological randomness as everything else — even in death, your worth is determined by what your body happened to produce.
