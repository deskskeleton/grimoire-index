## Mechanics

**White Prophecy** is a form of prescience possessed by the **Whites** — rare individuals born with colorless skin and hair who can perceive the branching paths of possible futures. A White Prophet sees not a single predetermined future but a vast web of possibilities — the consequences of every choice, action, and event spiraling outward in multiple directions. The prophet's task is to identify the path that leads to the best possible outcome and work to make it happen.

The mechanism of change is the **Catalyst** — a specific individual whose choices have outsized influence on which future comes to pass. The White Prophet cannot change the future alone; they need the Catalyst to act. The Fool identifies FitzChivalry Farseer as his Catalyst — the person whose actions, if guided correctly, can shift the entire world onto a better path. The prophet-Catalyst relationship is the core dynamic: the Fool provides vision (what needs to happen) and Fitz provides agency (making it happen, often without understanding why).

**Physical transformation**: as a White Prophet exercises their gift and events shift to match their visions, their body changes color — their white skin develops pigmentation in patterns that other Whites can read as a record of prophetic fulfillment. The Fool's skin darkens over the course of the series as prophecies are fulfilled, marking them as a prophet who has actively changed the world.

The vision itself is overwhelming in its scope. The Fool describes seeing thousands of possible futures simultaneously — a cognitive burden that produces certainty about some events (those that appear in nearly all futures) and agonizing uncertainty about others (those that depend on the Catalyst's choices). The prophet cannot simply tell the Catalyst what to do; premature disclosure of future events can alter the path unpredictably.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **emotional burden**. The prophet sees the suffering in every possible future and must choose which version of suffering to accept. No path is free of pain — the "best" future still involves loss, death, and tragedy. The Fool's melancholy and cryptic behavior stem partly from carrying this knowledge.

The second cost is **physical transformation and vulnerability**. The White's body is a record of prophecy — which means that those who know how to read it (other Whites, or the Servants of the Whites) can extract prophetic information through torture. The Fool's capture by the Servants is one of the series' darkest plot threads.

The third cost is **relationship strain**. The prophet must sometimes manipulate the Catalyst — withholding information, engineering situations, allowing suffering that could be prevented — to keep events on the correct path. This makes the prophet-Catalyst relationship simultaneously the deepest intimacy and the most painful manipulation, and Fitz's discovery of the Fool's manipulations is a recurring source of betrayal and reconciliation.

## Practitioner access

White Prophecy is restricted to Whites — individuals born with the prophetic gift, identifiable by their colorless appearance. Whites are extremely rare. The Servants (a cult that breeds and enslaves Whites for their prophetic abilities) maintain a population of Whites on the island of Clerres, harvesting their predictions and selling them. The Fool is a free White — escaped from Servants' control — and one of very few operating independently.

## Cultural role

In the Six Duchies, White Prophets are figures of legend — most people have heard of them but do not believe they currently exist. The Fool conceals their nature through various personas (King Shrewd's jester, Lord Golden, the Amber trading woman). The Servants of the Whites represent an institutional corruption of prophecy — they treat White Prophets as livestock, breeding them for predictive output. The destruction of the Servants in the final trilogy is presented as the liberation of prophecy from commodification.
