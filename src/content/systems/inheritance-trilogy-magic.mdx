## Mechanics

The magic of the Inheritance Trilogy operates across several tiers of power, all deriving ultimately from the Three — the creator gods who shaped existence. At the highest tier, the Three possess essentially unlimited reality-shaping power, each aligned with a fundamental aspect of existence. Nahadoth embodies chaos, darkness, and change; Itempas embodies order, light, and stasis; and Enefa embodied the balance between them — life, death, and the grey spaces in between. Their power is not learned or channelled but intrinsic to their nature as gods.

The godlings — children born from unions among the Three — possess lesser but still formidable divine power, each aligned with a specific concept or domain. Sieh is the godling of childhood and trickery; Zhakkarn is the godling of war; Kurue is the godling of wisdom. Their abilities are vast compared to mortal magic but constrained compared to the Three, and they are bound by the nature of their domains. A godling cannot act against their essential nature without suffering.

Godblood — the inheritance that flows from unions between gods or godlings and mortals — grants varying magical abilities to mortal descendants. These abilities are unpredictable, manifesting differently in each individual. Oree Shoth can see magic as colour and paint with light that becomes real. Other god-blooded mortals display gifts ranging from minor supernatural intuitions to significant reality-altering powers. The strength of godblood abilities generally diminishes with generational distance from the divine ancestor.

Scrivening is the mortal-developed system of magic, using the gods' language inscribed as sigils to produce magical effects. Scriveners study and write these sigils to create wards, weapons, tools, and other constructs. It is a learned discipline requiring training and precision, and its effects are far more limited than divine power — but it is the only form of magic accessible to mortals without godblood.

## Limitations and costs

Divine power at the level of the Three is practically unlimited in scope but constrained by the gods' natures and relationships. The Gods' War demonstrated that even gods can be defeated, killed, or enslaved. Nahadoth's enslavement by the Arameri stripped him of autonomy while leaving his power intact — he could destroy cities but could not refuse an Arameri command. This enslavement was maintained through the Stone of Earth, an artifact containing a fragment of Enefa's power.

Godling power is constrained by domain. A godling who acts against their essential nature risks weakening or destroying themselves. Sieh, the oldest godling, is bound to childhood — when forced to age, he begins to die. This domain-binding means godlings are powerful but inflexible, unable to adapt beyond their fundamental nature.

Godblood abilities in mortals carry physical and psychological costs. The power is often unstable, difficult to control, and potentially dangerous to the practitioner and those around them. God-blooded individuals may be hunted, exploited, or feared by ordinary mortals. The Arameri historically controlled god-blooded populations to maintain their monopoly on divine power.

Scrivening requires extensive study, precise execution, and carries the risk of catastrophic failure if sigils are inscribed incorrectly. A flawed sigil can produce unintended effects ranging from minor malfunction to deadly backlash. Scriveners are trained specialists whose work demands both intellectual rigour and steady hands.

## Practitioner access

Access to magic in the Inheritance Trilogy is fundamentally hierarchical. The Three stand at the apex — beings of cosmic power whose conflicts reshape reality. Below them, godlings serve, rebel, or endure according to their natures and the political order imposed after the Gods' War. God-blooded mortals inherit fragments of divine power through ancestry, making magical ability a matter of bloodline rather than merit or training.

Scrivening is the exception — a learned discipline accessible to any mortal with the intelligence and dedication to master it. However, even scrivening operates within the political structures of the Hundred Thousand Kingdoms, and the Arameri have historically controlled access to the most powerful sigils and techniques.

## Cultural role

Magic in the Inheritance Trilogy is inseparable from political power. The Arameri family's control of the enslaved gods gives them absolute authority over the Hundred Thousand Kingdoms — not through military conquest alone but through the literal ownership of divine beings who can destroy armies or reshape landscapes on command. This arrangement has persisted for two thousand years, creating a world order built on theological slavery.

The trilogy uses magic to examine colonialism, religious authority, and the costs of imposed order. When the divine hierarchy shifts — when enslaved gods are freed and the balance of power changes — the entire political and social order of the world transforms with it. Magic is not a tool wielded within a stable system but the system itself, and changing who holds divine power changes everything.
