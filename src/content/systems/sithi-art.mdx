## Mechanics

The **Art** of the Sithi (and their darker kin, the Norns) is an ancient magical tradition practiced by the immortal non-human races of Osten Ard. Unlike human magical traditions (which are limited, fragmentary, and poorly understood), the Sithi Art is deeply integrated into their perception and being. The Sithi do not cast spells in the conventional sense — they alter their awareness of the world's underlying patterns, and reality responds.

Known manifestations include: manipulation of light, shadow, and perception (the Sithi can make themselves effectively invisible or alter how others perceive them); weather influence (the Storm King, Ineluki, derives his name from his ability to command storms even in undeath); healing through realignment of biological patterns; communication across vast distances through the **Witnesses** (crystalline devices that serve as magical communication and scrying tools); and the creation of powerful wards and barriers.

The **Witnesses** (also called the Master Witnesses and Smaller Witnesses) are the most systematized element of the Art. These crystal or mirror-like devices enable communication between distant users, scrying of remote locations, and in some cases, the projection of power across distances. Utuk'ku, the ancient Norn Queen, uses a Master Witness to coordinate her forces and project her will across continents.

The **Norns** practice a darker variant of the Art — the same fundamental tradition, but directed toward domination, undeath, and the manipulation of fear and cold. Utuk'ku's millennia of experience and her connection to the dead Ineluki's power make her the most powerful practitioner in the setting. The difference between Sithi and Norn Art is primarily one of philosophy and purpose rather than mechanism.

Human practitioners of magical arts exist in Osten Ard but operate with far less power and understanding. The Scrollbearers (a secret society of scholars) preserve fragmentary magical knowledge, and some humans demonstrate limited abilities (healing, scrying), but human magic is a pale shadow of what the Sithi and Norns can accomplish.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **temporal investment**. Sithi Art is developed over immortal lifetimes — centuries of practice and deepening understanding. No Sithi becomes a master of the Art quickly. This means that younger Sithi (even those several centuries old) are significantly less capable than ancient practitioners like Utuk'ku or the Storm King.

The second cost is **diminishment**. The Sithi are a declining race — their numbers dwindle, their connection to the world weakens, and their Art becomes less potent as the world changes. The Norns counter this through Utuk'ku's obsessive preservation of power, but even they are diminished from their ancient heights.

The third cost is **emotional and spiritual toll**. The Art requires deep engagement with the world's patterns, and prolonged or extreme use affects the practitioner's mental state. The Storm King's descent into madness and hatred after his death and return is the extreme example — his Art is now driven by rage rather than understanding, making it more destructive but less controlled.

## Practitioner access

The Art is innate to the Sithi and Norn races — it is a function of their non-human nature and their extended relationship with the world. Humans cannot learn the Art in any meaningful sense, though some humans possess minor abilities (often attributed to distant Sithi ancestry or natural talent). There is no formal school; the Art is transmitted through cultural immersion and centuries-long practice within Sithi or Norn society.

## Cultural role

Among the Sithi, the Art is not a separate discipline but an aspect of their existence — they do not distinguish between magical and non-magical life in the way humans do. Among the Norns, the Art has been weaponized and institutionalized under Utuk'ku's millennia-long rule, becoming primarily a tool of military power and political control. In the human world of Osten Ard, Sithi and Norn Art is the stuff of legends and nightmares — most humans have never encountered it directly, and those who have (during the Storm King's war and subsequent conflicts) regard it with awe and terror.
