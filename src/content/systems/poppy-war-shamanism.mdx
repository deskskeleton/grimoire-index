## Mechanics

Shamanism in the *Poppy War* trilogy involves opening a connection between the practitioner and a god in the **Pantheon** — a parallel spiritual realm where sixty-four deities reside, each associated with a domain of elemental or conceptual power. The Phoenix commands fire and destruction; the Dragon commands water and storms; the Tortoise governs the earth; the Seer grants visions. When a shaman opens their mind to a god, they become a channel for that god's power, manifesting effects far beyond normal human capability.

The initial connection is typically achieved through **altered states of consciousness**. The historical method — practised at Sinegard's academy by the enigmatic Master Jiang — involves meditation, fasting, and psychoactive substances. Opium and its derivatives are the most common gateway drugs, though other substances and techniques can work. The drugs do not create the connection; they lower the mental barriers that normally prevent a human mind from perceiving the Pantheon. Once the gateway is opened, a shaman can learn to access it without chemical assistance — but the temptation to use drugs for easier, faster access persists.

Once connected, the shaman channels the god's power through their body. Rin, connected to the Phoenix, can summon and control fire on a massive scale — immolating armies, raising walls of flame, projecting fire across battlefields. The power scales with the depth of connection: a shallow trance produces modest effects, while a deep communion can produce devastation on a strategic scale. At the trilogy's climax, Rin achieves effects comparable to nuclear weapons.

The gods, however, are not tools. They are **conscious entities** with their own desires, and their primary desire is to act in the material world through their shamans. Each time a shaman opens the connection, the god pushes to widen it — to take more control, to experience more sensation, to act more fully through its host. The relationship is less partnership than possession: the god offers power, and in exchange it consumes the shaman's identity, memories, and autonomous will.

The **Cike** are a military unit of shamans — broken, dangerous, and kept on a short leash by the Nikara military. They represent what shamans become with sustained use: powerful but unstable, addicted, partially consumed by their patron gods, and treated as weapons rather than people.

## Limitations and costs

The primary cost is **sanity**. Every connection to a god erodes the boundary between the shaman's identity and the god's. Sustained use produces dissociative episodes, hallucinations, personality fragmentation, and ultimately complete loss of self — the shaman becomes the god's puppet, acting on divine impulse rather than human will. Every member of the Cike shows signs of this erosion.

**Addiction** is both literal and metaphorical. The drugs used to open the initial connection are themselves addictive, and the experience of channelling godlike power produces its own form of dependency. Shamans who try to stop using their abilities find themselves drawn back by the memory of that power and by the god's continuous, seductive call.

**Physical toll** accompanies heavy use. Channelling fire through a human body burns the practitioner from within; other gods' powers produce analogous damage. The body is not designed to contain divine energy, and sustained channelling accelerates aging, causes internal injuries, and can kill outright.

The deepest cost is **moral**. The gods do not care about human ethics, alliances, or restraint. A shaman in deep communion acts on the god's values, not their own — and the gods' values tend toward maximum expression of their domain. The Phoenix wants to burn; the Seer wants to see everything, regardless of the psychological cost. Rin's arc is defined by her progressive inability to distinguish her own rage from the Phoenix's hunger.

## Practitioner access

Shamanic ability appears to be partly innate — not everyone can open the connection, and those who can often display signs from childhood (vivid dreams, emotional volatility, sensitivity to altered states). However, the innate capacity must be activated and trained. Master Jiang's curriculum at Sinegard is designed to identify and develop potential shamans, but his methods are deliberately obscured from the military establishment, which officially considers shamanism extinct.

The Speerly people — Rin's ethnic group, subjected to genocide by the Federation of Mugen — appear to have had a disproportionately high rate of shamanic ability, particularly connection to the Phoenix. This suggests a hereditary or ethnic component, though the mechanism is never fully explained.

Access is further limited by **institutional suppression**. The Nikara establishment publicly denies that shamanism exists, treating it as superstition. This is a deliberate policy: shamans are too dangerous and too uncontrollable to be acknowledged as real. The Cike operates as a black-ops unit precisely because its existence cannot be officially admitted.

## Cultural role

Shamanism occupies a paradoxical position in Nikara society. Officially, it does not exist — the academy at Sinegard teaches that the old stories of god-callers are myths, and the military establishment treats shamanic power as an embarrassing relic. Unofficially, the government maintains the Cike as a weapon of last resort and has historically used shamans in its wars while denying their existence afterward.

This denial mirrors the trilogy's broader engagement with how states use and discard their most dangerous weapons and their most marginalised people. The shamans are drawn from society's margins (orphans, minorities, the desperate), granted terrible power, deployed in atrocity, and then erased from the official record. The magical system is inseparable from this political dynamic.
