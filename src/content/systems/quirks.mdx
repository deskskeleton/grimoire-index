## Mechanics

A Quirk is a single, inheritable superhuman ability that manifests in roughly four out of five children, typically by the age of four. Each Quirk is unique to its bearer, though hereditary Quirks pass down family lines with variation — children sometimes inherit a parent's Quirk, sometimes a recombination of two parents' Quirks, sometimes a Quirk that resembles neither.

Quirks are catalogued by Japan's Hero Public Safety Commission into broad operational types: Emitter (project something away from the body, e.g., fire, ice, kinetic blasts), Transformation (alter the user's body, e.g., gigantification, hardening), and Mutant (always-on physical alteration, e.g., the user is born with a non-human body shape). The categories are rough; many Quirks span more than one. What matters mechanically is that each individual Quirk is a discrete, defined ability with named limits — an Emitter who shoots fire has a defined range, intensity, and tolerance, and a Transformation user who hardens their skin has a defined surface area and duration.

Within those defined limits, practical use is largely a matter of training. A young hero student spends years learning to operate at the edge of their Quirk's parameters: extending duration, refining control, finding novel applications. The series treats this as an athletic and intellectual project, with formal coursework, instructors, and combat exams.

A small number of canonical exceptions break the one-Quirk-per-person rule. The most prominent — One For All — is a stockpiling Quirk that can be deliberately transferred between users and accumulates the strength of past holders, allowing it to function as a vessel for additional inherited Quirks. Its dark counterpart, All For One, can take Quirks from others and grant them at will, reframing the rules of the system within the antagonist's faction.

## Limitations and costs

The first cost is biological. Most Quirks impose a physical toll on the user — overuse damages the body in Quirk-specific ways. A character who throws too many fire emissions overheats; one who pushes their muscle-Quirk past tolerance tears tendons; one whose Quirk has a "drawback" (intentional in fiction — many Quirks come with built-in costs) pays a defined price every time. The protagonist's One For All in particular causes severe self-injury when used at the edge of his physical capacity, and the early arc is structured around his learning to dose its use without breaking himself.

A second cost is knowledge. Quirks have to be learned, not just possessed. Untrained users are weaker, less precise, and more dangerous to themselves. Hero schools (UA High, Shiketsu, others) exist to train aspirants in their own Quirks and in supplementary skills (combat, rescue, law).

A third cost is social. Quirk use outside licensed contexts is illegal in the series' setting. Vigilantism, illegal property damage from out-of-control Quirks, and unlicensed civilian Quirk use are all crimes. The licensing system is rigorous: prospective heroes must pass exams and provisional licensing, and private use of a public-power-grade Quirk in self-defense is a contested legal area.

A fourth cost is the genetic ceiling. Quirks have their parameters set at manifestation; a user can train within those parameters, but cannot fundamentally exceed them. A character with a small flame Quirk will never be a city-leveling pyromancer through sheer practice. The one major counter-mechanism — One For All — is a closely held secret and the central engine of the protagonist's plot.

## Practitioner access

Universally distributed and innate. The ~80% of the population born with Quirks have them from early childhood; the ~20% who are Quirkless live as a stigmatized minority — the protagonist begins the series in this category. Inheritance is genealogical and somewhat unpredictable; pairing two strong Quirks does not guarantee a strong Quirk in the child, and major plot threads in the series (especially around the Todoroki family) interrogate the eugenic logic of selective marriage for Quirk strength.

## Cultural role

The system has reorganized society. Heroes are public servants supported by sponsorship, agencies, and merchandising; villainy is a recognized criminal subcategory; civilian Quirk use is regulated by law; "Quirk discrimination" exists as both a real social problem and a legal category. The series treats the existence of Quirks not as a hidden mystery but as the foundational fact of its world — schools teach Quirk theory, news media cover hero rankings, insurance contracts have Quirk damage clauses, and the everyday texture of life assumes that most people you meet can do something physically improbable.
