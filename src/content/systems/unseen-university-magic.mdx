## Mechanics

Wizardly magic on the **Discworld** manipulates the **thaumic field** — the ambient magical energy that permeates the Disc (a flat world carried on the backs of four elephants standing on a giant turtle). Magic is a fundamental force of the Disc's physics, as real as gravity but considerably less well-behaved. The eighth color, **octarine** — visible only to magical practitioners — suffuses areas of high thaumic concentration.

Wizards access magic through study, knowledge, and — when absolutely necessary — the actual casting of spells. The Unseen University maintains a library (guarded by the Librarian, an orangutan) that contains every magical text ever written, including some that have not been written yet. Spellbooks are semi-alive and dangerous; the most powerful grimoires are chained to their shelves to prevent them from attacking each other.

In theory, wizardly magic can accomplish almost anything: teleportation, transformation, elemental manipulation, summoning, divination, and reality alteration. In practice, the wizards of the modern Disc have learned that actually doing these things is catastrophically irresponsible. The central insight of modern Discworld wizardry is that **magic should not be used** — or rather, that a wizard's primary function is to prevent magic from being used by channeling the Disc's thaumic energy safely rather than deploying it.

This is because excessive magical use weakens the barriers between the Discworld and the **Dungeon Dimensions** — a formless void populated by incomprehensible Lovecraftian entities that desperately want to exist. Major magical events attract these entities, and holes torn in reality can allow them through. The wizards' institutional function is to act as **magical resistors** — absorbing and safely grounding the Disc's ambient magical energy so that it does not build up and cause spontaneous reality breaches.

HEX, the Unseen University's magical computer (built by Ponder Stibbons from ants, beehives, and rams' skulls), represents a newer approach: systematizing magic through proto-scientific methodology, much to the discomfort of traditionally-minded faculty.

## Limitations and costs

The primary limitation is **catastrophic risk**. Using magic at significant scale risks tearing reality and admitting Dungeon Dimension entities. The wizards' institutional memory includes several near-apocalyptic events caused by magical excess, and modern practice is defined by learned caution.

The second cost is **the sourcery problem**. The eighth son of an eighth son is a wizard; the eighth son of a wizard is a **sourceror** — a being of unlimited magical power who can reshape reality at will. Sourcerers are existential threats: the last one nearly destroyed the Disc. The wizards' celibacy tradition exists specifically to prevent sourcerers from being born.

The third cost is **institutional inertia**. The Unseen University discourages the practical use of magic so effectively that most wizards are spectacularly bad at actually casting spells. Rincewind — the Disc's most famous wizard — cannot reliably cast a single spell (though one of the most powerful spells in existence has lodged itself in his head and refuses to leave).

## Practitioner access

Wizardry is restricted to men (by Disc tradition, not metaphysical necessity — the equal-but-different separation between wizards and witches is cultural). Wizards are trained at the Unseen University, which accepts students through a combination of aptitude testing and the ability to survive faculty politics. The university is effectively an all-you-can-eat buffet with a magic problem attached.

## Cultural role

In Ankh-Morpork, the Unseen University is a venerable institution that the city tolerates because the alternative (unregulated wizards) is worse. The Patrician, Lord Vetinari, maintains a careful relationship with the University: he allows them their enormous food budget and internal autonomy in exchange for their not destroying the city. Wizards are recognized as a distinct social class — eccentric, impractical, and dangerous in the way that a loaded cannon is dangerous (mostly to itself, but you wouldn't want to stand nearby).
