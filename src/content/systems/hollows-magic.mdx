## Mechanics

Witch magic in the Hollows is organized into three branches, each with distinct energy sources, methods, and social implications. **Earth magic** is the slowest and most socially accepted branch. Practitioners use herbs, minerals, and organic materials to create potions, amulets, and charms — physical objects imbued with magical effects. An earth-magic sleep charm requires specific ingredients combined under specific conditions, and the finished amulet can be activated later by anyone who invokes it. Earth magic is limited in power but versatile and safe; it is the foundation of professional witch practice and the basis of a substantial commercial industry in charms and potions.

**Ley-line magic** draws raw energy from the ley lines — channels of magical power that crisscross the landscape, intersecting at nodes. A ley-line witch can tap a line directly, channeling energy through their body to produce faster and more powerful effects than earth magic allows: force blasts, shields, circles of containment, and direct magical attacks. The trade-off is danger — ley-line energy is harder to control, and channeling too much risks physical harm or loss of control. Ley-line magic requires no material components but demands greater skill and carries greater risk.

**Demon magic** is the most powerful branch and the most feared. Demons — ancient, immensely powerful beings trapped in the ever-after (a decaying parallel dimension accessible through ley lines) — can perform workings that dwarf anything a witch can accomplish alone. Accessing demon magic requires making a **deal** with a demon: the practitioner receives a curse, charm, or piece of knowledge, and in exchange owes the demon a service, usually taking the form of indentured labor in the ever-after. Demon curses are identifiable by the distinctive black stain they leave on the practitioner's aura, marking them as someone who has trafficked with demons.

The three branches interact: a skilled witch like Rachel Morgan can work both earth and ley-line magic, and the series follows her gradual, reluctant entanglement with demon magic as she faces threats that the first two branches cannot handle alone.

## Limitations and costs

Earth magic is limited by material requirements and preparation time. Every charm requires specific ingredients, properly prepared and combined according to established recipes. Improvisation is possible but risky, and the effects of earth magic are generally modest in scale — useful for everyday problems but inadequate against serious supernatural threats. The commercial charm industry means that some earth-magic effects are available to anyone who can afford to buy an amulet, democratizing access but also limiting the profession's exclusivity.

Ley-line magic's costs are physical and neurological. Drawing too much energy from a line causes pain, disorientation, and — in extreme cases — permanent damage. The practitioner's body acts as a conduit, and every conduit has a capacity limit. Additionally, ley-line workings require a **familiar** (a bound entity that helps filter and manage the energy flow) for safe practice at higher levels. Without one, the risk of burnout increases dramatically.

Demon magic's costs are the most severe and the most clearly structured. Every demon deal creates an **imbalance** that must be repaid, and demons are meticulous accountants. The price is always significant: time spent in servitude, a favor owed, a piece of knowledge surrendered. The black smut on a practitioner's aura from demon curses is publicly visible to anyone with witch-sight, carrying severe social stigma. Witch society treats demon magic as categorically evil, and practitioners who use it face ostracism, legal consequences, and the attention of the coven — the governing body of witch society.

## Practitioner access

Witch magic is broadly accessible within the witch population, which constitutes a significant minority of Inderlander society. Most witches learn earth magic from family or formal education, and many never progress beyond it. Ley-line aptitude requires additional talent and training, and not all witches possess it. Demon magic is theoretically available to any witch willing to make a deal, but the social prohibitions and practical dangers limit its practice to the desperate, the ambitious, and the foolish.

Elves, a separate Inderlander species, practice their own tradition of wild magic that predates the witch system and operates on different principles. Vampires (both living and undead) and werewolves possess innate supernatural abilities unrelated to the witch magic framework.

## Cultural role

In a world where the supernatural is publicly known and legally regulated, the three-branch system serves as both a practical taxonomy and a moral framework. Earth magic is respectable, ley-line magic is tolerated with caution, and demon magic is forbidden — a classification that maps loosely onto conventional moral categories of white, grey, and black magic. The series systematically complicates this classification as Rachel Morgan discovers that the boundaries between branches are less stable than witch society insists, and that the historical relationship between witches and demons is far more intimate than orthodox teaching admits.

The coven of ethical standards enforces magical law within witch society, but its authority is limited and its judgments are often politically motivated. The broader Inderlander legal system treats magical crimes as a category within its jurisdiction, but enforcement is inconsistent and the law frequently lags behind the reality of magical practice.
