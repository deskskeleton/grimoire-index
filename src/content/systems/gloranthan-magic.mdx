## Mechanics

Gloranthan magic is distinguished from virtually every other TTRPG magic system by its refusal to present a single unified theory of how magic works. Instead, the setting posits three fundamentally distinct magical traditions — Spirit Magic, Rune Magic, and Sorcery — each grounded in a different metaphysical claim about the nature of reality. Spirit Magic operates on the animist principle that the world is populated by spirits who can be bargained with, bound, or propitiated. Rune Magic operates on the theist principle that the gods created the world and continue to sustain it through cosmic Runes whose power can be accessed through devotion and sacrifice. Sorcery operates on the philosophical principle that magical energy obeys discoverable laws that can be manipulated through intellect and technique. These are not three flavors of the same system — they use different mechanical subsystems, different resource economies, and different character advancement paths.

Spirit Magic in RuneQuest functions as the most accessible magical tradition, available to virtually any character regardless of cultural background. Spells are acquired by bargaining with spirits during spirit combat — a contested POW roll that determines whether the character can bind the spirit's knowledge. Once learned, Spirit Magic spells cost Magic Points to cast (a recoverable resource derived from the POW characteristic) and produce relatively modest, immediate effects: Bladesharp adds damage to a weapon, Healing restores hit points, Disruption deals direct magical damage. The mechanical simplicity of Spirit Magic — fixed-cost spells with straightforward effects — makes it the system's baseline magical competency, the thing that almost everyone in Glorantha can do to some degree. It also establishes the principle that magic in Glorantha is not extraordinary but ordinary, woven into the fabric of daily life.

Rune Magic represents the setting's most culturally significant magical tradition and the one most tightly integrated with the character advancement system. To access Rune Magic, a character must join a cult — a religious organization dedicated to a specific deity — and advance through its ranks by demonstrating devotion. The mechanical cost of Rune Magic is the permanent sacrifice of POW points to the deity, which are converted into Rune Points that fuel divine spells. This is not a recoverable resource in the conventional sense; Rune Points refresh only through worship at holy sites during sacred time, creating a liturgical calendar that shapes the rhythm of play. The spells themselves are tied to the deity's Runes — Orlanth grants storm and movement magic, Ernalda grants earth and fertility magic, Humakt grants death and truth magic — meaning that a character's magical capabilities are a direct expression of their religious identity.

Sorcery, the third tradition, is the most mechanically complex and the least accessible to starting characters. Practiced primarily by the Malkioni cultures of western Glorantha, Sorcery treats magic as a technical discipline governed by manipulable parameters: Range, Duration, Intensity, and number of targets can all be adjusted by the caster, creating a flexible but demanding casting system. A sorcerer does not simply cast a spell; they decide how far it reaches, how long it lasts, and how powerful it is, spending Magic Points proportional to the total manipulation. This produces a system where sorcerers have theoretically unlimited flexibility but practically limited resources, and where the optimal configuration of a spell depends on precise situational assessment. The design models the Malkioni cultural self-image as rationalists and philosophers while ensuring that their magic feels qualitatively different from the spirit-negotiation and god-worship of other traditions.

## Limitations and costs

The POW sacrifice required for Rune Magic creates the system's most distinctive cost structure and its most powerful worldbuilding tool. When a character sacrifices a point of POW to gain a Rune Point, they are permanently reducing their personal magical reservoir — Magic Points derive from POW, so sacrifice literally diminishes the character's baseline magical capacity in exchange for access to more powerful divine effects. This is not just a mechanical trade-off; it models the theological concept that communion with the divine requires genuine self-surrender. A Rune Lord who has sacrificed extensively to their god is simultaneously more powerful (more Rune Points, access to greater divine magic) and more diminished (lower POW, fewer Magic Points, more vulnerable in spirit combat). The system makes devotion costly in ways that are felt at the table, not just described in fiction.

The social costs of magical practice in Glorantha are mechanical rather than merely narrative. Cult membership, required for Rune Magic, comes with behavioral obligations enforced through the game's Passions and Reputation systems. An initiate of Humakt must forswear resurrection and sever ties with their family. A devotee of Ernalda must prioritize community welfare over personal advancement. Violating these obligations can result in loss of Rune Point access — the god literally withdrawing their favor — which means that a character's moral choices have direct mechanical consequences for their magical capability. This transforms cult membership from a character background detail into an ongoing source of dramatic tension, as characters must balance their religious obligations against the practical demands of adventuring.

Spirit Magic's limitations are less dramatic but equally important for establishing the system's internal balance. Spirit Magic spells are capped in intensity by the caster's available Magic Points, recover relatively quickly, and produce effects that are useful but rarely decisive. A character relying solely on Spirit Magic can enhance their combat capability or provide minor healing, but they cannot reshape the battlefield, commune with gods, or manipulate fundamental forces. This ceiling ensures that Spirit Magic serves as a foundation rather than a replacement for the more demanding traditions, and it models the Gloranthan principle that deeper magical engagement requires deeper commitment — you get what you give.

Sorcery's limitations center on the tension between flexibility and efficiency. Every parameter a sorcerer manipulates (Range, Duration, Intensity, targets) costs additional Magic Points, meaning that a maximally configured spell may be prohibitively expensive. Sorcerers must also learn manipulation techniques separately from spells, so a beginning sorcerer may know powerful spells but lack the technique to cast them at useful ranges or durations. The extended casting times for complex sorcery — some configurations require multiple rounds of concentration — create vulnerability windows that Spirit Magic and Rune Magic users do not face. The design ensures that Sorcery's theoretical superiority in flexibility is counterbalanced by practical limitations in resource management and action economy.

## Practitioner access

Glorantha's most radical design assertion is that magic is universally accessible. There are no non-magical characters in the setting — every farmer, herder, and craftsperson in Glorantha knows at least basic Spirit Magic, and most belong to a cult that grants them some degree of Rune Magic. This universality is not a setting detail that the mechanics ignore; it is baked into character creation, where all starting characters receive Spirit Magic spells and cult membership as part of their cultural package. The design consequence is that magic in Glorantha is not a specialization that sets certain characters apart but a baseline competency that everyone shares. What distinguishes characters is not whether they have magic but what kind of magic they have and how deeply they have committed to it.

The cult initiation system creates a structured path of escalating commitment and reward. An initiate receives access to cult Spirit Magic and limited Rune Magic. A Rune-level member (Rune Priest or Rune Lord) gains expanded Rune Magic access and divine intervention capability but must dedicate a significant portion of their time and resources to cult activities. A devotee or High Priest gains the most powerful magic but is essentially a full-time religious functionary. Each tier requires greater sacrifice — more POW, more time, more behavioral restriction — and grants greater capability. The system models religious hierarchy as a genuine progression path rather than a background detail, and it produces characters whose magical power is inseparable from their social and religious identity.

Cultural tradition determines which magical system a character primarily uses, and this determination is not merely cosmetic. An Orlanthi character from Sartar practices Spirit Magic and Rune Magic through their tribal cults and has no meaningful access to Sorcery. A Malkioni character from Seshnela practices Sorcery as their primary tradition and may view Spirit Magic and Rune Magic as primitive superstitions. A Praxian nomad emphasizes Spirit Magic and shamanic practice, with Rune Magic accessed through different cults than the Orlanthi worship. These cultural packages mean that a character's magical capabilities are a function of where they come from, not just what they choose at character creation, and the system uses this to teach players about the diversity of Gloranthan cultures through direct mechanical experience.

Shamanism represents a specialized access path within the Spirit Magic tradition that deserves separate consideration. Shamans undergo a transformative initiation that grants them access to the Spirit World — a parallel plane of existence populated by spirits of varying power and disposition. A practicing shaman can fetch spirits, negotiate with them, bind them into objects or allies, and travel the Spirit World to seek knowledge or power. This is mechanically demanding — shamanic characters require mastery of the spirit combat system and careful management of bound spirits — but it produces a unique play experience where the character's primary interaction is with the invisible world rather than the material one. The shaman's role in Gloranthan society as healer, advisor, and spiritual guardian is directly modeled by their mechanical capabilities.

## Cultural role

Gloranthan magic functions primarily as an anthropological worldbuilding tool, and this is its most important contribution to TTRPG design. By tying magical traditions to cultural worldviews, Stafford created a setting where learning about a culture's magic is identical to learning about its values, cosmology, and social structure. When a player discovers that Orlanthi Rune Magic emphasizes storm, movement, and freedom — reflecting the Orlanthi cultural values of independence and resistance to tyranny — they are simultaneously learning game mechanics and absorbing worldbuilding. This dual function means that the magic system does not compete with the setting for cognitive bandwidth; instead, they reinforce each other, making both more memorable.

The tri-partite structure also serves as a commentary on religious pluralism and cultural relativism that is unusually sophisticated for a TTRPG. In most fantasy settings, there is a true cosmology that players can eventually discover, and all magical traditions are ultimately expressions of the same underlying reality. Glorantha is deliberately ambiguous on this point. Theists claim that the gods are real and that spirits and sorcerous forces are subordinate to divine will. Animists claim that spirits are the fundamental reality and that gods are merely very powerful spirits. Sorcerers claim that both are wrong and that magical energy follows impersonal laws. The game does not adjudicate between these claims — mechanically, all three traditions work — and this irresolution is a feature rather than a flaw. It models a world where cultural perspective genuinely shapes experience, not just interpretation.

The Rune system — cosmic forces like Air, Earth, Fire, Water, Death, Fertility, Harmony, Disorder — provides a symbolic language that permeates every aspect of the setting and produces emergent narrative effects at the table. Characters have Rune affinities that affect their personality, their magic, and their relationships with cults. A character with a high Death Rune affinity is drawn toward Humakti worship and martial magic but struggles with relationships and community, because Death opposes Fertility in the Rune cosmology. These mechanical relationships create character dynamics that feel organic rather than prescribed — a character's magical development and personal growth are linked through the Rune system in ways that generate story naturally.

The universal accessibility of magic in Glorantha also makes a quiet but important statement about the nature of fantasy settings. By refusing to create a class of non-magical characters, the system rejects the common fantasy premise that magic is rare and mysterious. Instead, it proposes that magic can be ordinary and pervasive without being uninteresting — that the dramatic potential of a magic system lies not in its scarcity but in its diversity and its integration with social structures. This design choice has influenced a generation of subsequent games that have moved away from the wizard-as-specialist model toward settings where magical engagement is a spectrum rather than a binary. The insight that "everyone has magic but different magic" produces richer worldbuilding than "most people have no magic" has proven to be one of Glorantha's most durable contributions to the form.
