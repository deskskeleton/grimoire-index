The Felix Castor novels (Mike Carey, 2006-2009) are set in a London where, roughly a decade before the series begins, the dead started coming back. Nobody knows why. Ghosts became commonplace, zombies (the dead returning to their own bodies) appeared in increasing numbers, and demons — entities from somewhere else that possess living hosts — arrived alongside them. The phenomenon is global, unexplained, and ongoing, and society has adapted in pragmatic, unglamorous ways: ghost-infested buildings lose property value, the church debates theological implications without resolution, and exorcists have become a recognized if unregulated profession, somewhere between pest control and private investigation on the social spectrum.

The world is deliberately mundane in its treatment of the supernatural. There is no hidden magical society, no ancient conspiracy, no grand cosmological framework — just a world where the dead are an ambient urban nuisance and the people who deal with them are working-class freelancers hustling for their next job. Felix Castor operates out of this milieu, a tin-whistle-playing exorcist whose sensitivity to the dead is a knack he was born with rather than a power he earned, and whose cases tend to pull him into the messier intersections of ghost activity, demonic possession, and human crime.
