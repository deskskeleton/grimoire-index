## Setting

Studio Ghibli's films do not share a single continuity but constitute a unified magical tradition through their consistent approach to the supernatural. Across dozens of films, Ghibli presents worlds where spirits inhabit rivers and forests, where witches fly on broomsticks as a matter of professional tradition, where cursed princes transform into wolves, and where soot sprites perform custodial duties in boiler rooms. The magic in these worlds is characterized by its integration with nature and domesticity — Kiki's magic is tied to her emotional confidence, Chihiro navigates the spirit world through labor and compassion, the forest spirits in Mononoke are ecological forces rather than fantasy creatures, and Totoro is simultaneously a nature god and a fuzzy neighbor. This approach treats magic not as a system to be mastered but as a dimension of reality that requires emotional attunement to perceive and participate in, making Ghibli's collective body of work one of the most influential magical traditions in visual storytelling.
