## Setting

Alan Garner's fantasy novels are set in real places — Alderley Edge in Cheshire, the Dee valley in North Wales — but treat the landscape as a repository of living myth. In *The Weirdstone of Brisingamen* (1960) and *The Moon of Gomrath* (1963), the wizard Cadellin Silverbrow guards a company of sleeping knights beneath Alderley Edge, waiting for the day they will be needed. The children Colin and Susan stumble into this hidden world when Susan's bracelet — the Weirdstone of Brisingamen, a protective talisman — is revealed to be the stone that maintains the knights' enchanted sleep. Beneath the Edge, the forces of Nastrond (the svart-alfar, the Morrigan) press against Cadellin's defences.

In *The Owl Service* (1967), Garner moves from high fantasy to psychological myth. Three teenagers in a Welsh valley discover a dinner service patterned with owls, and the ancient story of Blodeuwedd — the woman made of flowers in the *Mabinogion* who was transformed into an owl as punishment for betrayal — begins to replay through them. The valley itself holds the myth's energy, and the characters are drawn into the roles of Lleu, Gronw, and Blodeuwedd whether they choose to or not. Magic in Garner's work is not a skill to be learned but a force that inheres in place and story, activated by proximity and emotional resonance.
