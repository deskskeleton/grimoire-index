## Setting

Scott Hawkins's *The Library at Mount Char* (2015) is set in what appears to be modern suburban America, but the familiar surface conceals a metaphysical structure of staggering scope. **Father** — a being who may be the Judeo-Christian God, or something that killed and replaced God — maintains a **Library** that exists partially outside normal space and time. The Library contains all knowledge that exists or could exist, organized into **twelve catalogs**, each covering a fundamental domain of reality: languages, war, healing, the future, animals, death, and others.

Father adopted twelve children from the ruins of a neighbourhood he destroyed and assigned each child to master one catalog. The training was brutal, extending over thousands of subjective years and involving repeated death and resurrection. Each child who masters their catalog gains absolute, godlike authority within that domain — Carolyn speaks every language including those of animals and the dead, David is an unstoppable engine of violence, Jennifer can heal any wound. Father himself has mastered all twelve catalogs and is functionally omnipotent. The novel follows Carolyn's plan to replace Father after his apparent death, and the reader is kept deliberately uncertain about the true nature and limits of the Library's power.
