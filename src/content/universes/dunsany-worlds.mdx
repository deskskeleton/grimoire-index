## Setting

Lord Dunsany (Edward Plunkett, 18th Baron of Dunsany) was among the earliest architects of modern fantasy, and his influence radiates through Tolkien, Lovecraft, Le Guin, and the entire tradition. His works do not share a single consistent setting but rather a sensibility: a world (or many worlds) where the numinous is always close at hand, where gods act from caprice rather than justice, and where the boundary between the mundane and the marvellous is a matter of attention rather than geography.

In *The Gods of Pegana* (1905) and *Time and the Gods* (1906), Dunsany creates an original mythology — the god MANA-YOOD-SUSHAI sleeps while lesser gods play at creating worlds, and when he wakes, all things end. In *The King of Elfland's Daughter* (1924), the most sustained of his fantasies, Elfland is a realm of absolute beauty and arrested time that borders on the English countryside. The parliament of the village of Erl sends Alveric to win an elf-bride, but the collision between mortal time and Elfland's timelessness proves tragic for both sides. *The Book of Wonder* (1912) and *A Dreamer's Tales* (1910) offer shorter excursions into cities at the world's edge, thieves who steal from the gods, and idle kings whose empires crumble into legend.
