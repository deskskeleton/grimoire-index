## Overview

In Gaiman's America, every wave of immigrants brought their gods with them — Norse, Slavic, West African, Egyptian, and dozens more. These gods are real, physical beings whose power is proportional to the belief invested in them. As worship fades, the old gods diminish, scraping by on small hustles and half-forgotten rituals in roadside motels and small-town diners across the heartland.

Meanwhile, new gods have risen from the things Americans actually worship now: Media, Technology, the Internet, the stock market. The central conflict of the setting is a brewing war between old and new for the finite, dwindling resource of American attention. The landscape itself matters — the novel treats American geography as mythic space, with roadside attractions, sacred sites, and forgotten crossroads serving as nexus points where divine power concentrates.
