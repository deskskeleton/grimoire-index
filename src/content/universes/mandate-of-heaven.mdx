## Setting

Shelley Parker-Chan's Radiant Emperor duology (*She Who Became the Sun*, 2021; *He Who Drowned the World*, 2022) reimagines the rise of the Ming dynasty through the lens of gender, desire, and fate. The Mongol Yuan dynasty rules a starving China, and peasant rebellions are rising across the land. In this world, the Mandate of Heaven is not merely a political concept but a tangible metaphysical reality: those destined for greatness burn with visible fate, while those without it are nothing — literally invisible to the supernatural order, fated to be forgotten.

Zhu Chongba, born a peasant girl in a devastated village, steals the identity and fate of her dead brother and claws her way from a Buddhist monastery to the leadership of a rebel army. Her power comes not from training or bloodline but from sheer refusal to accept nothingness — from desire so fierce it bends fate itself. Opposing her is General Ouyang, a eunuch warrior whose fate is entangled with vengeance, and Madam Zhang, who perceives and manipulates the threads of destiny. The world is haunted by hungry ghosts — the spirits of the fateless who linger without purpose — and the question of who deserves a fate is inseparable from questions of gender, identity, and the violence required to claim a place in history.
