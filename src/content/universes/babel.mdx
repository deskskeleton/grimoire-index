## Setting

R. F. Kuang's *Babel, or the Necessity of Violence: An Arcane History of the Oxford Translators' Revolution* (2022) is set in an alternate 1830s England where the British Empire's industrial and military supremacy derives not from coal and steam alone but from **silverwork** — a magical technology based on silver bars inscribed with matched pairs of words from different languages. The magic is powered by the semantic gap between the words: the meaning that exists in one language but cannot be perfectly captured in another. The greater the linguistic and cultural distance between the source languages, the more potent the effect.

Oxford's **Royal Institute of Translation** (Babel) is the centre of this system, training translators from across the world — China, India, Haiti, West Africa — whose native fluency in distant languages makes them uniquely valuable. The Institute is simultaneously a place of genuine scholarship and a mechanism of imperial extraction: the scholars whose linguistic knowledge powers British industry are drawn from the very nations Britain colonises, and the system cannot function without the diversity it exploits. The novel's central conflict follows Robin Swift, a Canton-born translator, as he is drawn into Hermes, an underground resistance movement that seeks to break Babel's monopoly on silverwork and end the imperial machinery it sustains.
